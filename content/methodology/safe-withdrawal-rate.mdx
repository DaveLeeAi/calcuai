## The Origin of the 4% Rule

In 1994, financial planner William Bengen published "Determining Withdrawal Rates Using Historical Data" in the *Journal of Financial Planning*. He tested every possible 30-year retirement period using U.S. stock and bond returns from 1926 onward, asking: what is the highest initial withdrawal rate that never depleted a portfolio?

His answer: **4.15%** from a portfolio allocated 50–75% to stocks and the rest to intermediate-term government bonds. A retiree starting with $1,000,000 could withdraw $41,500 in year one, increase that amount by inflation each year, and never run out of money over any historical 30-year window.

## The Mathematical Framework

The safe withdrawal rate problem is a recursive simulation. For each year $t$:

$$B_t = (B_{t-1} - W_t) \times (1 + R_t)$$

Where:

- **$B_t$** = portfolio balance at end of year $t$
- **$W_t$** = withdrawal in year $t$ (inflation-adjusted: $W_t = W_1 \times (1 + i)^{t-1}$)
- **$R_t$** = portfolio return in year $t$ (weighted by asset allocation)
- **$i$** = inflation rate
- **$W_1$** = initial withdrawal amount = $B_0 \times SWR$

The portfolio survives if $B_t > 0$ for all $t$ from 1 to $N$ (the retirement horizon). The safe withdrawal rate is the highest $SWR$ such that survival holds across all historical periods.

## The Trinity Study Extension

In 1998, professors Philip Cooley, Carl Hubbard, and Daniel Walz at Trinity University expanded Bengen's work in "Retirement Savings: Choosing a Withdrawal Rate That Is Sustainable." They tested multiple asset allocations, withdrawal rates, and time horizons:

| Withdrawal Rate | 15 Years | 20 Years | 25 Years | 30 Years |
|----------------|----------|----------|----------|----------|
| 3% | 100% | 100% | 100% | 100% |
| 4% | 100% | 100% | 100% | 95% |
| 5% | 100% | 94% | 85% | 76% |
| 6% | 91% | 79% | 63% | 56% |
| 7% | 79% | 59% | 41% | 36% |

*Success rates for a 50% stock / 50% bond portfolio, 1926–1995, inflation-adjusted withdrawals.*

The 4% rate achieved 95% success over 30 years. The 5% failure cases occurred primarily when retirement began just before a prolonged bear market (e.g., starting in 1966 before 15+ years of high inflation and poor real returns).

## Sequence of Returns Risk

The critical insight from both Bengen and Trinity is **sequence of returns risk**: the order of investment returns matters as much as the average return during retirement. Two retirees with identical average 7% returns over 30 years can have vastly different outcomes if one experiences poor returns early (while making withdrawals) and the other experiences poor returns late.

A retiree starting in 1973 with $1,000,000 and a 5% withdrawal ($50,000/year, inflation-adjusted) would have been depleted by year 25 — not because average returns were bad, but because the 1973–1974 bear market (-37% cumulative) hit during the critical early withdrawal years.

This risk does not exist during the accumulation phase, because early losses are offset by continued contributions. It only emerges when you're withdrawing from the portfolio.

## Modern Adjustments to the 4% Rule

Since Bengen's original work, several factors have led researchers to question whether 4% remains appropriate:

**Arguments for a lower rate (3.0%–3.5%):**
- Lower future expected returns (bond yields historically lower than 1926–1990 averages)
- Longer retirements (FIRE practitioners may need 50+ year horizons, not 30)
- International markets have historically returned less than US equities
- Fees and taxes reduce effective returns

**Arguments for a higher rate (4.5%–5.0%):**
- Flexible spending strategies — reducing withdrawals 10–25% during bear markets dramatically improves survival
- Part-time work or Social Security supplements
- Most historical periods ended with the retiree having 2–5x their starting balance, suggesting 4% is overly conservative for the median case
- The guardrails approach: raise withdrawals when portfolio grows, cut when it shrinks

## Variable Withdrawal Strategies

Modern retirement planning has moved beyond fixed-percentage rules:

**Guardrails Method (Guyton-Klinger):** Set upper and lower spending limits (e.g., ±20% from target). If portfolio growth pushes spending below the floor, raise it. If portfolio decline pushes spending above the ceiling, cut it. This approach has supported 5.2%–5.6% initial rates with high success.

**Required Minimum Distribution (RMD) Method:** Each year, divide the portfolio by remaining life expectancy. This automatically reduces withdrawals as the portfolio declines and increases them as it grows. The downside is income volatility.

**Constant-Percentage Method:** Withdraw a fixed percentage (e.g., 4%) of the current portfolio each year — not a fixed dollar amount. This guarantees the portfolio never reaches zero, but creates income volatility.

## Assumptions and Limitations

1. **U.S. market data only** — historical success rates are based on U.S. equity and bond returns, which have been among the best in the world. A global portfolio may warrant a lower SWR.
2. **No taxes modeled** — withdrawals from traditional IRAs and 401(k)s are taxed as ordinary income, reducing the effective withdrawal.
3. **No healthcare costs** — medical expenses in retirement often exceed general inflation, potentially requiring higher withdrawals.
4. **Fixed asset allocation** — most studies assume annual rebalancing to maintain the stock/bond split. Failure to rebalance increases risk.
5. **30-year horizon** — the 4% rule was designed for a 30-year retirement (age 65 to 95). Early retirees needing 40–60 year horizons should use 3.0%–3.5%.
6. **No Social Security** — the model assumes the portfolio is the sole income source. Social Security income effectively reduces the required withdrawal rate.
