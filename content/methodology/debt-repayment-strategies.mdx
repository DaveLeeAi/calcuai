## The Three Primary Debt Repayment Methods

When carrying multiple debts, the order in which you pay them off significantly affects total interest paid and the psychological experience of debt reduction. The three dominant strategies are the **debt avalanche** (highest rate first), the **debt snowball** (smallest balance first), and **debt consolidation** (single replacement loan).

## Debt Avalanche: Mathematically Optimal

The avalanche method ranks debts by interest rate, highest first. You make minimum payments on all debts except the highest-rate one, which receives all available extra payments. When that debt is paid off, the freed payment rolls to the next-highest-rate debt.

**Example:** Three debts:
- Credit Card A: $8,000 at 22% APR, $200 minimum
- Credit Card B: $3,000 at 18% APR, $75 minimum
- Personal Loan: $12,000 at 8% APR, $250 minimum

With $200/month extra ($725 total), the avalanche targets Credit Card A first:

$$\text{Interest saved vs. snowball} = \text{Higher-rate debt eliminated first}$$

**Result:** Total interest paid = $4,812. Debt-free in 38 months.

The avalanche is always the mathematically optimal strategy because it minimizes the time money spends accruing interest at the highest rate. Every dollar of extra payment toward the highest-rate debt saves more interest per dollar than directing it anywhere else.

## Debt Snowball: Psychologically Powerful

The snowball method ranks debts by balance, smallest first, regardless of interest rate. The mathematical sacrifice is trading some extra interest for faster "wins" — the psychological boost of eliminating entire debts sooner.

Using the same debts above, the snowball targets Credit Card B ($3,000) first. It's paid off in approximately 7 months, freeing up $275/month ($75 minimum + $200 extra) to roll into Credit Card A.

**Result:** Total interest paid = $5,341 — approximately $529 more than avalanche. Debt-free in 39 months (one month longer).

The snowball's advantage is behavioral. A 2012 study by researchers at Northwestern's Kellogg School of Management (published in the *Journal of Consumer Research*) found that consumers who used the snowball method were more likely to persist and eliminate all debt, even though the avalanche was cheaper. The "small wins" of eliminating accounts provide motivation that keeps people on track.

## When Snowball Beats Avalanche in Practice

The snowball outperforms the avalanche in total cost when:
- The highest-rate debt also has the smallest balance (the methods converge)
- Rate differences between debts are small (<2–3 percentage points)
- The psychological benefit prevents the borrower from abandoning the plan

The avalanche advantage grows when:
- Rate spreads are large (e.g., 22% credit card vs. 4% student loan)
- High-rate debts have large balances
- The borrower is disciplined regardless of strategy

## Debt Consolidation: One Loan to Replace Many

Consolidation replaces multiple debts with a single loan, ideally at a lower interest rate. The primary vehicle types:

| Method | Typical Rate | Term | Risk Level |
|--------|-------------|------|-----------|
| Personal loan | 6%–12% | 2–7 years | Low (unsecured) |
| Balance transfer card | 0% intro (12–21 months) | 1–2 years | Medium (rate jumps after promo) |
| Home equity loan/HELOC | 5%–9% | 5–30 years | High (secured by home) |
| 401(k) loan | Prime +1% | Up to 5 years | High (retirement risk) |

**Using the same example:** Consolidating all $23,000 at 8% for 5 years:
- Monthly payment: $467
- Total interest: $5,004
- Monthly cash flow freed: $258 ($725 - $467)

Consolidation interest ($5,004) exceeds the avalanche ($4,812) but reduces the monthly payment by $258. It saves $337 vs. the snowball ($5,341).

## The Consolidation Trap

Consolidation fails when borrowers:
1. **Run up new balances** on freed credit cards — the most common failure mode. Now they have the consolidation loan *plus* new credit card debt.
2. **Extend the term** too long — a 7-year consolidation at 8% costs more total interest than an aggressive 3-year avalanche at 18%, because the lower rate is offset by years of additional compounding.
3. **Take a secured loan** (home equity) for unsecured debt — converting credit card debt into mortgage debt puts the home at risk.

Consolidation works best when combined with a commitment to stop using credit cards during repayment and when the rate reduction is significant (e.g., 18% → 8%, not 12% → 10%).

## Hybrid Strategies

Experienced debt planners often combine approaches:

- **Consolidate high-rate debts** into a personal loan, then **avalanche** any remaining debts
- Use a **0% balance transfer** for the highest-rate card, then **snowball** the rest
- **Avalanche** mathematically but set a "quick win" — pay off one small debt first for motivation, then switch to avalanche order

## Assumptions and Limitations

1. **Fixed minimum payments** — in practice, minimum payments decrease as balances drop. Most calculators use the initial minimum for consistency.
2. **No new debt** — all strategies assume no additional borrowing during repayment.
3. **Fixed rates** — variable-rate debts (credit cards, HELOCs) may change, altering the optimal strategy.
4. **No fees** — balance transfer fees (3–5%), origination fees, and prepayment penalties affect total cost.
5. **Tax implications** — home equity loan interest may be deductible (consult a tax advisor); credit card interest is not.
