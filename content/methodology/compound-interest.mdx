## The Compound Interest Formula

The standard compound interest formula calculates the future value of a principal amount that earns interest on both the initial deposit and previously accumulated interest:

$$A = P\left(1 + \frac{r}{n}\right)^{nt}$$

Where:

- **$A$** = final amount (principal + interest)
- **$P$** = principal (initial deposit)
- **$r$** = annual interest rate (as a decimal, e.g., 0.07 for 7%)
- **$n$** = number of compounding periods per year
- **$t$** = time in years

This formula is the foundation for virtually every savings, investment, and retirement calculator. It appears in standardized financial education materials from the SEC, CFA Institute, and Federal Reserve.

## Derivation: From Simple Interest to Exponential Growth

Simple interest pays a flat amount each period. If you invest $10,000 at 7% simple interest for 10 years, you earn $700/year for a total of $17,000.

Simple interest formula:

$$A_{\text{simple}} = P(1 + rt) = 10{,}000 \times (1 + 0.07 \times 10) = \$17{,}000$$

Compound interest reinvests each period's earnings. After year 1, your $10,000 earns $700 in interest, giving you $10,700. In year 2, you earn interest on $10,700 — not just the original $10,000. This reinvestment creates exponential rather than linear growth.

For annual compounding, each year multiplies the balance by $(1 + r)$:

- After 1 year: $P(1 + r)$
- After 2 years: $P(1 + r)^2$
- After $t$ years: $P(1 + r)^t$

When interest compounds $n$ times per year, each period applies a rate of $r/n$, and there are $nt$ total periods — yielding the general formula $P(1 + r/n)^{nt}$.

## Compounding Frequency Comparison

How much does compounding frequency actually matter? Here is $10,000 invested at 7% annual rate for 10 years:

| Frequency | $n$ | Formula | Final Value | Interest Earned |
|-----------|-----|---------|-------------|-----------------|
| Annual | 1 | $10{,}000(1.07)^{10}$ | $19,671.51 | $9,671.51 |
| Semi-annual | 2 | $10{,}000(1.035)^{20}$ | $19,897.89 | $9,897.89 |
| Quarterly | 4 | $10{,}000(1.0175)^{40}$ | $20,015.97 | $10,015.97 |
| Monthly | 12 | $10{,}000(1.00583)^{120}$ | $20,096.61 | $10,096.61 |
| Daily | 365 | $10{,}000(1.000192)^{3650}$ | $20,137.05 | $10,137.05 |
| Continuous | ∞ | $10{,}000 \times e^{0.7}$ | $20,137.53 | $10,137.53 |

The jump from annual to monthly compounding adds $425.10 in interest. The jump from monthly to daily adds only $40.44. Beyond daily compounding, the gains are negligible — less than $0.48 separates daily from continuous over a decade.

## The Rule of 72

The Rule of 72 is a mental math shortcut for estimating how long it takes money to double at a given interest rate:

$$\text{Years to double} \approx \frac{72}{r\%}$$

At 7% interest: $72 / 7 \approx 10.3$ years. The exact answer using the compound interest formula is $\ln(2)/\ln(1.07) = 10.24$ years — the Rule of 72 is within 1% accuracy.

**When the Rule of 72 breaks down:** It is most accurate for rates between 4% and 12%. At very low rates (below 2%) or very high rates (above 20%), the approximation diverges. For rates above 20%, the Rule of 69.3 (using $\ln(2) \times 100 \approx 69.3$) provides better accuracy.

## Continuous Compounding

As compounding frequency increases toward infinity, the formula converges to:

$$A = Pe^{rt}$$

Where $e \approx 2.71828$ is Euler's number. This is derived from the mathematical limit:

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} P\left(1 + \frac{r}{n}\right)^{nt} = Pe^{rt}$$

Continuous compounding is primarily used in theoretical finance (Black-Scholes option pricing model) and as an upper bound for discrete compounding. In practice, the difference between daily and continuous compounding is trivially small for consumer savings products.

## Assumptions and Limitations

The compound interest formula assumes:

1. **Constant interest rate** — the rate $r$ never changes over the investment period. In reality, savings account rates fluctuate, and investment returns vary dramatically year to year.
2. **No withdrawals or additional deposits** — the formula models a single lump-sum investment left untouched. For regular contributions, you need the future value of an annuity formula.
3. **No taxes** — interest earned in taxable accounts is subject to income tax, which reduces the effective compounding rate. A 7% return taxed at 24% effectively compounds at approximately 5.32%.
4. **No inflation adjustment** — the formula returns nominal (not real) future value. At 3% inflation, $20,000 in 10 years has the purchasing power of roughly $14,882 in today's dollars.
5. **Interest is reinvested at the same rate** — the formula assumes earned interest can always be reinvested at rate $r$. This "reinvestment risk" is particularly relevant for bonds and CDs where rates may differ at renewal.

For long-term projections (20+ years), the constant-rate assumption introduces significant uncertainty. Historical stock market returns average roughly 10% nominal (7% real) per year, but actual yearly returns range from −37% to +53%. The compound interest formula gives an expected trajectory, not a guarantee.
