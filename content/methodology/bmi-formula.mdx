## The BMI Formula

Body Mass Index (BMI) is a numerical value derived from a person's weight and height. The metric formula is:

$$\text{BMI} = \frac{\text{weight (kg)}}{\text{height (m)}^2}$$

For imperial units (pounds and inches):

$$\text{BMI} = 703 \times \frac{\text{weight (lb)}}{\text{height (in)}^2}$$

The conversion factor 703 accounts for the unit difference: $1 \text{ kg} = 2.20462 \text{ lb}$ and $1 \text{ m} = 39.3701 \text{ in}$, so $(39.3701)^2 / 2.20462 \approx 703.07$.

A person who weighs 170 lb and stands 5'9" (69 inches):

$$\text{BMI} = 703 \times \frac{170}{69^2} = 703 \times \frac{170}{4761} = 25.1$$

## WHO Classification Thresholds

The World Health Organization defines BMI categories for adults (age 20+):

| Category | BMI Range | Risk Level |
|----------|-----------|------------|
| Underweight | Below 18.5 | Increased (nutritional deficiency) |
| Normal weight | 18.5 – 24.9 | Average |
| Overweight | 25.0 – 29.9 | Increased |
| Obese Class I | 30.0 – 34.9 | High |
| Obese Class II | 35.0 – 39.9 | Very High |
| Obese Class III | 40.0 and above | Extremely High |

These thresholds are based on epidemiological data correlating BMI ranges with all-cause mortality and morbidity rates across large populations. The "normal" range of 18.5–24.9 corresponds to the BMI range with the lowest statistical risk of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, and certain cancers.

## Historical Context: Quetelet's Index

Belgian mathematician Adolphe Quetelet developed the BMI formula in 1832 as part of his work on "social physics" — applying statistical methods to human populations. He called it the Quetelet Index.

Quetelet designed the formula to characterize the "average man" across populations, not to diagnose individual health. He observed that body weight scales roughly with the square of height in large population samples. The formula was never intended as a clinical screening tool for individuals.

The term "Body Mass Index" was coined by physiologist Ancel Keys in a 1972 paper that evaluated several weight-for-height indices and concluded that $W/H^2$ (Quetelet's formula) was the best simple proxy for body fatness in population studies. Keys explicitly noted that "BMI is appropriate for population studies and inappropriate for individual diagnosis."

## Known Limitations

BMI is widely used because it is cheap, fast, and requires no equipment beyond a scale and measuring tape. However, it has significant limitations:

**Does not distinguish muscle from fat.** A muscular athlete at 5'10" weighing 210 lb has a BMI of 30.1 (obese), despite potentially having 12% body fat. BMI systematically overestimates body fatness in people with above-average muscle mass.

**Varies by age and sex.** Women typically carry more body fat than men at the same BMI. Older adults tend to have more body fat and less muscle than younger adults at the same BMI. The fixed thresholds do not account for these differences.

**Varies by ethnicity.** Research published in *The Lancet* and *American Journal of Clinical Nutrition* shows that Asian populations experience higher rates of type 2 diabetes and cardiovascular disease at lower BMIs than European populations. This led the WHO to propose lower thresholds for Asian populations.

**Does not account for fat distribution.** Central (abdominal) fat carries significantly greater metabolic risk than peripheral fat. Two individuals with identical BMIs can have very different health risk profiles depending on where their fat is stored.

## Asian BMI Adjustments

The WHO Expert Consultation on BMI in Asian Populations (2004) recommended adjusted thresholds:

| Category | Standard WHO | Asian Adjustment |
|----------|-------------|------------------|
| Overweight | ≥ 25.0 | ≥ 23.0 |
| Obese | ≥ 30.0 | ≥ 27.5 |

These lower thresholds reflect data showing that Asian populations develop obesity-related comorbidities (particularly type 2 diabetes) at lower BMIs. The adjustments are used clinically in many Asian countries and by some US health systems for patients of Asian descent.

## BMI Prime

BMI Prime is the ratio of an individual's BMI to the upper limit of the "normal" range (25.0):

$$\text{BMI Prime} = \frac{\text{BMI}}{25.0}$$

A BMI Prime of 1.0 means you are exactly at the overweight threshold. Values below 1.0 are in the normal range; values above 1.0 indicate overweight. BMI Prime provides a dimensionless ratio that makes it easy to see how far an individual is from the threshold — for example, a BMI Prime of 1.12 means 12% above the upper limit of normal.

BMI Prime was proposed by researchers at the University of Michigan in 2000 as a way to simplify clinical communication. It has the advantage of being universal across populations: the adjusted Asian threshold simply uses a divisor of 23.0 instead of 25.0.

## Better Alternatives

Several metrics provide more accurate individual health assessment than BMI:

**Body fat percentage** — measured via DEXA scan, bioelectrical impedance, or skinfold calipers. Directly measures what BMI only estimates. Healthy ranges: 10–20% for men, 18–28% for women (American Council on Exercise).

**Waist circumference** — the National Institutes of Health classifies abdominal obesity as waist circumference >40 inches (men) or >35 inches (women). Waist circumference captures visceral fat, which BMI cannot distinguish from subcutaneous fat or lean mass.

**Waist-to-hip ratio (WHR)** — waist measurement divided by hip measurement. The WHO defines abdominal obesity as WHR >0.90 for men or >0.85 for women. WHR is a better predictor of cardiovascular disease risk than BMI alone.

**Waist-to-height ratio** — keeping your waist circumference below half your height ("keep your waist below half your height") is a simple rule that outperforms BMI for predicting cardiometabolic risk in multiple studies.

## Why BMI Persists Despite Its Limitations

Despite its well-documented shortcomings, BMI remains the most widely used screening metric globally because:

1. **Zero equipment cost** — only needs weight and height, which are already recorded in every clinical encounter
2. **Population-level accuracy** — while unreliable for individuals, BMI accurately tracks obesity prevalence across populations and over time
3. **Simplicity** — a single number is easier for public health messaging than multi-variable assessments
4. **Insurance and clinical protocol integration** — decades of clinical guidelines, insurance underwriting criteria, and eligibility thresholds are built around BMI categories

The medical consensus is that BMI should be used as an initial screening tool, not a diagnostic one. An elevated BMI should trigger further assessment (body fat measurement, waist circumference, metabolic markers) rather than being treated as a definitive health verdict.

## Assumptions and Limitations

1. **Adults only** — BMI categories do not apply to children and adolescents, who use age- and sex-specific BMI percentile charts (CDC growth charts).
2. **Not a measure of health** — BMI does not assess fitness, metabolic health, or disease risk directly. A "normal" BMI does not guarantee good health, and an "overweight" BMI does not necessarily indicate poor health.
3. **Population-derived thresholds** — the 25.0 and 30.0 cutoffs are statistical boundaries chosen to maximize predictive value across large populations. Individual risk varies widely within each category.
4. **Height squared is an approximation** — the exponent 2 works well for average-height adults but tends to overestimate BMI for very tall individuals and underestimate it for very short individuals. Some researchers suggest an exponent of 2.5 would be more accurate.
