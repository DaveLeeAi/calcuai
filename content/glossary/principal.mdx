## What Is Principal?

Principal is the original amount of money borrowed in a loan or the initial amount deposited or invested — before any interest accrues. In lending, it is the base balance on which interest charges are calculated.

On a $250,000 mortgage at 7%, your first monthly payment of $1,663 splits into roughly $1,458 of interest and only $205 toward principal. As you pay down the principal balance, more of each subsequent payment shifts toward principal and less toward interest — a process called amortization. By the final years of a 30-year mortgage, nearly the entire payment goes to principal.

Understanding principal matters because interest compounds on the remaining principal balance. A $5,000 extra payment toward principal early in a mortgage can save over $14,000 in interest over the remaining term. On the investment side, a $10,000 principal deposit growing at 8% annually becomes $21,589 in 10 years through compounding. On a $350,000 home with 20% down, the principal is $280,000. Making an extra $200 monthly payment toward principal on a 30-year mortgage at 6.5% can shorten the loan by over 6 years and save more than $95,000 in interest.
