## What Is APY?

APY (Annual Percentage Yield) is the effective annual rate of return on a deposit or investment, accounting for the effect of compounding interest. Unlike APR (Annual Percentage Rate), which states the nominal rate without compounding, APY shows what you actually earn over a year when interest is reinvested.

A savings account advertising 5.00% APR with daily compounding actually yields 5.13% APY — meaning $10,000 earns $512.67 in one year, not $500.00. The formula is APY = (1 + r/n)^n − 1, where r is the nominal rate and n is the number of compounding periods per year. The more frequently interest compounds, the higher the APY relative to the APR: daily compounding (n=365) produces a slightly higher APY than monthly (n=12), which produces a higher APY than quarterly (n=4).

The FDIC's Truth in Savings Act (Regulation DD) requires banks to disclose APY on all deposit accounts, making it the correct metric for comparing savings accounts, CDs, and money market accounts across institutions. When comparing two accounts, always compare APY to APY — a 4.95% APY is actually better than a 5.00% APR compounded annually (which equals exactly 5.00% APY).
