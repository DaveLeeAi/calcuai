## What Is Amortization?

Amortization is the process of spreading a loan into a series of fixed payments over time, where each payment covers both interest and a portion of the principal balance. With each successive payment, the interest share decreases and the principal share increases.

On a $300,000 mortgage at 7% over 30 years, the monthly payment is $1,996. In month one, $1,750 goes to interest and only $246 to principal. By month 180 (halfway), the split is roughly $1,145 interest and $851 principal. In the final month, nearly the entire $1,996 pays down principal. Over the full 30 years, total interest paid reaches approximately $418,527 — more than the original loan amount.

An amortization schedule is a table showing this payment breakdown for every month of the loan term. It reveals exactly when you cross the tipping point where more of each payment goes to principal than interest (typically around year 19–22 on a 30-year mortgage). Understanding amortization helps you evaluate whether extra payments, refinancing, or a shorter loan term could save significant money. On a 30-year $250,000 mortgage at 6%, roughly $750 of your first $1,499 payment goes to interest — but by year 20, over $1,100 goes to principal.
