## What Is an Effective Rate?

The effective interest rate (also called the effective annual rate or EAR) is the actual annual rate of return or cost when compounding is taken into account. It converts a nominal rate with any compounding frequency into an equivalent annual rate, enabling apples-to-apples comparison.

A savings account advertising 5.00% compounded daily has an effective rate of 5.13% — meaning $10,000 earns $513 per year, not $500. The formula is EAR = (1 + r/n)^n − 1, where r is the nominal annual rate and n is the number of compounding periods per year. The more frequently interest compounds, the higher the effective rate relative to the nominal rate.

This distinction matters most when comparing financial products with different compounding frequencies. A CD offering 4.90% compounded monthly (EAR: 5.01%) actually pays more than one offering 5.00% compounded annually (EAR: 5.00%). On the borrowing side, a credit card charging 24% nominal rate compounded daily has an effective rate of 27.11% — significantly higher than it appears, meaning you actually pay $271.10 in interest per $1,000 of balance carried for a full year.
