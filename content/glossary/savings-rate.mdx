## What Is a Savings Rate?

Savings rate is the percentage of your income that you save or invest rather than spend. It's calculated by dividing your total savings (including retirement contributions, emergency fund deposits, and investment contributions) by your gross or net income. A person earning $80,000 after tax who saves $20,000 per year has a 25% savings rate.

The U.S. personal savings rate averaged 3.4% in 2024 according to the Bureau of Economic Analysis — meaning the typical American saves roughly $1 out of every $30 earned. Financial planners generally recommend a minimum 15–20% savings rate for retirement (including employer 401k matches), while FIRE practitioners target 50–70% to achieve financial independence in 10–17 years rather than the traditional 40-year career timeline.

The savings rate is the single most powerful lever in building wealth. A 10% increase in savings rate (from 20% to 30%) reduces the working years needed to retire by approximately 5–8 years depending on investment returns. Unlike investment returns — which you cannot control — your savings rate is entirely within your control through spending decisions.
