## What Is Overtime?

Overtime is hours worked beyond the standard workweek, typically compensated at a premium rate. Under the federal Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA), non-exempt employees must receive at least 1.5 times their regular hourly rate (time-and-a-half) for all hours worked over 40 in a workweek.

An employee earning $25/hour who works 50 hours in a week receives $25 × 40 = $1,000 at the regular rate, plus $37.50 × 10 = $375 at the overtime rate, for a total of $1,375. That same 50-hour week at straight time would pay only $1,250 — overtime adds $125 (10% more). Some states have additional rules: California requires daily overtime after 8 hours and double-time after 12 hours.

Not all workers qualify for overtime. The FLSA exempts certain salaried employees in executive, administrative, professional, and computer roles who earn above a threshold (currently $35,568/year, with proposed increases). Misclassifying employees as exempt when they should receive overtime is one of the most common wage-and-hour violations. For employers, overtime costs compound quickly: 10 employees each working 5 hours overtime per week at $25/hour costs an extra $1,875 weekly or $97,500 annually.
