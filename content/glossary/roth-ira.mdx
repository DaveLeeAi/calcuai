## What Is a Roth IRA?

A Roth IRA is a retirement account funded with after-tax dollars that grows tax-free and allows tax-free withdrawals in retirement. Unlike a traditional IRA (where contributions are tax-deductible but withdrawals are taxed), Roth contributions are made with money you've already paid taxes on — in exchange, all future growth and qualified withdrawals are completely tax-free.

For 2025, the annual Roth IRA contribution limit is $7,000 ($8,000 if age 50 or older). Income limits apply: single filers with modified AGI above $150,000 face phase-outs, and those above $165,000 cannot contribute directly. However, there is no income limit on Roth conversions — the strategy of converting traditional IRA or 401(k) funds to a Roth by paying taxes now in exchange for tax-free growth.

A Roth conversion makes mathematical sense when your current tax rate is lower than your expected retirement tax rate. Converting $100,000 at a 24% rate costs $24,000 in taxes today, but if the balance grows to $386,968 over 20 years at 7% return, the entire amount is withdrawn tax-free. In a traditional account, that same $386,968 withdrawal at a 22% retirement rate would cost $85,133 in taxes — far more than the $24,000 paid upfront.
