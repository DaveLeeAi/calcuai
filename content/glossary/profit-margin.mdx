## What Is Profit Margin?

Profit margin is the percentage of revenue that remains as profit after costs are subtracted. It measures how efficiently a business converts sales into profit. The formula is: Profit Margin = (Revenue − Cost) / Revenue × 100.

A product sold for $80 with a cost of $50 has a profit margin of 37.5% — meaning $30 of every $80 sale is profit. This differs from markup, which is calculated against cost: the same $30 profit on a $50 cost is a 60% markup. Confusing margin and markup is one of the most common pricing mistakes in business.

There are three levels of profit margin: **gross margin** (revenue minus cost of goods sold), **operating margin** (minus operating expenses like rent and salaries), and **net margin** (minus all expenses including taxes and interest). A retail business might have a 50% gross margin but only a 5–10% net margin after all costs. Healthy margins vary dramatically by industry — software companies average 20–30% net margins, while grocery stores operate on 1–3%. A retail business generating $800,000 in revenue with $640,000 in total costs has a 20% profit margin, keeping $160,000 before taxes.
