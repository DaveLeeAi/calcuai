## What Is a Safe Withdrawal Rate?

A safe withdrawal rate (SWR) is the percentage of a retirement portfolio you can withdraw annually with a high probability of not running out of money over a given time horizon. The most widely cited SWR is 4%, based on the 1994 research by financial planner William Bengen and the subsequent 1998 Trinity Study by professors at Trinity University.

In the original research, a retiree starting with $1,000,000 withdraws $40,000 in year one (4%), then increases the withdrawal by inflation each subsequent year. Across every 30-year period in U.S. market history from 1926 to 1992, a 4% initial withdrawal rate from a 50/50 stock-bond portfolio never depleted the portfolio — a 100% success rate over the studied periods.

The 4% rule has important caveats: it was calibrated to U.S. equities during a period of strong returns, it assumes a fixed 30-year horizon, and it doesn't account for taxes or variable spending. More conservative planners use 3.0%–3.5% for longer retirements or lower-return expectations. Conversely, flexible spending strategies — reducing withdrawals during bear markets — can support higher initial rates.
