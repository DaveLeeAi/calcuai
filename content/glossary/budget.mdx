## What Is the 50/30/20 Budget Rule?

The 50/30/20 rule is a percentage-based budgeting framework that allocates after-tax income into three categories: 50% for needs (housing, utilities, groceries, insurance, minimum debt payments), 30% for wants (dining out, entertainment, subscriptions, travel), and 20% for savings and extra debt repayment.

For someone with $5,000 monthly take-home pay, the 50/30/20 split allocates $2,500 to needs, $1,500 to wants, and $1,000 to savings. The framework was popularized by Senator Elizabeth Warren and her daughter Amelia Warren Tyagi in their 2005 book *All Your Worth*. Its appeal lies in simplicity — instead of tracking every dollar across 20+ categories, you monitor just three buckets.

The rule works best as a starting point. In high-cost cities where housing alone exceeds 30% of income, a 60/20/20 or 70/20/10 split may be more realistic. Aggressive savers pursuing FIRE may flip the framework entirely — allocating 30% to needs and 50%+ to savings. The key insight is that the savings percentage matters far more than the exact split between needs and wants: increasing your savings allocation from 20% to 30% can shave years off your retirement timeline.
