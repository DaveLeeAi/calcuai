## What Is Payroll?

Payroll is the total amount a business pays to its employees for a given period, including wages, salaries, bonuses, and deductions. It also refers to the process of calculating and distributing those payments while withholding the correct taxes and contributions.

For an employee earning $60,000 annually, the employer's total payroll cost is approximately $67,590–$70,800 after adding the employer share of Social Security (6.2%), Medicare (1.45%), federal unemployment tax (FUTA, 0.6%), state unemployment (varies, ~2.7% average), and workers' compensation insurance. These employer-side taxes add 12.6–18% on top of the base salary.

Payroll processing involves calculating gross pay, subtracting employee withholdings (federal/state income tax, Social Security, Medicare, retirement contributions, health insurance premiums), adding employer-side taxes, and issuing net pay. Errors in payroll are among the most costly compliance mistakes a business can make — the IRS penalizes late or incorrect payroll tax filings with penalties starting at 2% of the unpaid amount, and state labor departments enforce strict pay timing rules. Misclassifying a $60,000-per-year employee as an independent contractor can trigger back taxes, penalties, and interest exceeding $15,000.
