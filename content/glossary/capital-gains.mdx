## What Are Capital Gains?

Capital gains are the profit earned when you sell an asset (stocks, real estate, bonds, crypto) for more than you paid. The gain equals the sale price minus your cost basis (original purchase price plus any qualifying expenses like broker commissions). If you bought stock for $50,000 and sold it for $80,000, your capital gain is $30,000.

The IRS taxes capital gains at different rates depending on how long you held the asset. **Short-term capital gains** (held one year or less) are taxed as ordinary income at your regular federal bracket rate — up to 37% in 2025. **Long-term capital gains** (held longer than one year) receive preferential rates of 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on your taxable income. A single filer with $80,000 taxable income pays 15% on long-term gains; below $48,350 taxable income, the long-term rate drops to 0%.

This rate difference creates a powerful incentive to hold investments for at least one year and one day. On a $30,000 gain, the tax difference between short-term (22% bracket) and long-term (15%) is $2,100 — simply by waiting past the one-year mark. Capital losses can offset gains dollar-for-dollar, and up to $3,000 of net losses can be deducted from ordinary income annually, with excess carried forward.
