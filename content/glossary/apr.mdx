## What Is APR?

APR (Annual Percentage Rate) is the total yearly cost of borrowing money, expressed as a percentage. Unlike a basic interest rate, APR includes lender fees, closing costs, discount points, and other charges — giving you a more complete picture of what a loan actually costs.

For instance, a mortgage with a 6.5% interest rate might carry a 6.8% APR once origination fees and points are factored in. On a $300,000 loan, that 0.3% gap represents roughly $900 per year in additional cost that the interest rate alone does not reveal. Federal law (the Truth in Lending Act) requires lenders to disclose APR so borrowers can compare offers on equal footing.

APR differs from APY (Annual Percentage Yield), which accounts for compounding and is typically used for savings and investment products. APR assumes simple interest over one year; APY reflects the actual return when interest compounds. When comparing loan offers, always compare APR to APR — not APR to a basic rate. Comparing APRs across lenders reveals the true cost difference that interest rates alone can obscure — even a 0.25% APR gap on a $300,000 mortgage adds up to roughly $15,000 over 30 years.
