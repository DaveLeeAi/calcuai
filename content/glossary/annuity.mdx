## What Is an Annuity?

An annuity is a series of equal payments made at regular intervals over a specified period. In finance, the term refers both to the mathematical concept (any recurring payment stream) and to insurance products that provide guaranteed income, typically in retirement.

A simple example: investing $500 per month for 30 years at a 7% annual return produces a future value of approximately $566,764. That same monthly contribution at 5% yields only $416,129 — a $150,635 difference driven entirely by the rate of return. The annuity formula — FV = PMT × [((1 + r)^n − 1) / r] — is the foundation of retirement planning calculations.

Annuities come in two timing variants: an **ordinary annuity** makes payments at the end of each period (most loans and investments), while an **annuity due** makes payments at the beginning (rent, insurance premiums). Insurance-product annuities can be fixed (guaranteed payout), variable (market-linked), or indexed. They are commonly used to convert a lump sum into predictable retirement income. A $500,000 immediate annuity purchased at age 65 might pay roughly $2,700–$3,100 per month for life, depending on the insurer and current interest rates. Deferred annuities, by contrast, grow tax-deferred until withdrawals begin.
