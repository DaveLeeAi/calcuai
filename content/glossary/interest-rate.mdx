## What Is an Interest Rate?

An interest rate is the percentage a lender charges a borrower for the use of money, or the percentage a bank pays a depositor for holding their funds. It is expressed as an annual percentage of the principal balance.

For example, a $200,000 mortgage at a 6.5% interest rate costs roughly $1,264 per month in principal and interest over 30 years — compared to $1,074 at 5.0%, a difference of $190 per month or $68,400 over the life of the loan. Even a fraction of a percent shifts thousands of dollars between borrower and lender.

Interest rates come in two forms: **fixed**, which stays the same for the entire term, and **variable** (or adjustable), which fluctuates based on a benchmark index like the federal funds rate or SOFR. The rate you receive depends on creditworthiness, loan type, term length, and current market conditions.

When you see a rate quoted on a savings account or CD, that is the rate the bank pays you. When quoted on a loan or mortgage, that is the rate you pay the lender.
