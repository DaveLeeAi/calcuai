## What Is Debt-to-Income Ratio?

Debt-to-income ratio (DTI) is your total monthly debt payments divided by your gross monthly income, expressed as a percentage. Mortgage lenders use DTI as a primary qualification metric — it measures how much of your income is already committed to debt obligations before you take on a new loan.

There are two versions: **front-end DTI** includes only housing costs (mortgage, property tax, insurance), while **back-end DTI** includes all recurring debts (housing plus car loans, student loans, credit card minimums, and other obligations). A borrower earning $6,000/month gross with $2,100 in total debt payments has a back-end DTI of 35%.

The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau's Qualified Mortgage (QM) rule sets a 43% back-end DTI as the general threshold for most conventional loans. FHA loans allow up to 50% DTI with compensating factors. Most lenders prefer back-end DTI below 36% and front-end DTI below 28% — these thresholds originate from the old "28/36 rule" that remains the industry's conservative benchmark.
