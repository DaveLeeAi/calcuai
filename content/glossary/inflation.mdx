## What Is Inflation?

Inflation is the rate at which the general level of prices for goods and services rises over time, reducing the purchasing power of each dollar. The U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics measures it primarily through the Consumer Price Index (CPI).

At the historical average of about 3.2% annual inflation, $100 today would buy only $72.60 worth of goods in 10 years. This means money sitting in a savings account earning 1% interest is actually losing purchasing power every year. Inflation is why financial planners insist that long-term savings and retirement funds must grow faster than inflation to maintain real value.

Inflation affects nearly every financial decision: the real return on investments, the future cost of retirement, whether a fixed-rate loan becomes cheaper over time (it does, in real terms), and how much income you will need decades from now. Central banks target a moderate inflation rate (the Federal Reserve targets 2%) because mild inflation encourages spending and investment, while deflation discourages it. Even at a moderate 2.5% rate, a $50,000 annual income needs to grow to roughly $64,000 within 10 years just to maintain the same standard of living.
