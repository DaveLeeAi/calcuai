## The Formula

### Days Between Two Dates

The total number of days between two dates is computed using absolute time difference:

$$\text{totalDays} = \left\lfloor \frac{|D_{\text{end}} - D_{\text{start}}|}{86{,}400{,}000} \right\rfloor$$

Where:
- **$D_{\text{end}}$** = end date as milliseconds since the Unix epoch (January 1, 1970 00:00:00 UTC)
- **$D_{\text{start}}$** = start date as milliseconds since the epoch
- **86,400,000** = number of milliseconds in one day (1000 ms x 60 s x 60 min x 24 h)

The year/month/day decomposition uses the same Gregorian calendar algorithm described in the Age Calculator:

$$Y = \text{endYear} - \text{startYear} - \delta_y$$

$$M = (\text{endMonth} - \text{startMonth} + 12) \bmod 12 - \delta_m$$

$$D = \begin{cases} \text{endDay} - \text{startDay} & \text{if endDay} \geq \text{startDay} \\ \text{daysInPrevMonth} + \text{endDay} - \text{startDay} & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

Where $\delta_y$ and $\delta_m$ are borrowing adjustments (0 or 1) that account for incomplete years and months.

### Add/Subtract Days

Date addition is straightforward calendar arithmetic:

$$D_{\text{result}} = D_{\text{start}} + (n \times 86{,}400{,}000)$$

Where:
- **$D_{\text{result}}$** = resulting date in milliseconds
- **$D_{\text{start}}$** = starting date in milliseconds
- **$n$** = number of days to add (positive) or subtract (negative)

The resulting millisecond value is converted back to a calendar date (year, month, day, day of week) using standard Gregorian calendar rules.

**Weeks and remaining days:**

$$\text{weeks} = \left\lfloor \frac{\text{totalDays}}{7} \right\rfloor \qquad \text{remainderDays} = \text{totalDays} \bmod 7$$

**Total hours and minutes:**

$$\text{totalHours} = \text{totalDays} \times 24 \qquad \text{totalMinutes} = \text{totalDays} \times 1{,}440$$

**Source:** Gregorian calendar system per ISO 8601 (International Organization for Standardization). Julian Day Number arithmetic as described in Meeus, *Astronomical Algorithms* (2nd edition, 1998), Chapter 7.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Days Between Two Dates — Project Duration

**Scenario:** A construction project started on February 15, 2025 and completed on November 3, 2025. How long did the project take?

**Inputs:**
- Start Date: February 15, 2025
- End Date: November 3, 2025

**Step-by-step:**

1. Year difference: 2025 - 2025 = 0 years
2. Month difference: November - February = 9 months tentative. Since day 3 < day 15, borrow one month → **8 months**
3. Day difference: From October 15 to November 3. October has 31 days. Days remaining in October: 31 - 15 = 16. Days into November: 3. Total: 16 + 3 = **19 days**
4. Total days: Count each month — Feb (13 days remaining) + Mar (31) + Apr (30) + May (31) + Jun (30) + Jul (31) + Aug (31) + Sep (30) + Oct (31) + Nov (3 days) = **261 days**
5. Total weeks: 261 / 7 = **37 weeks** and 2 days
6. Total hours: 261 x 24 = **6,264 hours**

**Result:** 8 months, 19 days (261 total days / 37 weeks and 2 days)

**Interpretation:** The project took just under 9 months. At 261 calendar days, this is roughly 186 business days (excluding weekends) — or 37.3 standard 5-day work weeks. If the contractor quoted "8-10 months," the project finished within the estimated range.

### Example 4: Business Days for a 90-Day Contract Deadline

**Scenario:** A consulting contract requires delivery within 90 calendar days of signing on March 1, 2024. How many business days does the team actually have to work?

**Inputs:**
- Start Date: March 1, 2024
- End Date: May 30, 2024 (90 calendar days later, calculated from Example 2 logic)

**Step-by-step:**

1. March 1–31: 31 calendar days. March 2024 has 21 weekdays (March 1 is a Friday, March 31 is a Sunday). Excluding weekends: **21 business days**
2. April 1–30: 30 calendar days. April 2024 has 22 weekdays. **22 business days**
3. May 1–30: 30 calendar days through May 30. May 2024 has 22 weekdays through May 30 (May 30 is a Thursday). **22 business days**

Project managers who estimate timelines in calendar days without converting to business days consistently overcommit: a "90-day project" with 64 working days leaves no buffer for sick days, internal meetings, or review cycles. Convert to business days first, then subtract 10–15% for non-project overhead.

## Key Factors That Affect Your Results

**Leap years add an extra day every 4 years.** When counting days across a February boundary, you get 366 days in a leap year instead of 365. The interval from January 1, 2024 to January 1, 2025 is 366 days (2024 is a leap year), while January 1, 2025 to January 1, 2026 is 365 days. Over a 10-year span, you cross 2-3 leap years, adding 2-3 extra days beyond the 3,650-day estimate (10 x 365). The next leap years are 2028, 2032, and 2036.

**"Days" vs. "months" are not interchangeable.** Adding 30 days to January 1 gives you January 31. Adding "1 month" gives you February 1. The difference is 1 day in most years, but it compounds over longer intervals. Adding 12 "months" always returns the same calendar date next year, but adding 365 days may land on the day before (in a year preceding a leap year) or the day after (coming out of a leap year). Always clarify whether a deadline means "calendar days" or "calendar months."

**Business days vs. calendar days.** This calculator counts calendar days (every day including weekends and holidays). A 30-calendar-day period contains approximately 22 business days, but the exact count depends on which days fall in the range and any holidays. Legal deadlines, financial settlement periods, and shipping estimates each follow their own conventions — some count calendar days, others business days. When a contract specifies "30 days" without qualification, the default interpretation in most U.S. jurisdictions is calendar days.

**Holiday impact on date calculations.** The U.S. federal government observes 11 paid holidays per year, reducing the annual working-day total from 261 weekdays to approximately 250. When a holiday falls on a Saturday, the preceding Friday is observed; when it falls on a Sunday, the following Monday is observed — so the actual day off may differ from the calendar holiday date.

**Adding "1 month" is ambiguous.** Calendar month arithmetic does not have a single universally agreed definition. Adding 1 month to January 31 could yield February 28 (capping at the last day of the target month), March 1 (preserving the day count), or March 2 (adding exactly 30 days). Most date libraries and this calculator use the "cap at last day" convention: January 31 + 1 month = February 28 (or 29 in a leap year). But adding 1 month to February 28 yields March 28, not March 31 — the operation is not reversible. This asymmetry is why financial contracts almost always specify exact calendar day counts rather than month intervals.

**Timezone and date line crossings.** If you are calculating dates across time zones, the calendar date depends on which time zone you reference. A flight departing Los Angeles at 11 PM on March 10 arrives in Tokyo at 4 AM on March 12 (crossing the International Date Line). This calculator uses abstract calendar dates without time zone conversion, which is correct for most practical purposes — legal deadlines, age calculations, and contract durations are all defined by calendar dates, not timestamps.

**Historical calendar transitions.** If you are calculating intervals involving dates before 1582 (when the Gregorian calendar was adopted), results may not match historical records. Many countries adopted the Gregorian calendar at different times — Britain and its colonies switched in 1752, Russia in 1918, and Turkey in 1926. The "missing days" from each transition are not accounted for in this calculator.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Confusing "days between" with "days including."** The number of days from March 1 to March 10 is 9, not 10. The calculation counts elapsed days — the interval between the dates. If you need to count both the start and end date (for example, "how many days is my hotel stay from check-in on the 1st to check-out on the 10th"), add 1 to the result for nights or use the standard calculation for elapsed days.

**Assuming "3 months" equals "90 days."** Three calendar months from January 15 is April 15 (90 days in a non-leap year). But three months from November 15 is February 15 (92 days if crossing a 31-day month). The discrepancy can be up to 3 days depending on which months are involved. Always specify whether you mean calendar months or a fixed day count.

**Forgetting about leap years in multi-year calculations.** The interval from March 1, 2023 to March 1, 2027 is 1,461 days (not 1,460). The extra day comes from the leap year 2024. Over a 4-year period, this is always either 1,460 or 1,461 days. Over 100 years, the leap year correction adds 24 or 25 extra days compared to a naive 365-day-per-year estimate.

**Using "30 days" as a universal month proxy.** Insurance companies, banks, and government agencies each define "one month" differently. The federal government's 30-day comment period means 30 calendar days. A mortgage "monthly payment" is due on the same calendar date each month regardless of how many days that month contains. A credit card "billing cycle" is typically 28-31 days. When in doubt, count calendar days rather than estimating months.

**Mixing up weekdays and calendar days.** A "10-day waiting period" could mean 10 calendar days or 10 business days. Ten business days is typically 14 calendar days (10 weekdays plus 2 weekends), possibly more with holidays. If a deadline doesn't specify which type, calendar days is the default in most U.S. jurisdictions.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- Counts **calendar days** only — does not exclude weekends, holidays, or any non-business days. If you need business days, subtract approximately 2 days per 7 for weekends and account for public holidays separately.
- Operates on the **Gregorian calendar** exclusively. Dates before the Gregorian adoption (1582 in Catholic countries, later in others) are projected using the proleptic Gregorian calendar, which may not match historical records.
- **Time of day is not considered.** The calculator treats dates as whole calendar days. The difference between 11:59 PM on March 9 and 12:01 AM on March 10 is 1 day in this calculator, even though only 2 minutes elapsed.
- The **maximum interval** is 36,500 days (approximately 100 years) in add/subtract mode. The days-between mode has no practical upper limit for dates within the 1900-2099 range.
- **Daylight Saving Time** transitions do not affect results because the calculator uses UTC dates internally. DST causes one day per year to be 23 hours and one to be 25 hours, but the calendar date count remains the same.
- **International Date Line** crossings are not modeled. If you need to account for date changes due to eastbound or westbound travel, adjust your input dates manually.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How many days are there in a year?

A standard Gregorian year has 365 days. A leap year has 366 days. Leap years occur every 4 years (2024, 2028, 2032), except for century years not divisible by 400 — so 1900 was not a leap year, but 2000 was. The average Gregorian year length is 365.2425 days, which is why the calendar stays aligned with the solar year (365.2422 days) to within 1 day every 3,236 years. For practical calculations, use 365.25 as a reasonable average when converting between days and years.

### What is the difference between calendar days and business days?

Calendar days include every day — weekdays, weekends, and holidays. Business days (also called working days) typically include only Monday through Friday, excluding public holidays. A 30-calendar-day period contains approximately 22 business days, depending on holidays. To convert, multiply calendar days by 5/7 for an estimate: 90 calendar days is roughly 64 business days. This calculator counts calendar days only. For precise business-day calculations, you would need a holiday calendar for your specific jurisdiction.

### How do I calculate the number of weeks between two dates?

Divide the total number of days by 7. The quotient is complete weeks, and the remainder is extra days. For example, 100 days equals 14 weeks and 2 days. This calculator provides both the total weeks and the remainder automatically. Note that "weeks" in common usage can mean different things: "two weeks" usually means 14 calendar days, but "biweekly" can mean every two weeks (14 days) or twice per week (every 3-4 days), depending on context.

### Does this calculator account for daylight saving time?

No, and it does not need to. Daylight Saving Time affects clock time, not calendar dates. When clocks spring forward, one day has only 23 hours; when they fall back, one day has 25 hours. But the calendar date still advances by exactly one day in both cases. This calculator counts calendar days, so DST transitions have no effect on the result. The total hours figure (totalDays x 24) is technically approximate by +/-1 hour around DST transitions, but this difference is negligible for practical purposes.

### How far apart are two dates that are exactly "6 months" apart?

It depends on which months are involved. Six months from January 15 is July 15 (181 days in a non-leap year). Six months from July 15 is January 15 (184 days). Six months from August 31 is February 28 or 29 (because there is no February 31). The concept of "N months" is inherently ambiguous because months have 28-31 days. When precision matters, always specify the exact number of calendar days rather than using month-based intervals. Legal contracts often define "month" as 30 days to avoid this ambiguity.

### How do I calculate the number of business days between two dates?

Multiply the total calendar days by 5/7 for a quick estimate, then subtract any holidays in the period. For a precise count: count the full weeks (each has 5 business days), then count the remaining partial-week days, excluding Saturday and Sunday. A 30-calendar-day period contains approximately 22 business days, but the exact count varies by 1-2 days depending on which day of the week you start. Federal holidays reduce the count further — the U.S. observes 11 federal holidays per year.

### Can I use this calculator for historical dates before the Gregorian calendar?

The calculator uses the proleptic Gregorian calendar for all dates, which means it applies modern calendar rules retroactively. For dates before October 15, 1582 (when the Gregorian calendar was adopted), results may not match contemporary records. Britain and its colonies did not switch until September 1752, skipping 11 days. George Washington's birthday is recorded as both February 11, 1731 (Julian/Old Style) and February 22, 1732 (Gregorian/New Style). If historical accuracy is critical, consult a Julian-to-Gregorian conversion reference for the specific country and date.

## Real-World Uses

**Lease and rental period tracking.** Landlords calculating exact lease durations need precise day counts to prorate rent. A tenant moving in on March 15 and out on November 3 occupies the unit for 233 days. At \$1,800/month (\$60/day), the prorated final month (3 days of November) costs \$180, not a full month's rent. Property management software relies on this exact arithmetic.

**Legal deadline compliance.** Statutes of limitations, filing deadlines, and notice periods are measured in calendar days. A 180-day statute of limitations on a personal injury claim filed on April 10, 2026 expires on October 7, 2026. Missing this deadline by even one day forfeits the right to sue. Courts calculate deadlines to the exact day, not by month approximations.

**Project management and sprint planning.** Agile teams running 2-week sprints need to know exactly how many business days fall in each sprint. A sprint from March 16 to March 27 (10 business days) is shorter in working hours than one from April 13 to April 24 if a company holiday falls within the second period. Precise date arithmetic prevents overcommitting the team.

**Age-dependent eligibility verification.** School enrollment cutoffs (often September 1), voting eligibility (18 years before election day), and retirement benefit start dates all require counting exact days between a birth date and a target date. A child born on September 2 misses the kindergarten cutoff by one day and must wait an entire year — a calculation where precision matters enormously to the family.
