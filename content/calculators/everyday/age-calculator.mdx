## The Formula

Age calculation uses Gregorian calendar decomposition — there is no single closed-form equation because months have different lengths. The algorithm works in three passes:

**Pass 1 — Count complete years:**

$$Y = \text{endYear} - \text{startYear} - \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if end month/day is before start month/day} \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

**Pass 2 — Count remaining complete months:**

$$M = (\text{endMonth} - \text{startMonth} + 12) \mod 12 - \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if endDay} < \text{startDay} \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

When the end day is less than the start day, we borrow one month and add the number of days in the previous month to the day count.

**Pass 3 — Count remaining days:**

$$D = \begin{cases} \text{endDay} - \text{startDay} & \text{if endDay} \geq \text{startDay} \\ \text{daysInPrevMonth} + \text{endDay} - \text{startDay} & \text{if endDay} < \text{startDay} \end{cases}$$

**Total days** use the simpler millisecond-based calculation:

$$\text{totalDays} = \left\lfloor \frac{\text{endDate}_{\text{ms}} - \text{startDate}_{\text{ms}}}{86{,}400{,}000} \right\rfloor$$

Where:
- **Y** = complete years between dates
- **M** = remaining complete months after years (0-11)
- **D** = remaining days after months (0-30)
- **daysInPrevMonth** = number of days in the month before the end month, accounting for leap years
- **86,400,000** = milliseconds in one day (1000 x 60 x 60 x 24)

**Leap year rule:** A year is a leap year if divisible by 4, except century years (divisible by 100) are not leap years, unless also divisible by 400. This means 2000 was a leap year, but 1900 was not.

**Source:** Gregorian calendar reform established by Pope Gregory XIII in the papal bull *Inter gravissimas* (February 24, 1582). Leap year rules per ISO 8601 and the Explanatory Supplement to the Astronomical Almanac (Seidelmann, 2005).

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Standard Age Calculation — Born January 15, 1990

**Inputs:**
- Date of Birth: January 15, 1990
- Age as of: March 10, 2026

**Step-by-step:**

1. Complete years: 1990 to 2026 = 36 years tentative. Since March 10 is after January 15, the birthday has passed → **36 complete years**
2. Remaining months: January to March = 2 months tentative. Since day 10 < day 15, borrow one month → **1 complete month**
3. Remaining days: From February 15 to March 10. February 2026 has 28 days. Days remaining in February: 28 - 15 = 13. Days into March: 10. Total: 13 + 10 = **23 days**
4. Total days: March 10, 2026 minus January 15, 1990 = **13,203 days**
5. Total weeks: 13,203 / 7 = **1,886 weeks** and 1 day
6. Total hours: 13,203 x 24 = **316,872 hours**

**Result:** 36 years, 1 month, 23 days (13,203 total days)

**Interpretation:** At 36 years old, this person is approaching their late thirties. They have lived through 9 leap years since birth (1992, 1996, 2000, 2004, 2008, 2012, 2016, 2020, 2024), adding 9 extra days to their life compared to a calendar of uniform 365-day years. Their next birthday is January 15, 2027 — 311 days away.

## Key Factors That Affect Your Results

**Month length variation** — The Gregorian calendar has months ranging from 28 to 31 days. This means "one month from January 31" could be February 28 (or 29), creating a 2-3 day discrepancy depending on the year. The calculator handles this by capping the day to the maximum valid day in the target month, matching the convention used by legal and financial systems. Someone born on the 31st of a month will see their "monthly anniversary" fall on the 28th, 29th, or 30th in shorter months.

**Leap years** — Leap years add an extra day every 4 years, with exceptions for centuries. Over a 100-year lifespan, a person experiences approximately 24 or 25 leap years, adding 24-25 extra days beyond what a simple 365-day-per-year calculation would predict. This means a person who turns 50 has lived approximately 18,262 days, not 18,250 (50 x 365). The 12-day difference matters for precise calculations in legal, medical, and actuarial contexts.

**Timezone considerations** — This calculator operates on calendar dates (not timestamps), so timezone does not affect the result. If you were born at 11:55 PM in New York, your birth certificate shows that date regardless of what time it was in Tokyo. The calculator counts complete calendar days between two dates, which is the standard for age determination worldwide.

**Calendar system differences** — The Gregorian calendar is used by default in nearly all countries for civil purposes. The Hebrew, Islamic, Chinese, and other calendar systems calculate age differently. In the traditional Korean age system (만 나이 vs. 세는 나이), a baby is considered 1 year old at birth and gains a year every January 1 — not on their birthday. Someone born on December 31 would be "2 years old" by Korean reckoning the very next day, despite being less than 24 hours old. South Korea officially adopted the international age-counting system for legal and administrative purposes in June 2023, but the traditional system persists in everyday conversation. In the Chinese age system (虚岁), a similar convention applies, with age incrementing at Chinese New Year rather than January 1. This calculator uses exclusively the Gregorian international system, where age increments on each birthday anniversary.

**Leap year birthday recognition** — Approximately 5 million people worldwide were born on February 29 (roughly 1 in 1,461 births). In non-leap years, most legal jurisdictions treat their birthday as either February 28 or March 1, but the convention varies. In England and Wales, a person born on February 29 is legally considered to have their birthday on March 1 in non-leap years (for purposes such as turning 18). In most U.S. states and in this calculator, February 28 is used instead. New Zealand and Taiwan also default to February 28, while Hong Kong uses March 1 for legal age advancement.

**Precision requirements vary by context** — Different documents and systems require different levels of age precision. Birth certificates record the exact date (and sometimes time) of birth. Passport applications need the full date of birth but calculate age only to the year. Insurance actuarial tables use age in complete years (a 35-year-old and a 35-year-11-month-old are in the same bracket). Medical growth charts for infants track age in weeks and days for the first 2 years. Social Security uses the "day before" rule — a person born on January 1 is considered to reach retirement age on December 31 of the preceding year, which can affect benefit calculations by an entire calendar year.

**Legal age cutoffs** — Many jurisdictions specify the exact moment a person reaches a legal age. In the U.S., most states consider you to have reached an age at the start of your birthday (midnight). A person born on June 15, 2005 turns 21 at midnight on June 15, 2026, not at the exact time they were born. The calculator's day-level precision is sufficient for all standard legal age checks.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Counting the end date when you shouldn't.** The interval between March 1 and March 10 is 9 days, not 10. If you leave on March 1 and arrive on March 10, you traveled for 9 days. The calculator returns the elapsed difference between dates — it does not count both endpoints. If you need to include both dates (for example, counting work days including start and end), add 1 to the result.

**Assuming all months are 30 days.** A common shortcut is dividing total days by 30 to get months. This produces errors of up to 3 days per month. The calculator avoids this by counting actual calendar months with their real lengths (28, 29, 30, or 31 days). Over a decade, the 30-day approximation accumulates roughly a 30-day error.

**Forgetting about Feb 29 birthdays.** If you were born on February 29, you do not age 0.25 years per year in common years. The legal and conventional approach is to treat February 28 as your birthday in non-leap years. The calculator follows this convention. Some systems use March 1 instead — if your jurisdiction does, mentally adjust the result by one day.

**Confusing calendar age with exact age.** A baby born at 11:00 PM on January 1 is 0 years old for the entire next day, even though they have been alive for 25 hours by 12:00 AM on January 3. Calendar-based age calculators (including this one) operate on dates, not timestamps. The difference is almost never more than 1 day.

**Using the wrong "as of" date.** If you need your age on a specific past date (for tax filing, insurance enrollment, or school enrollment cutoffs), make sure you set the "Age as of" field to that exact date rather than today. Many age-dependent eligibility rules use a specific cutoff date (like September 1 for school enrollment or December 31 for tax years).

## Assumptions & Limitations

- Assumes the **Gregorian calendar** throughout. Dates before October 15, 1582 (when the Gregorian calendar was adopted) may not align with the Julian calendar used at the time. Most countries adopted the Gregorian calendar between 1582 and 1923.
- Treats all dates as **calendar dates without time zones or timestamps**. Your age in years/months/days is the same regardless of your time zone, but the total hours figure is approximate (within +/-24 hours) if birth and target dates are in different time zones.
- February 29 birthdays are handled by using **February 28 in non-leap years**, following the most common legal convention. Some jurisdictions (notably the UK for certain purposes) use March 1 instead.
- Assumes the **proleptic Gregorian calendar** for dates before 1582. This means the leap year rule is applied retroactively to years that historically used the Julian calendar.
- Does not account for **historical calendar anomalies** such as the 10 days skipped when the Gregorian calendar was adopted (October 4 was followed by October 15 in 1582) or country-specific adoption dates.
- Maximum supported date range is approximately **January 1, 1900 to December 31, 2099**. Dates outside this range may produce inaccurate results due to JavaScript Date object limitations.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How is age calculated if I was born on February 29?

February 29 birthdays occur only in leap years, which happen every 4 years (with exceptions for century years). In non-leap years, most legal systems and this calculator treat your birthday as February 28. This means a person born on February 29, 2000 turned 1 year old on February 28, 2001. In a leap year, the actual February 29 date is used. Over a 72-year period, a leap-year baby has only 18 actual Feb 29 birthdays but ages 72 years, just like everyone else.

### What is the difference between chronological age and biological age?

Chronological age is the exact number of years, months, and days since your birth — the figure this calculator provides. Biological age measures how your body has aged relative to population averages, using biomarkers like telomere length, DNA methylation, blood pressure, and organ function. A 50-year-old marathon runner might have a biological age of 40, while a sedentary 50-year-old could test at 60. Chronological age is objective and fixed; biological age is estimated and changeable through lifestyle modifications.

### Why does my age show a different number of months than I expected?

Month counting follows calendar rules, not a fixed 30-day cycle. If you were born on January 31 and check your age on March 2, the result is 1 month and 2 days — not 1 month and 0 days — because February is the intervening month and it only has 28 (or 29) days. The calculation borrows from the shorter month, which can produce results that feel unintuitive. The key rule: a "month" in age calculation means advancing from one calendar month-day to the same day in the next month, with shorter months capped at their maximum day.

### At what exact moment do I legally turn a certain age?

In most U.S. states, you reach a new age at the start of your birthday — midnight (12:00 AM) on the date shown on your birth certificate. This matters for legal thresholds: you can legally drink at midnight on your 21st birthday, not at the exact time you were born. A few jurisdictions use the "birthday eve" rule, where you legally turn a year older the day before your birthday. Federal law (for Social Security purposes) also uses the day-before convention: a person born on January 1 reaches retirement age on December 31 of the preceding year.

### How accurate is the total days calculation?

The total days calculation is accurate to within 1 day for all dates within the supported range (1900-2099). It uses the millisecond difference between two UTC dates divided by 86,400,000 (the number of milliseconds in 24 hours) and rounds to the nearest integer. This method correctly accounts for every leap year in the range. Daylight saving time does not affect the result because the calculation uses UTC dates. For dates before 1900 or after 2099, precision may degrade due to JavaScript Date object limitations.

### How do I calculate my age in months for medical or legal purposes?

Count complete calendar months from your birth date to the target date, then add remaining days. A person born on March 15 is exactly 6 months old on September 15, regardless of how many days each intervening month contains (March has 31, April 30, May 31, June 30, July 31, August 31). Pediatricians track infant age in completed months and days for growth chart accuracy — a baby at "5 months and 20 days" is developmentally different from one at "6 months and 2 days," even though they may be only 12 calendar days apart.

### What is the average human lifespan in days?

The average American lives approximately 28,470 days (77.9 years, per CDC 2023 data). Globally, the average is about 26,645 days (73.0 years, per WHO 2023). Japanese women have the highest life expectancy at approximately 31,755 days (87.0 years). To put daily perspective on it: at age 30 (10,957 days), you have statistically used about 38.5% of your days; at age 50 (18,262 days), about 64.1%. The total-days view makes the finite nature of time more concrete than the years-only figure.

## Real-World Uses

**Insurance premium calculation.** Life insurance premiums increase with each birthday. A 34-year-old applying for a 20-year term policy pays significantly less than a 35-year-old — often \$50-\$150/year less on a \$500,000 policy. A child born on September 2 in a state with a September 1 cutoff must wait a full year to enroll. Parents of summer-born children use exact age calculations to evaluate "redshirting" — intentionally delaying enrollment so the child is among the oldest in their class. Research shows that the oldest children in a grade (born in September/October in states with September cutoffs) outperform the youngest (born in July/August) in early grades, though the gap narrows by high school.

**Legal age thresholds worldwide.** Different countries set legal age milestones at different points: voting (16 in Brazil, 18 in the U.S., 25 for senate candidacy), driving (14 in South Dakota for farm permits, 16 for general permits, 18 in most of Europe), and drinking (18 in the UK, 21 in the U.S.).
