## What Your Result Shows

The result shows the discounted sale price, the dollar amount saved, the effective total discount percentage (important for stacked discounts), and the final total including optional sales tax. Stacked discounts are multiplicative, not additive — a 25% + 10% double discount equals a 32.5% total discount, not 35%. This distinction matters more than most shoppers realize: two 20% discounts don't save 40% — they save 36%, costing you $64 on a $100 item instead of the $60 you might expect.

The effective total discount percentage is especially useful for comparing deals across retailers. A store offering "30% off everything" gives a better deal than one offering "20% off + extra 15% off" — the stacked version only yields 32% total, not 35%.

For calculating the tax portion of a purchase, use the [Sales Tax Calculator](/calculators/finance/sales-tax-calculator). For tip calculations, use the [Tip Calculator](/calculators/everyday/tip-calculator).

## How to Use This Calculator

1. **Enter the "Original Price"** — the item's full retail price before any discounts. Use the price on the tag or product listing page, not a previously marked-down price.
2. **Enter the "Discount (%)"** — the primary discount percentage (e.g., enter 25 for a 25%-off sale). This is typically the store-wide or item-specific sale discount.
3. **Enter the "Additional Discount (%)" (optional)** — a second stacked discount such as a coupon code, loyalty reward, employee discount, or credit card cashback. Leave at 0 if you only have one discount.
4. **Enter the "Sales Tax Rate (%)" (optional)** — your local sales tax rate, which varies by state and municipality. The average combined U.S. state and local sales tax rate is 8.2% as of 2026. Tax is applied after all discounts are subtracted.
5. **Read the results** — the "Sale Price," "You Save" amount, "Total Discount" percentage, and "Total with Tax" appear instantly.

## The Formula

**Single discount:**

$$
\text{salePrice} = \text{originalPrice} \times \left(1 - \frac{d}{100}\right)
$$

**Stacked (two discounts):**

$$
\text{salePrice} = \text{originalPrice} \times \left(1 - \frac{d_1}{100}\right) \times \left(1 - \frac{d_2}{100}\right)
$$

**With sales tax:**

$$
\text{total} = \text{salePrice} \times \left(1 + \frac{\text{taxRate}}{100}\right)
$$

Where:
- **$d$**, **$d_1$**, **$d_2$** = discount percentages
- **taxRate** = sales tax rate as a percentage

Stacked discounts are multiplicative: 25% + 10% = \$1 - (1-0.25)(1-0.10)$ = 32.5%, not 35%.

*Source: Standard percentage arithmetic. Stacked discount convention follows FTC pricing guidelines — the second discount applies to the already-discounted price, not the original.*

## Worked Example

### Example: Black Friday Double Discount

A \$349.99 jacket is 30% off, and you have an additional 15% employee discount. Local sales tax is 8%.

**Step 1:** First discount: $349.99 × (1 − 0.30) = $349.99 × 0.70 = **\$245.00**
**Step 2:** Second discount: $245.00 × (1 − 0.15) = $245.00 × 0.85 = **\$208.25**
**Step 3:** Total savings: \$349.99 − $208.25 = **$141.74** (40.5% total discount)
**Step 4:** With tax: $208.25 × 1.08 = **$224.91**

*Interpretation:* The combined 30% + 15% stacked discount saves you \$141.74 (40.5%), not the \$157.50 (45%) you would get from adding the percentages. The multiplicative stacking still saves substantially — nearly half the original price.

### Example: Simple Single Discount

A pair of running shoes originally priced at \$89.95 is marked 20% off with no additional discounts. No sales tax applies (e.g., shopping in Oregon).

**Step 1:** Savings: \$89.95 × 0.20 = **\$17.99**
**Step 2:** Sale price: \$89.95 − \$17.99 = **\$71.96**

*Interpretation:* A single 20% discount is straightforward — you pay exactly 80% of the original price. For quick mental math, move the decimal one place left to get 10% (\$9.00), then double it to get the 20% savings (\$18.00).

{/* Section 10: Common Mistakes to Avoid */}
## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Adding stacked discount percentages together.** The single most common error: assuming 25% off + 10% off equals 35% off. It does not. The second discount applies to the already-reduced price, producing a 32.5% total discount. On a \$500 purchase, the difference between 35% (\$175 savings) and 32.5% (\$162.50 savings) is \$12.50. The gap widens with larger discounts — two 50% discounts yield 75% off, not 100%.

**Applying the discount to the post-tax total.** Discounts apply to the pre-tax price. A \$100 item with 8% tax and a 20% discount should be calculated as: \$100 × 0.80 = \$80, then \$80 × 1.08 = \$86.40. Some shoppers mistakenly compute \$108 × 0.80 = \$86.40 — same result in this case, but the tax base is technically the discounted price, and in jurisdictions with itemized tax receipts, the math must follow the correct order.

**Forgetting that "buy one get one 50% off" is only 25% off.** BOGO deals sound generous but are mathematically modest. Buying two \$40 shirts with "BOGO 50% off" costs \$60 — a 25% average discount per shirt, not 50%. "Buy one get one free" is a true 50% average discount per item.

**Using the discount percentage to calculate what you paid.** If you saved 30%, you paid 70% of the original — not 30%. A \$200 jacket at 30% off costs \$140, not \$60. This sounds obvious, but it is the second most searched discount calculation error online.

**Not checking whether the "original price" is inflated.** Retailers sometimes raise the reference price before a sale to make the discount appear larger. A \$120 item "marked down from \$200" shows a 40% discount, but if the item was never actually sold at \$200, the effective savings are illusory. The FTC requires that reference prices reflect genuine former selling prices.

## Real-World Uses

**Retail and e-commerce comparison shopping.** When two stores offer different discount structures on the same item — one at 30% off, another at 20% off plus a 15% promo code — the stacked discount calculator reveals which deal saves more. The single 30% discount wins: 30% total vs. 32% total for the stacked version. Wait — that means the stacked discount actually wins in this case. Without computing, most shoppers would guess wrong.

**Credit card cashback optimization.** A 5% cashback credit card effectively functions as an additional stacked discount. A \$300 TV marked 20% off (\$240 sale price) with 5% cashback saves an additional \$12.00, bringing the effective price to \$228 — a total effective discount of 24% off the original price.

**Inventory clearance pricing.** Retailers often apply sequential markdowns: 30% off initial markdown, then an additional 25% off clearance, then a final "extra 20% off" weekend sale. On a \$150 item: \$150 → \$105 → \$78.75 → \$63.00. The total effective discount is 58%, not 75% (30+25+20). Three aggressive-sounding markdowns still leave 42% of the original price.

**Negotiating service contracts.** Freelancers and small business owners use discount stacking logic when offering combined discounts for bundled services. A web designer quoting \$5,000 who offers a 10% "new client discount" and a 5% "prepayment discount" should quote \$4,275, not \$4,250.

{/* Section 13: Assumptions & Limitations */}
## Assumptions & Limitations

- Assumes discounts are **percentage-based**, not fixed dollar amounts. A "\$20 off" coupon is not a percentage discount and should be subtracted directly from the price rather than entered into this calculator.
- Stacked discounts are applied **sequentially** (second discount on the already-reduced price), following standard retail convention per FTC guidelines. Some promotions apply both discounts to the original price simultaneously — verify the store's policy.
- Sales tax is applied **after all discounts**, which is the legal requirement in all U.S. states that charge sales tax. Tax-inclusive pricing (common in Europe) is not modeled.
- Does not account for **minimum purchase thresholds** (e.g., "20% off orders over \$50") or **maximum discount caps** (e.g., "25% off, up to \$100 savings").
- The calculator does not model **buy-one-get-one (BOGO)** or **tiered quantity discounts** (e.g., "10% off 2 items, 15% off 3+"). These require different arithmetic.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is a 20% discount plus another 20% discount the same as 40% off?

No. Two stacked 20% discounts produce a 36% total discount, not 40%. The first 20% reduces the price to 80% of the original. The second 20% takes 20% off that 80%, removing another 16% of the original price. Total removed: 20% + 16% = 36%. On a \$100 item, you pay \$64 instead of the \$60 you would pay with a true 40% discount.

### Does the order of stacked discounts matter?

No. Multiplication is commutative, so applying 30% first then 10% produces the same result as applying 10% first then 30%. Both yield a 37% total discount. The intermediate "sale price" differs between the two orders, but the final price is identical. This holds for any number of stacked percentage discounts applied in any sequence.

### How do I calculate the original price from a sale price and discount percentage?

Divide the sale price by (1 − discount/100). If you paid \$75 after a 25% discount: \$75 ÷ (1 − 0.25) = \$75 ÷ 0.75 = \$100 original price. For stacked discounts, divide by each discount factor separately: if you paid \$63 after 25% + 10% off, the original was \$63 ÷ 0.75 ÷ 0.90 = \$93.33. This reverse calculation is useful for verifying receipts and computing pre-sale values for returns.

### What is the difference between "percent off" and "percent of"?

"30% off" means you save 30% and pay 70% of the original price. "30% of" means you pay 30% of the original — a 70% discount. These are opposite operations. A \$200 item at "30% off" costs \$140. A \$200 item at "30% of the original price" costs \$60. Retailers use "percent off" exclusively; "percent of" appears in wholesale and manufacturing contexts.

## How This Is Calculated

This calculator multiplies the original price by the discount percentage divided by 100 to find the savings amount, then subtracts that from the original price for the final price. For stacked discounts, each percentage is applied sequentially to the running total — not added together — because a 20% + 10% discount is 28% total savings, not 30%.
