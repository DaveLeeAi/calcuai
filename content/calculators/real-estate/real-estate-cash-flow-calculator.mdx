## What Your Results Mean

**Monthly Cash Flow** is the net amount deposited to your account each month after every expense and mortgage payment. Positive = the property earns money. Negative = it costs money each month and is a liability until rents rise or the mortgage is paid down.

**Annual Cash Flow** is monthly cash flow multiplied by 12. This is the number to use when evaluating cash-on-cash returns or comparing to other investments.

**Cash-on-Cash Return** expresses annual cash flow as a percentage of total cash invested (down payment + closing costs + initial repairs). It is the most widely used metric for comparing leveraged real estate deals to one another and to alternative investments.

**Net Operating Income (NOI)** is annual income minus all operating expenses — but before mortgage payments. NOI is the property's income independent of financing. It is used to calculate cap rate and property value in commercial real estate.

**Expense Ratio** is total monthly operating expenses (excluding mortgage) divided by gross rent. A healthy residential rental typically runs 35–50%. Above 55% indicates thin margins.

## How to Use This Calculator

1. Enter **Monthly Gross Rent** — the full rent before any deductions.
2. Set **Vacancy Rate** — 5% is a reasonable national average for well-located single-family or small multifamily. Adjust upward for markets with weaker rental demand or seasonal rentals.
3. Enter each operating expense category: property tax, insurance, maintenance, management fee %, and any other recurring costs (HOA, lawn care, snow removal).
4. Enter your **Monthly Mortgage Payment** (principal + interest only — not escrow, as that is already captured in tax and insurance fields).
5. Enter **Total Cash Invested** to calculate your cash-on-cash return.
6. Review Monthly Cash Flow — if it is negative, use the scenario comparison feature to test what rent increase or expense reduction creates break-even.

## The Formula

<div className="formula-section">

**Effective Gross Income (after vacancy):**

$$
\text{EGI} = \text{Monthly Rent} \times (1 - \text{Vacancy Rate})
$$

**Net Operating Income (annual):**

$$
\text{NOI} = (\text{EGI} - \text{Total Monthly Operating Expenses}) \times 12
$$

**Monthly Cash Flow:**

$$
\text{Monthly CF} = \text{EGI} - \text{Total Monthly Operating Expenses} - \text{Monthly Mortgage}
$$

**Cash-on-Cash Return:**

$$
\text{CoC} = \frac{\text{Annual Cash Flow}}{\text{Total Cash Invested}} \times 100
$$

Where:
- **EGI** = Effective Gross Income (rent adjusted for vacancy)
- **Operating Expenses** = Tax + Insurance + Maintenance + Management Fee + Other (excludes mortgage)
- **Management Fee** = Management Fee % × Monthly Gross Rent

*Source: Investopedia — Net Operating Income Definition (2025); BiggerPockets — Cash Flow Analysis Guide (2025)*

</div>

## Worked Example

A single-family rental property has the following profile:

| Input | Value |
|---|---|
| Monthly gross rent | $2,200 |
| Vacancy rate | 5% |
| Monthly property tax | $300 |
| Monthly insurance | $120 |
| Monthly maintenance | $165 |
| Management fee | 8% |
| Other monthly costs | $50 |
| Monthly mortgage (P&I) | $1,400 |
| Total cash invested | $70,000 |

**Step 1 — Effective Gross Income:**
$2,200 × (1 − 0.05) = **$2,090/month**

**Step 2 — Monthly operating expenses:**
Tax $300 + Insurance $120 + Maintenance $165 + Management ($2,200 × 8% = $176) + Other $50 = **$811/month**

**Step 3 — NOI:**
($2,090 − $811) × 12 = $1,279 × 12 = **$15,348/year**

**Step 4 — Monthly Cash Flow:**
$2,090 − $811 − $1,400 = **−$121/month** (negative!)

Wait — this deal is cash-flow negative with those inputs. This highlights why running the numbers matters. At the default assumption of $1,400 mortgage, the deal bleeds $1,452/year. To reach break-even, rent would need to be $2,363 or the mortgage payment would need to drop to $1,279.

**Cash-on-Cash Return (at break-even):**
$0 / $70,000 = **0%** — uninspiring but common in 2024–2025 rate environments.

At $2,400/month rent with identical costs: $2,280 EGI − $811 operating − $1,400 mortgage = $69/month positive = $828/year = **1.2% CoC** on $70,000. Still modest — which is why many investors in high-rate environments accept thin cash flow in exchange for strong appreciation potential.

## Key Factors That Affect Cash Flow

- **Vacancy rate:** A 5% vacancy assumption means roughly 18 days per year of lost rent. In strong rental markets (sub-3% vacancy), your effective income is higher. In resort markets or areas with seasonal demand, vacancy can run 15–25%, dramatically altering the math.
- **Maintenance reserve:** The 1% rule (budget 1% of property value annually for maintenance) is a starting point. Older homes, properties with aging systems, and multi-family units warrant 1.5–2%. Under-budgeting maintenance is the most common cause of unexpected negative cash flow.
- **Mortgage rate:** At 7% vs. 5% on a $240,000 loan, the monthly P&I difference is $330/month — $3,960/year. Interest rate environment has a larger impact on cash flow than most investors realize when evaluating deals.
- **Rent growth over time:** This calculator shows a point-in-time snapshot. Rents historically rise with inflation over time. A property that breaks even today may generate meaningful cash flow in years 3–5 after rent increases, even without refinancing.
- **Property management:** Self-managing saves 8–12% of gross rent but adds time and availability requirements. Factor in the value of your time when comparing managed vs. self-managed scenarios.
- **CapEx vs. maintenance:** This calculator captures routine maintenance. Large capital costs (roof, HVAC, water heater, appliances) are separate and lumpy. Maintain a CapEx reserve fund distinct from your operating maintenance budget.

## What Is a Good Cash-on-Cash Return?

| CoC Return | Assessment (2025 Rate Environment) |
|---|---|
| Below 0% | Negative cash flow — loss each month |
| 0–3% | Marginal; acceptable only with strong appreciation thesis |
| 3–6% | Average; comparable to dividend stocks with inflation hedge |
| 6–10% | Strong; typical in secondary/tertiary markets |
| 10%+ | Excellent; often requires distressed purchase or high-yield market |

In 2021–2022 at 3% mortgage rates, CoC returns of 6–8% were achievable in mid-tier markets. At 7% rates (2024–2025), the same properties often produce 0–3% CoC. This is why many experienced investors shifted to all-cash purchases or held properties rather than buying in high-rate environments.

## FAQ

<div className="faq-section">

**Q: What is NOI in real estate?**

A: Net Operating Income (NOI) is a property's annual income after operating expenses but before mortgage payments and income taxes. It equals effective gross income (rent adjusted for vacancy) minus property tax, insurance, maintenance, management fees, and other operating costs. NOI is used to calculate cap rate (NOI / Property Value) and is the standard profitability metric used in commercial real estate valuation. Unlike cash flow, NOI is unaffected by your financing — two investors with different mortgages on the same property have identical NOIs.

**Q: What is cash-on-cash return and how does it differ from ROI?**

A: Cash-on-cash (CoC) return measures annual cash flow divided by total cash invested — it reflects only the income you receive in your pocket each year relative to what you put in. ROI (total return on investment) includes all sources of wealth creation: cash flow, mortgage principal paydown, appreciation, and tax benefits over the entire holding period. CoC is a current-year income metric; ROI is a total-return metric. For a complete picture of rental property returns, use the Landlord ROI Calculator alongside this cash flow tool.

**Q: How can I improve negative cash flow on a rental property?**

A: There are five levers: (1) Increase rent to market rate — audit comparable rentals in your area; (2) Reduce vacancy by improving tenant retention (responsive maintenance, reasonable rent increases); (3) Cut operating expenses by self-managing, refinancing at a lower rate, or appealing a tax assessment; (4) Pay down the mortgage balance to reduce monthly P&I; (5) Reassess the market — if rents cannot support break-even cash flow at current valuations, the property may be priced above what rental economics can support.
