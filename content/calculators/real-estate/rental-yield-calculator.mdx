## What Rental Yield Actually Tells You

**Gross Rental Yield** is the simplest measure of return: annual rent divided by property value. It ignores all costs, making it useful for quick comparisons between properties but never sufficient for an actual investment decision.

**Net Rental Yield** is what you actually earn. It subtracts every operating expense — property tax, insurance, maintenance, and management fees — before dividing by property value. This is the number that matters.

**Annual Net Income** is the dollar amount left after expenses but before any mortgage payments. If this number is negative, the property cannot support itself even without financing.

**Expense Ratio** (annual expenses divided by annual rent) is a useful benchmark. A healthy residential rental typically runs 35–50% expense ratio. If yours is above 55%, profitability requires either higher rent or lower purchase price.

## How to Use This Calculator

1. Enter the current market value or your purchase price in **Property Value**.
2. Enter the monthly rent you receive (or the market rent if evaluating a potential purchase).
3. Fill in your annual property tax, insurance premium, and expected maintenance costs. If you don't know maintenance yet, use 1% of property value as a starting estimate.
4. Enter your property management fee percentage if you use a manager, or 0% if self-managing.
5. Read your **Gross Yield** and **Net Yield** — then compare to the benchmarks below.

## The Formula

<div className="formula-section">

**Gross Rental Yield:**

$$
\text{Gross Yield} = \frac{\text{Annual Rent}}{\text{Property Value}} \times 100
$$

**Net Rental Yield:**

$$
\text{Net Yield} = \frac{\text{Annual Rent} - \text{Annual Expenses}}{\text{Property Value}} \times 100
$$

Where:
- **Annual Rent** = Monthly Rent × 12
- **Annual Expenses** = Property Tax + Insurance + Maintenance + (Management Fee % × Annual Rent)
- **Property Value** = Purchase price or current market value

*Source: Investopedia — Rental Yield Definition (2025); National Association of Realtors — Rental Property Analysis Guide (2025)*

</div>

## Worked Example

A duplex is listed at $320,000. Each unit rents for $1,050/month, giving $2,100/month total rent.

**Annual rent:** $2,100 × 12 = $25,200

**Annual expenses:**
- Property tax: $4,200
- Insurance: $1,400
- Maintenance: $3,200 (1% of value)
- Management (9%): $2,268
- **Total expenses: $11,068**

**Gross yield:** $25,200 / $320,000 × 100 = **7.9%**

**Net yield:** ($25,200 − $11,068) / $320,000 × 100 = **4.4%**

At 4.4% net yield, this property generates $14,132 annually before mortgage payments. With a 20% down ($64,000) and a 7% rate 30-year mortgage, monthly P&I is $1,704 ($20,448/year) — meaning cash flow is negative by $6,316/year. The deal only works if appreciation compensates or if you can increase rents.

## Why Your Yield Could Be Higher (or Lower)

- **Purchase price vs. market rent ratio:** Markets with high home prices relative to rents (coastal cities, major metros) typically produce lower yields — often 3–5% gross. Secondary markets and Midwest cities often yield 7–10% gross due to lower property values relative to rents.
- **Vacancy rate:** This calculator assumes 100% occupancy. Actual net yield should be reduced by your expected vacancy. At 8% vacancy, 12 months of rent becomes 11 months, cutting gross yield proportionally. Always model a realistic vacancy assumption.
- **Management fees:** Self-managing adds 8–12% to your effective yield but costs you time. A property that yields 5.5% net with a manager may yield 7% if self-managed — a meaningful difference at scale.
- **CapEx reserves:** This calculator includes a maintenance line, but significant capital costs (roof replacement, HVAC, appliances) are separate. For older properties, budget an additional 1–2% of value annually in CapEx reserves on top of routine maintenance.
- **Financing costs:** Net yield ignores your mortgage. To evaluate actual cash flow, use the Real Estate Cash Flow Calculator, which accounts for monthly mortgage payments.
- **Tax treatment:** Mortgage interest, depreciation, and expense deductions can improve your after-tax return significantly. Net yield is a pre-tax metric; consult a tax professional to calculate your after-tax yield.

## What Is a Good Rental Yield?

There is no universal "good" yield — it depends on your market and investment goals.

| Market Type | Typical Gross Yield | Typical Net Yield |
|---|---|---|
| Gateway cities (NYC, SF, LA) | 2–4% | 1–2.5% |
| Mid-size metros (Atlanta, Dallas) | 5–7% | 3–5% |
| Secondary/tertiary markets | 7–12% | 5–8% |
| Rural/small towns | 10–15%+ | 7–12% |

Higher yields in secondary markets often come with lower appreciation and higher vacancy risk. Lower yields in gateway cities are typically offset by stronger long-term appreciation. Your yield threshold should reflect whether you are primarily an income investor or a total-return investor.

Most professional real estate investors target a minimum 5% net yield as a floor for a deal to make financial sense, though this benchmark rises when financing costs are high.

## FAQ

<div className="faq-section">

**Q: What is rental yield?**

A: Rental yield is the annual rent income expressed as a percentage of the property's value. Gross yield uses rent alone; net yield subtracts operating expenses. It measures how efficiently a property generates rental income relative to its price — similar to a dividend yield in stock investing.

**Q: What is the difference between gross and net rental yield?**

A: Gross yield divides annual rent by property value and ignores all costs — it is a quick comparison tool. Net yield deducts operating expenses (tax, insurance, maintenance, management fees) before dividing, giving a realistic picture of actual returns. The gap between gross and net is typically 2–4 percentage points. Always use net yield when making investment decisions.

**Q: What is a good rental yield?**

A: A net yield of 5–7% is generally considered solid for a residential rental in a mid-size US market (2025 data). Below 4% net, the property may be cash-flow negative once mortgage costs are factored in. Above 8% net yield often signals higher risk — either from a distressed market, higher vacancy, or deferred maintenance. The "right" yield depends on your financing cost, risk tolerance, and whether you are prioritizing cash flow or appreciation.
