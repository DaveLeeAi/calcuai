## What Your Results Mean

**Subject Property $/Sq Ft** is the purchase price divided by total living area square footage. This normalizes price so properties of different sizes can be compared fairly on a per-unit basis.

**Average Comparable $/Sq Ft** is the mean price per square foot across all comparable sales you entered. This represents the current market rate for similar homes in the same area.

**Estimated Value at Avg Comp PSF** multiplies your subject property's square footage by the average comparable PSF. If the property is priced above this estimate, it trades at a premium; below this estimate, it trades at a discount.

**Premium/Discount to Comps** expresses the pricing difference as a percentage. Positive = property costs more per sq ft than comps; negative = property costs less. A 5–10% variance is often within normal adjustment range; 15%+ warrants further investigation.

**Comparables Table** displays all four properties side by side with price, square footage, and $/sq ft for each, making it easy to spot outliers.

## How to Use This Calculator

1. Enter your **subject property's sale price** and **square footage** (heated living area only — exclude garage, unfinished basement, and covered porches unless they are climate-controlled living space).
2. Enter up to 3 comparable sales. Use properties that sold within the last 90 days, are within 0.5 miles (or 1 mile in rural areas), have the same number of bedrooms and bathrooms, and are similar in age and condition.
3. Review the Premium/Discount percentage. A discount of 1–5% is meaningfully favorable. A premium of 10%+ requires a specific justification (renovated kitchen, better lot, corner lot, premium view).
4. Use the Estimated Value as a reference point when making an offer or setting a list price.

## The Formula

<div className="formula-section">

**Price Per Square Foot:**

$$
\text{PSF} = \frac{\text{Sale Price}}{\text{Square Footage}}
$$

**Average Comparable PSF:**

$$
\text{Avg Comp PSF} = \frac{\text{PSF}_1 + \text{PSF}_2 + \text{PSF}_3}{n}
$$

**Estimated Value:**

$$
\text{Estimated Value} = \text{Subject Sq Ft} \times \text{Avg Comp PSF}
$$

**Premium or Discount (%):**

$$
\text{Premium/Discount} = \frac{\text{Subject PSF} - \text{Avg Comp PSF}}{\text{Avg Comp PSF}} \times 100
$$

Where:
- **n** = number of comparable sales entered (1–3)
- A positive result = trading at a premium to comps
- A negative result = trading at a discount to comps

*Source: Zillow Research — Price Per Square Foot Methodology (2025); NAR — Comparable Sales Analysis (2025)*

</div>

## Worked Example

You are evaluating a 4-bedroom, 2.5-bathroom home listed at $425,000 with 2,100 sq ft of heated living area. Three recent sales in the same neighborhood:

| Property | Sale Price | Sq Ft | $/Sq Ft |
|---|---|---|---|
| Subject | $425,000 | 2,100 | $202.38 |
| Comp 1 (2 blocks north) | $400,000 | 2,000 | $200.00 |
| Comp 2 (same street) | $450,000 | 2,200 | $204.55 |
| Comp 3 (adjacent block) | $410,000 | 1,950 | $210.26 |

**Average Comp PSF:** ($200.00 + $204.55 + $210.26) / 3 = **$204.94**

**Estimated Value:** 2,100 × $204.94 = **$430,374**

**Premium/Discount:** ($202.38 − $204.94) / $204.94 × 100 = **−1.3% (discount)**

Interpretation: The subject property at $425,000 is priced approximately $5,374 below estimated market value based on comparable sales. This is a favorable entry point. However, note that Comp 3 (the highest PSF at $210.26) was only 1,950 sq ft — smaller homes typically command a higher PSF premium. If you adjust for this size effect, the true market PSF for a 2,100 sq ft home may be closer to $202–$204, making the subject property fairly priced rather than discounted.

## Key Factors That Affect Price Per Square Foot

- **Size adjustment (the inverse size effect):** Smaller homes almost always command a higher price per square foot than larger homes in the same neighborhood. A 1,200 sq ft bungalow at $300/sq ft and a 2,400 sq ft home at $230/sq ft may both be priced correctly. When selecting comps, try to use homes within 10–15% of your subject's size to minimize this distortion.
- **Condition and renovation level:** A fully renovated kitchen and bathrooms can add $20–$40/sq ft in premium markets. A property needing $40,000 in work should trade at a discount to fully updated comps of similar size. PSF comparisons must account for condition — check listing photos and property history for comp sales.
- **Lot size:** PSF measures only the structure, not the land. Two identical 1,800 sq ft homes may sell at very different PSF values if one sits on 0.25 acres and the other on 1.5 acres. In land-constrained markets, lot value is a significant independent variable.
- **Age and architecture:** Newer construction generally commands a PSF premium due to modern layouts, energy efficiency, and reduced maintenance. Historic homes in desirable neighborhoods may also command premiums. Mid-century homes in poor condition typically trade at deep discounts.
- **Location within the neighborhood:** Even within the same zip code, a property one block closer to a highway, a cell tower, or a commercial zone may trade 5–15% below identical homes on quieter streets. Street-level PSF variation within neighborhoods is real.
- **Above-grade vs. total square footage:** Appraisers typically value above-grade square footage (main floor + upper floors) more highly than below-grade (finished basement). Some listing agents include finished basement in total sq ft, inflating the denominator and understating PSF. Always verify how square footage was measured.

## How Appraisers Use Price Per Square Foot

Professional real estate appraisers use the Sales Comparison Approach, which incorporates PSF as one input alongside numerous adjustments for bedroom/bathroom count, garage, lot size, condition, and location. A trained appraiser will apply dollar adjustments (typically $8,000–$15,000 per bathroom, $5,000–$10,000 per bedroom) rather than relying on PSF alone.

This calculator provides a simplified PSF comparison — useful for quick screening and offer negotiation but not a substitute for a full appraisal. When the stakes are high (large purchase, refinance, dispute), commission a licensed appraiser.

## FAQ

<div className="faq-section">

**Q: What is price per square foot in real estate?**

A: Price per square foot (PSF) is the sale price of a property divided by its total heated living area in square feet. For example, a $400,000 home with 2,000 sq ft has a PSF of $200. It is the most common normalization metric for comparing homes of different sizes — similar to price-per-ounce for commodities. PSF allows buyers, sellers, and investors to quickly gauge whether a property is priced in line with, above, or below similar homes in the same market.

**Q: What is the average price per square foot in the US?**

A: The national median price per square foot for single-family homes sold in the US was approximately $185–$195 in 2025, according to Zillow Research and the National Association of Realtors. However, this average is nearly meaningless for individual transactions because PSF varies by a factor of 10 between rural markets and gateway cities. The relevant benchmark is always local: what did similar homes sell for per square foot within the last 90 days in the same neighborhood.

**Q: What are the limitations of using price per square foot to evaluate a home?**

A: PSF is a useful screening metric but has four major limitations. First, it ignores lot value — land can account for 20–60% of a home's value in some markets. Second, smaller homes have higher PSF than larger homes even at the same market value (the inverse size effect). Third, it cannot capture condition, layout quality, or view premiums. Fourth, above-grade and below-grade square footage are not equally valuable, but many PSF calculations treat them identically. Use PSF as a starting point, not a final verdict — always review actual comparable sales with adjustments for condition, lot, and features.
