## What Your Results Mean

**Total study hours** is the estimated active study time including all review passes — not calendar time. **Days to complete** divides that by your daily study hours budget. **Minutes per page** is your personal study pace for this material type, useful for future planning. The **study schedule outline** gives you a day-by-day breakdown with a first-pass reading phase and review phases allocated separately.

## How to Use This Calculator

1. Count the pages in your study material (or use the chapter/unit breakdown from your syllabus).
2. Estimate your reading speed — 200–300 WPM is typical for adults reading non-fiction; 150–200 WPM for dense technical material.
3. Select difficulty: easy for narrative/fiction, medium for standard textbooks, hard for technical content, very hard for professional exam material (bar exam, USMLE, CPA).
4. Set review repetitions: 2 is the minimum for retention; 3–4 for high-stakes exams using spaced repetition.
5. Enter how many hours you realistically study per day — be honest about interruptions.

## The Formula

<div className="formula-section">

**Formula:**
$$
\text{Base Hours} = \frac{\text{Pages} \times \text{Words/Page}}{\text{WPM} \times 60}
$$
$$
\text{Total Hours} = \text{Base Hours} \times D_{\text{multiplier}} \times \text{Repetitions}
$$

Where:
- **WPM** = your reading speed in words per minute
- **D multiplier:** easy = 1.0, medium = 1.5, hard = 2.0, very hard = 2.8
- **Repetitions** = number of complete review passes (minimum 2 for exam retention)

*Source: Dunlosky et al. (2013); Cepeda et al. (2006) — spaced repetition efficacy research*

</div>

## Worked Example

**MCAT prep:** 400 pages of review material, 200 WPM (dense medical content), "very hard" difficulty, 3 repetitions, 4 hours/day.

- Base hours: (400 × 300) ÷ (200 × 60) = **10 hours** (first pass only)
- Adjusted for difficulty (2.8×): 10 × 2.8 = **28 hours**
- With 3 repetitions: 28 × 3 = **84 hours total**
- Days at 4 hrs/day: **21 days (3 weeks)**

This matches real MCAT prep timelines. Beginner students typically need 3–6 months for a full prep course because review repetitions keep multiplying the total commitment.

## Difficulty Multipliers Explained

| Difficulty | Multiplier | Material Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Easy | 1.0× | Fiction, biography, casual non-fiction |
| Medium | 1.5× | Standard textbooks, history, psychology |
| Hard | 2.0× | Engineering, statistics, chemistry |
| Very Hard | 2.8× | Law, medicine, advanced math, board exams |

The multiplier accounts for slower processing speed, re-reading sentences, making notes, and solving practice problems embedded in the reading.

## FAQ

<div className="faq-section">

**Q: How many hours per day should I study?**
A: Research on cognitive performance (Ericsson, 1993) suggests 3–4 hours of focused, deliberate study per day is the sustainable maximum for most people. Beyond 4 hours, diminishing returns set in rapidly — a 6-hour "study marathon" often produces less retention than two 2.5-hour focused sessions with a break in between. Elite students who appear to study 8+ hours/day are typically spending 4 hours in genuine focused study and 4 hours in lower-intensity review, re-reading, or practice problems.

**Q: Does re-reading actually help retention?**
A: Passive re-reading is one of the least effective study strategies according to Dunlosky et al.'s landmark 2013 meta-analysis in Psychological Science. It creates a "familiarity illusion" — the material feels known without being retrievable under test conditions. Instead, use **active recall** (close the book and try to recall key points), **practice testing** (answer questions, do problem sets), and **spaced repetition** (review at expanding intervals: 1 day, 3 days, 1 week, 2 weeks). These strategies produce 2–4× better long-term retention than re-reading.

**Q: What is the best study schedule for exam prep?**
A: A research-backed schedule uses spaced repetition and interleaving: (1) Initial pass — read the full chapter; (2) Review at 24 hours — active recall without the book; (3) Review at 3 days — flashcards or practice questions; (4) Review at 7 days — full chapter practice test; (5) Final review 1 day before — light scan of weak areas only. This schedule produces significantly better retention than cramming (Cepeda et al., 2006). For multi-week exams, divide material into modules and cycle through all modules repeatedly rather than mastering one module before moving on.

## Sources & Methodology

Difficulty multipliers and review repetition values are calibrated against the Dunlosky et al. (2013) study technique efficacy rankings published in Psychological Science in the Public Interest and Cepeda et al.'s (2006) spacing effect research. Reading speed norms (200–300 WPM for adult non-fiction, 150–200 WPM for technical material) are based on Brysbaert (2019) published in the Journal of Memory and Language. The calculator assumes active, focused study — not passive highlighting or re-reading.
