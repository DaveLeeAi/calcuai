## What Your Results Mean

The calculator returns four bedtime options: 4 cycles (6 hrs), 5 cycles (7.5 hrs), 6 cycles (9 hrs), and an early 7-cycle option for those with flexible schedules. **Optimal bedtime** is the 5 or 6-cycle option recommended for most adults — matching the National Sleep Foundation's 7–9 hour guidance. The quality rating indicates which options provide enough REM sleep (concentrated in the final 2 cycles) for cognitive recovery.

Sleep onset time is subtracted automatically because your clock starts when your head hits the pillow, not when you actually fall asleep.

## How to Use This Calculator

1. Enter the time you need to wake up (or want to wake up — be realistic about your schedule).
2. Set your sleep onset time — 14 minutes is the US average, but anxious sleepers may need 20–30 minutes.
3. Review all four bedtime options and choose the one that fits your evening schedule while still getting 5–6 cycles.
4. Set your alarm for the target wake time and your bedtime reminder for the matching bedtime.

## The Formula

<div className="formula-section">

**Formula:**
$$
\text{Bedtime} = \text{Wake Time} - (N \times 90\text{ min}) - t_{\text{onset}}
$$

Where:
- **N** = number of sleep cycles (4, 5, 6, or 7 for the four options)
- **90 min** = standard human sleep cycle duration (NREM + REM)
- **t_onset** = time to fall asleep (minutes)

*Source: Walker, Matthew — Why We Sleep (2017); National Sleep Foundation — Sleep Duration Recommendations (2015)*

## Sleep Cycle Structure

Each 90-minute cycle consists of distinct stages:

| Stage | Duration | Function |
|---|---|---|
| N1 (Light sleep) | 5–10 min | Transition, onset |
| N2 (True sleep) | 20–25 min | Memory consolidation begins |
| N3 (Deep sleep) | 20–40 min | Physical repair, immune function |
| REM sleep | 10–25 min | Emotional processing, creativity |

Deep sleep (N3) is most concentrated in the first 2 cycles (before midnight for most people). REM sleep dominates cycles 4–6, which is why cutting sleep short by 1–2 cycles disproportionately reduces REM — impairing emotional regulation and learning.

## FAQ

<div className="faq-section">

**Q: How long is one sleep cycle?**
A: One complete sleep cycle lasts approximately **90 minutes** for most adults, though it can range from 80 to 110 minutes individually. Each cycle progresses through N1 light sleep, N2 true sleep, N3 deep/slow-wave sleep, and REM sleep. The first cycle of the night is N3-heavy (mostly deep sleep), while later cycles are REM-heavy. Waking during N3 deep sleep causes the worst grogginess — which is why hitting snooze for 9 minutes never helps.

**Q: How many hours of sleep do adults need?**
A: The National Sleep Foundation recommends **7–9 hours** for adults aged 18–64, and **7–8 hours** for those 65+. This corresponds to 5–6 complete sleep cycles. Sleeping 6 hours (4 cycles) chronically impairs cognitive performance, immune function, and metabolic health — even if you don't feel tired, because chronic sleep debt impairs your ability to accurately assess your own impairment. Only about 3% of the population carries a genetic variant (BHLHE41) allowing genuinely healthy function on 6 hours.

**Q: Why do I wake up tired even after 8 hours of sleep?**
A: Waking tired after 8 hours usually means one of four things: (1) **Mid-cycle waking** — your alarm went off during deep sleep (N3), causing sleep inertia that takes 15–60 minutes to clear. This calculator helps you avoid this. (2) **Sleep fragmentation** — sleep apnea, environmental noise, or alcohol causes micro-arousals that prevent reaching restorative deep sleep stages. (3) **Circadian misalignment** — sleeping and waking at inconsistent times disrupts your internal clock. (4) **Poor sleep quality** — alcohol, late caffeine (caffeine has a 5–6 hour half-life), blue light exposure, or a warm room (ideal sleep temp: 65–68°F) all reduce deep sleep.

## Sources & Methodology

Sleep cycle duration of 90 minutes is the consensus value from sleep science research, including Matthew Walker's peer-reviewed work and the National Sleep Foundation's clinical guidelines. The 14-minute sleep onset average is from the American Sleep Association's survey data. Sleep stage timing and proportions per cycle are based on polysomnography data from Walker (2017) and the AASM's International Classification of Sleep Disorders. Recommended cycles (5–6) align with the National Sleep Foundation's 7–9 hour guideline for adults.
