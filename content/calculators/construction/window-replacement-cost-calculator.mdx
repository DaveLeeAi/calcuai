## What This Estimate Shows

This calculator provides installed cost ranges for seven window types — single-hung, double-hung, casement, sliding, bay/bow, picture, and awning — based on your frame material, glass type, window size, installation method, and region. It produces a low/mid/high project total, a per-window cost, and a comparison table showing what the same project would cost across all seven window styles.

The **material cost** covers the window unit itself: frame, glass, hardware, and weatherstripping. The base cost per window depends on window type ($200–$2,500 range), then multiplied by frame material (0.90x for aluminum to 1.35x for wood), glass type (1.0x for standard double-pane to 1.45x for Low-E triple-pane), and window size (1.0x for standard 3x4 to 1.75x for oversized 5x6). Material costs are consistent across all regions.

The **labor cost** covers professional installation: removal of old windows, fitting, shimming, insulating, flashing, and trim work. Labor varies by installation type — replacement-in-frame ($150–$300 per window) reuses the existing frame, full-frame replacement ($300–$600) removes everything down to rough opening, and new openings ($500–$1,000) require cutting into walls and structural framing. Labor is the component most affected by your region: the South is 15% below the national average, while the West Coast is 25% above.

The **energy savings estimate** provides expected annual heating and cooling savings based on your glass type selection. Low-E triple-pane windows can save 20–30% on energy bills compared to standard double-pane, potentially recovering the upgrade cost in 5–10 years in cold climates.

## The Formula

Window replacement cost is estimated per window using base material cost, three material multipliers (frame, glass, size), and a regional labor rate.

**Material cost per window:**

$$C_{mat} = B_{type} \times M_{frame} \times M_{glass} \times M_{size}$$

**Labor cost per window:**

$$C_{lab} = R_{install} \times M_{region}$$

**Total project cost:**

$$C_{low} = (C_{mat,low} + C_{lab,low}) \times N$$

$$C_{high} = (C_{mat,high} + C_{lab,high}) \times N$$

$$C_{mid} = \frac{C_{low} + C_{high}}{2}$$

Where:

- **B_type** = base material cost per window by window type (low/high range)
- **M_frame** = frame material multiplier (0.90–1.35)
- **M_glass** = glass type multiplier (1.0–1.45)
- **M_size** = window size multiplier (1.0–1.75)
- **R_install** = installation labor rate per window (low/high range)
- **M_region** = regional labor multiplier (0.85–1.25)
- **N** = number of windows

## Worked Example: 10 Double-Hung Vinyl Windows, Replacement-in-Frame, National Average

A homeowner is replacing 10 standard double-hung windows with vinyl frames, standard double-pane glass, standard 3x4 size, replacement-in-frame installation, at national average labor rates.

**Step-by-step:**

1. Look up double-hung base material cost: $250–$500 per window
2. Apply vinyl frame multiplier: $250 × 1.0 = **$250 low** | $500 × 1.0 = **$500 high**
3. Apply double-pane glass multiplier: $250 × 1.0 = **$250 low** | $500 × 1.0 = **$500 high**
4. Apply standard 3x4 size multiplier: $250 × 1.0 = **$250 low** | $500 × 1.0 = **$500 high**
5. Look up replacement-in-frame labor: $150–$300 per window
6. Apply national labor multiplier (1.0): **$150 low** | **$300 high**
7. Cost per window low: $250 + $150 = **$400**
8. Cost per window high: $500 + $300 = **$800**
9. Total low: $400 × 10 = **$4,000**
10. Total high: $800 × 10 = **$8,000**
11. Total mid: ($4,000 + $8,000) / 2 = **$6,000**
12. Cost per window mid: $6,000 / 10 = **$600**

**Interpretation:** At $6,000 midpoint, a 10-window vinyl replacement is the most cost-effective whole-house upgrade. Upgrading to wood frames would push the midpoint to $8,100 (35% more on materials). Adding Low-E triple-pane glass to vinyl frames would bring the total to approximately $8,700. For the same 10 windows on the West Coast, labor increases 25%, pushing the midpoint from $6,000 to $6,563.

## Factors That Affect Window Replacement Cost

**Window type** drives the largest cost variance. Standard single-hung and sliding windows start at $200 per unit for materials, while bay/bow windows reach $2,500. Most homeowners replace like-for-like to avoid structural modifications that add $500–$1,000 per opening.

**Frame material** creates a 0.90x–1.35x swing on material cost. Vinyl is the default at 55% market share because it costs the least and requires zero maintenance. Wood frames offer the highest R-value (insulation) and best aesthetics but require painting every 3–5 years. Fiberglass is the performance compromise — nearly as strong as aluminum, insulates like vinyl, and costs 25% more.

**Glass type** is the most impactful energy decision. Upgrading from standard double-pane to Low-E triple-pane adds 45% to material cost but can save 20–30% on annual heating and cooling. In cold climates (heating degree days above 5,000), Low-E triple-pane typically pays for itself in 7–10 years through energy savings of $200–$500 per year on a 10-window home.

**Installation type** determines labor cost. Replacement-in-frame ($150–$300 per window) is the most common — the existing frame stays, and the new sash slides in. Full-frame replacement ($300–$600) is necessary when the frame is rotted, water-damaged, or you want to change window size. New openings ($500–$1,000) require cutting into siding and framing and are typically structural projects requiring a permit.

**Window size** affects material cost. Standard 3x4 ft windows are priced at the base rate. Upgrading to 4x5 ft adds 35%, and oversized 5x6 ft windows add 75%. Labor per window stays roughly the same regardless of size for replacement-in-frame installation.

**Project volume** impacts real-world pricing. Most contractors offer 5–15% discounts for whole-house replacements (10+ windows) due to setup efficiency. This calculator uses per-window rates without volume discount — actual quotes for large projects may come in below the estimate.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- All costs are **per-window installed prices** including materials, labor, and basic trim. Custom trim, exterior capping, and decorative grilles are not included.
- Cost ranges reflect **2025–2026 U.S. national averages** from HomeAdvisor and Angi. Local pricing may be 15–25% higher or lower depending on regional labor and material availability.
- The calculator assumes **standard rectangular openings** in good structural condition. Non-standard shapes (arched, circular, hexagonal) typically cost 50–100% more than standard windows.
- **Replacement-in-frame** assumes the existing frame is in sound condition with no rot, water damage, or structural issues. If frame repair is needed, full-frame pricing is more accurate.
- **Disposal costs** for old windows ($25–$50 per window) are included in labor estimates. Lead paint abatement for pre-1978 homes is not included and may add $200–$500 per window.
- Energy savings estimates are **approximate ranges** from ENERGY STAR and vary by climate zone, home insulation, existing window condition, and HVAC efficiency. Actual savings depend on local energy rates and heating/cooling usage patterns.
- This calculator does not account for **permits** ($50–$200 for new openings in most jurisdictions), **structural engineering** if load-bearing walls are involved, or **exterior finish work** (siding repair, stucco patching) around new openings.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much does it cost to replace a window?

A single standard double-hung vinyl window costs $400–$800 installed with replacement-in-frame installation at national average labor rates. The material portion is $250–$500 per window, and labor is $150–$300 per window. Upgrading to wood frames increases the per-window cost to $488–$975 (material), while Low-E triple-pane glass adds 45% to material cost. Full-frame replacement pushes labor to $300–$600 per window, and new structural openings cost $500–$1,000 per window for labor alone.

### How much does it cost to replace all windows in a house?

A whole-house window replacement for a typical 10-window home costs $4,000–$8,000 with vinyl frames and replacement-in-frame installation. A 20-window home runs $8,000–$16,000. Upgrading to wood frames adds 35% to materials, and Low-E triple-pane glass adds 45%. Most contractors offer 5–15% discounts for whole-house projects of 10+ windows, which is not reflected in per-window estimates. Budget $6,000–$12,000 for a mid-range 15-window home with double-hung vinyl and standard double-pane glass.

### What is the cheapest type of window to replace?

Single-hung vinyl windows with standard double-pane glass and replacement-in-frame installation are the cheapest option at $350–$700 per window installed. Aluminum frames reduce material cost by 10% ($315–$630), but aluminum conducts heat poorly and is not recommended in extreme climates. Sliding windows are similarly priced at $350–$750 per window. For the absolute lowest cost, aluminum-frame single-hung windows with replacement-in-frame installation run $315–$630 per window.

### Are vinyl or wood windows better?

Vinyl windows are better for budget and maintenance — they cost 35% less than wood and never need painting or staining. Wood windows are better for aesthetics, insulation (highest R-value of any frame material), and historic home compliance. Fiberglass is the middle ground: 25% more than vinyl, nearly as strong as wood, and maintenance-free. For most homeowners replacing 10+ windows, vinyl provides the best value per dollar. If budget allows and appearance matters, fiberglass offers the best performance-to-maintenance ratio.

### How long do replacement windows last?

Vinyl windows last 20–40 years with zero maintenance. Wood windows last 30–50+ years but require repainting every 3–5 years and regular caulking. Fiberglass windows last 30–50 years with minimal maintenance. Aluminum windows last 20–30 years but may develop condensation issues in cold climates due to thermal conductivity. The glass type also affects longevity — Low-E coatings degrade very slowly and maintain performance for 15–20 years. ENERGY STAR-rated windows carry a minimum 10-year warranty on glass seal failure.

### Should I replace all windows at once or one at a time?

Replacing all windows at once is almost always more cost-effective. Most contractors offer 5–15% discounts on whole-house projects (10+ windows) because they can mobilize once, set up scaffolding once, and order materials in bulk. Individual window replacements cost more per window due to minimum trip charges ($150–$300 per visit) and the inability to batch material orders. The exception is bay/bow windows ($950–$2,800 each) — their high cost may justify phased replacement.
