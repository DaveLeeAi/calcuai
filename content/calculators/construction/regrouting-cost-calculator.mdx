## What This Estimates

This calculator provides installed cost ranges for regrouting existing tile using three grout types — standard sanded, epoxy, and urethane — broken down into material cost, labor cost, old grout removal, and optional sealing. It produces a low/mid/high estimate based on your selections and a comparison table showing what the same job would cost in every grout type.

The **material and labor split** reflects industry-standard pricing: approximately 30% of installed grout cost is materials and 70% is labor. Standard sanded grout runs $5–$10 per square foot installed, epoxy runs $8–$15, and urethane runs $10–$18. These ranges come from HomeAdvisor and Angi 2025–2026 national averages.

The **regional multiplier** adjusts labor costs only — not materials — to reflect geographic pricing differences. Northeast labor runs 20% above national average, while Southern states run 15% below. Material costs remain constant because grout is a commodity product with relatively uniform national pricing.

The **grout removal cost** is a separate line item because removal method significantly affects both cost and risk. Manual removal with a grout saw costs $3 per square foot but minimizes chip risk. Power-tool removal with an oscillating tool costs $1.50 per square foot but carries slight risk of chipping adjacent tiles. Choosing no removal (overlay) is the cheapest option but is generally not recommended by tile professionals — new grout adheres poorly over old grout.

## How to Use This Calculator

1. **Enter the tile area** — measure the total square footage of tile to be regrouted. For a bathroom floor, multiply length by width. For a shower, measure each wall separately and add the floor. A standard bathtub surround is approximately 50–60 square feet; a full bathroom with floor and shower is 80–120 square feet.

2. **Choose your grout type** — standard sanded grout is the most common and cheapest option, suitable for grout lines 1/8-inch or wider. Epoxy grout is waterproof and stain-resistant, ideal for showers, kitchen backsplashes, and high-moisture areas. Urethane grout offers the best flexibility and crack resistance, best for areas with slight movement or vibration.

3. **Select the removal method** — manual removal is recommended for delicate or expensive tile. Power-tool removal is faster and cheaper but increases the risk of chipping, especially on porcelain and glass tile. No removal (overlay) is not recommended but may work for very shallow grout lines with minimal damage.

4. **Choose sealing** — standard sealer ($1.50/sq ft) protects against moisture and stains for 1–3 years. Premium sealer ($2.50/sq ft) provides 3–5 years of protection and is recommended for shower floors and kitchen areas. Epoxy grout does not require sealing. Urethane grout does not require sealing.

5. **Select your region** — this adjusts labor rates for your area. Material costs remain the same nationwide.

## The Formula

Regrouting cost is estimated by splitting installed cost into material and labor components, then adding removal and sealing.

**Component costs:**

$$C_{material} = A \times R_m$$

$$C_{labor} = A \times R_l \times M_r$$

$$C_{removal} = A \times R_{rem}$$

$$C_{sealing} = A \times R_{seal}$$

**Total cost range:**

$$C_{low} = C_{material,low} + C_{labor,low} + C_{removal} + C_{sealing}$$

$$C_{high} = C_{material,high} + C_{labor,high} + C_{removal} + C_{sealing}$$

$$C_{mid} = \frac{C_{low} + C_{high}}{2}$$

Where:

- **A** = tile area in square feet
- **R_m** = material rate per sq ft (varies by grout type)
- **R_l** = labor rate per sq ft (varies by grout type)
- **M_r** = regional labor multiplier (0.85–1.25)
- **R_rem** = removal rate per sq ft (manual: $3, power-tool: $1.50, none: $0)
- **R_seal** = sealing rate per sq ft (none: $0, standard: $1.50, premium: $2.50)

## Factors That Affect Regrouting Cost

**Grout type** creates the largest cost difference. Epoxy costs 60% more than standard sanded grout but lasts twice as long and never needs sealing — making it cheaper over a 20-year span. Urethane is the most expensive upfront but offers the best crack resistance for floors with slight movement.

**Old grout removal method** is the second-largest variable. Manual removal at $3 per square foot doubles the removal cost compared to power tools, but it is strongly recommended for natural stone, glass mosaic, and any tile worth more than $10 per square foot. Skipping removal entirely saves $90–$180 on a 60-square-foot job but risks adhesion failure within 1–3 years.

**Tile area** drives total cost linearly. A small backsplash (15–20 sq ft) might cost $150–$300 total, while a full master bathroom (100–150 sq ft) runs $800–$2,000+ depending on grout type and options.

**Regional labor rates** swing labor cost by up to 40% — a $420 labor bill in the national average becomes $357 in the South and $525 on the West Coast. Material and removal costs are not affected by region.

**Tile condition** affects actual cost beyond this estimate. If existing tiles are cracked, loose, or water-damaged behind the grout, the job expands from regrouting into tile repair or replacement. A professional will assess this during the quoting process.

**Sealing requirements** depend on grout type. Standard sanded grout must be sealed — budget $1.50–$2.50 per square foot for initial sealing plus resealing every 1–2 years. Epoxy and urethane grouts are inherently waterproof and never need sealing, which offsets part of their higher upfront cost.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- All costs are **installed prices** including labor and materials. DIY material-only costs are approximately 30% of the installed price shown.
- Cost ranges reflect **2025–2026 U.S. national averages** from HomeAdvisor and Angi. Your local market may be 15–25% higher or lower depending on regional labor rates.
- The calculator assumes **existing tile is in good condition** — no cracked, loose, or water-damaged tiles requiring repair or replacement before regrouting.
- **Grout removal rates** assume standard ceramic or porcelain tile with cement-based grout. Removing epoxy grout from existing joints costs 50–100% more than removing standard grout.
- The calculator does not account for **shower pan or waterproofing membrane repair**, **tile replacement**, **mold remediation behind tile**, or **structural substrate repair**.
- **Minimum job charges** apply — most tile contractors have a minimum of $300–$500 regardless of area size. Very small jobs (under 20 sq ft) may cost more per square foot than the rates shown.
- Sealing costs assume a **single application**. Annual re-sealing of standard grout is an ongoing maintenance expense not included in this estimate.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much does regrouting cost per square foot?

Regrouting costs $5–$10 per square foot for standard sanded grout, $8–$15 for epoxy grout, and $10–$18 for urethane grout — all installed prices at national average labor rates. Add $1.50–$3.00 per square foot for old grout removal and $1.50–$2.50 for sealing (standard grout only). For a typical 60-square-foot bathroom, total cost ranges from $300 to $1,260 depending on grout type, removal method, and sealing.

### Is regrouting worth it vs. retiling?

Regrouting costs 15–20% of full tile replacement and is worth it when the existing tile is in good condition but the grout is cracked, stained, or moldy. A 60-square-foot regrouting job costs $300–$780, while replacing the same tile costs $3,000–$5,000. Regrouting is not worth it if more than 20% of tiles are cracked or loose, if there is water damage behind the tile, or if you want to change the tile style entirely.

### How long does regrouting take?

Professional regrouting takes 1–3 days for a typical bathroom. Standard sanded grout requires 1–2 days of work plus 24 hours before the area gets wet. Epoxy and urethane grout take 2–3 days of work plus 48 hours for full cure. The old grout removal step takes the most time — manual removal of a 60-square-foot area takes 4–8 hours, while power-tool removal takes 2–4 hours.

### What type of grout lasts the longest?

Epoxy grout lasts 20–30 years and is the most durable option — it is waterproof, stain-proof, and never needs sealing. Urethane grout lasts 15–25 years and offers the best flexibility for floors with slight movement. Standard sanded grout lasts 10–15 years with proper sealing every 1–2 years. For showers and wet areas, epoxy grout is the best long-term investment despite costing 60% more upfront than standard grout.
