## What This Estimate Shows

This calculator estimates the total cost to tear off an existing deck and replace it with new decking. It produces a low/mid/high cost range for your selected material, broken down into six line items: demolition, decking material, decking labor, substructure work, railing, and stairs. It also shows a side-by-side comparison of all five decking materials at national rates.

**Demolition** is charged at a flat $5 per square foot, covering tear-off of existing decking boards, disposal, and haul-away. This rate assumes standard residential demolition — a deck with built-in features (hot tub platforms, integrated planters, multi-level transitions) may cost more to demo.

**Decking cost** is split into material (~45%) and labor (~55%). Material rates cover the decking boards themselves. Labor rates cover cutting, fastening, and finishing. The regional multiplier applies to labor only — material prices are relatively consistent nationally, but labor rates vary 15–25% by region.

**Substructure work** ranges from $0 (boards-only replacement on a sound frame) to $15–$25 per square foot for full frame replacement including joists, beams, ledger board, and posts. A boards-only replacement is appropriate when the existing frame passes inspection — no rot, no structural sag, no code violations.

**Railing and stairs** are priced per linear foot and per tread, respectively. Enter 0 for either if you are keeping existing railing or have no stairs.

## The Formula

Deck replacement cost combines demolition, decking (material + labor), substructure, railing, and stairs.

**Area:**

$$A = L \times W$$

**Demolition:**

$$D = A \times \$5$$

**Decking cost:**

$$C_{mat} = A \times R_{mat}$$

$$C_{lab} = A \times R_{lab} \times M_{region}$$

**Substructure:**

$$C_{sub} = A \times R_{sub}$$

**Railing:**

$$C_{rail} = L_{rail} \times R_{rail}$$

**Stairs:**

$$C_{stair} = N_{treads} \times R_{stair}$$

**Totals:**

$$C_{low} = D + C_{mat,low} + C_{lab,low} + C_{sub,low} + C_{rail,low} + C_{stair,low}$$

$$C_{high} = D + C_{mat,high} + C_{lab,high} + C_{sub,high} + C_{rail,high} + C_{stair,high}$$

$$C_{mid} = \frac{C_{low} + C_{high}}{2}$$

Where:

- **L, W** = deck length and width in feet
- **R_mat** = material cost per sq ft (varies by decking type)
- **R_lab** = labor cost per sq ft (varies by decking type)
- **M_region** = regional labor multiplier (0.85–1.25)
- **R_sub** = substructure rate per sq ft (none: $0, partial: $5–$10, full: $15–$25)
- **R_rail** = railing rate per linear foot (wood: $20–$40, composite: $35–$60, metal: $50–$80, cable: $60–$100)
- **R_stair** = stair rate per tread ($250–$450)

4. Composite labor low: 192 x $13.75 x 1.0 = **$2,640** | high: 192 x $24.75 x 1.0 = **$4,752**

6. Wood railing low: 28 x $20 = **$560** | high: 28 x $40 = **$1,120**

8. Total low: $960 + $2,160 + $2,640 + $960 + $560 + $1,000 = **$8,280**
9. Total high: $960 + $3,888 + $4,752 + $1,920 + $1,120 + $1,800 = **$14,440**

## Factors That Affect Deck Replacement Cost

**Decking material** is the largest cost variable. Pressure-treated lumber at $15–$25/sq ft installed is one-third the price of tropical hardwood at $35–$60/sq ft. However, lifetime cost tells a different story — pressure-treated boards need replacement every 10–15 years, while composite and hardwood last 25–75 years with minimal maintenance.

**Substructure condition** can double the project cost. A boards-only replacement on a sound frame is straightforward. Full substructure replacement — new joists, beams, ledger board, and posts — adds $15–$25 per square foot, essentially rebuilding the entire deck. Have a structural inspection before committing to boards-only replacement.

**Railing** adds $560–$2,800 for a typical 28-foot perimeter in wood to cable. Code requires railing on any deck 30 inches or more above grade, and most jurisdictions require 36-inch minimum height with balusters spaced no more than 4 inches apart.

**Stairs** cost $250–$450 per tread installed, including stringers and risers. A deck 3–4 feet above grade requires 4–6 treads. Stair railing is typically priced separately from deck railing.

**Regional labor rates** vary 15–25% from the national average. The South and Midwest run 10–15% below national average due to lower cost of living and larger labor pools. The Northeast and West Coast run 20–25% above due to higher wages and permitting complexity.

**Permits** are required in most jurisdictions for deck replacement, especially when substructure work is involved. Permit fees range from $100 to $500. Some municipalities require engineered drawings for decks over a certain size or height.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much does it cost to replace a deck?

Replacing a 192-square-foot deck costs $3,840–$12,480 depending on material — from $3,840–$5,760 for pressure-treated lumber to $7,680–$12,480 for tropical hardwood. These figures include demolition at $5 per square foot and new decking installation at national average rates. Add $960–$4,800 for substructure work if the frame needs repair, plus $560–$2,800 for new railing and $250–$450 per stair tread.

### Should I repair or replace my deck?

Replace if the substructure shows significant rot, structural sag, or code violations — repair costs escalate quickly once framing is compromised. Replace if more than 25% of decking boards are damaged — spot-replacing boards creates an uneven appearance and does not reset the maintenance clock. Repair is worthwhile when the frame is sound and only surface boards need attention. A structural inspection ($200–$400) before deciding saves money by preventing unnecessary full replacement.

### Is composite decking worth the extra cost?

Composite decking costs 60–80% more upfront than pressure-treated lumber ($25–$45 vs $15–$25 per square foot installed). However, composite requires no staining, sealing, or board replacement for 25–50 years. Pressure-treated lumber needs $200–$400 in annual maintenance and full board replacement every 10–15 years. Over a 30-year period, the total cost of ownership for composite is typically 10–20% lower than pressure-treated when maintenance and replacement cycles are included.

### How long does deck replacement take?

A standard 192-square-foot deck replacement with pressure-treated or cedar boards takes 3–5 days — 1 day for demolition and 2–4 days for building. Composite and PVC take 4–7 days due to hidden fastener systems. Tropical hardwood takes 5–10 days because every fastener hole must be pre-drilled. Add 1–3 days for substructure repair or full replacement. Weather delays, permit inspections, and complex designs can extend the timeline further.
