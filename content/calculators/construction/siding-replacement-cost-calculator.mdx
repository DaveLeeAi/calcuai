## What This Estimate Shows

This calculator estimates the total cost to replace exterior siding on a home. It produces a low/mid/high cost range for your selected material, broken down into five line items: siding material, installation labor, old siding removal, housewrap, and trim/accessories. It also shows a comparison of all six siding materials at national rates.

**Siding cost** is split into material (~40%) and labor (~60%). The material portion covers the siding panels or planks themselves. The labor portion covers installation, cutting, fitting around windows and doors, and standard flashing. The story multiplier and regional multiplier both apply to the labor portion only — material prices are relatively consistent nationally.

**Old siding removal** ranges from $0 (installing over existing or new construction) to $1.50–$3 per square foot for standard removal, and $5–$15 per square foot for asbestos abatement. Homes built before 1980 may contain asbestos cement siding, which requires licensed abatement contractors and special disposal procedures.

**Housewrap** (Tyvek or equivalent weather-resistant barrier) costs $0.50–$1.50 per square foot and is strongly recommended when removing old siding — it protects against moisture infiltration and improves energy efficiency. Many building codes now require it for full siding replacement.

**Trim and accessories** are estimated at $0.75 per square foot of wall area. This covers corner trim, J-channel, soffit trim, and window/door casing. Complex trim work (multiple gables, bay windows, decorative elements) may cost more.

## The Formula

Siding replacement cost combines material, labor (adjusted for story height and region), removal, housewrap, and trim.

**Material cost:**

$$C_{mat} = A_{wall} \times R_{mat}$$

**Labor cost:**

$$C_{lab} = A_{wall} \times R_{lab} \times M_{story} \times M_{region}$$

**Removal, housewrap, and trim:**

$$C_{remove} = A_{wall} \times R_{remove}$$

$$C_{wrap} = A_{wall} \times R_{wrap}$$

$$C_{trim} = A_{wall} \times \$0.75$$

**Totals:**

$$C_{low} = C_{mat,low} + C_{lab,low} + C_{remove,low} + C_{wrap,low} + C_{trim}$$

$$C_{high} = C_{mat,high} + C_{lab,high} + C_{remove,high} + C_{wrap,high} + C_{trim}$$

$$C_{mid} = \frac{C_{low} + C_{high}}{2}$$

Where:

- **A_wall** = exterior wall area in square feet
- **R_mat** = material cost per sq ft (varies by siding type)
- **R_lab** = labor cost per sq ft (varies by siding type)
- **M_story** = story multiplier (1-story: 1.0, 2-story: 1.20, 3-story: 1.40)
- **M_region** = regional labor multiplier (0.85–1.25)
- **R_remove** = removal rate per sq ft (none: $0, standard: $1.50–$3, asbestos: $5–$15)
- **R_wrap** = housewrap rate per sq ft (none: $0, standard: $0.50–$1.50)

## Factors That Affect Siding Replacement Cost

**Siding material** drives the largest cost variation — the same 1,500-square-foot home ranges from $4,500 in vinyl to $45,000 in stone veneer, a 10x difference. Choose material based on budget, desired lifespan, maintenance tolerance, and architectural style. Fiber cement is the fastest-growing segment because it offers fire resistance, durability, and realistic wood-grain textures at a moderate price.

**Number of stories** increases labor costs due to scaffolding rental ($500–$1,500 per project) and reduced worker productivity at height. Two-story homes pay 20% more in labor; three-story homes pay 40% more. The story premium applies to installation labor only, not material, removal, or housewrap.

**Old siding removal** adds $1.50–$3 per square foot for standard tear-off and disposal. Asbestos cement siding — common in homes built 1920–1980 — requires licensed abatement at $5–$15 per square foot due to hazardous material handling, air monitoring, and special disposal. Always test before removing siding on pre-1980 homes.

**Regional labor rates** vary significantly. Installation labor in San Francisco or New York runs 25% above the national average, while labor in the rural South runs 15% below. This 40% spread means the same vinyl siding job could cost $4,500 in Alabama or $7,000 in Connecticut.

**Insulation upgrades** are often added during siding replacement, since the walls are already exposed. Adding rigid foam insulation under new siding costs $1–$2 per square foot and can reduce heating/cooling costs by 10–20%. This calculator does not include insulation — budget separately if planned.

**Architectural complexity** affects labor time. Homes with multiple gables, dormers, bay windows, and decorative trim require more cuts, fitting, and flashing work. Expect 10–30% higher labor costs for complex facades compared to simple rectangular homes.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much does it cost to reside a house?

Residing a 1,500-square-foot home costs $4,500–$45,000 depending on material — from $4,500–$10,500 for vinyl to $18,000–$45,000 for stone veneer. These are installed prices at national average rates including material, labor, and trim. Add $2,250–$4,500 for old siding removal and $750–$2,250 for new housewrap. Two-story homes cost 20% more in labor than single-story. Regional pricing varies 15–25% from the national average.

### What is the most cost-effective siding?

Vinyl siding is the most cost-effective option at $3–$7 per square foot installed, with a 20–40 year lifespan and virtually zero maintenance. For cost-per-year of service, vinyl averages $0.13–$0.23/sq ft/year. Fiber cement at $6–$13 installed lasts 30–50 years and averages $0.16–$0.33/sq ft/year — slightly more per year but with superior durability and fire resistance. Aluminum at $3–$6 installed is comparable to vinyl upfront and lasts 40–50 years but may dent and requires repainting.

### How long does siding replacement take?

Vinyl and aluminum siding on a standard 1,500-square-foot home takes 1–2 weeks. Fiber cement and wood take 2–3 weeks due to heavier material handling and more precise cutting. Stone veneer takes 3–6 weeks because of mortar set time and skilled masonry requirements. These timelines assume a crew of 2–4 workers and good weather. Add 2–3 days for old siding removal and 1 day for housewrap installation.

### Should I add insulation when replacing siding?

Adding rigid foam insulation behind new siding is one of the most cost-effective energy upgrades available, since the walls are already exposed during siding replacement. Rigid foam costs $1–$2 per square foot and reduces heating and cooling costs by 10–20%. The payback period is typically 3–7 years depending on climate and energy prices. If your home was built before 1980 and has minimal wall insulation, adding foam during siding replacement is strongly recommended.
