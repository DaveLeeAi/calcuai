## What Your Renovation Estimate Shows

The calculator produces a line-item cost breakdown across six budget categories, giving you a realistic total before you solicit contractor bids.

**Base renovation cost** is the largest component — your home's square footage multiplied by a per-square-foot rate determined by the quality level you select. Basic renovations ($50–$75/sq ft) cover cosmetic updates: fresh paint, new flooring, updated fixtures, and minor repairs. Mid-range renovations ($75–$150/sq ft) include cabinet replacement, new countertops, updated tile, and upgraded electrical and plumbing fixtures. High-end renovations ($150–$300/sq ft) add custom cabinetry, stone countertops, hardwood flooring throughout, and premium fixtures. Luxury renovations ($300–$500/sq ft) involve architect-designed spaces, imported materials, smart home integration, and top-tier craftsmanship.

**Kitchen renovation** is priced as a separate premium because kitchens cost significantly more per square foot than general living areas. At mid-range quality, a kitchen renovation adds approximately $35,000 to the project — covering cabinets, countertops, appliances, backsplash, and plumbing. At luxury quality, kitchen premiums can reach $80,000 for professional-grade appliances, custom cabinetry, and stone surfaces.

**Bathroom renovations** are priced per bathroom. Each mid-range bathroom adds approximately $20,000 for new tile, vanity, fixtures, shower/tub, and plumbing updates. Luxury bathroom renovations can add $50,000 per bathroom for heated floors, frameless glass showers, freestanding tubs, and designer tile.

**Structural changes** add a 20% surcharge on the base renovation cost. This covers engineering assessments, load-bearing wall removal, beam installation, foundation modifications, and the additional permits and inspections structural work requires.

**Permits** are estimated as a percentage of the project subtotal — typically 1–2% of the total project cost. Your municipality may charge flat fees or scale fees based on project value. This calculator uses a percentage-based estimate that you can adjust between 0% and 5%.

**Contingency buffer** is the insurance policy for your budget. Industry professionals universally recommend 10–20% for renovation projects because hidden issues — water damage, outdated wiring, asbestos, structural rot — only reveal themselves once demolition begins. The default 15% is the most commonly recommended buffer for whole-home projects.

## The Formula

Whole-home renovation cost is the sum of base renovation, room-specific premiums, structural surcharges, permits, and contingency:

**Base cost:**

$$\text{Base Cost} = \text{Square Footage} \times \text{Cost per Sq Ft}$$

Where cost per sq ft depends on quality level: basic = $62.50, mid-range = $112.50, high-end = $225.00, luxury = $400.00 (midpoints of each range).

**Room premiums:**

$$\text{Kitchen Cost} = \text{Kitchen Premium}(\text{quality level})$$
$$\text{Bathroom Cost} = \text{Bathroom Premium}(\text{quality level}) \times \text{Number of Bathrooms}$$

**Structural surcharge (if applicable):**

$$\text{Structural Cost} = \text{Base Cost} \times 0.20$$

**Subtotal, permits, contingency:**

$$\text{Subtotal} = \text{Base} + \text{Kitchen} + \text{Bathrooms} + \text{Structural}$$
$$\text{Permits} = \text{Subtotal} \times \frac{\text{Permit \%}}{100}$$
$$\text{Contingency} = \text{Subtotal} \times \frac{\text{Contingency \%}}{100}$$

**Total:**

$$\text{Total} = \text{Subtotal} + \text{Permits} + \text{Contingency}$$

**Source:** HomeAdvisor 2024 True Cost Guide; Remodeling Magazine 2024 Cost vs. Value Report; National Association of Home Builders (NAHB) Remodeling Market Index.

## Worked Example

### 2,000 Sq Ft Mid-Range Renovation with Kitchen and 2 Bathrooms

A homeowner is renovating a 2,000-square-foot home to mid-range quality, including a kitchen renovation and two bathroom renovations, with no structural changes, a 15% contingency, and 1.5% for permits.

**Step-by-step:**

1. **Base cost:** 2,000 sq ft x $112.50/sq ft = **$225,000.00**
2. **Kitchen premium:** Mid-range kitchen = **$35,000.00**
3. **Bathroom premium:** 2 bathrooms x $20,000 = **$40,000.00**
4. **Structural cost:** No structural changes = **$0.00**
5. **Subtotal:** $225,000 + $35,000 + $40,000 + $0 = **$300,000.00**
6. **Permits (1.5%):** $300,000 x 0.015 = **$4,500.00**
7. **Contingency (15%):** $300,000 x 0.15 = **$45,000.00**
8. **Total:** $300,000 + $4,500 + $45,000 = **$349,500.00**
9. **Effective cost per sq ft:** $349,500 / 2,000 = **$174.75/sq ft**

**Interpretation:** At $174.75 per square foot all-in, this mid-range renovation falls within the national average of $100–$200 per finished square foot reported by HomeAdvisor for comprehensive remodels. The kitchen and two bathrooms add $75,000 (25% of the subtotal), confirming the industry rule that kitchens and bathrooms drive the highest per-room costs. The $45,000 contingency provides a comfortable buffer for a home of this size — enough to cover one major surprise (e.g., full rewiring at $8,000–$15,000) plus several minor ones.

## Key Factors That Affect Renovation Cost

**Quality level is the dominant cost driver.** The difference between a basic and luxury renovation on a 2,000 sq ft home is $675,000 in base cost alone ($125,000 basic vs. $800,000 luxury). Most homeowners choose mid-range for primary residences and high-end for homes in neighborhoods where comparable sales demand premium finishes.

**Kitchen scope has the highest per-room impact.** A mid-range kitchen renovation adds $35,000 — more than any single bathroom. For homeowners on a tight budget, a kitchen refresh (paint cabinets, replace countertops and hardware, keep layout) can cost $10,000–$15,000 versus $35,000–$80,000 for a full gut renovation. Use the kitchen remodel calculator for a detailed line-item estimate.

**Bathroom count multiplies quickly.** Two bathrooms at mid-range add $40,000; at high-end quality, two bathrooms add $70,000. Master bathroom renovations typically cost 30–50% more than secondary bathrooms due to larger footprints and premium fixtures (double vanities, walk-in showers, soaking tubs).

**Structural changes add 20% to the base cost** and often trigger additional permit requirements, engineering fees ($2,000–$5,000 for load-bearing analysis), and longer timelines. Wall removal projects typically add 2–4 weeks to the construction schedule.

**Regional labor rates vary 30–50%.** A $300,000 renovation in Des Moines may cost $400,000–$450,000 in San Francisco or New York. This calculator uses national midpoints — adjust 15–25% up or down based on your metro area's cost of living index.

**Permit costs vary widely by municipality.** Some cities charge flat fees ($500–$2,000). Others calculate fees as a percentage of project value (1–3%). A few require separate permits for electrical, plumbing, and structural work — each with its own fee. Call your local building department for exact figures before finalizing your budget.

- **Costs use national midpoint pricing.** Your actual costs will vary based on geographic region, contractor rates, material availability, and local labor market conditions.
- **Quality level applies uniformly** across the entire project. In practice, homeowners often mix quality levels — basic in bedrooms, mid-range in living areas, high-end in kitchens and bathrooms. Use individual room calculators for a more granular estimate.

- **The estimate does not include** furniture, appliance costs beyond kitchen packages, landscaping, exterior work, design fees, temporary housing during construction, or financing costs.
- **Contingency is calculated on the subtotal** (base + rooms + structural) before permits, which aligns with industry budgeting practice.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much does it cost to renovate a 2,000 sq ft house?

A whole-home renovation of a 2,000 sq ft house costs $125,000–$800,000 depending on quality level. A basic cosmetic renovation runs $100,000–$150,000. A mid-range renovation with new finishes throughout, kitchen, and two bathrooms costs $300,000–$350,000 including contingency. A high-end renovation reaches $450,000–$650,000, and a luxury renovation can exceed $800,000. These ranges reflect national averages from the HomeAdvisor 2024 True Cost Guide.

### How much should I budget for renovation contingency?

Budget 10–20% of your total renovation cost for contingency. The industry standard recommendation is 15% for most whole-home projects. Older homes (built before 1970) should budget 20% because they are more likely to have hidden issues — knob-and-tube wiring, lead pipes, asbestos insulation, structural rot, or non-code-compliant framing. Newer homes in good condition can budget 10%. The National Association of Home Builders recommends never going below 10% regardless of the home's age or condition.

### Is it cheaper to renovate or build new?

Renovation is typically 20–40% cheaper than building new on the same lot, according to NAHB data. A mid-range whole-home renovation costs $75–$150 per sq ft, while new construction costs $150–$300 per sq ft in most markets. However, renovation costs can exceed new construction when the existing structure requires extensive foundation repair, full rewiring, re-plumbing, and structural reinforcement — at which point a teardown and rebuild may offer better value.

### How long does a whole-home renovation take?

A whole-home renovation takes 4–12 months depending on scope. A cosmetic refresh (paint, flooring, fixtures) takes 2–4 months. A mid-range renovation with kitchen and bathrooms takes 4–8 months. A high-end or luxury renovation with structural changes, custom materials, and detailed finishes takes 8–12 months. Add 2–6 weeks for permit approval depending on your municipality. Most general contractors recommend planning for 50% longer than the initial estimate to account for material delays and change orders.

### What renovations add the most home value?

Kitchen and bathroom renovations deliver the highest return on investment. A mid-range kitchen remodel recoups 75–80% of its cost at resale, and a mid-range bathroom remodel recoups 60–70%, according to the 2024 Remodeling Magazine Cost vs. Value Report. Other high-ROI projects include garage door replacement (94% ROI), manufactured stone veneer (89% ROI), and minor kitchen facelifts (96% ROI). Luxury renovations typically have the lowest ROI (50–60%) because they exceed what most buyers in the neighborhood are willing to pay.

### Do I need permits for a whole-home renovation?

Yes, most whole-home renovations require permits. Any work involving structural changes, electrical rewiring, plumbing relocation, HVAC modifications, or changes to the building envelope (windows, doors, roofline) requires a building permit in nearly all US jurisdictions. Cosmetic work — painting, flooring, cabinet refacing, fixture swaps — generally does not require a permit. Permit fees typically run 1–3% of the total project cost. Working without required permits can result in fines, forced demolition of non-compliant work, and complications when selling the home.

### Should I live in the house during renovation?

Living in the home is feasible during cosmetic renovations but not recommended during gut renovations. If the project involves demolishing kitchens, bathrooms, or HVAC systems, plan to move out. Temporary housing costs range from $1,500–$4,000 per month depending on your area. Living in during construction extends timelines by 15–25% because contractors must work around occupants, stage materials differently, and maintain livable conditions. Factor housing costs into your total budget if you plan to move out.

### What is the most expensive part of a home renovation?

The base renovation (flooring, walls, finishes, fixtures) is the largest overall cost at 60–70% of the total budget. Within specific rooms, kitchens are the most expensive — averaging $35,000–$80,000 for a mid-to-high-end renovation. Bathrooms are second at $20,000–$50,000 each. Structural work, when required, adds the fastest incremental cost — a 20% surcharge on a $225,000 base adds $45,000 for what may be a single wall removal. Labor accounts for 35–40% of material costs across all renovation categories.
