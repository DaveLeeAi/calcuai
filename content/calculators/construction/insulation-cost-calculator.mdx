## What This Estimates

This calculator provides installed cost ranges for six insulation types — fiberglass batt, blown-in cellulose, open-cell spray foam, closed-cell spray foam, rigid board, and mineral wool — based on your area, R-value target, application location, and region. It produces a low/mid/high cost estimate, a cost-per-square-foot figure, and a comparison table showing all six types for your area at baseline settings.

The **material cost** multiplies your square footage by the material's cost-per-square-foot rate, then adjusts for R-value target. Higher R-values require thicker insulation, increasing material use. R-13 uses 80% of the baseline material amount, while R-49 uses 130%. R-30 is the baseline (1.0x) and the standard DOE recommendation for most attics.

The **labor cost** multiplies your square footage by the insulation type's labor rate, then adjusts for application area difficulty and regional labor rates. Attic floor installation is the baseline (1.0x). Walls add 15% for cutting and fitting between studs. Crawl spaces add 10% for confined-space work. Basements add 20% for moisture management and wall preparation.

The **removal cost** applies when existing insulation must be removed before new installation. At $1.00–$2.50 per square foot, removing 1,000 sq ft of old insulation adds $1,000–$2,500 to the project. Removal is necessary when existing insulation is contaminated, damaged, or incompatible with the new material.

## The Formula

Insulation cost is estimated using area times material and labor rates, with multipliers for R-value target, application area difficulty, and regional labor differences.

**Material cost:**

$$C_{mat} = A \times R_{mat} \times M_{rv}$$

**Labor cost:**

$$C_{lab} = A \times R_{lab} \times M_{app} \times M_{reg}$$

**Removal cost (if applicable):**

$$C_{rem} = A \times R_{rem}$$

**Total cost range:**

$$C_{low} = C_{mat,low} + C_{lab,low} + C_{rem,low}$$

$$C_{high} = C_{mat,high} + C_{lab,high} + C_{rem,high}$$

$$C_{mid} = \frac{C_{low} + C_{high}}{2}$$

Where:

- **A** = area in square feet
- **R_mat** = material cost per square foot (low/high range by insulation type)
- **R_lab** = labor cost per square foot (low/high range by insulation type)
- **M_rv** = R-value target multiplier (R-13: 0.80, R-19: 0.90, R-30: 1.00, R-38: 1.15, R-49: 1.30)
- **M_app** = application area multiplier (attic: 1.0, crawl space: 1.10, walls: 1.15, basement: 1.20)
- **M_reg** = regional labor multiplier (0.85–1.25)
- **R_rem** = removal rate ($1.00–$2.50/sq ft, or $0 if no removal)

## Factors That Affect Insulation Cost

**Insulation type** is the biggest cost driver. The same 1,000-square-foot attic ranges from $2,250 with fiberglass batt to $12,000 with closed-cell spray foam — a 5.3x difference. Fiberglass batt and blown-in cellulose are the most common residential choices, covering 75% of installations. Spray foam costs significantly more but provides superior air sealing that can reduce heating and cooling costs by 15–25% according to the U.S. DOE.

**R-value target** directly affects material quantity and cost. Moving from R-30 (standard attic) to R-49 (cold climate attic) increases material cost by 30%. The DOE recommends R-30 to R-60 for attics in most of the continental U.S. (Zones 3–7), with R-13 to R-23 for wall cavities. Under-insulating saves upfront cost but increases energy bills for the life of the home.

**Application area** affects labor difficulty and cost. Attic floor insulation is the simplest installation at 1.0x labor. Wall cavity insulation requires cutting batts to fit between studs or drilling injection holes for blown-in material — adding 15% to labor. Crawl space work in tight, low-clearance conditions adds 10%. Basement insulation requires moisture management and often rigid board or closed-cell foam — adding 20% to labor.

**Old insulation removal** adds $1.00–$2.50 per square foot when existing insulation is damaged, contaminated with mold or rodent droppings, or contains vermiculite (potential asbestos). For attics where existing insulation is clean and undamaged, you can blow new material on top to increase total R-value without removal.

**Regional labor costs** shift the total by 15–25%. The labor component of a $3,625 attic project shifts from roughly $637 in the South (0.85x) to $937 on the West Coast (1.25x). Material costs are the same nationwide — only labor changes by region.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- All costs are **installed prices** including professional labor and materials. This calculator does not estimate DIY-only material costs — use the [Insulation Calculator](/calculators/construction/insulation-calculator) for material quantity estimates.
- Cost ranges reflect **2025–2026 U.S. national averages** from HomeAdvisor and Angi. Your local market may vary 15–25% depending on contractor availability and material supply.
- The calculator assumes a **standard installation** on accessible surfaces in reasonable condition. Structural repairs, electrical relocation, air sealing beyond spray foam, or separate vapor barrier installation are not included.
- **R-value multipliers** approximate the cost difference of achieving higher R-values by using thicker material. Actual thickness depends on the specific product's R-value per inch.
- **Removal cost** covers standard insulation removal (fiberglass, cellulose). Hazardous material removal (asbestos-containing vermiculite) requires licensed abatement contractors and costs significantly more ($15–$25/sq ft).
- Application area multipliers reflect **labor difficulty** only. Material costs are not affected by where the insulation is installed.
- This calculator does not account for **air sealing** (caulking, weatherstripping, foam around penetrations) which the DOE recommends completing before adding insulation. Air sealing typically costs $1,000–$3,000 as a separate project.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much does insulation cost per square foot?

Installed insulation costs $2.25–$12.00 per square foot depending on the type. Fiberglass batt is the cheapest at $2.25–$5.00/sq ft (material + labor). Blown-in cellulose runs $2.50–$5.00/sq ft. Open-cell spray foam costs $4.00–$8.50/sq ft. Mineral wool costs $4.50–$8.00/sq ft. Closed-cell spray foam is the most expensive at $6.00–$12.00/sq ft. All prices include professional installation at national average labor rates with R-30 target.

### What is the best type of insulation?

The best insulation depends on your application. Blown-in cellulose is best for attic retrofits — it fills gaps around joists and wiring better than batts and costs $2.50–$5.00/sq ft. Closed-cell spray foam is best for basements, crawl spaces, and moisture-prone areas — it provides the highest R-value per inch (R-5.5 to R-7.0) and acts as a vapor barrier, but costs $6.00–$12.00/sq ft. Fiberglass batt is best for budget projects and new construction walls where studs provide clean cavities at $2.25–$5.00/sq ft.

### What R-value do I need?

The U.S. DOE recommends R-30 to R-60 for attics, R-13 to R-23 for exterior walls, and R-25 to R-30 for floors over unheated spaces, depending on your climate zone. DOE Zone 1 (southern Florida, Hawaii) needs R-30 attic minimum. Zones 4–5 (mid-Atlantic, Midwest) need R-38 to R-49. Zones 6–7 (northern states, Alaska) need R-49 to R-60. Under-insulating saves 20–30% upfront but can cost $200–$500 more per year in heating and cooling.

### Should I remove old insulation before adding new?

Remove old insulation only if it is damaged, contaminated (mold, rodent droppings, water damage), or contains vermiculite (potential asbestos risk). In most cases, you can blow new cellulose or lay new batts over existing insulation to increase total R-value — adding R-19 over existing R-19 gives approximately R-38. Removal is necessary before spray foam application and costs $1.00–$2.50 per square foot ($1,000–$2,500 for a 1,000 sq ft attic).

### Is spray foam insulation worth the extra cost?

Spray foam costs 2–3x more than fiberglass batt but provides superior air sealing, higher R-value per inch, and can reduce heating/cooling costs by 15–25% according to the U.S. DOE. Closed-cell spray foam also acts as a vapor barrier and adds structural rigidity. The payback period is typically 5–8 years through energy savings, making it a strong investment for homeowners who plan to stay long-term or live in extreme climate zones. Open-cell spray foam at $4.00–$8.50/sq ft offers good air sealing at a lower price point but lacks the vapor barrier properties.

### How long does insulation installation take?

Professional insulation installation takes 1–3 days for a typical 1,000-square-foot attic. Blown-in cellulose is the fastest at about 1 day with a blowing machine. Fiberglass batt takes 1–2 days. Open-cell spray foam takes 1–2 days plus 24 hours cure time. Closed-cell spray foam takes 1–3 days plus 24 hours cure time. Rigid board takes 2–3 days due to cutting and fitting. Add half a day if old insulation removal is required.
