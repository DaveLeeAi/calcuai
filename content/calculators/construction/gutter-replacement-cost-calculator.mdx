## What This Estimates

This calculator provides installed cost ranges for five gutter materials — aluminum, vinyl, steel, copper, and zinc — priced per linear foot. It produces a low/mid/high estimate for your selected material, a cost-per-linear-foot figure, and a comparison table showing what the same gutter run would cost in every material.

The **base cost** splits into material (~40%) and labor (~60%). Material cost covers the gutter channel, fittings, and hardware. Labor cost covers measurement, fabrication (for seamless gutters), installation, and fastening. These rates reflect 2025–2026 national averages from HomeAdvisor and Angi.

The **style multiplier** adjusts for gutter profile. K-style gutters (the flat-front residential standard) are the baseline at 1.0x. Half-round gutters — the traditional curved profile seen on historic and upscale homes — cost 25% more for both material and labor because they require specialized hangers and more precise installation.

The **story adjustment** adds a per-linear-foot surcharge for taller homes. Working on 2-story homes adds $1.50 per linear foot for ladder work and safety equipment. Three-story homes add $4.00 per linear foot due to lift equipment requirements and increased risk premiums.

**Regional multipliers** adjust labor costs (not material) for local market conditions. West Coast and Northeast labor runs 20–25% above national average, while the South and Midwest run 10–15% below.

6. **Select old gutter removal** — choose "yes" if replacing existing gutters. Removal includes pulling down the old system, disposing of it, and inspecting the fascia board for rot or damage.

7. **Select home height** — taller homes cost more due to ladder work, safety equipment, and slower installation speed.

8. **Choose your region** — adjusts labor rates for local market conditions. Material costs are relatively consistent nationwide; labor varies significantly by region.

## The Formula

Gutter replacement cost is estimated per linear foot with separate material and labor components. The regional multiplier applies to labor only.

**Material cost:**

$$C_{mat} = L \times R_{mat} \times S$$

**Labor cost:**

$$C_{lab} = L \times (R_{lab} \times S + A_{story}) \times M_{region}$$

**Gutter cost:**

$$C_{gutter} = C_{mat} + C_{lab}$$

**Total cost:**

$$C_{total} = C_{gutter} + C_{downspout} + C_{guard} + C_{removal}$$

Where:

- **L** = gutter length in linear feet
- **R_mat** = material cost per linear foot
- **R_lab** = labor cost per linear foot
- **S** = style multiplier (K-style: 1.0, half-round: 1.25)
- **A_story** = story adjustment per linear foot (1-story: $0, 2-story: $1.50, 3-story: $4.00)
- **M_region** = regional labor multiplier (0.85–1.25)
- **C_downspout** = downspout count × $15–$25 per unit
- **C_guard** = gutter length × guard rate per linear foot
- **C_removal** = gutter length × $1.50–$3.00/lin ft (if removing old gutters)

A homeowner is replacing gutters on a single-story home — 150 linear feet of aluminum K-style with 4 downspouts, basic screen gutter guards, and old gutter removal.

**Step-by-step:**

3. Labor cost (low): 150 × ($3.60 × 1.0 + $0) × 1.0 = **$540**
4. Labor cost (high): 150 × ($7.20 × 1.0 + $0) × 1.0 = **$1,080**
5. Gutter cost: low $900, high $1,800
6. Downspout cost: 4 × $15 = $60 (low), 4 × $25 = $100 (high)
7. Guard cost: 150 × $4 = $600 (low), 150 × $6 = $900 (high)
8. Removal cost: 150 × $1.50 = $225 (low), 150 × $3.00 = $450 (high)
9. Total low: $900 + $60 + $600 + $225 = **$1,785**
10. Total high: $1,800 + $100 + $900 + $450 = **$3,250**
11. Total mid: ($1,785 + $3,250) / 2 = **$2,517.50**
12. Cost per linear foot: $2,517.50 / 150 = **$16.78/lin ft**

**Interpretation:** At $2,518 midpoint, this project includes $1,350 for the gutters themselves, $80 for downspouts, $750 for basic screen guards, and $338 for old gutter removal. ## K-Style vs. Half-Round Gutters

**K-style gutters** have a flat back and decorative front profile that mimics crown molding. They hold more water per inch of width than half-round, making them the practical choice for heavy rainfall areas. K-style is the standard for 90% of U.S. residential installations and costs less because installers work with it daily.

**Half-round gutters** have a smooth, curved interior that self-cleans more effectively — debris slides out rather than catching on flat surfaces. They are the traditional choice for historic homes, Craftsman-style architecture, and European-influenced designs. The 25% cost premium comes from specialized hanger systems and the need for precise slope alignment on the curved profile.

For most homeowners, K-style is the better value. Choose half-round if architectural style is a priority or if you want a gutter system that requires less frequent cleaning.

## Factors That Affect Gutter Cost

**Gutter material** creates the largest cost swing. The same 150-linear-foot system ranges from $450 in vinyl to $5,250 in copper — a 12x difference. Choose material based on climate, budget, desired lifespan, and architectural style.

**Home height** is a significant labor cost driver. Working at 2-story heights adds $1.50 per linear foot for additional ladder time. Three-story homes add $4.00 per linear foot because they typically require boom lifts or scaffolding.

**Gutter guards** can nearly double the project cost. Basic screen guards add $4–$6 per linear foot ($600–$900 for 150 feet). Premium micro-mesh systems add $6–$10 per linear foot ($900–$1,500). The payoff is reduced gutter cleaning — a professional cleaning runs $150–$300 per visit, so guards can pay for themselves in 3–5 years.

**Old gutter removal** adds $1.50–$3.00 per linear foot ($225–$450 for 150 feet). This includes pulling down the old system, checking fascia boards for rot, and disposal. If fascia repair is needed, expect an additional $6–$20 per linear foot for board replacement.

**Regional labor rates** adjust the labor portion of the estimate. A project that costs $1,800 in labor at national average would cost $2,250 on the West Coast (1.25x) or $1,530 in the South (0.85x).

**Seamless vs. sectional** — this calculator assumes seamless gutters, which are fabricated on-site from continuous rolls. Sectional gutters (pre-cut lengths joined with connectors) cost 10–15% less but are more prone to leaks at joints.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much do new gutters cost?

New gutters cost $3–$35 per linear foot installed, depending on material. For a typical 150-linear-foot home, aluminum gutters run $900–$1,800, vinyl runs $450–$1,050, steel runs $1,200–$2,250, zinc runs $2,250–$3,750, and copper runs $2,700–$5,250. Add $60–$100 for 4 downspouts and $225–$450 if removing old gutters. These are 2025–2026 national average prices.

### Are gutter guards worth it?

Gutter guards are worth the investment for most homeowners. Basic screen guards cost $4–$6 per linear foot ($600–$900 for 150 feet), while professional gutter cleaning costs $150–$300 per visit, 2–3 times per year. Guards typically pay for themselves in 3–5 years through eliminated cleaning costs. Micro-mesh guards ($6–$10/lin ft) offer the best protection against fine debris like pine needles and shingle grit. However, no guard is maintenance-free — annual inspection and occasional rinsing is still recommended.

### How often do gutters need to be replaced?

Aluminum gutters last 20–30 years, vinyl lasts 10–15 years, steel lasts 20–25 years, and copper or zinc can last 50–100 years. Signs that gutters need replacement include visible cracks or splits, sagging sections, peeling paint or rust, water pooling around the foundation, and separated joints. If damage is limited to one section, spot repair is cheaper than full replacement. If more than 30% of the system shows wear, full replacement is more cost-effective.

### What type of gutters last the longest?

Copper gutters last the longest at 50–100 years with virtually no maintenance. They develop a green patina over time that many homeowners consider an aesthetic feature. Zinc gutters are a close second at 50–80 years with similar durability and lower cost ($15–$25/lin ft vs $18–$35/lin ft). Both copper and zinc are self-healing — small scratches seal themselves through oxidation. For the best balance of lifespan and cost, aluminum at 20–30 years and $6–$12 per linear foot is the most popular choice.
