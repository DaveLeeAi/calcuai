## What This Estimate Shows

Mulch is sold two ways: by the bag at retail (typically 2-cubic-foot bags) and by the cubic yard in bulk from landscape suppliers. This calculator converts your bed area and desired depth into both units so you can compare pricing and choose the right purchasing method for your project size.

For beds under 5 cubic yards (roughly 500 square feet at 3-inch depth), bagged mulch from a home improvement store is convenient but costs 2–3 times more per cubic yard than bulk delivery. Above 5 cubic yards, bulk delivery at \$45–\$65 per cubic yard saves significant money and eliminates the labor of splitting open dozens of bags. A single cubic yard of mulch covers approximately 108 square feet at 3 inches deep — the depth recommended by most university extension programs for effective weed suppression and moisture retention.

## How to Use This Calculator

1. **Enter the bed length** — measure the longest dimension of your planting bed in feet. For irregularly shaped beds, estimate the average length or break the bed into rectangular sections and calculate each separately.

2. **Enter the bed width** — measure the shorter dimension in feet. For curved or kidney-shaped beds, use the average width across the bed.

3. **Set the mulch depth** — the recommended depth is 2–4 inches for most applications. Use 2 inches around shallow-rooted annuals, 3 inches for general landscape beds, and 4 inches for weed-heavy areas or play surfaces. Never exceed 4 inches, as deeper mulch suffocates plant roots and traps excess moisture.

4. **Select the bag size** — choose 2 cubic feet (standard retail bag) or 3 cubic feet (contractor bag). The calculator divides total volume by bag size and rounds up to the next whole bag.

5. **Read your results** — the calculator displays total cubic yards, total cubic feet, bag count, and cost estimates for both bagged and bulk purchasing options.

## The Formula

Mulch volume uses the same rectangular prism formula as any fill material, with depth converted from inches to feet:

$$V_{\text{cu ft}} = L \times W \times \frac{D}{12}$$

$$V_{\text{cu yd}} = \frac{V_{\text{cu ft}}}{27}$$

$$\text{Bags} = \left\lceil \frac{V_{\text{cu ft}}}{\text{Bag Size (cu ft)}} \right\rceil$$

Where:

- **L** = bed length (feet)
- **W** = bed width (feet)
- **D** = mulch depth (inches)
- **27** = cubic feet per cubic yard (the standard bulk ordering unit in the U.S.)
- **Bag Size** = volume per bag (typically 2 cu ft for retail or 3 cu ft for contractor bags)

Bags are rounded up to the next whole number — partial bags are not sold. For multiple beds, calculate each bed separately and sum the total cubic yards before ordering bulk.

**Source:** Volume formula is standard geometry. The 27 cubic feet per cubic yard conversion is the U.S. landscape industry standard per the **National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST)**. Mulch depth recommendations from **University of Florida IFAS Extension** and **Clemson Cooperative Extension** landscape management guidelines.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Flower Bed — 20 x 10 ft, 3-Inch Depth

A homeowner is refreshing the mulch in a front flower bed measuring 20 feet long by 10 feet wide, applying the standard 3-inch depth.

**Inputs:**
- Length: 20 ft
- Width: 10 ft
- Depth: 3 in
- Bag Size: 2 cu ft

**Step-by-step:**

1. Convert depth to feet: 3 ÷ 12 = **0.25 ft**
2. Calculate volume: 20 × 10 × 0.25 = **50 cu ft**
3. Convert to cubic yards: 50 ÷ 27 = **1.85 cu yd**
4. Calculate bags: 50 ÷ 2 = **25 bags** of 2 cu ft

**Cost estimate:**
- Bagged: 25 bags × $4.00 = **$100**
- Bulk: 1.85 cu yd × $50 = **$92.50** (but most suppliers have a 2 cu yd minimum delivery)

**Interpretation:** At just under 2 cubic yards, this project falls in the crossover zone between bagged and bulk. Bagged mulch is slightly more expensive but lets you transport it in a car trunk (25 bags at roughly 20 lbs each = 500 lbs total). Bulk delivery would require meeting the supplier's minimum order, meaning you would pay for 2 cubic yards regardless.

### Example 2: Large Garden — 30 x 20 ft, 4-Inch Depth

A homeowner is mulching a large backyard garden bed to suppress weeds in a new planting area. The bed measures 30 by 20 feet and requires a full 4-inch depth for aggressive weed control.

**Inputs:**
- Length: 30 ft
- Width: 20 ft
- Depth: 4 in
- Bag Size: 2 cu ft

**Step-by-step:**

1. Convert depth to feet: 4 ÷ 12 = **0.3333 ft**
2. Calculate volume: 30 × 20 × 0.3333 = **200 cu ft**
3. Convert to cubic yards: 200 ÷ 27 = **7.41 cu yd**
4. Calculate bags: 200 ÷ 2 = **100 bags** of 2 cu ft

**Cost estimate:**
- Bagged: 100 bags × $4.00 = **$400**
- Bulk: 7.41 cu yd × $50 = **$370.50** (plus \$50–\$75 delivery fee)

**Interpretation:** At 7.41 cubic yards, bulk delivery is the clear winner. One hundred bags of mulch weighs approximately 2,000 lbs and takes hours to open and spread individually. A bulk delivery dumps the material in one pile that you can wheelbarrow into place in a fraction of the time. At 4-inch depth, this heavy application will suppress most annual weeds for a full growing season, but keep mulch 3–4 inches away from plant stems and tree trunks to prevent crown rot.

## Key Factors That Affect Your Results

**Mulch depth is the dominant variable.** Increasing depth from 2 inches to 4 inches doubles the material volume for the same bed area. A 500-square-foot landscape requires 3.1 cubic yards at 2-inch depth versus 6.2 cubic yards at 4 inches. Most established beds only need 1–2 inches of annual top-up mulch, not a full 3–4 inch fresh application.

**Mulch type affects weight but not volume.** Hardwood bark mulch weighs approximately 400–600 lbs per cubic yard, while pine straw weighs only 150–250 lbs per cubic yard. Cedar mulch falls between at 300–500 lbs per cubic yard. Volume calculations remain the same regardless of material — but weight determines how many bags you can carry per trip and whether your vehicle can handle the load.

**Settling reduces effective depth by 15–25%.** Freshly applied mulch compacts within the first 2–4 weeks as it absorbs moisture and settles under its own weight. A 3-inch application may settle to 2.25 inches. If you need a consistent 3-inch final depth, consider applying 3.5–4 inches initially and letting it compact naturally.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- Assumes a **flat, rectangular bed** with uniform depth. For sloped beds, mulch migrates downhill and you may need 20–30% more material to maintain coverage on the high side.
- Bag weights are **approximate** and vary by mulch type and moisture content. A "2-cubic-foot" bag of wet hardwood mulch can weigh 40–50 lbs, while the same bag of dry pine bark may weigh 15–20 lbs.
- **Cost estimates** use typical 2026 U.S. retail pricing. Bagged mulch costs \$3–\$6 per 2 cu ft bag at home improvement stores. Bulk mulch costs \$30–\$65 per cubic yard at landscape supply yards, with delivery fees of \$50–\$100 depending on distance.
- Does not account for **displacement by existing plants**, stepping stones, edging, or other bed features. Subtract the footprint of large objects from the total bed area before calculating.
- Annual **mulch top-up** typically requires only 1–2 inches of fresh material over existing decomposed mulch, not the full initial depth.
