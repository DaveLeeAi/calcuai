## What This Estimate Shows

This calculator provides installed cost ranges for four painting project types — interior walls, interior ceilings, exterior siding, and exterior trim — based on your paintable area, paint quality, number of coats, surface preparation needs, ceiling height, and region. It produces a low/mid/high estimate, a cost-per-square-foot figure, a paint gallon estimate, a project timeline, and a comparison table showing costs across all four project types.

The **material cost** covers paint only, calculated at per-square-foot rates that reflect the quality tier you select. Builder-grade paint runs $0.50–$1.00 per square foot per coat. Mid-range brands (Behr, Valspar) cost $0.65–$1.25. Premium brands (Benjamin Moore, Sherwin-Williams) cost $0.85–$1.75. The coats multiplier adjusts material cost: one coat uses 0.75x the two-coat baseline, and three coats uses 1.20x.

The **labor cost** is the largest component — typically 50–60% of the total for interior work and 55–65% for exterior. Interior wall painting labor runs $1.50–$3.00 per square foot. Interior ceiling work adds a $1.00–$2.00 surcharge on top of the base rate due to overhead positioning and drop-cloth requirements. Exterior siding labor is $2.00–$4.00 per square foot due to ladder and scaffolding time. Exterior trim is the highest at $3.00–$6.00 per square foot because of detailed brush work around windows, doors, and fascia.

The **surface preparation cost** is often underestimated by homeowners. Minimal cleaning runs $0.25–$0.50 per square foot and is suitable for walls in good condition with no peeling. Standard sand-and-prime at $0.50–$1.00 per square foot is the most common prep for repaints. Extensive scrape-and-repair at $1.50–$3.00 per square foot applies to peeling exterior surfaces, water damage, or lead paint remediation.

The **paint gallons estimate** uses the industry standard coverage rate of 350 square feet per gallon per coat. This is separate from our paint calculator, which provides a more detailed gallon estimate with room dimensions, window/door deductions, and finish adjustments.

## How to Use This Calculator

1. **Enter the total paintable area** — for interior rooms, calculate wall area as perimeter (feet) times wall height, then subtract windows and doors. A standard 12×14 ft room with 8 ft ceilings has about 416 sq ft of wall area before deductions. For exterior siding, measure the total exterior surface area of the home. A typical 1,500 sq ft ranch-style home has 1,800–2,200 sq ft of exterior siding.

2. **Select the project type** — interior walls is the most common project. Interior ceiling painting adds a surcharge for overhead work. Exterior siding covers the main house surfaces. Exterior trim covers windows, doors, fascia, soffits, and decorative elements only.

3. **Choose paint quality** — builder-grade is the cheapest option, suitable for rental properties and utility spaces. Mid-range paints (Behr, Valspar) are the standard for most homeowners. Premium paints (Benjamin Moore Regal, Sherwin-Williams Duration) offer better coverage, durability, and color retention — often requiring fewer coats to achieve full coverage.

4. **Select the number of coats** — two coats is standard for most repaints. Use one coat for minor touch-ups or color refreshes with the same shade. Three coats is necessary when changing from a dark color to a light color or painting over stains and patches.

5. **Choose surface preparation level** — this is the most commonly underquoted part of a painting project. Minimal prep works only for clean walls in good condition. Standard sand-and-prime is needed for most repaints. Extensive scrape-and-repair is required for peeling exterior paint, water-damaged surfaces, or older homes with multiple paint layers.

6. **Select ceiling height** — standard 8 ft ceilings use the baseline labor rate. Taller ceilings (9–10 ft) add 15% to labor for extension poles and ladders. Extra tall ceilings (12+ ft) add 35% for scaffolding setup and safety equipment.

7. **Select your region** — this adjusts labor costs for regional pricing differences. The South and Midwest have below-average labor rates. The Northeast and West Coast are above average. Material costs are the same nationwide.

## The Formula

Painting cost is estimated using area times separate material, labor, and prep rates with multipliers for coats, ceiling height, and region.

**Material cost:**

$$C_{mat} = A \times R_{paint} \times M_{coats}$$

**Labor cost:**

$$C_{lab} = A \times R_{labor} \times M_{coats} \times M_{height} \times M_{region}$$

**Prep cost:**

$$C_{prep} = A \times R_{prep}$$

**Total cost range:**

$$C_{low} = C_{mat,low} + C_{lab,low} + C_{prep,low}$$

$$C_{high} = C_{mat,high} + C_{lab,high} + C_{prep,high}$$

$$C_{mid} = \frac{C_{low} + C_{high}}{2}$$

**Paint gallons needed:**

$$G = \frac{A \times N_{coats}}{350}$$

Where:

- **A** = paintable area in square feet
- **R_paint** = paint material rate per sq ft (varies by quality tier)
- **R_labor** = labor rate per sq ft (varies by project type)
- **R_prep** = surface preparation rate per sq ft
- **M_coats** = coats multiplier (0.75 / 1.0 / 1.20)
- **M_height** = ceiling height multiplier (1.0 / 1.15 / 1.35)
- **M_region** = regional labor multiplier (0.85–1.25)
- **N_coats** = actual number of coats (1, 2, or 3)
- **350** = square feet of coverage per gallon (industry standard)

## Factors That Affect Painting Cost

**Surface preparation** is the single biggest variable after project size. A home in good condition with clean, well-adhered paint needs only minimal prep at $0.25–$0.50 per square foot. A home with peeling exterior paint, water stains, or decades of built-up layers may need extensive scraping, sanding, patching, and priming at $1.50–$3.00 per square foot — adding $1,500–$3,750 to a 1,500 sq ft project. Professional painters report that prep work accounts for 20–40% of total project time.

**Interior vs exterior** creates a fundamental cost difference. Interior wall painting at $2.65–$4.25 per square foot (all-in) is more affordable because painters work in controlled conditions with easy wall access. Exterior siding at $3.15–$5.25 per square foot requires ladder or scaffold setup, weather scheduling, and more extensive surface prep. Exterior trim at $4.35–$7.75 per square foot is the highest-cost option because every window frame, door casing, and fascia board requires precise brush work.

**Paint quality** affects both material cost and long-term value. Builder-grade paint at $0.50–$1.00 per square foot provides basic coverage but typically needs repainting in 3–5 years. Mid-range paints at $0.65–$1.25 per square foot from Behr, Valspar, and Glidden offer 7–10 year durability. Premium paints from Benjamin Moore and Sherwin-Williams at $0.85–$1.75 per square foot offer 10–15 year durability, better coverage (often requiring fewer coats), and superior color accuracy.

**Ceiling height** directly impacts labor cost for interior projects. Standard 8 ft ceilings use the baseline rate. Tall 9–10 ft ceilings add 15% to labor for extension poles. Extra tall ceilings at 12+ ft add 35% for scaffolding setup, which can cost $200–$500 per room in rental fees alone.

**Number of coats** affects both paint consumption and labor time. Two coats is standard for most repaints. Three coats is necessary when changing from dark to light colors — a dark navy-to-white conversion may need a tinted primer plus two topcoats. One coat is only appropriate for minor touch-ups or same-color refreshes.

**Regional labor rates** shift the total by 15–25%. A $5,925 interior painting job in the national average becomes $5,250 in the South (0.85x labor) or $6,863 on the West Coast (1.25x labor). Material costs are the same nationwide — only labor varies by region.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- All costs are **fully installed prices** including labor, materials, prep, and cleanup. Costs do not include furniture moving, floor protection beyond standard drop cloths, or color consultation services.
- Cost ranges reflect **2025–2026 U.S. national averages** from HomeAdvisor, Angi, and the Painting Contractors Association. Your actual quote may be 15–25% higher or lower depending on local labor market conditions and painter experience.
- The calculator assumes **standard residential surfaces** in accessible locations. Cathedral ceilings, stairwells, multi-story exteriors, or surfaces requiring lead paint abatement will cost significantly more than these estimates.
- **Interior ceiling surcharge** ($1–$2/sq ft) is applied only to the interior-ceiling project type. The surcharge covers overhead positioning, additional drop-cloth setup, and slower application speed.
- **Paint gallon estimate** uses 350 sq ft per gallon per coat, which is the industry average for flat and eggshell finishes on smooth surfaces. Textured walls, bare drywall, or high-gloss finishes may reduce coverage to 250–300 sq ft per gallon. For a precise gallon count, use our [paint calculator](/calculators/construction/paint-calculator).
- This calculator does not account for **wallpaper removal**, **mold remediation**, **structural repair**, or **specialty finishes** (faux, metallic, Venetian plaster).

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much does it cost to paint a room?

Painting a standard 12×14 ft room (about 400–500 sq ft of wall area) costs $1,060–$2,100 with mid-range paint, standard prep, and 2 coats at national average rates. That breaks down to roughly $2.65–$4.25 per square foot installed. A smaller bedroom (10×12 ft, 350 sq ft of walls) runs $925–$1,840. A larger living room (16×20 ft, 575 sq ft of walls) costs $1,525–$3,020. These prices include labor, paint, prep, and cleanup but not ceiling painting or trim work.

### How much does it cost to paint the exterior of a house?

Painting the exterior of a typical 1,500 sq ft home (approximately 2,000 sq ft of siding) costs $6,300–$10,500 at national average rates with mid-range paint and standard prep. A 2,500 sq ft two-story home with 3,000 sq ft of siding runs $9,450–$15,750. Exterior painting costs $3.15–$5.25 per square foot all-in, which is 20–50% more than interior painting due to ladder work, weather constraints, and more intensive surface preparation.

### Is it cheaper to paint yourself or hire a painter?

DIY painting costs $0.50–$1.75 per square foot for materials only — roughly 60–75% less than hiring a professional at $2.65–$5.25 per square foot installed. For a 1,500 sq ft interior project, DIY costs $750–$2,625 in materials versus $3,975–$7,875 with a professional. However, DIY takes 3–5x longer (a professional crew of 2–3 painters completes a whole-house interior in 2–3 days, while a DIY homeowner may need 2–3 weekends), and professional results are more consistent, especially on ceilings and trim.

### How long does a professional painting project take?

A professional crew of 2–3 painters can complete a single room in 4–8 hours, a whole-house interior (1,500 sq ft) in 2–3 days, and a whole-house exterior in 3–7 days depending on size, prep needs, and weather. Extensive surface preparation (scraping, sanding, patching) can add 1–2 extra days. Exterior projects require dry weather with temperatures above 50 degrees F and below 90 degrees F.

### How much does surface prep cost?

Surface preparation costs $0.25–$3.00 per square foot depending on wall condition. Minimal cleaning (dusting, light wipe-down) runs $0.25–$0.50 per square foot and is appropriate for walls in good condition with no peeling. Standard sand-and-prime at $0.50–$1.00 per square foot is the most common prep level for repaints — it smooths minor imperfections and ensures paint adhesion. Extensive scrape-and-repair at $1.50–$3.00 per square foot covers peeling paint, water stains, drywall patching, and caulking around windows and doors. For a 1,500 sq ft interior project, prep adds $375–$4,500 to the total. Professional painters report that prep work accounts for 20–40% of total project time but is the single most important factor in how long a paint job lasts.

### How many coats of paint do I need?

Two coats is standard for most interior repaints and provides full, even coverage with proper color depth. One coat is sufficient only for minor touch-ups or same-color refreshes using high-quality paint with built-in primer. Three coats are necessary when painting light over dark (such as white over navy) or covering stains, patches, and heavily repaired surfaces. Each additional coat adds roughly 20% to material cost and 15–20% to labor time. Using a tinted primer as a first coat when making dramatic color changes can reduce the total coats needed from three to two.
