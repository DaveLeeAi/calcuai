## What This Estimate Shows

Topsoil is sold by the cubic yard for bulk delivery and by the bag for small projects. Ordering the right amount prevents two common mistakes: buying too little and paying a second delivery fee ($50–$150), or buying too much and ending up with a clay-heavy pile you cannot easily move. One cubic yard of topsoil weighs approximately 2,200 pounds — more than a ton — so even a modest garden project involves serious weight.

This calculator converts your project area and depth into cubic yards (the bulk ordering unit), tons (for suppliers who price by weight), and 40-pound bag counts (for retail purchases at home improvement stores). It also provides cost ranges for both bulk and bagged purchasing so you can compare the most economical option for your project size. The underlying volume formula is the same rectangular prism calculation used by the [Mulch Calculator](/construction/mulch-calculator) and [Gravel Calculator](/construction/gravel-calculator), with topsoil-specific density and cost factors applied.

## The Formula

Topsoil volume uses the standard rectangular prism formula with a density conversion for weight:

$$V_{\text{cu ft}} = L \times W \times \frac{D}{12}$$

$$V_{\text{cu yd}} = \frac{V_{\text{cu ft}}}{27}$$

$$\text{Weight (tons)} = V_{\text{cu yd}} \times 1.1$$

$$\text{Bags (40 lb)} = \left\lceil \frac{\text{tons} \times 2{,}000}{40} \right\rceil$$

Where:

- **L** = project area length (feet)
- **W** = project area width (feet)
- **D** = topsoil depth (inches)
- **12** = inches per foot (depth conversion)
- **27** = cubic feet per cubic yard
- **1.1** = topsoil density in tons per cubic yard (~2,200 lbs/cu yd)
- **40** = weight of one standard retail bag (pounds)

The 1.1 tons-per-cubic-yard density assumes screened topsoil at typical moisture content. Unscreened or wet topsoil can weigh 1.3–1.5 tons per cubic yard.

**Source:** Volume formula follows standard geometry per **NIST** measurement standards. Topsoil density of approximately 2,200 lbs/cu yd references the **USDA Natural Resources Conservation Service (NRCS)** soil bulk density data and the **Soil Science Society of America** reference tables. Cost ranges from **HomeAdvisor** and regional landscape supplier surveys (2025–2026).

## Worked Examples

### Example: Raised Garden Bed — 10 x 10 ft, 4-Inch Depth

A homeowner is adding topsoil to an existing garden bed that needs fresh soil for spring planting. The bed measures 10 feet by 10 feet and requires 4 inches of new topsoil.

**Inputs:**
- Length: 10 ft
- Width: 10 ft
- Depth: 4 in

**Step-by-step:**

1. Convert depth to feet: 4 / 12 = **0.3333 ft**
2. Calculate volume: 10 x 10 x 0.3333 = **33.33 cu ft**
3. Convert to cubic yards: 33.33 / 27 = **1.23 cu yd**
4. Calculate weight: 1.23 x 1.1 = **1.36 tons** (2,716 lbs)
5. Calculate bags: ceil(2,716 / 40) = **68 bags** (at 40 lbs each — but see note below)

**Cost comparison:**
- Bulk delivery: 1.23 cu yd x $20–$50/cu yd = **$25–$62** (plus $50–$100 delivery fee)
- Bagged: 68 bags x $5/bag = **$340**

**Interpretation:** At 1.23 cubic yards, this project is right at the threshold where bulk delivery becomes worthwhile despite the delivery fee. Bulk topsoil at $25–$62 plus a $75 delivery fee totals $100–$137 — less than half the $340 bagged cost. Most landscape suppliers have a 1-yard minimum for delivery, so this order qualifies. If you have a pickup truck, buying bulk directly from a landscape yard eliminates the delivery fee entirely.

## Bulk vs. Bagged: Which to Choose

| Factor | Bulk Delivery | Bagged (40 lb) |
|---|---|---|
| Best for | Projects over 1 cu yd | Small beds under 0.5 cu yd |
| Cost per cu yd | $20–$50 | $120–$200 equivalent |
| Delivery fee | $50–$150 (distance-based) | Free (self-transport) |
| Minimum order | Usually 1 cu yd | 1 bag |
| Quality | Varies — inspect before accepting | Consistent, screened |
| Convenience | Dumped in driveway; you distribute | Carry to exact location |
| Shelf life | Use immediately — exposed to elements | Bags store for months |

**Rule of thumb:** If you need more than 1 cubic yard, buy bulk. Below half a cubic yard, bags are more practical despite the premium. Between 0.5 and 1 cubic yard, check if your supplier offers a small-load delivery rate or whether you can haul it yourself in a truck bed (one cubic yard of topsoil fills a standard 8-foot truck bed and weighs about 2,200 lbs — check your truck's payload rating).

## Recommended Topsoil Depth by Project

| Project Type | Depth (inches) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Overseeding existing lawn | 1–2 | Thin top-dress; rake into existing grass |
| New lawn from seed | 4–6 | Minimum 4" for root establishment |
| New lawn from sod | 4–6 | Sod roots need 4–6" of quality soil |
| Flower beds | 6–8 | Deeper for perennials with deep roots |
| Vegetable gardens | 8–12 | Most vegetables need 8"+ of loose soil |
| Raised beds on ground | 8–12 | Fill depth minus expected settling (10–15%) |
| Raised beds on hard surface | 10–12 | Full depth — no native soil contribution |
| Tree and shrub planting | 12+ | Backfill hole with 50/50 topsoil and native soil |

Topsoil settles 10–15% after watering and natural compaction. Order 10–15% more than the calculated volume for new beds and raised beds to account for this settling.

## Frequently Asked Questions

<details>
<summary>How much does a cubic yard of topsoil weigh?</summary>

A cubic yard of screened topsoil weighs approximately 2,200 pounds (1.1 tons). Unscreened topsoil with clay content or higher moisture can weigh 2,600–3,000 pounds per cubic yard. Always check your vehicle's payload capacity before attempting to haul topsoil yourself — one cubic yard already exceeds the payload rating of most half-ton pickup trucks.

</details>

<details>
<summary>How many 40-pound bags are in a cubic yard of topsoil?</summary>

Approximately 55 bags of 40-pound topsoil equal one cubic yard by weight (2,200 lbs / 40 lbs = 55 bags). However, bagged topsoil is often sold by volume (typically 0.75–1.0 cubic feet per bag), and the actual weight per bag varies with moisture content. At $4–$6 per bag, buying 55 bags costs $220–$330 compared to $20–$50 for the same cubic yard in bulk.

</details>

<details>
<summary>What is the difference between topsoil, garden soil, and compost?</summary>

Topsoil is the top 4–12 inches of natural earth, screened to remove rocks and debris. Garden soil is topsoil blended with compost and other amendments for improved drainage and nutrients. Compost is fully decomposed organic matter used as a soil amendment, not a standalone growing medium. For filling beds and leveling lawns, use topsoil. For planting directly, garden soil or a topsoil-compost blend (60/40 ratio) performs better.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Should I buy screened or unscreened topsoil?</summary>

Screened topsoil has been passed through a mesh to remove rocks, roots, and clumps, producing a uniform texture ideal for lawns and garden beds. Unscreened topsoil is cheaper ($15–$25/cu yd vs. $25–$50/cu yd screened) but contains debris that makes it difficult to rake smooth. Use screened for lawns, gardens, and raised beds. Unscreened is acceptable for rough grading, filling holes, and areas that will be covered by mulch or gravel.

</details>

<details>
<summary>How much topsoil do I need for a new lawn?</summary>

A new lawn requires 4–6 inches of topsoil for healthy root establishment. For a 1,000-square-foot lawn at 4 inches deep, you need approximately 12.3 cubic yards (13.6 tons). At 6 inches deep, that increases to 18.5 cubic yards (20.4 tons). Spread topsoil evenly, grade for drainage away from foundations, and compact lightly before seeding or sodding.

</details>
