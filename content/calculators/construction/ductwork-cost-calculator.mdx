## What This Estimates

This calculator provides installed cost ranges for residential ductwork projects — new installations, full replacements, and partial modifications — adjusted for your choice of duct material, insulation level, access difficulty, and region. It produces a low/mid/high total estimate, a cost breakdown by component, cost per square foot, and a comparison table showing what each duct material costs for your home size.

The **material cost** represents 40% of the adjusted project total. It covers galvanized sheet metal, flexible duct, or fiberglass board duct — plus fittings, registers, boots, plenums, and takeoffs. Sheet metal is the 1.0x baseline. Flexible duct costs 30% less (0.70x) because it requires fewer fittings and less fabrication. Fiberglass board costs 15% more (1.15x) because the rigid panels must be precisely cut and sealed on site.

The **labor cost** represents 60% of the adjusted project total, further adjusted by access difficulty and regional multiplier. Accessible attics and basements are baseline (1.0x). Crawl spaces add 15% to labor because technicians work in confined positions. In-wall and ceiling installations add 35% because they require cutting drywall, routing ducts through framing cavities, and patching afterward.

The **insulation cost** is a flat adder. Standard R-6 wrap insulation is included in the base price. Upgrading to R-8 adds $500–$1,500 for the thicker wrap and additional labor. Opting for no insulation provides a $300–$500 credit, but uninsulated ducts in unconditioned spaces lose 20–30% of heating and cooling energy.

## The Formula

Ductwork cost is calculated from a home-size base price adjusted by material and project type multipliers, then split into material (40%) and labor (60%) components. Labor is further adjusted for access difficulty and regional pricing. An insulation adder is applied to the total.

**Adjusted base cost:**

$$C_{adjusted} = B_{home} \times M_{material} \times M_{project}$$

**Material cost (40%):**

$$C_{material} = C_{adjusted} \times 0.40$$

**Labor cost (60% with access and region adjustments):**

$$C_{labor} = C_{adjusted} \times 0.60 \times M_{access} \times M_{region}$$

**Total cost:**

$$C_{total} = C_{material} + C_{labor} + C_{insulation}$$

Where:

- **B_home** = home size base cost range (e.g., medium: $3,500–$6,000)
- **M_material** = duct material multiplier (sheet-metal: 1.0, flexible: 0.70, fiberglass-board: 1.15)
- **M_project** = project type multiplier (new: 1.0, replacement: 1.20, modification: 0.50)
- **M_access** = access difficulty multiplier on labor (attic: 1.0, crawlspace: 1.15, in-wall/ceiling: 1.35)
- **M_region** = regional labor multiplier (0.85–1.25)
- **C_insulation** = insulation adder (standard R-6: $0, upgraded R-8: +$500–$1,500, none: −$300–$500)

## Factors That Affect Ductwork Cost

**Home size** is the primary cost driver. A small home under 1,000 sq ft may need 6–8 supply runs and 2–3 return runs, while a home over 2,500 sq ft may need 15–20 supply runs with multiple trunk lines. Every additional run adds material, fittings, and labor hours.

**Duct material** creates a 0.70x to 1.15x swing on total project cost. Sheet metal is the industry standard — rigid galvanized steel ducts with sealed joints that last 25–30 years with minimal maintenance. Flexible duct (insulated plastic over a wire coil) costs 30% less and installs faster, but sags over time, is more prone to tears, and loses efficiency when runs are kinked or exceed 25 feet. Fiberglass board duct costs 15% more and includes built-in R-value insulation, making it the quietest option — ideal for home offices and media rooms.

**Project type** has a major impact. New installations in open framing (new construction or gut remodels) are the baseline. Replacements add 20% because old ducts must be disconnected, removed, and disposed of before new ducts can be installed. Modifications — adding a room or rerouting a section — cost roughly half the full-system price because only part of the system is affected.

**Access difficulty** affects the labor portion significantly. Accessible attics and unfinished basements are baseline. Crawl spaces add 15% to labor because of confined working conditions, limited tool access, and moisture protection requirements. In-wall and ceiling installations add 35% because they require cutting into finished surfaces, routing ducts through joist bays, and patching drywall afterward — effectively combining HVAC work with carpentry.

**Insulation level** is an often-overlooked cost factor. Standard R-6 is included in the base price and meets code in most jurisdictions. Upgrading to R-8 adds $500–$1,500 but reduces energy loss by an additional 10–15% for ducts running through unconditioned spaces. Skipping insulation entirely saves $300–$500 but wastes 20–30% of conditioned air in attic or crawl space runs — a false economy that increases utility bills by $200–$400 per year in most climates.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- All costs are **fully installed prices** including ductwork, fittings, registers, boots, plenums, hangers, sealant, and professional installation labor. Permits, HVAC equipment (furnace, AC unit), and thermostat are not included.
- Cost ranges reflect **2025–2026 U.S. national averages** from HomeAdvisor and Angi. Your local market may be 15–25% higher or lower depending on regional labor rates and material availability.
- The calculator assumes a **standard residential layout** with typical run lengths and complexity. Homes with cathedral ceilings, multiple stories, or complex layouts may exceed these estimates.
- **Material/labor split** (40/60) is an industry average. Actual split varies by material type — flexible duct is more labor-heavy in proportion because material costs are lower.
- Base costs assume a **complete duct system** sized per ACCA Manual D standards. Undersized or oversized systems require a Manual D load calculation ($200–$500 additional) for optimal performance.
- This calculator estimates **supply and return ductwork**. It does not include duct cleaning, duct sealing (Aeroseal), or duct testing (duct blaster) as separate line items.
- **Replacement costs** assume old ducts are accessible for removal. If old ducts are entombed in concrete or inaccessible walls, abandonment plus new routing may be more cost-effective than removal.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much does ductwork installation cost?

New ductwork installation costs $2,000–$14,000 depending on home size and duct material. A medium-sized home (1,000–1,500 sq ft) with sheet metal ducts costs $3,500–$6,000 installed with standard R-6 insulation. Flexible duct reduces that to $2,450–$4,200, while fiberglass board increases it to $4,025–$6,900. Replacing existing ductwork adds approximately 20% for demolition, bringing a medium home replacement to $4,200–$7,200 in sheet metal. All prices include materials, labor, registers, and fittings at national average rates.

### Is flexible duct or sheet metal better?

Sheet metal is better for longevity, airflow efficiency, and resale value. Galvanized sheet metal ducts last 25–30 years, maintain consistent airflow, and resist mold and pest damage. Flexible duct costs 30% less and installs faster, making it popular for budget-conscious projects and retrofit situations where rigid duct cannot be routed. However, flexible duct lasts only 10–15 years, loses efficiency when runs exceed 25 feet, and sags over time — reducing airflow by 10–20% if not properly supported every 4–5 feet. For new construction, most HVAC professionals recommend sheet metal for trunk lines with flexible duct limited to final 6-foot connections to registers.

### What are signs you need new ductwork?

The top signs you need new ductwork include: uneven temperatures between rooms (3–5 degree variance), visible duct damage (disconnected joints, crushed flexible duct, rust holes in sheet metal), excessive dust despite regular filter changes, unexplained increases in heating or cooling bills (20%+ year over year), musty odors when the system runs (indicating mold in ductwork), and ductwork over 15 years old for flexible or 25+ years for sheet metal. A professional duct inspection ($200–$400) with a duct blaster test can confirm whether repair or full replacement is the better investment.

### How long does ductwork installation take?

New ductwork installation takes 2–5 days for a complete system in a typical residential home. The timeline depends on home size, access, and duct material. A small home with accessible attic runs takes 2–3 days. A large home with mixed access points (attic, walls, crawl space) takes 4–5 days. Replacement projects take 2–4 days because demolition of old ducts adds roughly half a day to a full day. Partial modifications take 1–2 days. Sheet metal fabrication on site adds time compared to flexible duct, which unrolls and connects quickly. Most HVAC contractors can schedule installation within 1–2 weeks of the initial assessment.
