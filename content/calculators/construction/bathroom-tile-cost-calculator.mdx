## What This Estimate Shows

This calculator provides installed cost ranges for five common bathroom tile materials — ceramic, porcelain, natural stone, glass, and large-format — based on your project scope, area, demolition and waterproofing needs, waste factor, and region. It produces a low/mid/high estimate for your selected tile, a cost-per-square-foot figure, and a comparison table showing what the same project would cost in every material.

The **material cost** covers the tile itself, calculated on your area plus the waste factor (default 10%) to account for cuts, breakage, and pattern matching. Bathroom tile projects typically generate more waste than flat-wall installations due to fixtures, niches, and corners.

The **labor cost** covers professional tile installation — surface preparation, waterproofing, layout, cutting, setting, grouting, and cleanup. Bathroom tile labor is more expensive than standard wall tile because of moisture management, tight spaces, and the precision required around drains, fixtures, and shower benches. The calculator applies a regional multiplier to labor only.

The **waterproofing cost** applies a per-square-foot rate to your raw tile area. Standard liquid-applied waterproofing runs $2–$4 per square foot. Premium sheet membrane systems (Schluter Kerdi, Laticrete Hydro Ban) run $4–$7 per square foot but provide superior moisture protection for shower and tub areas.

The **supplies cost** covers grout, thin-set mortar, cement backer board, and spacers at a flat $3 per square foot. This is higher than backsplash supplies because bathroom installations require backer board and more robust setting materials for wet environments.

## The Formula

Bathroom tile cost is estimated using effective area (with waste) times material and labor rates, plus demolition, waterproofing, and supplies.

**Effective area (with waste):**

$$A_e = A \times \left(1 + \frac{W}{100}\right)$$

**Cost range:**

$$C_{low} = A_e \times R_{mat,low} + A_e \times R_{lab,low} \times M_r + D_{low} + WP_{low} + S$$

$$C_{high} = A_e \times R_{mat,high} + A_e \times R_{lab,high} \times M_r + D_{high} + WP_{high} + S$$

$$C_{mid} = \frac{C_{low} + C_{high}}{2}$$

Where:

- **A** = tile area in square feet
- **W** = waste factor percentage (5–25%)
- **R_mat** = material cost per square foot (low/high range)
- **R_lab** = labor cost per square foot (low/high range)
- **M_r** = regional labor multiplier (0.85–1.25)
- **D** = demolition cost (area × $4–$8 if applicable)
- **WP** = waterproofing cost (area × $2–$4 standard, $4–$7 premium)
- **S** = supplies cost (area × $3)

## Factors That Affect Bathroom Tile Cost

**Project scope** is the primary cost driver. A 15-square-foot shower floor costs 8–12% of a full 180-square-foot bathroom tiling project. However, smaller projects often have a higher cost per square foot because setup, cutting, and fixture work take the same amount of time regardless of area.

**Tile material** creates the largest per-square-foot cost swing. Ceramic at $10–$20 per square foot installed is the most common residential choice. Natural stone at $20–$40 per square foot is 2x the cost but offers unique veining and a luxury aesthetic. Porcelain at $12–$25 per square foot offers the best balance of durability and affordability for wet areas.

**Waterproofing** adds $2–$7 per square foot but is not optional in wet areas. Standard liquid-applied membranes (RedGard, AquaDefense) at $2–$4 per square foot are adequate for most residential showers. Premium sheet membranes (Schluter Kerdi, Laticrete Hydro Ban Board) at $4–$7 per square foot are preferred for steam showers, curbless showers, and high-end installations where failure is not acceptable.

**Demolition** of existing bathroom tile runs $4–$8 per square foot — higher than backsplash demo because bathroom tile is typically installed over cement board with mortar. Full demolition down to studs may be necessary if water damage is found behind the old tile.

**Regional labor rates** shift the total cost 15–25% from the national average. A $1,560 ceramic shower in the South (0.85x labor multiplier) costs roughly $1,480, while the same project on the West Coast (1.25x) costs roughly $1,692.

**Complexity and obstacles** increase labor time. Shower niches (recessed shelves) add $150–$350 each. Built-in shower benches add $200–$500. Decorative border strips and accent bands add $5–$15 per linear foot. Each plumbing fixture (showerhead, valve, drain) requires precise cutting.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much does it cost to tile a shower?

Tiling a standard 80-square-foot shower with ceramic tile costs $1,120–$2,000 installed at national average rates. This covers tile material, professional labor, grout, and backer board. Add $320–$640 for old tile demolition and $160–$320 for standard waterproofing. Porcelain runs $1,296–$2,440, and natural stone costs $2,000–$3,760 for the same area. Total budget for a ceramic shower retile with demo and waterproofing: $1,600–$2,960.

### Should I waterproof before tiling?

Yes — waterproofing is essential for all shower walls, shower floors, and tub surrounds. Without waterproofing, water penetrates grout lines and reaches the substrate, causing mold, rot, and structural damage over time. Standard liquid-applied waterproofing membranes (RedGard, AquaDefense) cost $2–$4 per square foot and are applied directly to cement board before tile installation. Premium sheet membranes (Schluter Kerdi) at $4–$7 per square foot provide superior protection. Dry bathroom floors away from the shower do not require waterproofing.

### How much does tile demolition cost?

Bathroom tile demolition costs $4–$8 per square foot, covering tile removal, adhesive scraping, and debris disposal. For an 80-square-foot shower, expect $320–$640 in demolition costs. The rate is higher than backsplash demolition because bathroom tile is typically set in mortar over cement board. If water damage is found behind the old tile, remediation and backer board replacement can add $500–$2,000 depending on the extent of damage.

### What tile is best for bathroom floors?

Porcelain tile is the best choice for bathroom floors at $12–$25 per square foot installed. It has a water absorption rate below 0.5% (making it virtually waterproof), excellent slip resistance in textured finishes, and requires minimal maintenance. Ceramic tile at $10–$20 per square foot is a good budget alternative but is more porous. Natural stone at $20–$40 per square foot is beautiful but requires sealing every 6–12 months. For shower floors specifically, small mosaic tiles (2×2 inch or smaller) are preferred because they conform to the sloped drain pitch.
