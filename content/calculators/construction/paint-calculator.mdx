## What This Estimate Shows

Paint is sold by the gallon, but walls are measured in square feet. This calculator bridges that gap by computing the total paintable wall area of your room, subtracting doors and windows, multiplying by the number of coats, and dividing by the coverage rate to give you the exact number of gallons to purchase.

The **door and window deduction** matters more than most people realize. A standard interior door opening is approximately 21 square feet (3 x 7 ft). A typical window is about 15 square feet (3 x 5 ft). In a bedroom with 2 doors and 2 windows, those openings total 72 square feet — roughly 17% of the wall area. Skipping the deduction means overbuying by nearly a full gallon on a standard room.

The **number of coats** is the other critical variable. Painting a similar color over a primed surface needs 1–2 coats. Going from dark to light (or vice versa) requires 2–3 coats, sometimes with a tinted primer coat first. Each additional coat doubles the paint needed for that pass. This calculator accounts for your coat count to prevent mid-project store runs. For calculating the total wall area before painting, see our [Square Footage Calculator](/calculators/construction/square-footage-calculator).

## How to Use This Calculator

1. **Enter room length** — measure the longest wall in feet. For L-shaped rooms, either calculate each section separately or measure the total perimeter manually and enter it in the perimeter override field if available.

2. **Enter room width** — measure the shorter wall in feet. For square rooms, length and width are the same.

3. **Enter ceiling height** — standard residential ceilings are 8 feet. Older homes may have 9 or 10-foot ceilings. Measure from the finished floor to the ceiling — do not include crown molding.

4. **Enter the number of doors** — count all doorways in the room, including closet doors. Each standard door opening (3 x 7 ft) deducts 21 sq ft from the paintable area. French doors or sliding glass doors are larger — count these as 2 doors each.

5. **Enter the number of windows** — count all windows. Each standard window (3 x 5 ft) deducts 15 sq ft. Large picture windows or bay windows should be counted as 2 windows each.

6. **Set the number of coats** — choose 1 for a touch-up coat over the same color, 2 for a standard repaint, or 3 for dramatic color changes. When in doubt, select 2 — it is the most common requirement.

7. **Select the coverage rate** — the default 350 sq ft per gallon applies to smooth drywall. Reduce to 300 for lightly textured walls, or 250 for heavy knockdown texture, bare drywall, or porous surfaces.

## The Formula

Paint quantity is calculated by finding the total wall area, subtracting non-paintable surfaces, multiplying by the number of coats, and dividing by the per-gallon coverage rate.

**Total wall area:**

$$A_{\text{walls}} = 2 \times (L + W) \times H$$

**Deductions for doors and windows:**

$$A_{\text{deductions}} = (D \times 21) + (W_n \times 15)$$

**Net paintable area:**

$$A_{\text{net}} = A_{\text{walls}} - A_{\text{deductions}}$$

**Gallons required:**

$$G = \frac{A_{\text{net}} \times C_{\text{coats}}}{R_{\text{coverage}}}$$

Where:

- **L** = room length (feet)
- **W** = room width (feet)
- **H** = ceiling height (feet)
- **D** = number of standard doors (each deducts 21 sq ft)
- **W_n** = number of standard windows (each deducts 15 sq ft)
- **C_coats** = number of coats (typically 1–3)
- **R_coverage** = coverage rate in sq ft per gallon (typically 300–400)
- **G** = gallons of paint needed (rounded up to the nearest quart or gallon)

Standard door area: 3 ft x 7 ft = 21 sq ft. Standard window area: 3 ft x 5 ft = 15 sq ft. These are industry-standard deduction values used by professional painters.

**Source:** Coverage rate of 350–400 sq ft per gallon per the Paint Quality Institute and manufacturer specifications (Benjamin Moore, Sherwin-Williams, Behr). Door and window deduction standards per the Painting and Decorating Contractors of America (PDCA) estimating guidelines.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Standard Bedroom — 14 x 12 ft, 8-ft Ceiling, 2 Doors, 2 Windows

A homeowner is repainting a bedroom from beige to a medium gray — a moderate color change requiring 2 coats over existing paint.

**Inputs:**
- Length: 14 ft
- Width: 12 ft
- Ceiling Height: 8 ft
- Doors: 2
- Windows: 2
- Coats: 2
- Coverage Rate: 350 sq ft/gal

**Step-by-step:**

1. Total wall area: 2 x (14 + 12) x 8 = 2 x 26 x 8 = **416 sq ft**
2. Door deductions: 2 x 21 = **42 sq ft**
3. Window deductions: 2 x 15 = **30 sq ft**
4. Net paintable area: 416 - 42 - 30 = **344 sq ft**
5. Total area with 2 coats: 344 x 2 = **688 sq ft of coverage needed**
6. Gallons required: 688 / 350 = **1.97 gallons**
7. Purchase: **2 gallons** (round up)
8. Cost estimate: 2 x \$32–$60 = **$64–\$120**

**Interpretation:** Two gallons covers this bedroom with almost no waste. Buy exactly 2 gallons of your chosen color. If you are making a dramatic color change (dark to white or white to dark), consider buying a quart of tinted primer as a first coat, then 2 gallons of finish paint for 2 topcoats.

### Example 2: Large Living Room — 20 x 15 ft, 10-ft Ceiling, 1 Door, 4 Windows

A homeowner is painting a living room with high ceilings. The room has one wide entryway (counted as 1 door) and four standard windows.

**Inputs:**
- Length: 20 ft
- Width: 15 ft
- Ceiling Height: 10 ft
- Doors: 1
- Windows: 4
- Coats: 2
- Coverage Rate: 350 sq ft/gal

**Step-by-step:**

1. Total wall area: 2 x (20 + 15) x 10 = 2 x 35 x 10 = **700 sq ft**
2. Door deductions: 1 x 21 = **21 sq ft**
3. Window deductions: 4 x 15 = **60 sq ft**
4. Net paintable area: 700 - 21 - 60 = **619 sq ft**
5. Total area with 2 coats: 619 x 2 = **1,238 sq ft of coverage needed**
6. Gallons required: 1,238 / 350 = **3.54 gallons**
7. Purchase: **4 gallons** (round up)
8. Cost estimate: 4 x \$32–$60 = **$128–\$240**

**Interpretation:** The 10-foot ceilings add 25% more wall area compared to standard 8-foot ceilings. Four gallons provides a small buffer for touch-ups and roller lap marks. For a room this size, use a roller with a 3/8-inch nap for smooth walls or 1/2-inch nap for light texture. Expect 4–6 hours of painting time for 2 coats with proper drying between coats (2–4 hours for latex paint).

## Key Factors That Affect Your Results

**Wall texture dramatically reduces coverage.** Smooth drywall allows 350–400 sq ft per gallon because paint sits on a flat surface. Knockdown or orange-peel texture has peaks and valleys that increase actual surface area by 15–30%, dropping effective coverage to 250–300 sq ft per gallon. Heavy stucco texture can drop coverage to 200 sq ft per gallon. If your walls have any texture, reduce the coverage rate accordingly.

**Color change severity determines coat count.** Painting the same shade requires 1 coat for touch-ups, 1–2 for a fresh look. Changing between similar light colors needs 2 coats. Going from dark to light (navy to white) requires 2–3 coats of finish paint, often with a white-tinted primer first — effectively 3–4 total applications. Each additional coat doubles the paint consumed for that pass.

**Paint quality affects coverage and durability.** Budget paints (\$20–\$30/gallon) contain less pigment and resin, requiring more coats to achieve opacity. Premium paints (\$45–\$75/gallon) cover better per coat and last longer. Sherwin-Williams Duration, Benjamin Moore Regal, and Behr Marquee are rated for one-coat coverage in many color-over-color situations, potentially halving your paint volume on repaints.

**Ceiling and trim are separate calculations.** This calculator computes wall paint only. Ceiling paint (typically flat white) is calculated as length x width. Trim paint (baseboards, crown molding, door frames) is calculated by linear feet x trim height, and uses a different sheen than wall paint. A 14 x 12 room ceiling needs about 0.5 gallons; trim needs about 1 quart.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- Assumes **rectangular rooms** with uniform ceiling height. For rooms with vaulted ceilings, cathedral ceilings, or half-walls, measure each wall section individually and sum the areas.
- Uses **standard door size** of 3 x 7 ft (21 sq ft) and **standard window size** of 3 x 5 ft (15 sq ft). Oversized openings (sliding glass doors, picture windows, bay windows) should be counted as 2 units each.
- Coverage rate of **350 sq ft per gallon** is the industry average for smooth, primed drywall. Actual coverage varies by paint brand, finish (flat covers more than semi-gloss), surface porosity, and application method (spraying uses 10–15% more than rolling).
- Does **not include ceiling or trim paint** — these require separate calculations with different paint types and sheens.
- Assumes walls are **previously painted or properly primed**. Bare drywall, new plaster, or patched areas require a dedicated primer coat before finish paint, which is not included in the coat count.
- Does **not account for accent walls** painted a different color. Calculate accent walls separately using their individual dimensions.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much paint do I need for one room?

One to two gallons covers most standard bedrooms (10 x 12 to 14 x 14 ft) with 8-foot ceilings and 2 coats. A small bathroom may need only 1 gallon. A large living room or master bedroom typically requires 2–3 gallons. The exact amount depends on wall area (length x width x height x 2 sides), minus doors and windows, multiplied by the number of coats, divided by 350 sq ft per gallon. A 12 x 12-foot room with 8-foot ceilings has about 344 net sq ft of paintable wall area after deducting a door and window, needing 2 gallons for 2 coats.

### What is the difference between paint finishes?

Flat (matte) finish hides wall imperfections and provides the most square footage per gallon (up to 400 sq ft) but is difficult to clean — best for ceilings and low-traffic rooms. Eggshell offers a subtle sheen with better washability, making it the most popular choice for living rooms and bedrooms. Satin has a soft luster and cleans easily — ideal for kitchens, bathrooms, and kids' rooms. Semi-gloss resists moisture and stains, used for trim, doors, cabinets, and high-humidity areas. High-gloss is extremely durable and reflective, typically reserved for front doors and accent trim. Higher sheens cover slightly less area per gallon (300–350 sq ft) because the resin-to-pigment ratio shifts toward more resin.

### How many coats of paint do I need?

Two coats is the standard for most repainting projects and provides the best balance of coverage, color depth, and durability. One coat works for touching up the same color or using a premium one-coat paint over a similar shade. Three coats is necessary when changing from a very dark color to a very light one (or vice versa), painting over bold reds, yellows, or oranges, or covering bare drywall without a primer. Using a tinted primer as a first coat can reduce finish coats from 3 to 2 when making dramatic color changes.

### How do I calculate paint for a ceiling?

Multiply the room length by the room width to get the ceiling area in square feet, then divide by the coverage rate (typically 350–400 sq ft per gallon for flat ceiling paint). A 14 x 12-foot room has a 168 sq ft ceiling, requiring about 0.5 gallons for one coat. Most ceilings need only 1 coat of flat white ceiling paint unless you are covering a dark color or significant stains, in which case use a stain-blocking primer first.
