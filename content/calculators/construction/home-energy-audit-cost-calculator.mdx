## What This Estimate Shows

This calculator estimates professional home energy audit costs by home size, audit level, optional diagnostic add-ons, and regional labor rates. It produces a low/mid/high estimate and a comparison table showing the cost at each audit level for your home size.

The **base audit cost** reflects the home size and audit level selected. Energy audits are 100% labor and professional service — there are no material costs. The base rate covers auditor travel, inspection time, and a written report with prioritized recommendations.

The **audit level** is the primary cost driver. A visual walkthrough is a basic inspector-level review with no specialized equipment. A standard diagnostic audit adds a blower door test to measure air infiltration, the most common professional audit for utility rebate eligibility. A comprehensive audit includes the blower door test plus detailed air sealing analysis, insulation depth measurements, and HVAC system efficiency testing. Full performance testing meets RESNET HERS and BPI certification standards required for weatherization assistance programs, FHA and VA energy-efficient mortgage programs, and most state energy office incentive programs.

The **add-ons** are specialized tests that complement the base audit. Thermal imaging uses infrared cameras to pinpoint insulation gaps and air leaks invisible to the naked eye. Duct leakage testing measures the percentage of conditioned air lost through leaky HVAC duct systems — a common source of 20–30% energy waste. Combustion safety testing is critical for homes with gas appliances, oil furnaces, or wood-burning fireplaces, checking for carbon monoxide spillage and backdrafting risks.

## How to Use This Calculator

1. **Select your home size** — larger homes require more inspection time. Select the range that best matches your conditioned floor area. Detached garages and unconditioned spaces typically are not included in the audited area.

2. **Choose the audit level** — for most utility rebate programs, a standard diagnostic audit is the minimum required level. 3. **Add thermal imaging** — strongly recommended for homes built before 1980, homes with high heating or cooling bills relative to neighbors, or homes with any visible moisture intrusion or drafts. The detailed report option provides annotated images mapped to specific locations in the home.

4. **Add duct testing** — recommended for homes with central forced-air HVAC systems, especially those with ducts running through unconditioned attics or crawl spaces. Leaky ducts are frequently the single largest source of energy waste in suburban homes built between 1980 and 2000.

5. **Add combustion safety testing** — required for homes with any gas appliances (furnace, water heater, range), oil furnaces, or wood-burning fireplaces or inserts. This test is frequently required by BPI-certified auditors before proceeding with air sealing in homes with combustion appliances.

6. **Select your region** — energy audits are 100% labor and service, so regional multipliers apply to the full cost. Cities with high cost-of-living and strong demand for energy efficiency services (Northeast, West Coast) have higher auditor rates.

## The Formula

Home energy audit cost is estimated as a per-home-size base rate multiplied by an audit level factor, plus flat add-on costs for specialized tests, then adjusted by a regional multiplier applied to the full cost.

**Base audit cost:**

$$C_{base} = R_{size} \times M_{level}$$

**Total with add-ons:**

$$C_{subtotal} = C_{base} + C_{thermal} + C_{duct} + C_{combustion}$$

**Total with regional adjustment:**

$$C_{total} = C_{subtotal} \times M_r$$

$$C_{mid} = \frac{C_{low} + C_{high}}{2}$$

Where:

- **R_size** = home size base rate (low/high range)
- **M_level** = audit level multiplier (1.0–2.75)
- **M_r** = regional multiplier applied to full cost (0.85–1.25)
- **C_thermal** = thermal imaging add-on ($0–$400)
- **C_duct** = duct testing add-on ($0–$450)
- **C_combustion** = combustion safety add-on ($0–$250)

## Key Cost Factors

**Audit level** is the primary cost driver because it determines the number of hours and specialized equipment required. A visual walkthrough takes 1–2 hours. A standard diagnostic audit with blower door test takes 3–4 hours. A full performance testing audit typically takes a full day (6–8 hours) and requires RESNET- or BPI-certified equipment and reporting software.

**Home size** directly increases labor time. A 5,000 sq ft home takes roughly 40% more inspection time than a 2,500 sq ft home with the same audit level. The cost difference reflects additional windows, outlets, penetrations, and duct runs to inspect, not a different hourly rate.

**Add-on tests** each require specialized equipment and reporting time beyond the base audit. Thermal imaging is most effective in winter when the temperature differential between inside and outside is greatest (40°F+ delta), or in summer in hot climates with air conditioning. Scheduling your audit during these conditions maximizes the diagnostic value.

**Regional availability** affects cost beyond just labor rates. In states with active weatherization programs (California, Massachusetts, New York, Minnesota), competition among BPI-certified auditors may keep prices at or below national average despite higher labor rates. In rural areas of any region, travel fees of $50–$150 may apply.

**Utility and government rebates** frequently offset audit costs. Many utilities offer free or subsidized energy audits for customers with high consumption. State energy offices may provide vouchers covering 50–100% of audit costs for income-qualifying homeowners. Check with your utility provider and state energy office before scheduling a paid audit.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much does a home energy audit cost?

A professional home energy audit costs $450–$750 for a standard diagnostic audit on a typical 2,500–3,500 sq ft home at national average rates. Simple visual walkthrough audits start at $150–$300 for small homes. Full BPI or RESNET-certified performance testing, required for many state incentive programs, costs $825–$1,375 for a standard home. Adding thermal imaging ($100–$400) and duct testing ($100–$450) is recommended for homes with high utility bills or suspected air leaks.

### Is a home energy audit worth it?

A home energy audit is worth it for most homeowners, particularly those with above-average utility bills. The DOE Building Technologies Office reports that homes implementing all recommended audit measures save 20–30% on heating and cooling costs. For a home spending $3,000/year on energy, a 20% reduction saves $600/year — recovering a $600 audit cost in one year. Audits also provide a prioritized roadmap for improvements, helping homeowners invest in the highest-return upgrades first rather than guessing.

### What does a home energy audit include?

A standard diagnostic home energy audit includes a blower door test (measures whole-home air leakage), a room-by-room inspection of insulation, air sealing, windows, and doors, a visual inspection of the HVAC system and ductwork, and a written report with prioritized recommendations ranked by cost-effectiveness. Optional add-ons include thermal imaging cameras (infrared photography showing heat loss), duct blower testing (measures duct leakage specifically), and combustion safety analysis (checks gas appliances for CO spillage and backdrafting).

### What is a blower door test?

A blower door test uses a calibrated fan mounted in an exterior door to depressurize the home. By measuring the airflow required to achieve a specific pressure differential, the auditor calculates the home's air changes per hour (ACH) — a standard measure of whole-home air tightness. Modern homes should achieve less than 3 ACH at 50 pascals (ACH50); older homes often measure 8–15 ACH50 or higher. The blower door is also used in conjunction with a smoke pencil or thermal camera to identify specific air leak locations while the house is under pressure.

### Do I need an energy audit before insulating or air sealing?

A professional energy audit before major weatherization work is strongly recommended and may be required for rebate eligibility. Without an audit, homeowners frequently invest in insulation when air sealing would provide greater return, or insulate attics while leaving major bypasses (recessed light cans, top plates, plumbing and electrical penetrations) unaddressed. An audit also identifies whether combustion safety testing is needed before tightening the building envelope — adding insulation without checking combustion appliances can create carbon monoxide risks in certain home configurations.
