## What This Calculates

This calculator converts your swimming pool dimensions into exact water volume — in U.S. gallons (the standard pool industry measurement), liters, cubic feet, and cubic meters. It supports four common pool shapes: rectangular, circular, oval, and kidney. For each shape, it computes volume using the appropriate geometric formula, then converts to gallons using the NIST-standard factor of 7.48052 gallons per cubic foot.

Beyond raw volume, the calculator provides three practical estimates that pool owners need on fill day. **Chemical dosage** tells you how much chlorine and pH adjuster to add for the initial fill — approximately 2 pounds of chlorine and 1.5 pounds of pH increaser per 10,000 gallons, following PHTA guidelines. **Fill time** estimates how long a standard garden hose at 540 gallons per hour will take to fill your pool. **Water cost** projects municipal water expense at the national average of $0.005 per gallon.

The **average depth** calculation uses the simple midpoint formula: add the shallow-end depth and deep-end depth, then divide by two. This works well for pools with a consistent slope from shallow to deep. For pools with a large flat deep end or a sudden drop-off, the actual volume may be 5-10% higher than the calculated average depth suggests — in those cases, use a waste factor or measure depth at multiple points.

## How to Use This Calculator

1. **Select your pool shape** — rectangular for standard in-ground pools, circular for above-ground and round pools, oval for elongated rounded pools, and kidney for freeform kidney shapes. If your pool is irregularly shaped, choose the closest match and add 10% to the result.

2. **Enter length and width** (or diameter for circular pools) — measure the inside water dimensions, not the outer shell or coping. For kidney pools, measure the longest dimension as length and the widest point as width.

3. **Set the shallow end depth** — measure from the water surface to the bottom at the shallowest point. Most residential pools have a 3-foot to 3.5-foot shallow end.

4. **Set the deep end depth** — measure at the deepest point. Common residential deep ends range from 6 to 8 feet. For a uniform-depth pool (such as a wading pool or above-ground), set both depths the same.

5. **Read your results** — the primary output is total gallons, which you need for chemical dosing, heater sizing, pump flow calculations, and water cost. The fill time estimate assumes a single garden hose; two hoses cut the time roughly in half.

## The Formulas

Pool volume is calculated differently depending on shape. All formulas compute volume in cubic feet, then convert to gallons.

**Rectangular pool:**

$$V = L \times W \times D_{avg}$$

**Circular pool:**

$$V = \pi \times \left(\frac{D}{2}\right)^2 \times D_{avg}$$

**Oval pool:**

$$V = \pi \times \frac{L}{2} \times \frac{W}{2} \times D_{avg}$$

**Kidney pool (industry approximation):**

$$V = 0.45 \times (L + W) \times L \times D_{avg}$$

**Average depth:**

$$D_{avg} = \frac{D_{shallow} + D_{deep}}{2}$$

**Gallons conversion:**

$$\text{Gallons} = V_{ft^3} \times 7.48052$$

Where:

- **L** = pool length in feet
- **W** = pool width in feet
- **D** = pool diameter in feet (circular only)
- **D_avg** = average depth in feet
- **7.48052** = U.S. gallons per cubic foot (NIST standard)

**Source:** Geometric volume formulas are standard mathematics. The gallon conversion factor is per the **National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST)**. The kidney pool multiplier (0.45) is an industry-standard approximation used by the **Pool & Hot Tub Alliance (PHTA)** and pool builders nationwide.

## Water Chemistry After Filling

Knowing your pool volume is essential for accurate chemical dosing. Underdosing leaves water unsafe; overdosing wastes money and can damage surfaces.

**Chlorine (initial fill):** Add 2 pounds of granular chlorine (calcium hypochlorite) per 10,000 gallons to reach shock level (10-12 ppm). After the initial treatment, maintain 1-3 ppm with daily doses of 2-4 ounces per 10,000 gallons or a chlorine tablet in a floater.

**pH adjustment:** Pool water typically fills at pH 7.2-7.8 from municipal sources. Add 1.5 pounds of pH increaser (sodium carbonate) per 10,000 gallons to raise pH by 0.2 units, or 1 pound of pH decreaser (sodium bisulfate) to lower it by 0.2 units. Target range: 7.4-7.6.

**Alkalinity:** Total alkalinity should be 80-120 ppm. Add 1.5 pounds of sodium bicarbonate per 10,000 gallons to raise alkalinity by 10 ppm.

**Fill time shortcuts:** A garden hose flows approximately 540 gallons per hour (9 GPM). A fire hydrant meter rental ($100-$300 from your water utility) flows 1,500-3,000 GPH and can fill a 20,000-gallon pool in 7-13 hours. Bulk water delivery trucks carry 4,000-6,000 gallons per load at $200-$400.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- Assumes **uniform slope** from shallow end to deep end. Pools with a flat deep-end floor or a sudden hopper drop-off may hold 5-10% more water than the average-depth calculation indicates.
- The **kidney pool formula** (0.45 multiplier) is an industry approximation. Actual kidney pools vary widely in shape — true volume can differ by up to 15% from this estimate. For precise volume on freeform shapes, consult your pool builder or use the displacement method.
- **Chemical dosage** estimates are starting points based on PHTA guidelines. Always test water with a reliable test kit before adding chemicals, and adjust based on actual readings.
- **Water cost** uses a national average of $0.005 per gallon ($5 per 1,000 gallons). Your actual rate may be $3 to $12 per 1,000 gallons depending on location, tiered pricing, and sewer surcharges.
- **Fill time** assumes a single garden hose at 540 GPH (9 GPM). Actual flow depends on hose diameter (5/8" standard), length, water pressure, and whether other fixtures are running simultaneously.
- This calculator does not account for **plumbing volume** (water in pipes, filter, and heater), which typically adds 50-200 gallons to total system volume.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How many gallons does a standard in-ground pool hold?

A standard residential in-ground pool (30 x 15 feet, 3-foot shallow end and 8-foot deep end) holds approximately 18,500 gallons. Smaller pools (24 x 12 ft) hold about 10,800 gallons, and larger pools (40 x 20 ft) hold around 33,000 gallons. The exact volume depends on the shape and depth profile of your specific pool.

### How do I calculate pool volume for an irregular shape?

Break the pool into simpler shapes — a rectangle plus a semicircle, for example — calculate each section separately, and add the volumes. For truly freeform pools, use the displacement method: measure your water meter before and after filling, or multiply pool surface area by average depth and multiply by 7.48 to convert cubic feet to gallons.

### How long does it take to fill a pool with a garden hose?

A standard 5/8-inch garden hose delivers approximately 540 gallons per hour (9 GPM) at typical residential water pressure (40-60 PSI). A 15,000-gallon pool takes about 28 hours with one hose, or 14 hours with two. A bulk water delivery truck can fill the same pool in under 3 hours at 3-4 loads.

### How much does it cost to fill a pool with water?

At the U.S. average water rate of $5 per 1,000 gallons, filling a 15,000-gallon pool costs approximately $75, and a 20,000-gallon pool costs about $100. Actual costs range from $45 to $240 depending on local water rates and whether your utility charges sewer surcharges on pool fills. Many utilities will issue a sewer credit for pool fills — call your water company first.

### How much chlorine do I need for a new pool fill?

For the initial fill, add 2 pounds of granular chlorine (calcium hypochlorite, 65% available chlorine) per 10,000 gallons to reach shock level of 10-12 ppm. A standard 18,500-gallon pool needs about 3.7 pounds for the first treatment. Wait 24 hours, test the water, and adjust to maintain 1-3 ppm ongoing.

### What is the kidney pool volume formula?

The industry-standard kidney pool approximation is: Volume = 0.45 x (Length + Width) x Length x Average Depth. This multiplier accounts for the kidney shape having roughly 45% of the area of the bounding rectangle. The formula is used by pool builders nationwide, though actual volumes can vary 10-15% depending on the exact curvature of your specific pool.

### How do I find my pool volume without measuring?

Check your pool builder's original documentation — the volume is usually listed on the permit application or equipment sizing sheet. Your pool pump and filter should also be sized to your pool volume, so the equipment manual may reference it. If no records exist, measure length, width, and depths at both ends, then use this calculator.

### How much water does an above-ground pool hold?

A 15-foot round above-ground pool at 4 feet deep holds about 5,289 gallons. An 18-foot round pool holds 7,620 gallons. A 24-foot round pool holds 13,536 gallons. Above-ground pools typically have uniform depth, so the calculation is straightforward: pi x radius squared x depth x 7.48.
