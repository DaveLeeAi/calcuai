## What This Estimate Shows

Gravel is ordered by the cubic yard or by the ton, and the conversion between them depends on the type of gravel you choose. Pea gravel weighs approximately 1.4 tons per cubic yard, crushed limestone runs 1.5 tons, and dense materials like river rock can reach 1.6 tons per cubic yard. Ordering the wrong amount because you confused volume with weight — or used the wrong density — means either a second delivery fee (\$50–\$100) or a pile of leftover rock in your yard.

This calculator handles the volume-to-weight conversion for you. Enter your project dimensions, select your gravel type, and get both cubic yards (for ordering from landscape suppliers who sell by volume) and tons (for quarries and aggregate dealers who sell by weight). The calculator uses the same volume formula as the [Mulch Calculator](/calculators/construction/mulch-calculator) — length times width times depth — but adds the density conversion step that makes gravel estimation different from organic fill materials.

## How to Use This Calculator

1. **Enter the area length** — measure the longest dimension of your project in feet. For driveways, this is the distance from the road to the garage. For walkways, measure the full path length.

2. **Enter the area width** — measure the shorter dimension in feet. Standard driveway widths are 10–12 feet for single-car and 20–24 feet for double-car.

3. **Set the gravel depth** — enter in inches. Common depths are 2 inches for walkway top layers, 4 inches for driveway base courses, and 6 inches for heavy-load areas or French drain beds.

4. **Select the gravel type** — choose from pea gravel (1.4 tons/cu yd), crushed stone (1.5 tons/cu yd), or river rock (1.6 tons/cu yd). Each type has a different density that affects the weight-based ordering quantity.

5. **Read your results** — the calculator displays total cubic feet, cubic yards, and tonnage, along with cost estimates for bulk delivery.

## The Formula

Gravel volume uses the same rectangular prism formula as other fill materials, with an additional density conversion for weight:

$$V_{\text{cu ft}} = L \times W \times \frac{D}{12}$$

$$V_{\text{cu yd}} = \frac{V_{\text{cu ft}}}{27}$$

$$\text{Weight (tons)} = V_{\text{cu yd}} \times \rho$$

Where:

- **L** = project area length (feet)
- **W** = project area width (feet)
- **D** = gravel depth (inches)
- **27** = cubic feet per cubic yard
- **$\rho$** = density of the gravel type (tons per cubic yard)

**Density values by gravel type:**

| Gravel Type | Density (tons/cu yd) | Density (lbs/cu ft) |
|---|---|---|
| Pea Gravel | 1.4 | 103.7 |
| Crushed Stone / Limestone | 1.5 | 111.1 |
| Crusher Run (with fines) | 1.5 | 111.1 |
| River Rock (1–3 inch) | 1.6 | 118.5 |
| Decomposed Granite | 1.35 | 100.0 |

These densities assume dry, loose material. Wet gravel can weigh 10–20% more, which may affect transport but does not change the volume you need.

**Source:** The base volume formula (length × width × depth) is shared with the Mulch Calculator and follows standard geometry per **NIST** measurement standards. Aggregate density values reference the **Portland Cement Association (PCA)** published aggregate data tables and **ASTM C29** standard test methods for bulk density of aggregates. Cost data from **HomeAdvisor** and regional aggregate supplier surveys (2025–2026).

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Garden Walkway — 20 x 3 ft, 2-Inch Depth, Pea Gravel

A homeowner is laying a pea gravel walkway between garden beds. The path is 20 feet long by 3 feet wide with a 2-inch top layer over landscape fabric.

**Inputs:**
- Length: 20 ft
- Width: 3 ft
- Depth: 2 in
- Gravel Type: Pea Gravel (1.4 tons/cu yd)

**Step-by-step:**

1. Convert depth to feet: 2 ÷ 12 = **0.1667 ft**
2. Calculate volume: 20 × 3 × 0.1667 = **10.0 cu ft**
3. Convert to cubic yards: 10.0 ÷ 27 = **0.37 cu yd**
4. Convert to tons: 0.37 × 1.4 = **0.52 tons** (approximately 1,040 lbs)

**Cost estimate:**
- Bulk: 0.52 tons × $45/ton = **$23.40** (but most suppliers require a 1-ton minimum)
- Bagged alternative: 10 cu ft ÷ 0.5 cu ft per bag = 20 bags × $5 = **$100**

**Interpretation:** At just over half a ton, this small project is below most bulk delivery minimums. Bagged pea gravel from a home improvement store is more practical despite the higher per-unit cost — 20 bags at roughly 50 lbs each totals 1,000 lbs, manageable in two car trips. Lay landscape fabric beneath the pea gravel to prevent it from mixing into the soil below.

### Example 2: Driveway Base — 30 x 12 ft, 4-Inch Depth, Crushed Stone

A homeowner is preparing a gravel base layer for a new asphalt driveway. The driveway measures 30 feet long by 12 feet wide, requiring a 4-inch compacted crushed stone base.

**Inputs:**
- Length: 30 ft
- Width: 12 ft
- Depth: 4 in
- Gravel Type: Crushed Stone (1.5 tons/cu yd)

**Step-by-step:**

1. Convert depth to feet: 4 ÷ 12 = **0.3333 ft**
2. Calculate volume: 30 × 12 × 0.3333 = **120 cu ft**
3. Convert to cubic yards: 120 ÷ 27 = **4.44 cu yd**
4. Convert to tons: 4.44 × 1.5 = **6.67 tons** (approximately 13,340 lbs)
5. Add 10% compaction allowance: 6.67 × 1.10 = **7.33 tons**

**Cost estimate:**
- Bulk crushed stone: 7.33 tons × $30/ton = **$219.90**
- Delivery fee (within 20 miles): **\$75**
- **Total: approximately \$295**

**Interpretation:** At 7.33 tons, bulk delivery from a quarry is the only practical option — this amount would require 147 fifty-pound bags at \$5 each (\$735), nearly triple the bulk cost. Order crusher run (crushed stone with fines) rather than clean crushed stone for a driveway base: the fine particles fill voids between larger stones and compact into a denser, more stable surface. Compact the base with a plate compactor (rental \$75/day) in 2-inch lifts for optimal stability.

## Key Factors That Affect Your Results

**Gravel type changes weight by up to 15% for the same volume.** One cubic yard of decomposed granite (1.35 tons) weighs 250 lbs less than the same volume of river rock (1.6 tons). When ordering by the ton, this density difference means you get more coverage per ton from lighter materials and less from heavier ones. Always confirm whether your supplier prices by volume or weight, and which density they use.

**Compaction reduces loose gravel volume by 10–15%.** Crushed stone with angular edges compacts more than rounded pea gravel. A 4-inch loose layer of crusher run compacts to approximately 3.5 inches. For base courses that require a specific finished depth, order 10–15% more than the calculated loose volume. Round pea gravel and river rock do not compact significantly because the smooth, round particles resist interlocking.

**Moisture content affects delivered weight but not volume.** Wet gravel from an uncovered stockpile can weigh 10–20% more than dry material. If you order 5 tons of wet crushed stone, you may receive the same volume as 4.2 tons of dry stone — because you are paying for the water weight. Ask your supplier whether they adjust pricing for moisture content, and order by volume (cubic yards) when possible to avoid overpaying.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- Assumes **flat, rectangular project areas** with uniform depth. Sloped areas, curved borders, and irregular shapes require adjusted measurements or separate calculations for each section.
- Density values are **dry, loose-material estimates** averaged across common commercial products. Actual density varies by quarry source, particle size distribution, and moisture content.
- **Does not include a compaction factor** by default. For base courses that will be compacted, manually add 10–15% to the calculated volume before ordering.
- **Cost estimates** reflect 2026 U.S. national average bulk pricing. Gravel prices vary significantly by region, distance from the nearest quarry, and fuel costs. Delivery fees are additional and depend on distance and truck size.
- Does not account for **edging, geotextile fabric, or sub-base preparation** materials, which are separate purchases required for most gravel installations.
- Tonnage calculations assume **standard commercial aggregate** densities. Specialty materials (lava rock, recycled glass aggregate, marble chips) have different densities not covered by the standard presets.
