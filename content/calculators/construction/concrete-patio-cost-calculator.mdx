## What This Estimates

This calculator provides installed cost ranges for concrete patios across five finish types — broom finish, stamped, exposed aggregate, colored/stained, and polished — based on your patio dimensions, slab thickness, and options. It produces a low/mid/high estimate for your selected finish, a cost-per-square-foot figure, and a comparison table showing what the same patio would cost in every finish.

The **material and labor split** reflects concrete industry pricing: approximately 40% of installed cost is materials (concrete, color, stamps, aggregate, forms) and 60% is labor (forming, pouring, finishing, curing). A basic broom-finish patio runs $6–$12 per square foot installed, while stamped concrete runs $12–$20 per square foot. These ranges come from HomeAdvisor, Angi, and Concrete Network 2025–2026 data.

The **regional multiplier** adjusts labor costs only. Concrete material costs are relatively uniform nationally, but labor rates vary significantly — West Coast labor runs 25% above national average, while Southern states run 15% below. This is the single largest geographic variable in concrete patio pricing.

The **thickness adjustment** adds $1 per square foot in material cost when upgrading from standard 4-inch to 6-inch slabs. The 6-inch option is recommended for patios supporting hot tubs (2,500–5,000 lbs loaded), heavy stone furniture, or vehicle traffic. Most residential patios use 4-inch slabs with 4,000 PSI concrete.

## How to Use This Calculator

1. **Enter the patio length** — measure the longest dimension of your planned patio in feet. For irregular shapes, use the length of the smallest rectangle that would contain your patio design.

2. **Enter the patio width** — measure the shorter dimension. A comfortable dining patio for 4–6 people is at least 12 × 12 feet. A large entertaining patio runs 16 × 20 feet or larger.

3. **Choose slab thickness** — 4-inch is standard for most residential patios and supports normal patio furniture, grills, and foot traffic. Choose 6-inch if you plan to place a hot tub, heavy stone furniture, or if the patio will support any vehicle traffic.

4. **Select the finish type** — broom finish is the most affordable and provides excellent traction. Stamped concrete mimics stone, brick, or wood patterns and is the most popular decorative option. Exposed aggregate reveals the stone within the concrete for a natural look. Colored/stained adds integral color or acid stain. Polished concrete creates a smooth, glossy surface best suited for covered patios.

5. **Select demolition** — choose "Remove Old Patio" if you are replacing an existing concrete, paver, or brick patio. Demolition adds $3–$6 per square foot for breaking, hauling, and disposing of the old surface.

6. **Choose extras** — steps are needed when the patio is above or below grade level. A seating wall is a built-in concrete wall (typically 18–24 inches high) around the patio perimeter that doubles as seating.

7. **Select your region** — this adjusts labor rates for your area. Material costs remain the same nationwide.

## The Formula

Concrete patio cost is estimated by splitting installed cost into material and labor components, then adding demolition and extras.

**Area:**

$$A = L \times W$$

**Component costs:**

$$C_{material} = A \times (R_m + T_{adj})$$

$$C_{labor} = A \times R_l \times M_r$$

$$C_{demo} = A \times R_{demo}$$

**Total cost range:**

$$C_{low} = C_{material,low} + C_{labor,low} + C_{demo,low} + C_{extras}$$

$$C_{high} = C_{material,high} + C_{labor,high} + C_{demo,high} + C_{extras}$$

$$C_{mid} = \frac{C_{low} + C_{high}}{2}$$

Where:

- **L** = patio length in feet
- **W** = patio width in feet
- **R_m** = material rate per sq ft (varies by finish type)
- **T_adj** = thickness adjustment ($0 for 4-inch, $1 for 6-inch)
- **R_l** = labor rate per sq ft (varies by finish type)
- **M_r** = regional labor multiplier (0.85–1.25)
- **R_demo** = demolition rate per sq ft ($0 or $3–$6)
- **C_extras** = flat cost for selected extras ($0–$1,500)

## Factors That Affect Concrete Patio Cost

**Patio size** is the primary cost driver — cost scales linearly with area. A 192-square-foot patio costs exactly half of a 384-square-foot patio in the same finish. However, very large patios (over 400 sq ft) may qualify for contractor volume discounts of 5–10% on labor.

**Finish type** creates the largest cost swing per square foot. The same 192-square-foot patio ranges from $1,152 in broom finish to $3,840 in stamped or polished concrete — a 3.3x difference. Stamped concrete is the most popular decorative option because it mimics expensive natural stone at 30–50% of the cost.

**Slab thickness** adds $1 per square foot when upgrading from 4-inch to 6-inch. For a 192-square-foot patio, that is $192 extra. The 6-inch upgrade is essential for hot tubs — a loaded hot tub weighs 3,000–5,000 pounds and requires the thicker slab plus reinforcement.

**Demolition** is a significant cost for replacement projects. Removing an old concrete patio runs $3–$6 per square foot, adding $576–$1,152 to a 192-square-foot project. Paver removal is on the lower end; reinforced concrete removal is on the higher end.

**Site preparation** includes grading, compacting the subbase, and addressing drainage. Flat, well-drained sites require minimal preparation. Sloped or poorly drained sites may need additional grading ($1–$3/sq ft), French drains ($10–$25/linear ft), or retaining walls that add thousands to the project.

**Regional labor rates** vary up to 40% across the U.S. A $2,304 labor bill on a stamped patio at national average becomes $1,958 in the South and $2,880 on the West Coast. Materials are priced more uniformly.

**Season and timing** affect pricing — concrete contractors are busiest April through October, and spring/summer pricing is typically 10–15% higher than winter rates in regions where year-round pouring is possible.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- All costs are **installed prices** including labor, materials, forming, pouring, finishing, and standard curing. Premium sealers and specialty reinforcement are not included.
- Cost ranges reflect **2025–2026 U.S. national averages** from HomeAdvisor, Angi, and Concrete Network. Your local market may be 15–25% higher or lower.
- The calculator assumes a **rectangular patio** on relatively flat, well-drained ground. Curved, multi-level, or irregularly shaped patios require custom estimating and typically cost 10–20% more.
- **Extras are single-select** in this calculator — in practice, you can combine steps and seating walls. Add the individual costs for a combined estimate.
- **Demolition rates** assume standard 4-inch unreinforced concrete. Removing reinforced concrete or thick slabs costs more. Hauling distance to the disposal site also affects cost.
- The calculator does not account for **permits** ($50–$500), **foundation/footer requirements** for frost-line depth, **drainage systems**, **lighting**, **outdoor kitchen plumbing/gas**, or **landscaping** around the patio.
- **Polished concrete** is best suited for covered patios — exposed polished surfaces become slippery when wet and may not meet local building codes for outdoor use without a covered structure.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much does a concrete patio cost?

A concrete patio costs $6–$20 per square foot installed, depending on the finish type. A standard 192-square-foot (16 × 12) broom-finish patio costs $1,152–$2,304. Stamped concrete costs $2,304–$3,840 for the same area. The national average for a mid-range concrete patio with a decorative finish is approximately $2,500–$4,000 for 200 square feet, including standard site preparation and forming.

### Is stamped concrete worth the extra cost?

Stamped concrete costs $12–$20 per square foot versus $6–$12 for broom finish — roughly double the price — but it mimics the look of natural flagstone, slate, or brick at 30–50% of the cost of those materials. A 192-square-foot stamped patio costs $2,304–$3,840 versus $5,000–$10,000+ for natural stone pavers. Stamped concrete lasts 25–35 years with proper sealing every 2–3 years, making it the best value for decorative outdoor concrete.

### How thick should a concrete patio be?

A standard residential patio should be 4 inches thick with 4,000 PSI concrete — this supports normal foot traffic, patio furniture, grills, and dining sets. Upgrade to 6 inches if the patio will support a hot tub (3,000–5,000 lbs loaded), heavy stone planters, or any vehicle traffic. The 6-inch upgrade adds $1 per square foot in material cost. All patios should be poured over a compacted gravel base (4–6 inches) with wire mesh or fiber reinforcement.

### How long does a concrete patio last?

A properly installed concrete patio lasts 25–35 years with minimal maintenance. Key longevity factors are proper slab thickness, adequate subbase compaction, control joints every 8–10 feet, and regular sealing every 2–3 years for decorative finishes. Broom-finish patios are the most maintenance-free. Stamped and colored concrete require resealing to maintain appearance. In freeze-thaw climates, use air-entrained concrete (5–7% air content) to prevent surface scaling and spalling.
