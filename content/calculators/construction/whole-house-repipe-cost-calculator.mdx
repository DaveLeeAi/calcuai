## What This Estimates

This calculator provides installed cost ranges for whole-house repiping across three common pipe materials -- copper, PEX (cross-linked polyethylene), and CPVC (chlorinated polyvinyl chloride). It factors in home size, number of stories, bathroom count, wall repair, permits, and regional labor rates. The result includes a low/mid/high estimate for your selected material plus a side-by-side comparison of all three pipe types.

The **base cost** is determined by home size, with four tiers from small (under 1,000 sq ft) to extra-large (over 2,500 sq ft). These ranges reflect 2025-2026 national averages from HomeAdvisor, Angi, and the Plumbing-Heating-Cooling Contractors Association (PHCC), covering pipe materials, fittings, labor, and standard fixture connections.

The **pipe material multiplier** creates the largest cost difference. Copper is the baseline at 1.0x -- it is the most expensive but offers the longest lifespan (50-70 years) and highest resale value. PEX at 0.60x is 40% cheaper because it installs faster (flexible tubing routes through walls without soldering) and the material itself costs less. CPVC at 0.55x is the cheapest option but has a shorter lifespan (25-40 years) and cannot be used outdoors or in UV-exposed areas.

The **story multiplier** adds 15% per additional story (2-story: 1.15x, 3-story: 1.30x) because multi-story homes require more vertical pipe runs, ceiling/floor penetrations, and scaffold or attic work.

The **bathroom multiplier** adjusts for fixture density. A 1-bath home uses a 0.85x multiplier because it has fewer pipe runs. A 2-bath home is the baseline at 1.0x. Three bathrooms add 15% (1.15x) and four or more add 30% (1.30x) because each additional bathroom means more supply lines, drain connections, and fixture hookups.

The cost splits into materials (35%) and labor (65%), with regional multipliers applied to the labor portion only. This reflects the labor-intensive nature of repiping, where cutting into walls, routing new pipe, and patching drywall consume the majority of project time.

## The Formula

Whole-house repiping cost is estimated using a home-size base rate adjusted by pipe material, stories, and bathrooms, then split into material and labor components with regional adjustment on labor only.

**Adjusted base cost:**

$$C_{\text{adjusted}} = C_{\text{base}} \times M_{\text{pipe}} \times M_{\text{story}} \times M_{\text{bath}}$$

**Piping cost (material + labor split):**

$$C_{\text{piping}} = C_{\text{adjusted}} \times 0.35 + C_{\text{adjusted}} \times 0.65 \times R$$

**Total cost:**

$$C_{\text{total}} = C_{\text{piping}} + C_{\text{wall}} + C_{\text{permit}}$$

$$C_{\text{mid}} = \frac{C_{\text{total,low}} + C_{\text{total,high}}}{2}$$

Where:

- **C_base** = home size base cost range (low/high -- see table below)
- **M_pipe** = pipe material multiplier (copper: 1.0, PEX: 0.60, CPVC: 0.55)
- **M_story** = story multiplier (1-story: 1.0, 2-story: 1.15, 3-story: 1.30)
- **M_bath** = bathroom multiplier (1-bath: 0.85, 2-bath: 1.0, 3-bath: 1.15, 4+: 1.30)
- **R** = regional labor multiplier (national: 1.0, northeast: 1.20, west-coast: 1.25, midwest: 0.90, south: 0.85)
- **0.35** = material cost share
- **0.65** = labor cost share
- **C_wall** = wall repair add-on (none: $0, patches: $500-$1,500, full repair: $1,500-$4,000)
- **C_permit** = permit cost (yes: $200-$500, no: $0)

## Signs You Need Whole-House Repiping

**Frequent leaks** are the clearest signal. If you are patching pinhole leaks every 6-12 months, the pipes have reached the end of their useful life. Spot repairs on failing pipes are like patching a tire with multiple punctures -- each fix shifts stress to the next weak point. When you have spent $1,000+ on individual plumbing repairs in a single year, repiping is almost always more cost-effective.

**Discolored water** (brown, yellow, or rusty) after sitting overnight indicates internal pipe corrosion. This is common in galvanized steel pipes (pre-1960s homes) and aging copper with aggressive water chemistry. A water quality test ($50-$150) can confirm whether discoloration comes from your pipes or the municipal supply.

**Low water pressure** throughout the house (not just one fixture) often signals mineral buildup or corrosion narrowing the pipe interior. Galvanized steel pipes lose up to half their internal diameter over 40 years from scale accumulation. If pressure is low at multiple fixtures simultaneously, the problem is systemic.

**Pipe material and age** are predictive. Galvanized steel (common in pre-1960 homes) lasts 40-60 years and should be proactively replaced. Polybutylene pipes (1978-1995, gray or blue flexible tubing) are failure-prone and many insurance companies refuse to cover homes with PB plumbing. Lead pipes (pre-1930) should be replaced for health safety regardless of condition.

**Slab leaks** -- leaks in pipes embedded in the concrete foundation -- cost $2,000-$4,000 per individual repair and often indicate system-wide deterioration. If you have had two or more slab leak repairs, full repiping is usually more cost-effective than continued spot fixes. A licensed plumber can assess overall pipe condition with a camera inspection for $200-$500.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- All costs are **installed prices** including pipe materials, fittings, labor, and standard fixture connections. Fixture replacement (faucets, valves, water heater connections) is not included.
- Cost ranges reflect **2025-2026 U.S. national averages** from HomeAdvisor, Angi, and PHCC. Your local market may be 20-40% higher or lower depending on plumber availability and local code requirements.
- The calculator assumes **standard residential construction** with accessible walls, crawl spaces, or basements. Homes built on slab foundations may cost more because pipes in the slab must be abandoned and new lines rerouted through the attic or walls.
- **Wall repair costs** are flat add-ons. Homes with plaster walls (vs drywall), tile walls, or extensive built-in cabinetry may cost significantly more to open and restore.
- **Permit fees** vary by jurisdiction from $100 to $800. The $200-$500 range covers most standard residential plumbing permits.
- The **35% material / 65% labor** cost split reflects PHCC industry data for residential repiping. Material-heavy projects (copper in large homes) may shift closer to 40/60.
- This calculator does not include costs for **drain/waste/vent (DWV) repiping**, **water heater replacement**, **main shutoff valve replacement**, **sewer line work**, or **outdoor irrigation piping** -- these are separate projects.
- The calculator does not account for **slab reroute costs** for homes on concrete slab foundations where pipes cannot be accessed through the slab and must be rerouted through attic or walls.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much does it cost to repipe a house?

A whole-house repipe costs $3,000-$18,000+ depending on home size, pipe material, and complexity. A medium home (1,000-1,500 sq ft) with copper costs $4,500-$8,000 for piping alone, or $5,200-$10,000 with drywall patches and permits. PEX cuts the piping cost to $2,700-$4,800 for the same home -- 40% less than copper. Large homes (1,500-2,500 sq ft) range from $7,000-$13,000 with copper. Add $500-$4,000 for wall repairs and $200-$500 for permits on top of the piping cost.

### Is PEX or copper better for repiping?

PEX is the better choice for most residential repipes because it costs 40% less than copper and installs 2-3x faster. PEX is flexible (bends around corners without fittings), freeze-resistant (expands without splitting), and lasts 40-50 years. Copper lasts longer (50-70 years), has higher resale value, and is universally code-compliant. Choose copper if your local code requires it, you plan to stay in the home 30+ years, or you have aggressive water chemistry that may degrade PEX. Choose PEX for cost savings, faster installation (3-5 days vs 5-7 days for copper), and freeze-prone climates.

### How long does whole-house repiping take?

Repiping takes 2-10 days depending on home size. A small single-story home takes 2-4 days. A medium home takes 3-5 days. A large 2-story home takes 4-7 days. Extra-large homes can take 5-10 days. PEX installation is typically 1-2 days faster than copper because it requires no soldering and routes more easily through existing wall cavities. Your water will be shut off during active pipe work (usually 6-8 hours per day) but restored each evening.

### What are the signs you need to repipe your house?

The five key warning signs are: frequent pinhole leaks (more than one per year in different locations), discolored or rusty water after sitting overnight, consistently low water pressure throughout the house, visible pipe corrosion or green patina on copper fittings, and age-based risk factors (galvanized steel over 40 years old, polybutylene pipes of any age, or lead pipes of any age). A licensed plumber can perform a pipe inspection with a camera for $200-$500 to assess overall condition before committing to a full repipe.

### Does repiping increase home value?

Repiping typically recoups 50-70% of its cost in added home value and makes a home significantly easier to sell. Buyers and home inspectors flag old galvanized or polybutylene pipes as major deficiencies that can kill deals or trigger price reductions of $10,000-$20,000. Copper repiping offers the highest resale premium. PEX is increasingly accepted by buyers and appraisers, especially in newer construction markets. Any repipe -- regardless of material -- eliminates a major inspection red flag and removes a common buyer objection.

### Do I need a permit for repiping?

Yes, nearly all jurisdictions require a plumbing permit for whole-house repiping. Permits cost $200-$500 and ensure the work is inspected for code compliance. The process typically involves a pre-inspection, an in-wall inspection of new pipe routing before walls are closed, and a final inspection after completion. Working without a permit can void your homeowner's insurance, create disclosure issues when selling, and result in fines. Always hire a licensed plumber who pulls the permit as part of the job.
