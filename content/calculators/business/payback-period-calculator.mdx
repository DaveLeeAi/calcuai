## What Your Results Mean

**Simple Payback Period** is the number of years required for cumulative cash flows to equal the initial investment. It answers the most basic question in capital budgeting: "How long until I get my money back?" A shorter payback period means lower risk because your capital is exposed for less time.

**Discounted Payback Period** adjusts future cash flows for the time value of money. A dollar received in year 5 is worth less than a dollar received today, so the discounted payback is always longer than the simple payback when the discount rate is greater than zero. This metric is more accurate for comparing projects with different cash flow timings.

**Year-by-Year Table** shows annual cash flows, their discounted present values, and cumulative totals. This helps you see exactly when the investment breaks even under both methods.

**Total Return** shows the total cash flow received over the payback period — useful for understanding how much of the investment is recovered and how much surplus is generated.

## How to Use This Calculator

1. **Enter Initial Investment** — the total upfront cost of the project, equipment, or business venture.
2. **Enter Annual Cash Flow** — the expected net cash inflow per year. This should be after operating expenses but before debt service. Use conservative estimates.
3. **Set Discount Rate** — enter 0 for simple payback only. For a more rigorous analysis, enter your company's cost of capital (often 8-15%) or the rate of return available from an alternative investment. Common choices are the weighted average cost of capital (WACC) or the risk-free rate plus a premium.
4. **Read your results** — compare the payback period to your company's maximum acceptable payback threshold. Many firms use 3-5 years as the cutoff.

## The Formula

<div className="formula-section">

**Simple Payback Period:**

$$
\text{Payback Period} = \frac{\text{Initial Investment}}{\text{Annual Cash Flow}}
$$

**Discounted Cash Flow (Year n):**

$$
\text{DCF}_n = \frac{\text{Annual Cash Flow}}{(1 + r)^n}
$$

**Discounted Payback Period:**

$$
\text{Year where } \sum_{n=1}^{N} \text{DCF}_n \geq \text{Initial Investment}
$$

Where:
- **Initial Investment** = total upfront capital outlay
- **Annual Cash Flow** = expected net cash inflow per year (assumed constant)
- **r** = discount rate (as a decimal, e.g., 0.10 for 10%)
- **n** = year number
- **N** = the payback year (interpolated for fractional years)

*Source: Brealey, Myers & Allen, Principles of Corporate Finance (13th ed., 2020). Discounted payback methodology follows standard corporate finance DCF analysis.*

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Equipment Purchase (Simple Payback)

A manufacturing company invests \$120,000 in a new CNC machine that increases output by \$40,000 per year in net cash flow.

**Simple Payback = \$120,000 / \$40,000 = 3.0 years**

| Year | Cash Flow | Cumulative |
|:----:|----------:|----------:|
| 1 | \$40,000 | \$40,000 |
| 2 | \$40,000 | \$80,000 |
| 3 | \$40,000 | **\$120,000** |
| 4 | \$40,000 | \$160,000 |

*Interpretation:* The machine pays for itself in exactly 3 years. If the company's cutoff is 4 years, the project passes. The \$160,000 cumulative at year 4 means \$40,000 in surplus above the investment — use [ROI Calculator](/calculators/business/roi-calculator) to measure the full return.

### Example 2: Software Implementation (Discounted Payback)

A company invests \$75,000 in a CRM system expected to generate \$25,000/year in additional revenue through improved sales efficiency. The company's cost of capital is 12%.

**Simple Payback = \$75,000 / \$25,000 = 3.0 years**

| Year | Cash Flow | Discounted CF | Cumulative DCF |
|:----:|----------:|--------------:|---------------:|
| 1 | \$25,000 | \$22,321 | \$22,321 |
| 2 | \$25,000 | \$19,930 | \$42,251 |
| 3 | \$25,000 | \$17,795 | \$60,046 |
| 4 | \$25,000 | \$15,888 | **\$75,934** |

**Discounted Payback = ~3.94 years** (interpolated from the year 3-4 boundary)

*Interpretation:* While simple payback suggests 3 years, the time value of money pushes the real payback to nearly 4 years. At a 12% cost of capital, the CRM barely meets a 4-year payback threshold. If the expected cash flows are uncertain, this project carries meaningful risk.

## Key Factors That Affect Your Results

1. **Cash Flow Timing** — Payback period assumes equal annual cash flows. Projects with growing cash flows (e.g., a new product gaining market share) have shorter effective payback periods than the calculator indicates. Projects with declining cash flows have longer actual payback.

2. **Discount Rate Selection** — The discount rate dramatically affects the discounted payback. At 5%, a 3-year simple payback might extend to 3.3 years discounted. At 15%, the same project might need 4+ years. Use your company's WACC for internal projects or the opportunity cost rate for external investments.

3. **Ignoring Post-Payback Cash Flows** — Payback period's biggest weakness is that it ignores all cash flows after the investment is recouped. A project that pays back in 2 years and then generates \$0 is ranked equally to one that pays back in 2 years and then generates \$1 million annually for a decade. Always pair payback analysis with NPV or ROI for a complete picture.

4. **Risk and Certainty** — Shorter payback periods are inherently less risky because there is less time for assumptions to go wrong. In volatile industries, a 2-year payback may be preferred over a 5-year payback with higher total returns because the forecast confidence degrades with time.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- Assumes equal annual cash flows — real-world cash flows are often uneven, seasonal, or growing
- Simple payback ignores the time value of money — receiving \$10,000 in year 5 is treated as equivalent to \$10,000 in year 1
- Neither method considers cash flows after the payback point, so it cannot distinguish between projects with different long-term returns
- Does not account for salvage value, tax implications, or depreciation benefits
- For projects with uneven cash flows, use the year-by-year table and track cumulative cash flow manually
- Maximum calculation window is 50 years — projects that never pay back within 50 years report "Never"

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is a good payback period for a business investment?

Most companies use 3 to 5 years as the maximum acceptable payback period for capital investments, according to a 2022 survey by Deloitte. High-risk industries (technology, startups) often require 2 years or less. Low-risk industries (utilities, infrastructure) may accept 7-10 years. The key principle: the payback threshold should reflect how long you can confidently forecast the cash flows. If your revenue projections beyond 3 years are speculative, a 3-year maximum payback is appropriate.

### When should I use discounted payback instead of simple payback?

Use discounted payback whenever the discount rate (cost of capital) is meaningful — typically any rate above 5% and any payback period beyond 2 years. For a small business evaluating a \$5,000 equipment purchase that pays back in 6 months, simple payback is sufficient. For a \$500,000 software implementation with a 4-year payback, the discounted version is essential because ignoring the time value of money at 10% cost of capital understates the real payback by 6-12 months.

### Why is payback period not sufficient on its own?

Payback period only answers "when do I get my money back?" It does not answer "how much total value does this create?" A project with a 2-year payback and \$10,000 total profit is ranked identically to one with a 2-year payback and \$500,000 total profit. For complete capital budgeting decisions, combine payback period with Net Present Value (NPV) for absolute value and Internal Rate of Return (IRR) for percentage return. Payback serves as a risk screen; NPV and IRR drive the investment decision.
