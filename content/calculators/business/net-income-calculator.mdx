## What This Calculator Shows

This calculator converts your gross income into net (take-home) income after accounting for federal income tax, state tax, local tax, Social Security, Medicare, 401(k) contributions, and health insurance premiums. Unlike the more detailed [Paycheck Calculator](/finance/paycheck-calculator) which models progressive federal tax brackets per pay period, this calculator uses your effective tax rates for a quick estimate — ideal for comparing job offers, estimating annual budgets, or understanding how changes in deductions affect your bottom line.

The **effective tax rate** tells you what percentage of gross income goes to taxes alone (not including 401(k) or insurance). The **take-home percentage** accounts for all deductions, showing what fraction of your gross pay actually reaches your bank account. These two numbers are the most useful benchmarks when evaluating whether a raise, new job, or relocation to a different tax state materially improves your finances.

## How to Use This Calculator

1. **Enter your Gross Income** — your total compensation before any deductions. If you know your monthly or biweekly pay instead, change the Pay Frequency dropdown and enter that amount.
2. **Select Pay Frequency** — annual, monthly, biweekly, or weekly. The calculator converts to annual for all calculations.

4. **Enter your State Tax Rate** — ranges from 0% (Texas, Florida, Nevada, and 6 others) to 13.3% (California top bracket).
5. **Enter Local Tax Rate** — some cities levy additional income tax (New York City: 3.876%, Philadelphia: 3.75%). Enter 0 if your city has none.
6. **Adjust FICA rates if needed** — Social Security (6.2%) and Medicare (1.45%) are standard for W-2 employees. Self-employed individuals pay double (12.4% + 2.9%).
7. **Add 401(k) and health insurance** — enter your retirement contribution as a percentage and monthly health premium in dollars.

## The Formula

$$\text{Net Income} = \text{Gross Income} - \text{Federal Tax} - \text{State Tax} - \text{Local Tax} - \text{SS Tax} - \text{Medicare} - \text{401(k)} - \text{Health Insurance}$$

Where:

$$\text{Federal Tax} = \text{Gross} \times \frac{\text{Federal Rate}}{100}$$

$$\text{SS Tax} = \min(\text{Gross},\; \$168{,}600) \times \frac{6.2}{100}$$

$$\text{Medicare Tax} = \text{Gross} \times \frac{1.45}{100}$$

$$\text{Effective Tax Rate} = \frac{\text{Total Taxes}}{\text{Gross Income}} \times 100$$

$$\text{Take-Home \%} = \frac{\text{Net Income}}{\text{Gross Income}} \times 100$$

- **Gross Income** = annual salary or wages before deductions
- **SS Wage Base** = \$168,600 for 2025 (Social Security tax cap)
- **Health Insurance** = monthly premium × 12

*Source: IRS Publication 15-T (2025 Federal Income Tax Withholding Methods); Social Security Administration — 2025 FICA contribution rates and wage base.*

## Worked Example

A software developer earns \$65,000 annually, single filer, living in Virginia (5% state tax), with no local tax, no 401(k), and no employer health deductions.

**Deductions:**
- Federal tax (22%): \$65,000 × 0.22 = **\$14,300**
- State tax (5%): \$65,000 × 0.05 = **\$3,250**
- Social Security (6.2%): \$65,000 × 0.062 = **\$4,030**
- Medicare (1.45%): \$65,000 × 0.0145 = **\$942.50**
- Total deductions: **\$22,522.50**

**Net income:**
- Annual: \$65,000 − \$22,522.50 = **\$42,477.50**
- Monthly: \$42,477.50 / 12 = **\$3,539.79**
- Biweekly: \$42,477.50 / 26 = **\$1,633.75**
- Weekly: \$42,477.50 / 52 = **\$816.87**

**Rates:**
- Effective tax rate: \$22,522.50 / \$65,000 = **34.65%**
- Take-home percentage: \$42,477.50 / \$65,000 = **65.35%**

*Interpretation:* Out of every dollar earned, roughly 65 cents reaches your bank account. The remaining 35 cents goes to federal tax (22¢), state tax (5¢), and FICA (7.65¢). Adding a 6% 401(k) contribution (\$3,900/year) would further reduce take-home to \$38,577.50 — but the retirement savings grow tax-deferred, making the true cost lower than it appears.

## Common Deductions Explained

**Federal income tax** is the largest deduction for most workers. Your effective rate depends on your filing status and taxable income after the standard deduction (\$15,000 single, \$30,000 married in 2025). A \$65,000 single filer's effective rate is roughly 12%–14% using progressive brackets, but many workers estimate using their marginal bracket (22% for income between \$48,475–\$103,350).

**Social Security (FICA)** takes 6.2% of gross income up to the wage base of \$168,600 in 2025. Income above this cap is exempt from Social Security tax. **Medicare** takes 1.45% of all income with no cap — and an additional 0.9% surcharge applies to income above \$200,000 (single) or \$250,000 (married filing jointly).

**State income tax** varies dramatically: 9 states (Texas, Florida, Nevada, Washington, Wyoming, Alaska, Tennessee, South Dakota, New Hampshire) have no state income tax. California tops out at 13.3%, Oregon at 9.9%, and New York at 10.9%. Your state rate significantly affects net income — a \$100,000 earner in Texas keeps \$5,000–\$13,300 more per year than the same earner in California or New York.

**401(k) contributions** are pre-tax, reducing your taxable income. A 6% contribution on \$65,000 saves \$858 in federal tax (at 22% bracket) while costing \$3,900 in cash flow — an effective cost of \$3,042 after the tax benefit.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the difference between gross and net income?

Gross income is your total earnings before any deductions — the salary or wage amount listed on your offer letter or employment contract. Net income is what remains after subtracting federal tax, state tax, local tax, Social Security, Medicare, retirement contributions, and health insurance premiums. On a \$65,000 gross salary with standard deductions, net income is approximately \$42,478 (65.35% of gross).

### How do I find my effective federal tax rate?

Your effective tax rate is total federal tax paid divided by total gross income. It is always lower than your marginal bracket because the U.S. uses progressive taxation — only the income within each bracket is taxed at that bracket's rate. A single filer earning \$65,000 in 2025 has a marginal rate of 22% but an effective rate of roughly 12%–14% after the standard deduction. Check line 24 (total tax) and line 15 (taxable income) of your most recent Form 1040 for your exact effective rate.

### Why is my take-home pay so much less than my salary?

The gap between gross salary and take-home pay surprises many workers. On \$65,000, the combined bite of federal tax (22%), state tax (5%), and FICA (7.65%) alone removes \$22,523 — over a third of your gross pay. Adding a 6% 401(k) (\$3,900) and \$300/month health insurance (\$3,600) pushes total deductions to \$30,023, leaving you with \$34,977 in take-home — only 53.8% of your gross salary.

### How does moving to a no-income-tax state affect my net income?

Moving from a 5% state tax state to a no-income-tax state (Texas, Florida, Nevada, etc.) saves you 5% of your gross income in state taxes. On a \$65,000 salary, that is \$3,250 per year or \$271 per month. On \$100,000, the savings reach \$5,000 per year. However, no-income-tax states often have higher property taxes or sales taxes to compensate, so the net benefit depends on your full financial picture.

### Should I use the effective or marginal tax rate in this calculator?

Use your effective rate for the most accurate net income estimate. Your effective rate reflects the blended average across all brackets and is always lower than your marginal rate. If you enter your marginal rate (e.g., 22% instead of an effective 14%), the calculator will overstate your tax burden and understate your take-home pay. The [Income Tax Calculator](/finance/income-tax-calculator) can help you determine your exact effective rate.
