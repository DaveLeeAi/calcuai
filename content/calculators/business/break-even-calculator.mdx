## What Your Results Mean

Break-even analysis answers the most fundamental question in business: "How much do I need to sell before I stop losing money?" The calculator gives you two critical numbers — break-even **units** and break-even **revenue** — plus the **contribution margin** that determines how fast you reach profitability.

The contribution margin (\$20 per unit in the example above) represents the amount each sale contributes toward covering your fixed costs. Once fixed costs are fully covered, that same \$20 becomes profit. The **contribution margin ratio** (40% in this case) tells you what percentage of each revenue dollar goes toward fixed cost recovery and profit.

If you've set a target profit, the calculator also shows how many units you need to achieve it — think of this as "break-even plus your desired earnings." For a fuller profitability picture, pair this with our [Margin Calculator](/calculators/business/margin-calculator) to see your percentage margins once you're past break-even.

## How to Use This Calculator

1. **Enter Fixed Costs** — your total monthly or annual costs that don't change with sales volume: rent, salaries, insurance premiums, loan payments, software subscriptions. Use the same time period consistently.
2. **Enter Price per Unit** — the amount you charge per unit sold. For services, this is your rate per job or project. For products, this is your selling price.
3. **Enter Variable Cost per Unit** — what it costs to produce or deliver one additional unit: materials, direct labor, shipping, packaging, payment processing fees.
4. **Enter Target Profit (optional)** — if you want to know the sales needed for a specific profit goal, enter it here. Leave at \$0 to see the pure break-even point.
5. **Review the chart** — the revenue vs. cost lines cross at your break-even point. The shaded area above the intersection represents profit; below represents loss.

## The Formula

<div className="formula-section">

**Break-Even in Units:**

$$
\text{Break-Even Units} = \frac{\text{Fixed Costs}}{\text{Price per Unit} - \text{Variable Cost per Unit}}
$$

**Contribution Margin:**

$$
\text{Contribution Margin} = \text{Price per Unit} - \text{Variable Cost per Unit}
$$

**Contribution Margin Ratio:**

$$
\text{CM Ratio} = \frac{\text{Contribution Margin}}{\text{Price per Unit}} \times 100
$$

**Break-Even in Revenue:**

$$
\text{Break-Even Revenue} = \frac{\text{Fixed Costs}}{\text{CM Ratio}}
$$

**Units for Target Profit:**

$$
\text{Units for Profit Target} = \frac{\text{Fixed Costs} + \text{Target Profit}}{\text{Contribution Margin}}
$$

Where:
- **Fixed Costs** = expenses that remain constant regardless of production volume
- **Price per Unit** = selling price per unit
- **Variable Cost per Unit** = cost to produce/deliver one additional unit
- **Contribution Margin** = revenue per unit available to cover fixed costs

*Source: Horngren, Datar & Rajan — "Cost Accounting: A Managerial Emphasis" (Pearson, 16th edition). Standard CVP (cost-volume-profit) analysis framework.*

For a deeper explanation of break-even analysis including multi-product calculations and sensitivity analysis, see our [methodology page on break-even analysis](/methodology/break-even-analysis).

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Coffee Shop — Monthly Break-Even

A coffee shop has \$8,000/month in fixed costs (rent, utilities, salaried staff, insurance). The average drink sells for \$5.50 with \$1.65 in variable costs (beans, milk, cup, sleeve).

**Step 1: Contribution Margin**
- CM = \$5.50 − $1.65 = **$3.85 per drink**
- CM Ratio = \$3.85 / \$5.50 = **70%**

**Step 2: Break-Even Units**
- Break-Even = \$8,000 / \$3.85 = **2,078 drinks per month**
- That's approximately 69 drinks per day (30-day month)

**Step 3: Break-Even Revenue**
- Revenue = 2,078 × $5.50 = **$11,429/month**

*Interpretation:* This coffee shop needs to sell about 69 drinks daily to cover all costs. At a busy location averaging 120 drinks/day, they'd generate 51 extra sales × $3.85 = $196/day in profit, or roughly \$5,900/month.

### Example 2: SaaS Business — Annual Break-Even

A SaaS startup has \$240,000 in annual fixed costs (engineering salaries, cloud hosting, office lease). Monthly subscription is \$49/user. Variable costs per user (support time, API costs, payment processing) are \$7/month or \$84/year.

**Step 1: Contribution Margin (annual per user)**
- CM = \$588 − $84 = **$504 per user/year**
- CM Ratio = \$504 / \$588 = **85.7%**

**Step 2: Break-Even Users**
- Break-Even = \$240,000 / \$504 = **477 paying users**

**Step 3: With \$100,000 Profit Target**
- Units = (\$240,000 + \$100,000) / \$504 = **675 users**

*Interpretation:* The business needs 477 paying subscribers to break even and 675 to generate \$100,000 in annual profit. The 85.7% contribution margin ratio is typical for SaaS — most of the subscription revenue directly covers fixed costs.

### Example 3: Product Manufacturing

A manufacturer has \$50,000/month in fixed costs. They sell widgets at \$25 each with \$15 in variable costs per unit.

**Step 1: Contribution Margin**
- CM = \$25 − $15 = **$10 per widget**
- CM Ratio = \$10 / \$25 = **40%**

**Step 2: Break-Even**
- Break-Even = \$50,000 / \$10 = **5,000 widgets/month**
- Revenue = 5,000 × $25 = **$125,000/month**

**If they raise price to \$28:**
- New CM = \$28 − $15 = $13
- New Break-Even = \$50,000 / \$13 = **3,847 units** (23% fewer units needed)

*Interpretation:* A \$3 price increase (12%) reduces the break-even point by 23% because the entire increase goes straight to contribution margin. This demonstrates why small pricing adjustments have outsized impact on break-even.

## Key Factors That Affect Your Results

1. **Fixed Cost Accuracy** — Underestimating fixed costs is the #1 break-even error. Include ALL recurring expenses: rent, insurance, salaried employees, software, loan payments, equipment leases. A 10% underestimate in a \$10,000 fixed cost moves break-even from 500 to 550 units.
2. **Variable Cost Completeness** — Include payment processing (2.9% + \$0.30 for Stripe/Square), shipping, packaging, returns allowance, and sales commissions. Missing \$2/unit in variable costs on a \$20 contribution margin shifts break-even by 10%.
3. **Pricing Power** — Price increases disproportionately lower break-even because the entire increase flows to contribution margin. A 10% price increase on \$50 adds \$5 to CM, potentially reducing break-even by 20%+.
4. **Volume Assumptions** — Break-even assumes you can actually sell that many units. Validate against market size and your current capacity before committing to a business plan.
5. **Mixed Products** — If you sell multiple products at different prices and margins, use the weighted-average contribution margin across your product mix.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Forgetting Semi-Variable Costs** — Some costs are fixed up to a point, then step up: a warehouse that costs \$5,000/month until you exceed capacity, then requires a second warehouse at \$10,000. Model these as step functions, not smooth lines.
2. **Using Break-Even as the Sales Target** — Break-even means zero profit. Your actual target should be break-even plus desired profit. Always use the "Target Profit" field.
3. **Ignoring Time Period** — Fixed costs of \$10,000/month vs. \$10,000/year produce wildly different break-even points. Ensure all inputs use the same time period (monthly or annually).
4. **Static Analysis for Dynamic Business** — Break-even assumes constant pricing, constant costs, and a single product. Reality involves seasonal pricing, bulk discounts, and changing supplier costs. Recalculate quarterly.
5. **Overlooking Contribution Margin Ratio** — A low CM ratio (below 20%) means you need massive volume to break even. If your CM ratio is below 15%, seriously reconsider your pricing or cost structure before scaling.

## Break-Even by Business Type

Different business models have fundamentally different break-even profiles. Here is how four common business types compare when you plug in realistic numbers:

| Business Type | Monthly Fixed Costs | Price per Unit | Variable Cost | Contribution Margin | Break-Even Units | Break-Even Revenue |
|:--|:-:|:-:|:-:|:-:|:-:|:-:|
| **Coffee shop** | \$8,000 | \$5.50/drink | \$3.00 | \$2.50 | 3,200 drinks/mo | \$17,600 |
| **SaaS product** | \$50,000 | \$30/subscriber/mo | \$3 | \$27 | 1,852 subscribers | \$55,556 |
| **Restaurant** | \$15,000 | \$28/avg check | \$20 | \$8 | 1,875 covers/mo | \$52,500 |
| **E-commerce store** | \$3,000 | \$45/order | \$33 | \$12 | 250 orders/mo | \$11,250 |

**Interpretation:** The coffee shop needs to sell 3,200 drinks per month — roughly 107 per day assuming 30-day operation — to cover its $8,000 in fixed costs. That is achievable for a high-traffic location but out of reach for a low-footfall neighborhood shop. The SaaS business needs 1,852 paying subscribers, which sounds daunting until you note the 90% contribution margin means every new subscriber after breakeven contributes $27 in pure margin. The restaurant must serve 1,875 covers (about 63 per day), which is within range for a 60-seat venue with 1.5 table turns per day. The e-commerce store has the lowest break-even threshold at just 250 orders per month — roughly 8 per day — because it carries only $3,000 in fixed costs (warehousing, software, hosting). The key insight: businesses with high contribution margins (SaaS, consulting) tolerate higher fixed costs, while low-margin businesses (restaurants, retail) must keep fixed costs minimal or drive very high volume.

## After Break-Even: Putting the Number to Work

Knowing your break-even point is step one. The real value comes from using it as a decision-making tool across your business.

**Pricing decisions.** Before changing your price, run the new number through this calculator. A 10% price increase on a $50 product adds $5 to your contribution margin — but if the increase causes 15% of customers to leave, you need to check whether the higher margin per unit compensates for the lost volume. Model both scenarios: higher price × lower volume versus current price × current volume.

**Hiring decisions.** Every new full-time hire adds $4,000-$8,000/month in fixed costs (salary, benefits, taxes, equipment). On a $20 contribution margin, a $6,000/month hire means you need 300 additional units sold per month just to cover that person. If the hire is a salesperson expected to close 500 units/month, the math works. If it's an operations role that doesn't directly generate revenue, the break-even point rises for every other unit you sell.

**Expansion decisions.** Opening a second location doubles (or more) your fixed costs while your contribution margin per unit stays the same. If your current location breaks even at 500 units and a second location adds $12,000/month in fixed costs, you need 600 additional units from the new location before it contributes any profit. Map this against realistic demand estimates before signing a lease.

**Seasonal planning.** If your business is seasonal, calculate break-even by month. A landscaping company with $8,000/month in fixed costs but revenue concentrated in April-October must cover 12 months of overhead in 7 months of active selling — effectively raising the monthly break-even by 71%.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- Assumes a single product at a uniform price (for multi-product businesses, use weighted-average contribution margin)
- All costs are perfectly classified as either fixed or variable (in reality, some costs are semi-variable)
- Fixed costs remain constant across all production levels (no step costs)
- No quantity discounts on variable costs (bulk purchasing can lower per-unit costs at higher volumes)
- Selling price is constant (no discounting or price tiers)
- All units produced are sold (no inventory buildup)
- Does not account for taxes or time value of money

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is a good contribution margin ratio?

A good contribution margin ratio depends on your industry, but generally 40% or higher is considered healthy for product businesses. SaaS companies typically achieve 80–90%, service businesses 50–70%, and retail/manufacturing 20–40%. If your contribution margin ratio falls below 20%, your business requires very high volume to cover fixed costs — a sign that pricing or cost structure needs revisiting before scaling.

### How often should I recalculate my break-even point?

Recalculate your break-even point at least quarterly, or immediately after any significant change to fixed costs, pricing, or variable costs. A new hire adding \$5,000/month in salary, a supplier price increase of \$2/unit, or a 5% price adjustment all shift break-even materially. Businesses that track break-even monthly catch margin erosion 3–4 months earlier on average than those who check annually.

### How does break-even differ for multi-product businesses?

Multi-product businesses must use a weighted-average contribution margin based on their sales mix. If Product A has a \$30 CM and represents 60% of sales, and Product B has a \$10 CM at 40% of sales, the blended CM is (\$30 × 0.60) + (\$10 × 0.40) = \$22. Break-even is then calculated using this blended figure. The key risk is that shifts in product mix change the blended CM — selling more low-margin products raises your break-even point even if total volume increases.

### What is the difference between break-even analysis and profit margin?

Break-even analysis tells you the minimum sales volume needed to avoid a loss, while profit margin tells you what percentage of revenue becomes profit at your current sales level. Break-even is forward-looking (a planning tool), and profit margin is backward-looking (a performance metric). A business can have a healthy 25% profit margin today but still need break-even analysis to evaluate whether a new product line, location, or hire will be profitable.

### Should I use monthly or annual fixed costs in break-even analysis?

Use whichever time period matches your decision-making cycle, but stay consistent across all inputs. Monthly break-even is more actionable for day-to-day operations — knowing you need 500 units this month is more useful than knowing you need 6,000 this year. Annual break-even is better for strategic planning and investor presentations. The break-even units will differ (monthly fixed costs produce a monthly unit target), but the underlying relationship is identical.
