## What This Calculator Shows

This calculator converts your annual salary into every common pay frequency so you can compare job offers, evaluate freelance rates, or understand what your time is actually worth. The primary output is your **base hourly rate** — your salary divided by total annual work hours. The **adjusted hourly rate** accounts for paid time off: since you receive your full salary while taking holidays and vacation, the hours you actually work are fewer, making each worked hour worth more.

The pay period breakdowns (monthly, biweekly, weekly, and daily) let you match your salary against bills, budgets, and side-income opportunities. For example, knowing your daily rate is $200 helps you quickly evaluate whether a freelance gig at $350/day is worth your evening hours.

If you need to go the other direction — converting an hourly rate to a salary — use our [Salary Calculator](/finance/salary-calculator). For a complete view of your take-home pay after taxes and deductions, see the [Paycheck Calculator](/finance/paycheck-calculator).

## How to Use This Calculator

1. **Enter your Annual Salary** — your gross (before-tax) yearly compensation. If your offer letter shows a monthly amount, multiply by 12.
2. **Set Hours per Week** — the standard is 40 for full-time positions. Enter your actual scheduled hours if you work part-time or an alternative schedule.
3. **Set Weeks per Year** — leave at 52 for standard employment. Some contract roles use 48 or 50 weeks.
4. **Enter Paid Holidays** — the number of company-paid holidays per year. The U.S. average for private industry is 8 paid holidays (BLS, 2024).
5. **Enter Paid Vacation Days** — your annual PTO/vacation day allotment. The U.S. average after 1 year of service is 11 days (BLS, 2024).
6. **Review your results** — the hourly rate, adjusted rate, and all pay period breakdowns appear instantly.

## The Formula

<div className="formula-section">

**Base Hourly Rate:**

$$
\text{Hourly Rate} = \frac{\text{Annual Salary}}{\text{Hours per Week} \times \text{Weeks per Year}}
$$

**Adjusted Hourly Rate (with PTO):**

$$
\text{Adjusted Rate} = \frac{\text{Annual Salary}}{\text{Hours per Week} \times \left(\text{Weeks per Year} - \frac{\text{Paid Holidays} + \text{Vacation Days}}{5}\right)}
$$

**Pay Period Breakdowns:**

$$
\text{Monthly} = \frac{\text{Annual Salary}}{12} \quad \text{Biweekly} = \frac{\text{Annual Salary}}{26} \quad \text{Weekly} = \frac{\text{Annual Salary}}{52} \quad \text{Daily} = \frac{\text{Weekly}}{5}
$$

Where:
- **Annual Salary** = gross yearly compensation before taxes
- **Hours per Week** = scheduled work hours per week (standard: 40)
- **Weeks per Year** = work weeks per year (standard: 52)
- **Paid Holidays + Vacation Days** = total paid days off, converted to weeks by dividing by 5

*Source: Bureau of Labor Statistics — the standard full-time work year is 2,080 hours (40 hours x 52 weeks), used as the baseline for federal pay rate calculations.*

## Worked Example

A marketing manager earns $65,000 per year, works 40 hours per week, and receives 10 paid holidays plus 15 vacation days.

**Base Hourly Rate:**
- Total work hours: 40 x 52 = 2,080 hours
- $65,000 / 2,080 = **$31.25/hour**

**Adjusted Hourly Rate:**
- PTO days: 10 + 15 = 25 days = 5 weeks
- Adjusted weeks: 52 - 5 = 47 weeks
- Adjusted hours: 40 x 47 = 1,880 hours
- $65,000 / 1,880 = **$34.57/hour**

**Pay Period Breakdowns:**
- Monthly: $65,000 / 12 = **$5,416.67**
- Biweekly: $65,000 / 26 = **$2,500.00**
- Weekly: $65,000 / 52 = **$1,250.00**
- Daily: $1,250 / 5 = **$250.00**

*Interpretation:* The base rate of $31.25/hour is what employers quote, but the adjusted rate of $34.57/hour reflects the true value of each hour actually worked. That $3.32/hour difference — a 10.6% premium — represents the hidden value of PTO benefits. When comparing a salaried position to freelance or contract work, use the adjusted rate as your benchmark.

## Understanding Your Actual Hourly Value

The gap between your base hourly rate and adjusted hourly rate reveals the hidden monetary value of your PTO benefits. A $60,000 salary with zero PTO equals $28.85/hour across 2,080 hours. Add 10 holidays and 15 vacation days (the U.S. median benefits package), and the same salary yields an adjusted rate of $31.91/hour across 1,880 actual work hours — a $3.06/hour premium, or roughly $5,753 in equivalent value annually.

This matters most when comparing job offers. A $65,000 salary with 25 PTO days ($34.57/hour adjusted) is more valuable per hour worked than a $68,000 salary with only 5 PTO days ($33.33/hour adjusted), even though the second offer has a higher nominal salary. Contract and freelance workers who receive no paid time off should target the adjusted rate — not the base rate — to maintain equivalent compensation.

The calculation also helps you evaluate overtime and side work. If your adjusted hourly rate is $34.57, any side project paying less than that amount represents a pay cut relative to your primary job, while anything above it is genuinely incremental income.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How many work hours are in a year?

A standard full-time work year contains 2,080 hours, calculated as 40 hours per week multiplied by 52 weeks. This is the baseline used by the U.S. Office of Personnel Management and the Bureau of Labor Statistics for converting between annual salaries and hourly rates. Actual hours worked are lower after accounting for paid holidays (average 8 days) and vacation time (average 11 days after one year), which reduces the total to roughly 1,928 hours for a typical private-sector employee.

### What is a good hourly rate for my salary?

Whether your hourly rate is "good" depends on your industry, location, and experience level. As a benchmark, the median hourly wage for all U.S. workers was $23.11 in 2024 (BLS). A $50,000 salary equates to $24.04/hour, slightly above the national median. In high-cost cities like San Francisco or New York, the living wage for a single adult exceeds $25/hour, so a $52,000 salary ($25/hour) would be considered entry-level in those markets but comfortable in lower-cost regions.

### Why is my adjusted hourly rate higher than my base rate?

Your adjusted hourly rate is higher because you earn the same annual salary while working fewer hours. Paid holidays and vacation days reduce your total work hours without reducing your pay. If you earn $52,000 with 20 PTO days, your base rate is $25.00/hour over 2,080 hours, but your adjusted rate is $27.08/hour over 1,920 hours. The salary stays the same — only the denominator changes.

### How do I convert salary to hourly for part-time work?

Use the same formula but change the hours per week to match your actual schedule. A part-time worker earning $26,000/year at 20 hours per week has an hourly rate of $25.00 ($26,000 / 1,040 hours), which is the same rate as a full-time worker earning $52,000. The key variable is hours per week, not weeks per year — most part-time salaried positions still operate across 52 weeks.

### Should I use the base rate or adjusted rate when comparing to freelance work?

Use the adjusted rate. Freelancers and independent contractors do not receive paid holidays, vacation, or employer-provided benefits like health insurance and retirement contributions. Your adjusted hourly rate accounts for PTO, but you should add another 20-30% on top to cover self-employment tax (15.3%), health insurance premiums, and the lack of employer 401(k) matching. A salaried worker earning $34.57/hour adjusted should target at least $42-$45/hour as a freelancer to maintain equivalent total compensation.

## Methodology & Sources

- **Bureau of Labor Statistics** — Employee Benefits Survey: average paid holidays (8 days) and vacation days (11 days after 1 year) for private industry workers (2024)
- **U.S. Office of Personnel Management** — Standard 2,080-hour work year used as the federal baseline for hourly rate conversions
- **Bureau of Labor Statistics** — Median hourly wage data from the Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics (OEWS) program (2024)

The calculator divides the annual salary by total annual work hours (hours per week multiplied by weeks per year) for the base rate. The adjusted rate subtracts PTO days — converted to weeks by dividing by 5 work days per week — from the weeks-per-year figure before calculating. Pay period breakdowns use standard divisors: 12 for monthly, 26 for biweekly, 52 for weekly, and 260 for daily (52 weeks x 5 days).
