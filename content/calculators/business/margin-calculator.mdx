## What Your Results Mean

Margin analysis reveals the fundamental profitability of your business at two levels. **Gross margin** measures production efficiency — how much profit remains after direct costs. A 40% gross margin means your product or service costs \$0.60 to deliver for every \$1.00 in revenue. **Net margin** goes further, subtracting operating expenses (rent, payroll, marketing) to show your true bottom line.

The calculator also shows **markup percentage**, which many confuse with margin. A 40% gross margin translates to a 66.67% markup — the two metrics express the same relationship from opposite directions. If you need to calculate markup pricing instead, use our [Markup Calculator](/calculators/business/markup-calculator) for cost-plus pricing scenarios.

The revenue comparison table projects how your margins shift at different revenue levels while holding costs constant, helping you model growth scenarios and identify operating leverage.

## How to Use This Calculator

1. **Enter your Revenue** — your total sales or income for the period you're analyzing (monthly, quarterly, or annually). Use gross revenue before any deductions.
2. **Enter Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)** — the direct costs to produce your goods or deliver your service. For a product business, this includes materials, manufacturing labor, and shipping. For a service business, this is billable labor and direct project costs.
3. **Enter Operating Expenses** — your overhead: rent, salaries (non-production), marketing spend, software subscriptions, insurance, and utilities. Leave at 0 to focus on gross margin only.
4. **Read your results** — the calculator instantly shows gross margin %, net margin %, markup %, and a pie chart breaking down where each revenue dollar goes.

## The Formula

<div className="formula-section">

The three core profitability formulas:

**Gross Margin:**

$$
\text{Gross Margin \%} = \frac{\text{Revenue} - \text{COGS}}{\text{Revenue}} \times 100
$$

**Net Margin:**

$$
\text{Net Margin \%} = \frac{\text{Revenue} - \text{COGS} - \text{Operating Expenses}}{\text{Revenue}} \times 100
$$

**Markup:**

$$
\text{Markup \%} = \frac{\text{Revenue} - \text{COGS}}{\text{COGS}} \times 100
$$

Where:
- **Revenue** = total sales or income
- **COGS** = cost of goods sold (direct costs)
- **Operating Expenses** = indirect costs (rent, admin, marketing)
- **Gross Profit** = Revenue − COGS
- **Net Profit** = Revenue − COGS − Operating Expenses

*Source: Corporate Finance Institute (CFI) — "Profit Margin" definitions and formulas (2024).*

For a deeper explanation of how contribution margin drives break-even analysis, see our [methodology page on break-even analysis](/methodology/break-even-analysis).

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: E-commerce Product Business

A seller generates \$150,000 in annual revenue. Product sourcing and shipping costs total \$82,500. Overhead (warehouse, software, marketing) is \$30,000.

**Gross Margin:**
- Gross Profit = \$150,000 − $82,500 = **$67,500**
- Gross Margin % = \$67,500 / \$150,000 × 100 = **45%**

**Markup:**
- Markup % = \$67,500 / \$82,500 × 100 = **81.82%**

**Net Margin:**
- Net Profit = \$150,000 − \$82,500 − $30,000 = **$37,500**
- Net Margin % = \$37,500 / \$150,000 × 100 = **25%**

*Interpretation:* This business retains 45 cents of each revenue dollar after direct costs, and 25 cents after all expenses. The 81.82% markup means products are priced at nearly double their cost — healthy for e-commerce. In practical terms, a 45% gross margin means that for every $100 in revenue, $55 goes to producing and shipping the product and $45 remains to cover overhead and generate profit. After the $30,000 in operating expenses, the owner keeps $37,500 — a 25% net margin that outperforms the typical e-commerce net margin of 10–15% (Shopify benchmark data, 2024). If this business scales to $300,000 in revenue while holding operating expenses at $30,000, the net margin jumps to 35% ($105,000 net profit) — a demonstration of operating leverage.

### Example 2: Freelance Consultant

A consultant bills \$200,000/year. Direct costs (subcontractors, tools) are \$20,000. Operating expenses (office, insurance, software, marketing) are \$35,000.

**Gross Margin:**
- Gross Profit = \$200,000 − $20,000 = **$180,000**
- Gross Margin % = \$180,000 / \$200,000 × 100 = **90%**

**Net Margin:**
- Net Profit = \$200,000 − \$20,000 − $35,000 = **$145,000**
- Net Margin % = \$145,000 / \$200,000 × 100 = **72.5%**

*Interpretation:* Service businesses typically have high gross margins because they sell time, not physical goods. A 72.5% net margin is exceptional and reflects low overhead.

### Example 3: Restaurant

A restaurant earns \$500,000/year. Food and beverage costs (COGS) are \$175,000 (35%). Operating expenses (staff, rent, utilities) are \$250,000.

**Gross Margin:**
- Gross Margin % = (\$500,000 − \$175,000) / \$500,000 × 100 = **65%**

**Net Margin:**
- Net Profit = \$500,000 − \$175,000 − $250,000 = **$75,000**
- Net Margin % = \$75,000 / \$500,000 × 100 = **15%**

*Interpretation:* A 65% gross margin looks strong, but after heavy operating costs, the net margin is 15% — close to the restaurant industry average of 3–9% for full-service and 6–9% for fast-casual (National Restaurant Association, 2024). This restaurant is performing above average.

## Key Factors That Affect Your Results

1. **COGS Accuracy** — The most common error is understating COGS by excluding shipping, packaging, payment processing fees, or platform commissions. Include every cost directly tied to delivering your product.
2. **Revenue Recognition** — Use net revenue (after returns and refunds), not gross bookings. A \$100,000 gross with 8% returns means your actual revenue is \$92,000.
3. **Fixed vs. Variable Cost Classification** — Operating expenses are typically fixed (rent doesn't change with sales), while COGS scales with volume. Misclassifying variable costs as fixed inflates gross margin.
4. **Industry Benchmarks** — A "good" margin varies dramatically by industry. Here are gross and net margin ranges by sector based on NYU Stern (Damodaran, January 2024) and industry association data: grocery retail averages 1–3% net margin on 25–28% gross; clothing and apparel retail runs 4–13% net on 50–55% gross; software and SaaS companies achieve 70–85% gross and 20–30% net; restaurants average 3–9% net on 60–65% gross (National Restaurant Association, 2024); professional consulting earns 15–25% net on 30–50% gross; and construction companies typically operate at 5–10% net on 15–25% gross. Comparing your margin to the wrong industry leads to false confidence or unnecessary panic.
5. **Scale Effects** — As revenue increases with fixed costs held constant, net margin expands. The comparison table below your results shows this operating leverage effect.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Confusing Margin and Markup** — A 50% margin means you keep \$0.50 per \$1.00 of revenue. A 50% markup means you charge \$1.50 for something that costs \$1.00 (which is a 33.3% margin). They are not the same.
2. **Ignoring COGS Entirely** — Service businesses often skip COGS calculation, reporting 100% gross margin. If you use subcontractors, project-specific tools, or any direct delivery cost, that's COGS.
3. **Comparing Net Margins Across Industries** — A 5% net margin is excellent for grocery retail but poor for software. Always benchmark within your industry.
4. **Using Gross Revenue Instead of Net** — Include returns, refunds, and chargebacks in your revenue figure. An 8% return rate drops a 40% gross margin to ~36.8%.
5. **Forgetting Owner Salary** — Solo business owners often exclude their own salary from operating expenses, inflating net margin artificially.

## Spotting Margin Compression Before It Becomes a Crisis

A healthy margin today tells you nothing about the trend. Margin compression — the gradual erosion of profitability — often goes undetected for months because revenue keeps growing while margins silently shrink.

**The early warning signs:**
- **Revenue up, gross profit flat.** If revenue grew 15% this quarter but gross profit grew only 5%, your COGS is rising faster than your prices. This is the earliest signal and the easiest to miss when you only watch the top line.
- **Gross margin stable, net margin falling.** Your product economics are fine, but overhead is creeping up — usually from headcount growth, software subscriptions, or rent increases that feel small individually but compound monthly.
- **Discounting to maintain volume.** If your average selling price dropped 5% over 6 months to keep customers, your margin compressed by 5 percentage points. On a 40% gross margin, that's a 12.5% reduction in profit per unit.
- **Supplier cost increases you haven't passed through.** Raw material or shipping costs rose, but you held prices to avoid losing customers. This is the most common margin compression cause in product businesses.

**The fix is always one of three levers:** raise prices, reduce COGS, or cut operating expenses. Enter the adjusted revenue or cost figure into this calculator and see exactly how each lever affects your net margin. A $2/unit COGS reduction on 10,000 monthly units drops straight to the bottom line as $20,000/month in recovered margin.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- Assumes all revenue and costs occur in the same period (no accrual timing differences)
- Does not account for taxes, interest, or depreciation (use EBITDA analysis for those)
- COGS should be direct costs only — indirect costs belong in operating expenses
- The revenue comparison table assumes COGS and operating expenses remain constant across revenue levels (which oversimplifies real-world scaling)
- Currency-based: all inputs assume a single currency denomination
- Does not model seasonal fluctuations or one-time expenses
- Markup calculation requires non-zero COGS (returns 0% if COGS is zero)

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is a good profit margin by industry?

Average net profit margins vary widely by industry. Software and SaaS companies average 20–30% net margin. Professional services (consulting, legal, accounting) average 15–25%. Retail averages 2–5%. Restaurants average 3–9% for full-service and 6–9% for fast-casual (National Restaurant Association, 2024). Manufacturing averages 5–10%. A margin significantly above your industry average signals competitive advantage; significantly below signals pricing or cost problems.

### How does margin change as revenue grows?

Gross margin stays relatively flat as revenue grows because COGS typically scales proportionally with sales. Net margin, however, tends to improve because fixed operating expenses (rent, salaries, insurance) are spread across more revenue — this is called operating leverage. A business with \$50,000 in fixed operating costs sees net margin jump from 10% at \$200,000 revenue to 17.5% at \$400,000 revenue, assuming the same 40% gross margin. This is why high-growth companies prioritize revenue scale.

### What is the difference between margin and markup?

Margin and markup both measure profit but use different denominators. Margin divides profit by revenue (selling price), while markup divides profit by cost. A product bought for \$60 and sold for \$100 has a 40% margin (\$40/\$100) but a 66.7% markup (\$40/\$60). The distinction matters for pricing: a "50% margin" means you keep half the selling price, but a "50% markup" means you only keep one-third (33.3% margin). Confusing the two can lead to underpricing by 17 or more percentage points.

### Why is my gross margin high but net margin low?

A high gross margin with a low net margin means your operating expenses are consuming most of your gross profit. This is common in businesses with heavy overhead: restaurants (65% gross, 5% net), retail stores with high rent, or startups spending aggressively on marketing and headcount. Calculate your operating expense ratio (operating expenses / revenue) — if it exceeds 80% of your gross margin, overhead is the bottleneck. Reducing operating expenses by just 5 percentage points can double a thin net margin.

### How often should I track my profit margins?

Track gross margin monthly and net margin quarterly at minimum. Monthly gross margin monitoring catches cost creep early — a 2% drop in gross margin over 3 months can signal supplier price increases, product mix shifts, or rising shipping costs before they become a crisis. Quarterly net margin reviews capture seasonal patterns and operating expense trends. Businesses that monitor margins monthly are 30% more likely to catch profitability issues before they require drastic action (SBA, 2023).
