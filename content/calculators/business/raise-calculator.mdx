## What Your Results Show

This calculator converts a percentage raise into real dollar amounts across every pay period. The key outputs are your **new annual salary**, the **dollar amount of the raise**, and breakdowns by month, biweekly period, week, and hour. The **monthly difference** and **biweekly difference** fields show exactly how much more each paycheck will be.

The pay frequency selector lets you enter your current pay in whatever format you know it — hourly rate, biweekly paycheck amount, monthly salary, or annual. The calculator converts to annual first, applies the raise, then breaks the new salary back down. This is especially useful if you're paid hourly and want to see what a percentage raise means in annual terms: a \$1.00/hour raise on a \$20/hour wage adds \$2,080/year (\$20 × 0.05 × 2,080 hours for a 5% raise).

For a full breakdown of your take-home pay after taxes, use the [Payroll Calculator](/business/payroll-calculator). To convert between salary and hourly, see the [Salary Calculator](/finance/salary-calculator).

## How to Use This Calculator

1. **Enter your current salary** — your base pay before the raise. Enter the amount in whatever format you have: annual salary, monthly pay, biweekly paycheck, weekly pay, or hourly rate.
2. **Enter the raise percentage** — the percentage increase you've been offered or are negotiating. Common ranges: 2–3% for cost-of-living adjustments, 3–5% for merit raises, 5–10% for promotions, and 10%+ for role changes or competitive offers.
3. **Select your pay frequency** — choose how the salary amount you entered is expressed. The calculator converts everything to annual before applying the raise.
4. **Review your results** — your new salary appears with breakdowns by every pay period, plus the dollar increase per month and per biweekly paycheck.

## The Formula

<div className="formula-section">

**Annual Salary Conversion (if not already annual):**

$$
\text{Annual} = \text{Input} \times \text{Periods per Year}
$$

Where periods per year = 12 (monthly), 26 (biweekly), 52 (weekly), or 2,080 (hourly).

**New Salary After Raise:**

$$
\text{New Salary} = \text{Current Annual Salary} \times \left(1 + \frac{\text{Raise \%}}{100}\right)
$$

**Raise Amount:**

$$
\text{Raise Amount} = \text{New Salary} - \text{Current Annual Salary}
$$

**Pay Period Breakdowns:**

$$
\text{Monthly} = \frac{\text{Annual}}{12} \quad \text{Biweekly} = \frac{\text{Annual}}{26} \quad \text{Weekly} = \frac{\text{Annual}}{52} \quad \text{Hourly} = \frac{\text{Annual}}{2{,}080}
$$

Where:
- **Current Annual Salary** = your base pay converted to annual terms (\$)
- **Raise %** = the percentage increase offered
- **2,080** = standard full-time work hours per year (40 hours × 52 weeks)
- **26** = biweekly pay periods per year (52 weeks ÷ 2)

*Source: Bureau of Labor Statistics — Employment Cost Index (2024). Standard payroll conversion factors per the American Payroll Association.*

## Worked Example

### 3% Cost-of-Living Adjustment on \$65,000

A marketing manager earning \$65,000/year receives a 3% cost-of-living adjustment (COLA).

**Step 1 — Calculate the raise amount:**
- \$65,000 × 3% = **\$1,950 annual raise**

**Step 2 — New annual salary:**
- \$65,000 + \$1,950 = **\$66,950**

**Step 3 — New pay period breakdowns:**
- Monthly: \$66,950 / 12 = **\$5,579.17**
- Biweekly: \$66,950 / 26 = **\$2,575.00**
- Weekly: \$66,950 / 52 = **\$1,287.50**
- Hourly: \$66,950 / 2,080 = **\$32.19**

**Step 4 — Per-paycheck increase:**
- Monthly increase: \$5,579.17 − \$5,416.67 = **\$162.50 more per month**
- Biweekly increase: \$2,575.00 − \$2,500.00 = **\$75.00 more per paycheck**

*Interpretation:* A 3% COLA adds \$162.50/month before taxes. With the Consumer Price Index running around 3.0% in 2024, this raise roughly keeps purchasing power flat — it offsets inflation rather than providing real income growth. To actually get ahead, you'd need a raise above the inflation rate.

## Raise vs. Inflation

A raise only increases your real purchasing power if it exceeds the inflation rate. In 2024, the Consumer Price Index (CPI) rose approximately 3.0%. A 3% raise keeps you even; a 5% raise gives you about 2% real income growth. Over 10 years, the compounding difference is significant: a worker starting at \$50,000 with consistent 5% annual raises reaches \$81,445, while 3% raises reach only \$67,196 — a \$14,249 gap from just 2 percentage points of difference compounded annually.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is a good raise percentage?

A good raise depends on context: 2–3% is a standard cost-of-living adjustment that matches inflation, 3–5% represents a solid merit increase for strong performance, 5–10% typically accompanies a promotion or expanded responsibilities, and 10–20% is common when switching employers or filling a high-demand role. The BLS Employment Cost Index shows the national average across all industries was approximately 4.1% in 2024.

### How do I convert a raise percentage to a dollar amount?

Multiply your current annual salary by the raise percentage as a decimal. For a 5% raise on \$60,000: \$60,000 × 0.05 = \$3,000 per year, or \$250 per month, or \$115.38 per biweekly paycheck. This calculator does the conversion automatically for any salary and percentage.

### Is my raise before or after taxes?

Raises are always quoted as gross (before-tax) amounts. Your actual take-home increase will be lower depending on your tax bracket. A \$2,500 annual raise for someone in the 22% federal bracket plus 5% state tax means roughly \$2,500 × (1 − 0.27) = \$1,825 in additional take-home pay, or about \$152/month.

### How many hours per year is full-time?

The standard full-time work year is 2,080 hours (40 hours per week × 52 weeks). This is the conversion factor used by the Bureau of Labor Statistics and most payroll systems to convert between hourly and annual pay. Some organizations use 1,950 or 2,000 hours to account for paid time off, but 2,080 is the most common standard.

### Should I negotiate my raise or accept the first offer?

Research from Salary.com shows that approximately 70% of managers expect salary negotiation and have budget room beyond the initial offer. Prepare by knowing the market rate for your role (check BLS, Glassdoor, or Levels.fyi), documenting your contributions, and being specific about the percentage or dollar amount you're requesting. Even a 1% improvement on a \$70,000 salary adds \$700/year — compounded over a career, that adds up to tens of thousands of dollars.

## Sources & Methodology

- **Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS)** — Employment Cost Index (ECI), Q4 2024. Tracks changes in labor costs including wages, salaries, and benefits across all civilian workers.
- **American Payroll Association** — Standard payroll conversion factors: 12 months, 26 biweekly periods, 52 weeks, and 2,080 work hours per year.
- **Bureau of Labor Statistics** — Consumer Price Index (CPI-U), 2024 annual average for inflation comparison.

This calculator applies the raise percentage to the annualized salary, then divides by standard pay period counts. All conversions use the payroll industry standard of 2,080 hours per year for hourly-to-annual conversion and 26 periods for biweekly pay. Results are gross (pre-tax) amounts.
