## What Your Results Mean

**Operating Cash Flow (OCF)** is the cash generated by your core business operations. Unlike net income, OCF adds back non-cash charges like depreciation and adjusts for changes in working capital. A company can report positive net income while running out of cash — OCF reveals the truth. Positive and growing OCF indicates a business that funds itself without relying on external financing.

**Free Cash Flow (FCF)** subtracts capital costs from operating cash flow. FCF represents the cash available to pay dividends, reduce debt, buy back shares, or reinvest in growth. Warren Buffett calls free cash flow "owner earnings" because it is the cash an owner can extract without impairing the business. Negative FCF is not necessarily bad — growth companies often invest heavily in CapEx — but sustained negative FCF requires external funding.

**Cash Flow Margin** measures operating cash flow as a percentage of revenue. It answers the question: "For every dollar of revenue, how many cents convert to actual cash?" A 15-20% cash flow margin is considered strong for most industries. Companies with margins below 5% are converting very little revenue into cash, regardless of their reported profit margin.

**Net Cash Position** shows your ending cash balance after all operating, investing, and financing activities. It is the number that tells you whether your bank account grew or shrank during the period.

## How to Use This Calculator

1. **Enter annual revenue** — Your total revenue for the period. This is used to calculate cash flow margin.
2. **Enter net income** — The bottom line from your income statement. This can be negative if you operated at a loss.
3. **Enter depreciation and amortization** — These non-cash expenses are added back because they reduce reported income without consuming cash.
4. **Enter the change in working capital** — A positive number means working capital increased (cash outflow). A negative number means working capital decreased (cash inflow). If accounts receivable grew by \$10,000 and accounts payable grew by \$3,000, the change is +\$7,000.
5. **Enter capital costs** — Annual spending on property, equipment, vehicles, or other long-term assets.
6. **Enter financing activities** — Net of new debt raised, debt repaid, and dividends paid. Positive if you raised more than you repaid.
7. **Enter beginning cash** — Your cash balance at the start of the period.
8. **Read your results** — Focus on free cash flow first. If FCF is negative, examine whether it is from heavy CapEx investment (often acceptable) or weak operating cash flow (a warning sign).

## The Formula

<div className="formula-section">

**Operating Cash Flow:**

$$
\text{OCF} = \text{Net Income} + \text{Depreciation} - \text{Change in Working Capital}
$$

**Free Cash Flow:**

$$
\text{FCF} = \text{OCF} - \text{Capital Costs}
$$

**Cash Flow Margin:**

$$
\text{Cash Flow Margin} = \frac{\text{OCF}}{\text{Revenue}} \times 100
$$

**Net Cash Position:**

$$
\text{Net Cash} = \text{Beginning Cash} + \text{OCF} - \text{CapEx} + \text{Financing Activities}
$$

Where:
- **Net Income** = revenue minus all expenses, taxes, and interest
- **Depreciation** = non-cash expense for asset wear and obsolescence
- **Change in Working Capital** = increase (positive) or decrease (negative) in current assets minus current liabilities
- **Capital Costs (CapEx)** = purchases of long-term assets
- **Financing Activities** = net cash from borrowing, repaying debt, and paying dividends

*Source: CFA Institute — "Financial Reporting and Analysis" (CFA Program Curriculum). The indirect method of calculating operating cash flow is the standard approach used in US GAAP financial reporting.*

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Profitable Small Business

A landscaping company reports \$500,000 in revenue, \$60,000 in net income, \$12,000 in depreciation on equipment, and a \$3,000 increase in working capital (due to growing accounts receivable). It purchased a \$18,000 truck this year and made no financing changes. Beginning cash was \$35,000.

**Operating Cash Flow = \$60,000 + \$12,000 - \$3,000 = \$69,000**

- Free Cash Flow = \$69,000 - \$18,000 = **\$51,000**
- Cash Flow Margin = (\$69,000 / \$500,000) x 100 = **13.8%**
- Net Cash Position = \$35,000 + \$69,000 - \$18,000 + \$0 = **\$86,000**

*Interpretation:* Despite modest net income of 12% of revenue, this business generates strong cash flow. The \$51,000 in free cash flow means the owner could take distributions, build reserves, or invest in a second truck without borrowing.

### Example 2: Growth-Stage SaaS Company

A software company reports \$2,000,000 in revenue, \$200,000 net loss (investing in growth), \$80,000 in depreciation, and a \$50,000 decrease in working capital (customers prepaying annual subscriptions). It spent \$300,000 on servers and infrastructure, raised \$500,000 in venture debt, and started the year with \$150,000 in cash.

**Operating Cash Flow = -\$200,000 + \$80,000 - (-\$50,000) = -\$70,000**

- Free Cash Flow = -\$70,000 - \$300,000 = **-\$370,000**
- Cash Flow Margin = (-\$70,000 / \$2,000,000) x 100 = **-3.5%**
- Net Cash Position = \$150,000 + (-\$70,000) - \$300,000 + \$500,000 = **\$280,000**

*Interpretation:* Negative free cash flow is expected for a growth-stage company investing heavily in infrastructure. The venture debt keeps the net cash position healthy at \$280,000, but the company must reach positive OCF before the debt comes due. At -3.5% cash flow margin, the operating model is close to breakeven — a 4% improvement in margin would flip OCF positive.

## Key Factors That Affect Your Results

1. **Depreciation Is Not Discretionary, But It Is Non-Cash** — Depreciation is determined by accounting policy, not cash movement. Two identical businesses using different depreciation methods (straight-line vs. accelerated) will report different net incomes but the same cash flow. Always look at OCF, not just net income.

2. **Working Capital Swings Create Cash Flow Volatility** — A manufacturer that extends 90-day payment terms to win a large contract may report record revenue while experiencing a cash crunch. A \$100,000 increase in accounts receivable means \$100,000 of revenue that has not converted to cash yet. Seasonal businesses routinely see 20-40% swings in working capital between quarters.

3. **CapEx Timing Creates Lumpy Free Cash Flow** — A restaurant investing \$200,000 in a kitchen renovation has negative FCF that year but may not need significant CapEx for 5-7 years afterward. Evaluate FCF over a 3-5 year average, not a single period.

4. **Financing Activities Mask Operating Problems** — A business can maintain a healthy cash balance while hemorrhaging cash operationally if it keeps raising debt or equity. Separate operating performance (OCF) from financial engineering (financing activities) when evaluating health.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- Uses the indirect method of calculating OCF, which starts from net income and adjusts for non-cash items and working capital changes
- Assumes all inputs reflect a consistent time period (annual, quarterly, or monthly — do not mix periods)
- Does not account for one-time or non-recurring items that distort net income (e.g., asset sales, legal settlements)
- Working capital change is entered as a single net figure — for more precision, track each component (receivables, payables, inventory) separately
- Does not discount future cash flows to present value — all figures are nominal

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is a good cash flow margin?

A cash flow margin of 15-25% is considered strong for most industries. Software companies and SaaS businesses often achieve 25-35% margins due to low variable costs. Manufacturing and retail typically range from 5-15%. A negative cash flow margin sustained over multiple periods signals that the business model is not converting revenue to cash efficiently, regardless of reported profitability.

### What is the difference between operating cash flow and free cash flow?

Operating cash flow measures the cash generated by day-to-day business operations, while free cash flow subtracts capital costs from OCF. The difference matters because a business must invest in equipment, property, and technology to maintain operations. Free cash flow is what remains after those necessary investments — the cash truly available to owners, debt holders, and future growth initiatives.

### Can a profitable company have negative cash flow?

Yes, and it happens frequently. The most common cause is rapid growth: a company doubles revenue but must purchase inventory and extend credit to customers before collecting payment. The income statement shows profit, but cash is tied up in receivables and inventory. Other causes include large one-time capital costs, debt repayments, or tax payments that lag reported income. This disconnect between profit and cash is precisely why cash flow analysis exists.

## Methodology & Sources

- **CFA Institute** — "Financial Reporting and Analysis" (CFA Program Curriculum), indirect method of operating cash flow calculation
- **FASB ASC 230** — Statement of Cash Flows, US GAAP standard for cash flow reporting
- **Damodaran, Aswath** — "Investment Valuation" (3rd ed., 2012), free cash flow to firm and equity frameworks
- **McKinsey & Company** — "Valuation: Measuring and Managing the Value of Companies" (7th ed.), cash flow margin benchmarks by industry

This calculator uses the indirect method — the most common approach in US GAAP financial statements — which reconciles net income to operating cash flow by adding back non-cash charges and adjusting for working capital changes. The direct method (which tallies cash receipts minus cash payments) produces the same result but is rarely used in practice because it requires more granular data.
