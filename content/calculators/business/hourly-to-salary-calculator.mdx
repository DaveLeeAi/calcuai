## What This Calculator Shows

This calculator converts your hourly rate into equivalent salary figures across every common pay period. The **annual salary** is your gross yearly income assuming you work the same hours every week for the number of weeks you specify. **Monthly pay** divides the annual figure by 12, **biweekly pay** divides by 26 (the number of biweekly pay periods in a year), **weekly pay** divides by 52, and **daily pay** divides by total working days (weeks per year times 5).

If you enter overtime hours, the calculator also shows your **annual overtime pay** and **total annual compensation** (base salary plus overtime). The **effective hourly rate** tells you what you actually earn per hour when overtime hours are factored in — this is always higher than your base rate when overtime is present because overtime hours are paid at a premium. Use the [Overtime Calculator](/business/overtime-calculator) for a deeper weekly breakdown, or the [Salary Calculator](/finance/salary-calculator) to convert in the opposite direction.

## How to Use This Calculator

1. **Enter your hourly rate** — your base pay before taxes or deductions. If you earn different rates for different shifts, use your most common rate.
2. **Set hours per week** — the default is 40, which is the standard full-time work week under the Fair Labor Standards Act. Adjust for part-time schedules.
3. **Set weeks per year** — the default is 52. Subtract any unpaid vacation or leave weeks. For example, if you take 2 weeks unpaid vacation, enter 50.
4. **Add overtime hours** — enter your average weekly overtime hours. Leave at 0 if you don't work overtime or are salaried-exempt.
5. **Select overtime rate** — 1.5x (time-and-a-half) is the FLSA standard. Select 2x for double-time if your employer or union contract provides it.
6. **Read your results** — the annual salary appears at the top, followed by monthly, biweekly, weekly, and daily breakdowns plus overtime totals.

## The Formula

<div className="formula-section">

$$
\text{Annual Salary} = \text{Hourly Rate} \times \text{Hours per Week} \times \text{Weeks per Year}
$$

$$
\text{Overtime Pay} = \text{Hourly Rate} \times \text{Overtime Multiplier} \times \text{Overtime Hours} \times \text{Weeks per Year}
$$

$$
\text{Total Compensation} = \text{Annual Salary} + \text{Overtime Pay}
$$

$$
\text{Effective Hourly Rate} = \frac{\text{Total Compensation}}{(\text{Hours per Week} + \text{Overtime Hours}) \times \text{Weeks per Year}}
$$

Where:
- **Hourly Rate** = your base pay per hour before taxes
- **Hours per Week** = regular hours worked each week (typically 40)
- **Weeks per Year** = weeks worked annually (52 minus unpaid time off)
- **Overtime Multiplier** = premium rate for overtime hours (1.5x standard, 2x double time)
- **Overtime Hours** = average weekly hours beyond regular schedule

*Source: U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics — Standard Work Week and Overtime Provisions under the Fair Labor Standards Act (29 U.S.C. § 207). The standard 2,080-hour work year (40 hours × 52 weeks) is the federal baseline for full-time hourly-to-salary conversions.*

## Worked Example

A warehouse supervisor earns \$30 per hour and works 40 regular hours plus 5 overtime hours per week at the standard 1.5x rate, 52 weeks per year.

**Step 1 — Base annual salary:**
- \$30 × 40 hours × 52 weeks = **\$62,400**

**Step 2 — Annual overtime pay:**
- \$30 × 1.5 × 5 hours × 52 weeks = **\$11,700**

**Step 3 — Total annual compensation:**
- \$62,400 + \$11,700 = **\$74,100**

**Step 4 — Pay period breakdowns (base salary only):**
- Monthly: \$62,400 ÷ 12 = **\$5,200.00**
- Biweekly: \$62,400 ÷ 26 = **\$2,400.00**
- Weekly: \$62,400 ÷ 52 = **\$1,200.00**
- Daily: \$62,400 ÷ 260 = **\$240.00**

**Step 5 — Effective hourly rate:**
- Total hours per year: (40 + 5) × 52 = 2,340
- \$74,100 ÷ 2,340 = **\$31.67/hr**

*Interpretation:* The supervisor's base salary equivalent is \$62,400, but consistent overtime pushes total compensation to \$74,100. The effective hourly rate of \$31.67 is higher than the base \$30 rate because overtime hours are paid at a 50% premium. If this person were offered a salaried position, they would need at least \$74,100 annually to match their current total income — not just \$62,400.

## Key Factors That Affect Your Salary

1. **Unpaid Time Off Reduces Annual Pay** — The standard conversion assumes 52 paid weeks. If you take 2 weeks of unpaid vacation, your actual annual earnings at \$25/hour drop from \$52,000 to \$50,000 — a \$2,000 difference. Always set weeks per year to reflect only the weeks you actually work and get paid for.

2. **Overtime Rules Vary by State** — While federal FLSA requires 1.5x pay for hours over 40 per week, some states have stricter rules. California mandates 1.5x after 8 hours per day and 2x after 12 hours per day. Alaska requires overtime after 8 hours per day. Check your state labor department for applicable rules.

3. **Benefits Add 20-40% to Total Compensation** — Hourly rates do not include the value of employer-provided benefits. According to the BLS, benefits average 29.4% of total compensation for private industry workers. A \$25/hour job with full benefits (health insurance, retirement match, paid leave) may be worth \$32–\$35/hour in total compensation.

4. **Salaried vs. Hourly Status Matters** — Salaried-exempt employees do not receive overtime pay regardless of hours worked. If you are comparing an hourly job to a salaried offer, factor in whether the salaried role typically requires more than 40 hours per week without extra pay.

5. **Tax Implications of Higher Brackets** — Earning more through overtime may push a portion of your income into a higher federal tax bracket. At \$52,000 annual salary (2025 single filer), you fall in the 22% bracket. Adding \$10,000 in overtime does not make all income taxed at a higher rate — only the income above the bracket threshold. ## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is $25 an hour a good salary?

At \$25 per hour working full time (40 hours, 52 weeks), you earn \$52,000 per year before taxes. The Bureau of Labor Statistics reports the median weekly earnings for full-time wage and salary workers was \$1,165 in Q4 2024, which equals roughly \$29.13 per hour. So \$25 per hour is below the national median but above the federal minimum wage of \$7.25. Whether it's "good" depends on your location — \$52,000 goes much further in a low-cost metro like Memphis than in San Francisco, where the median household income exceeds \$120,000.

### How do I account for unpaid time off?

Reduce the "weeks per year" field by the number of unpaid weeks off. If you take 2 weeks of unpaid vacation and 1 week of unpaid sick leave, enter 49 instead of 52. At \$25/hour, this changes your annual salary from \$52,000 to \$49,000. If your time off is paid (PTO), keep weeks at 52 since you receive income during those weeks. This distinction matters when comparing an hourly job (often no paid leave) to a salaried position with PTO benefits.

### Does salary include overtime?

The "annual salary" figure shown by this calculator does not include overtime — it represents only your base hours multiplied by your base rate. Overtime pay is calculated separately and added to produce the "total annual compensation" figure. When evaluating a job offer listed as a salary, keep in mind that salaried-exempt positions do not pay overtime regardless of hours worked, while hourly employees are entitled to overtime under the FLSA for hours exceeding 40 per week.

### How many work hours are in a year?

A standard full-time work year contains 2,080 hours (40 hours per week times 52 weeks). This is the figure used by employers, the BLS, and the federal government for hourly-to-salary conversions. However, actual hours worked are typically lower due to holidays (approximately 8 federal holidays = 64 hours), vacation (average 10 days = 80 hours), and sick leave. The average American worker logs approximately 1,790 hours per year after accounting for paid time off, according to OECD data.

### What is the difference between gross and net salary?

Gross salary is your total earnings before any deductions — this is what the calculator shows. Net salary (take-home pay) is what you actually receive after deductions for federal income tax, state income tax, Social Security (6.2%), Medicare (1.45%), and any voluntary deductions like health insurance or retirement contributions. At \$52,000 gross, a single filer with standard deductions can expect roughly \$40,000–\$43,000 in take-home pay depending on state taxes. Use the [Paycheck Calculator](/finance/paycheck-calculator) for a detailed net pay estimate.

## Methodology & Sources

- **U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics** — Standard Work Week definition, Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics (May 2024)
- **U.S. Department of Labor** — Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA), overtime pay requirements, 29 U.S.C. § 207
- **OECD** — Average annual hours actually worked per worker, United States (2024)

The annual salary formula multiplies hourly rate by weekly hours and annual weeks. Overtime is calculated separately at the specified premium rate and added to produce total compensation. Pay period breakdowns (monthly, biweekly, weekly, daily) are derived from the base annual salary only, reflecting how most employers structure regular paycheck amounts. The effective hourly rate divides total compensation by total hours worked, providing the blended rate across regular and overtime hours.
