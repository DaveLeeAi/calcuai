## What Your Results Mean

Profit is what remains after subtracting all costs from revenue. But "profit" is not one number — it's three, and each tells a different story about your business:

**Gross profit** (\$75,000 − \$30,000 = \$45,000 in the example above) shows how much you earn after direct production costs. If gross profit is low, your product pricing or manufacturing costs need attention — no amount of overhead reduction will fix a product that costs more to make than it sells for.

**Operating profit** (\$45,000 − \$20,000 = \$25,000) reveals whether the business is viable after covering rent, salaries, marketing, and other overhead. A positive operating profit means the core business works before taxes and financing enter the picture.

**Net profit** (\$25,000 − \$6,250 tax = \$18,750) is the final bottom line — what the owner takes home or reinvests. The pie chart breakdown shows exactly what percentage of each revenue dollar goes to COGS, overhead, taxes, and net profit.

For top-line sales forecasting, use the [Revenue Calculator](/calculators/business/revenue-calculator). To see your profitability as a percentage (margin), use the [Margin Calculator](/calculators/business/margin-calculator).

## How to Use This Calculator

1. **Enter Revenue** — total sales income for the period you're analyzing (monthly, quarterly, or annual). This is the top line before any deductions.
2. **Enter Cost of Goods Sold** — direct costs tied to producing your product or delivering your service. For a bakery: flour, butter, sugar, packaging, and hourly baker wages. For SaaS: server hosting and direct engineering costs.
3. **Enter Operating Expenses** — overhead costs not directly tied to production. Rent, salaried employees, marketing spend, insurance, utilities, and software subscriptions.
4. **Enter Tax Rate** — your combined federal and state effective tax rate. The 2024 U.S. federal corporate rate is 21%; most small businesses pay 25-35% combined.
5. **Enter Units Sold (optional)** — if you sell discrete units, enter the count to see profit per unit. Leave at zero if not applicable.

## The Formula

<div className="formula-section">

**Gross Profit:**

$$
\text{Gross Profit} = \text{Revenue} - \text{Cost of Goods Sold}
$$

**Operating Profit:**

$$
\text{Operating Profit} = \text{Gross Profit} - \text{Operating Expenses}
$$

**Net Profit:**

$$
\text{Net Profit} = \text{Operating Profit} - \left(\text{Operating Profit} \times \frac{\text{Tax Rate}}{100}\right)
$$

**Profit per Unit:**

$$
\text{Profit per Unit} = \frac{\text{Net Profit}}{\text{Units Sold}}
$$

Where:
- **Revenue** = total income from sales (\$)
- **COGS** = direct costs of producing goods or services (\$)
- **Operating Expenses** = indirect overhead costs (\$)
- **Tax Rate** = combined federal + state tax rate (%)
- **Units Sold** = number of units sold in the period

*Source: U.S. Small Business Administration (SBA) — Business profitability and financial statement guidelines (2024). Profit layers follow standard GAAP income statement structure.*

## Worked Example

### Small Bakery — Monthly Figures

Sarah runs a bakery in Austin, TX. Here are her monthly numbers:
- **Revenue:** \$75,000 (from 1,000 custom cake orders averaging \$75 each)
- **COGS:** \$30,000 (ingredients \$18,000 + hourly baker wages \$10,000 + packaging \$2,000)
- **Operating Expenses:** \$20,000 (rent \$5,500 + salaried staff \$9,000 + marketing \$2,500 + utilities \$1,200 + insurance \$800 + POS/software \$1,000)
- **Tax Rate:** 25% (combined federal + Texas franchise tax equivalent)

**Step 1 — Gross Profit:**
- \$75,000 − \$30,000 = **\$45,000**
- Gross margin = \$45,000 / \$75,000 = 60% (healthy for food service; industry average is 55-65%)

**Step 2 — Operating Profit:**
- \$45,000 − \$20,000 = **\$25,000**
- Operating margin = \$25,000 / \$75,000 = 33.3%

**Step 3 — Tax:**
- \$25,000 × 25% = **\$6,250**

**Step 4 — Net Profit:**
- \$25,000 − \$6,250 = **\$18,750**
- Net margin = \$18,750 / \$75,000 = 25%

**Step 5 — Profit per Unit:**
- \$18,750 / 1,000 orders = **\$18.75 profit per cake**

**Interpretation:** Sarah keeps \$18.75 from every \$75 cake order. Her 25% net margin is strong for a bakery — the National Restaurant Association reports average restaurant net margins of 3-9%. She could increase profit by negotiating better ingredient pricing (reducing COGS) or raising prices. A \$5 price increase per cake would add \$5,000/month to gross profit, flowing almost entirely to the bottom line — boosting net profit to roughly \$22,500/month after taxes.

## Methodology & Sources

- **U.S. Small Business Administration (SBA)** — Business profitability guidelines, income statement structure, and small business financial benchmarks (2024)
- **GAAP (Generally Accepted Accounting Principles)** — Standard three-tier profit structure: gross profit, operating profit, net profit
- **IRS** — Federal corporate tax rate of 21% (2024); pass-through entity taxation rules for small businesses
- **National Restaurant Association** — Industry profit margin benchmarks for food service businesses (2024)

This calculator follows the standard GAAP income statement waterfall: Revenue minus COGS equals Gross Profit, minus Operating Expenses equals Operating Profit, minus Taxes equals Net Profit. Taxes are calculated only on positive operating profit (losses do not generate a tax liability in this simplified model). For businesses with complex tax situations including deductions, credits, and loss carryforwards, consult a CPA for an accurate effective tax rate.
