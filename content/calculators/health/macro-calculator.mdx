## What Your Results Mean

Calories tell you how much to eat. Macros tell you **what** to eat. Two people eating 2,500 calories per day can have dramatically different body composition outcomes depending on whether those calories come from protein, carbohydrates, or fat. This calculator translates your total calorie target into specific gram amounts for each macronutrient, then distributes them across meals so you have an actionable daily eating framework.

The calculator first determines your Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE) using the Mifflin-St Jeor BMR equation and your activity level, then adjusts for your goal (lose, maintain, or gain weight), and finally applies one of four macro ratio presets. The **balanced** split (30/40/30) suits most general fitness goals. **High-protein** (40/30/30) supports muscle building and satiety during fat loss. **Low-carb** (35/25/40) suits those who prefer higher-fat eating patterns. **Keto** (25/5/70) implements a ketogenic ratio where carbs are restricted to 5% of calories.

Unlike our [Calorie Calculator](/calculators/health/calorie-calculator), which focuses on total energy targets, this tool goes one level deeper — translating those calories into the three macronutrient categories that drive body composition, performance, and satiety. For the underlying BMR calculation methodology, see our [BMR Calculator](/calculators/health/bmr-calculator).

## The Formula

The macro calculation follows a five-step pipeline from body stats to meal-level gram targets.

**Step 1 — BMR (Mifflin-St Jeor):**

$$BMR_{male} = (10 \times W) + (6.25 \times H) - (5 \times A) + 5$$
$$BMR_{female} = (10 \times W) + (6.25 \times H) - (5 \times A) - 161$$

**Step 2 — TDEE:**

$$TDEE = BMR \times AF$$

Where **$AF$** = activity factor (Sedentary: 1.2, Light: 1.375, Moderate: 1.55, Active: 1.725, Very Active: 1.9)

**Step 3 — Goal Adjustment:**

$$Calories = TDEE + \Delta$$

Where **$\Delta$** = −500 (lose), 0 (maintain), or +500 (gain)

**Step 4 — Macro Ratios:**

| Diet | Protein | Carbs | Fat |
|---|---|---|---|
| Balanced | 30% | 40% | 30% |
| High-Protein | 40% | 30% | 30% |
| Low-Carb | 35% | 25% | 40% |
| Keto | 25% | 5% | 70% |

**Step 5 — Convert to Grams:**

$$Protein_g = \frac{Calories \times P\%}{4} \quad Carbs_g = \frac{Calories \times C\%}{4} \quad Fat_g = \frac{Calories \times F\%}{9}$$

Protein and carbs provide 4 calories per gram; fat provides 9 calories per gram.

**Sources:** Mifflin MD, St Jeor ST, et al., *American Journal of Clinical Nutrition*, 1990. ACSM's Guidelines for Exercise Testing and Prescription, 11th ed. ISSN Position Stand on protein and exercise, 2017.

For a deeper explanation of how BMR and TDEE formulas work, see our [methodology page on calorie and BMR formulas](/methodology/calorie-formulas).

### Example 1: Male, Maintenance, Balanced Diet

**Inputs:** Male, 30 years, 180 lb (81.65 kg), 5'10" (177.8 cm), Moderately Active, Maintain, Balanced

1. **BMR:** $(10 \times 81.65) + (6.25 \times 177.8) - (5 \times 30) + 5 = 1,783$ kcal
2. **TDEE:** $1,783 \times 1.55 = 2,762$ kcal
3. **Goal:** Maintain → \$2,762$ kcal (no adjustment)
4. **Macro split (30/40/30):**
   - Protein: $2,762 \times 0.30 / 4 = 207$ g (829 kcal)
   - Carbs: $2,762 \times 0.40 / 4 = 276$ g (1,105 kcal)
   - Fat: $2,762 \times 0.30 / 9 = 92$ g (828 kcal)

   - Lunch: 829 kcal — 62 g protein, 83 g carbs, 28 g fat
   - Dinner: 829 kcal — 62 g protein, 83 g carbs, 28 g fat
   - Snack: 276 kcal — 21 g protein, 28 g carbs, 9 g fat

### Example 2: Female, Weight Loss, High-Protein Diet

**Inputs:** Female, 28 years, 145 lb (65.77 kg), 5'6" (167.6 cm), Lightly Active, Lose, High-Protein

1. **BMR:** $(10 \times 65.77) + (6.25 \times 167.6) - (5 \times 28) - 161 = 1,404$ kcal
2. **TDEE:** $1,404 \times 1.375 = 1,930$ kcal
3. **Goal:** Lose → $1,930 - 500 = 1,430$ kcal

## Key Factors That Affect Your Results

**Diet preference selection** — The macro ratio you choose fundamentally changes the composition of your diet even at the same calorie level. A 2,500-calorie balanced diet provides 188 g protein, while the same calories on a keto diet provide only 156 g protein but 194 g fat. Choose based on your training style, food preferences, and metabolic response — not popularity. If you are unsure, start with balanced and adjust after 2–4 weeks based on energy levels and satiety.

**Protein timing and quality** — While this calculator distributes protein evenly across meals, research from the ISSN suggests that spreading protein intake across 4–5 meals of 20–40 g per serving optimizes muscle protein synthesis. The meal breakdown provides a practical starting template, but you may benefit from adding a second snack if your protein target exceeds 150 g per day.

**Activity level accuracy** — Since macro targets derive from your TDEE, an inaccurate activity level cascades through every output. Overestimating by one level inflates your calorie target by 200–400 calories, which translates to 15–30 extra grams of each macronutrient. Track your actual intake and body weight for two weeks — if weight is stable, your inputs are calibrated correctly.

**Goal-based calorie adjustment** — The 500-calorie deficit or surplus is a standard starting point, not a universal rule. Individuals closer to their goal weight may benefit from a smaller 250-calorie adjustment to minimize muscle loss (when cutting) or fat gain (when bulking). The International Society of Sports Nutrition recommends 0.7–1.0% of body weight per week as a maximum safe rate of weight loss for athletes.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- Macro ratios are **fixed percentages** — they do not adjust dynamically based on body weight, training volume, or metabolic markers.
- Protein recommendations via percentage may not meet **absolute gram targets** recommended for athletes (1.6–2.2 g/kg). Compare your protein grams to your body weight and adjust if needed.
- The meal breakdown uses a **30/30/30/10 split** as a general starting point — individual meal timing preferences and schedules vary.
- Does not account for **micronutrient needs** — hitting your macro targets does not guarantee adequate vitamins, minerals, or fiber intake.
- The keto ratio (5% carbs) produces very low carbohydrate intakes that require **medical supervision** for individuals with diabetes or other metabolic conditions.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the best macro ratio for weight loss?

A high-protein split (40% protein, 30% carbs, 30% fat) is generally most effective for weight loss because protein increases satiety, preserves lean muscle during a calorie deficit, and has the highest thermic effect of food — your body burns 20–30% of protein calories just digesting them, compared to 5–10% for carbs and 0–3% for fat. A 2012 meta-analysis in the *British Journal of Nutrition* found that higher protein diets (25–35% of calories) produced greater fat loss and better lean mass retention than standard protein intakes during energy restriction.

### How many grams of protein do I actually need per day?

The minimum recommended daily allowance is 0.8 g per kg of body weight (about 56 g for a 154 lb person), but this is the floor for preventing deficiency, not the optimum for health or fitness. The International Society of Sports Nutrition recommends 1.4–2.0 g per kg for active individuals and up to 2.2 g per kg during calorie-restricted fat loss phases. For a 180 lb (82 kg) person, that translates to 115–180 g per day. If the percentage-based calculation in this tool falls below 1.4 g/kg for your body weight, increase your protein percentage or total calories.

### Should I track macros every day?

Tracking macros daily for 2–4 weeks builds awareness of portion sizes and food composition, but long-term rigid tracking is not necessary for most people. After an initial learning phase, most individuals can estimate portions accurately enough to stay within 10% of their targets without weighing every meal. Focus on hitting your protein target consistently — it is the macro most people under-consume and the one with the largest impact on body composition. Carb and fat ratios can flex day to day as long as your weekly averages stay in range.
