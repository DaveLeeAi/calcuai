## Your Result Explained

A logarithm answers the question: "To what power must I raise the base to get this number?" When you enter 1000 in common log mode, the result 3 tells you that 10 raised to the 3rd power equals 1,000. The antilog verification confirms this by computing b^result and showing you the original value.

Common logarithms (base 10) are used in decibel scales, pH chemistry, and the Richter earthquake magnitude scale. Natural logarithms (base e ≈ 2.71828) appear throughout calculus, compound interest formulas, and exponential decay models. Custom base logarithms are essential in computer science — log₂ determines how many binary digits are needed to represent a number, and log₂(1,000,000) ≈ 19.93 tells you that a million items require about 20 bits to address. ## The Formula

### Change of Base Formula

$$\log_b(x) = \frac{\ln(x)}{\ln(b)}$$

Where:

- **b** = the base of the logarithm (must be positive and not equal to 1)
- **x** = the argument (the number whose logarithm is being computed, must be positive)
- **ln** = the natural logarithm (base e ≈ 2.71828)

This formula converts any logarithm into a ratio of natural logarithms, which computers can evaluate directly using the Taylor series expansion of ln(x). For common logarithms, b = 10. For natural logarithms, b = e and the formula simplifies to ln(x)/ln(e) = ln(x)/1 = ln(x).

### Special Cases

- **log_b(1) = 0** for any valid base, because b⁰ = 1
- **log_b(b) = 1** for any valid base, because b¹ = b
- **log_b(0) = −∞** because no finite power of b yields 0
- **log_b(negative)** is undefined in the real numbers

### Antilog Verification

$$\text{antilog} = b^{\text{result}}$$

If the computation is correct, the antilog equals the original value x.

**Source:** Change of base formula — standard logarithmic identity documented in Abramowitz & Stegun, *Handbook of Mathematical Functions* (Dover, 1965), Section 4.1.

## Worked Example

### Example: How Many Times Must You Double \$1 to Reach \$1,000?

This is the same as asking: what is log₂(1000)?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Identify the base: b = **2** (doubling)
2. Identify the value: x = **1,000**
3. Apply the change of base formula: log₂(1000) = ln(1000) / ln(2)
4. Compute ln(1000) = **6.907755** (the natural log of 1000)
5. Compute ln(2) = **0.693147** (the natural log of 2)
6. Divide: 6.907755 / 0.693147 = **9.96578**

**Result:** log₂(1000) ≈ **9.966**, meaning you need to double approximately 10 times to turn \$1 into roughly \$1,000 (2¹⁰ = 1,024).

**Practical interpretation:** This is why computer scientists work with powers of 2. A binary search through 1,000 sorted items takes at most 10 comparisons (⌈log₂(1000)⌉ = 10). Each doubling halves the remaining search space, and 10 halvings narrows 1,000 items down to 1.

### Example 2: Earthquake Magnitude — Richter Scale Comparison

The Richter scale uses common logarithms. An earthquake measuring 7.0 releases how many times more energy than one measuring 5.0?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Magnitude difference: 7.0 − 5.0 = **2.0**
2. Energy ratio per magnitude unit: 10^1.5 ≈ **31.62** (each whole magnitude step represents 31.62× more energy)
3. For a 2-magnitude difference: 31.62² = **1,000×** more energy
4. Verify with logs: log₁₀(1000) = **3.0**, confirming that 10^3 = 1,000

**Practical interpretation:** A magnitude 7.0 earthquake releases approximately 1,000 times more energy than a magnitude 5.0 earthquake — not twice as much. This is the power of logarithmic scales: they compress enormous ranges into manageable numbers. The 2011 Tōhoku earthquake (9.1) released about 22,387 times more energy than the 7.0 threshold for "major" earthquakes.

### Example 3: Sound Intensity — Decibel Calculation

A rock concert produces sound at 110 dB. Normal conversation is 60 dB. How many times more intense is the concert?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Decibel difference: 110 − 60 = **50 dB**
2. Intensity ratio formula: 10^(dB difference / 10)
3. Compute: 10^(50/10) = 10^5 = **100,000**
4. Verify: log₁₀(100,000) = **5.0**

**Practical interpretation:** The concert is 100,000 times more intense than conversation, even though the decibel numbers differ by less than 2×. Every 10 dB increase represents a 10× increase in sound intensity. This logarithmic compression is why decibels exist — human hearing spans a range of about 1 trillion to 1 (0 dB to 120 dB), which is impractical to express on a linear scale.

## Real-World Uses

**Computer science — algorithm complexity.** Binary search through a sorted list of 1 million items requires at most ⌈log₂(1,000,000)⌉ = 20 comparisons. Database indexes, balanced binary trees, and divide-and-conquer algorithms all have O(log n) time complexity, meaning performance degrades slowly as data grows. Doubling the dataset adds only one extra step.

**Finance — compound growth and the Rule of 72.** To find how long it takes an investment to double at a given interest rate, divide 72 by the rate. The exact formula uses logarithms: t = ln(2) / ln(1 + r). At 8% annual return, doubling time = ln(2) / ln(1.08) = 0.6931 / 0.0770 = 9.0 years. The Rule of 72 approximation (72/8 = 9) is remarkably accurate for rates between 4% and 15%.

**Chemistry — pH scale.** pH = −log₁₀[H⁺], where [H⁺] is the hydrogen ion concentration in moles per liter. Pure water has [H⁺] = 10⁻⁷, so pH = 7 (neutral). Stomach acid has [H⁺] ≈ 10⁻¹, so pH = 1. Each pH unit represents a 10× change in acidity — lemon juice (pH 2) is 100,000 times more acidic than pure water (pH 7).

**Information theory — data compression.** The number of bits needed to represent N distinct values is ⌈log₂(N)⌉. An 8-bit byte stores 2⁸ = 256 values. A 16-bit integer stores 2¹⁶ = 65,536 values. Shannon entropy — the theoretical limit of data compression — is calculated using log₂, making logarithms foundational to how digital information is encoded and transmitted.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Entering zero or a negative number.** Logarithms are defined only for positive real numbers. log(0) approaches negative infinity and log(−5) is undefined in the real number system. If your calculation produces a negative input for a log function, check whether you need the absolute value or whether the formula expects a different transformation.

**Confusing log base 10 with natural log (ln).** In mathematics and most programming languages, "log" often means the natural logarithm (base e). In engineering, chemistry, and everyday usage, "log" typically means base 10. A calculator that returns 2.303 for "log(10)" is using the natural logarithm; one that returns 1.0 is using base 10. Always verify which convention your tool or textbook follows.

**Assuming log(a + b) = log(a) + log(b).** This is false. The correct identity is log(a × b) = log(a) + log(b). Logarithms convert multiplication into addition, not addition into addition. For example, log₁₀(3 + 7) = log₁₀(10) = 1, but log₁₀(3) + log₁₀(7) = 0.477 + 0.845 = 1.322.

**Forgetting that log₁(x) is undefined.** A base of 1 produces a division by zero in the change-of-base formula because ln(1) = 0. Intuitively, 1 raised to any power is always 1, so no exponent can make 1 equal any other number.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- This calculator operates on **real numbers only**. Complex logarithms (log of negative numbers) are not supported.
- Results are computed using **JavaScript's Math.log()** and **Math.log10()** functions, which provide 15–17 significant digits of precision — more than sufficient for scientific and engineering applications.
- The **custom base** must be a positive number not equal to 1. Bases between 0 and 1 are valid and produce negative logarithms for inputs greater than 1 (e.g., log₀.₅(8) = −3).
- Very large inputs (above 10³⁰⁸) or very small inputs (below 10⁻³⁰⁸) exceed JavaScript's floating-point range and will return Infinity or −Infinity.
- The antilog verification may show minor floating-point rounding discrepancies (e.g., 999.9999999 instead of 1000) due to the inherent limitations of IEEE 754 double-precision arithmetic.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the difference between log and ln?

"Log" (common logarithm) uses base 10: log₁₀(100) = 2 because 10² = 100. "Ln" (natural logarithm) uses base e ≈ 2.71828: ln(100) = 4.605 because e⁴·⁶⁰⁵ ≈ 100. Common logs appear in decibels, pH, and Richter scales. Natural logs dominate calculus, statistics, compound interest, and exponential decay. To convert between them: ln(x) = log₁₀(x) × 2.302585, or equivalently log₁₀(x) = ln(x) / 2.302585.

### Why is log₁₀(1) equal to zero?

Because 10⁰ = 1. The logarithm answers "what exponent produces this number?" and the zero exponent of any base (except 0) equals 1. This property holds for every valid base: log₂(1) = 0, ln(1) = 0, log₇(1) = 0. It follows from the fundamental exponent rule that b⁰ = 1 for all b > 0.

### How do logarithms relate to exponential growth?

Logarithms are the inverse of exponential functions. If a quantity grows exponentially — like bacteria doubling every hour — the logarithm tells you how many doubling periods have elapsed. After 8 hours with hourly doubling, you have 2⁸ = 256 bacteria; log₂(256) = 8 recovers the elapsed time. This inverse relationship makes logarithms essential for solving "how long until?" questions in finance (compound interest), biology (population growth), and physics (radioactive decay).

### What is a logarithmic scale and when should I use one?

A logarithmic scale spaces axis values by powers of the base rather than by equal increments. Each tick mark represents a 10× increase (for log₁₀ scales) or a 2× increase (for log₂ scales). Use log scales when your data spans multiple orders of magnitude — earthquake energies (1 to 10 billion joules), sound intensities (1 to 1 trillion), or stock prices over decades. On a linear scale, small values cluster invisibly near zero; on a log scale, all magnitudes are equally visible and comparable.
