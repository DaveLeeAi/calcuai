## The Formula

### The Quadratic Formula

$$x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$$

Where:

- **a** = coefficient of x² (must not be 0 for a quadratic)
- **b** = coefficient of x
- **c** = constant term
- **b² − 4ac** = the discriminant (Δ), which determines the nature of the roots

The ± symbol means there are two solutions: one using addition (x₁) and one using subtraction (x₂).

### The Discriminant

$$\Delta = b^2 - 4ac$$

The discriminant classifies the roots:

- **Δ > 0** — two distinct real roots. The parabola crosses the x-axis at two points.
- **Δ = 0** — one repeated real root (also called a double root). The parabola touches the x-axis at exactly one point (the vertex).
- **Δ < 0** — two complex conjugate roots. The parabola does not cross the x-axis. The roots are: x = (−b ± i√|Δ|) / (2a).

### Vertex of the Parabola

$$x_v = \frac{-b}{2a}, \quad y_v = c - \frac{b^2}{4a}$$

The vertex is the minimum point when a > 0 (parabola opens upward) or the maximum point when a < 0 (parabola opens downward).

### Linear Fallback (a = 0)

When a = 0, the equation reduces to bx + c = 0, a linear equation with solution x = −c/b.

**Source:** The quadratic formula is derived from completing the square on the general form ax² + bx + c = 0. This derivation is a standard result in algebra, documented in the *CRC Standard Mathematical Tables and Formulae* (33rd Edition, CRC Press).

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Two Distinct Real Roots — x² − 5x + 6 = 0

**Step-by-step:**

1. Identify coefficients: a = **1**, b = **−5**, c = **6**
2. Compute the discriminant: Δ = (−5)² − 4(1)(6) = 25 − 24 = **1**
3. Since Δ > 0, there are two distinct real roots
4. Compute x₁: x₁ = (−(−5) + √1) / (2 × 1) = (5 + 1) / 2 = **3**
5. Compute x₂: x₂ = (−(−5) − √1) / (2 × 1) = (5 − 1) / 2 = **2**
6. Vertex: x_v = −(−5) / (2 × 1) = **2.5**, y_v = 6 − 25/4 = **−0.25**

**Result:** The roots are **x = 3** and **x = 2**. The vertex of the parabola is at **(2.5, −0.25)**.

**Verification:** Substituting x = 3: 9 − 15 + 6 = 0. Substituting x = 2: 4 − 10 + 6 = 0. Both check out.

### Example 2: Complex Roots — x² + 2x + 5 = 0

**Step-by-step:**

1. Identify coefficients: a = **1**, b = **2**, c = **5**
2. Compute the discriminant: Δ = (2)² − 4(1)(5) = 4 − 20 = **−16**
3. Since Δ < 0, the roots are complex conjugates
4. Real part: −b / (2a) = −2 / 2 = **−1**
5. Imaginary part: √|−16| / (2 × 1) = 4 / 2 = **2**
6. x₁ = **−1 + 2i**, x₂ = **−1 − 2i**
7. Vertex: x_v = **−1**, y_v = 5 − 4/4 = **4**

**Result:** The roots are **−1 + 2i** and **−1 − 2i**. The parabola has its minimum at **(−1, 4)** and never crosses the x-axis.

**Practical interpretation:** A negative discriminant means the quadratic expression ax² + bx + c is always positive (when a > 0) or always negative (when a < 0) for all real x. In this case, x² + 2x + 5 is always at least 4, never reaching zero. In applied problems, no real roots carries concrete meaning. For projectile motion, the height h of a ball thrown upward is modeled as h(t) = −16t² + v₀t + h₀. If you set h(t) = 100 and the discriminant is negative, the ball never reaches 100 feet — the maximum height (at the vertex) is below your target. For example, a ball launched at 60 ft/s from ground level reaches a maximum height of only 56.25 feet (at t = 1.875 seconds), so solving −16t² + 60t = 100 yields Δ = 3,600 − 6,400 = −2,800 — confirming the ball falls short of 100 feet. Recognizing a negative discriminant immediately saves you from searching for a solution that does not exist.

## Key Factors That Affect Your Results

**The discriminant is the single most important diagnostic.** Before computing roots, check whether Δ = b² − 4ac is positive, zero, or negative. For 3x² − 12x + 12 = 0, Δ = 144 − 144 = 0, immediately telling you there is exactly one repeated root (x = 2) without further computation. A discriminant of −4 would mean complex roots, while +4 means two real roots separated by √4/(2a) units.

**The coefficient a determines whether the parabola opens upward or downward.** When a > 0, the vertex is the minimum point — the function decreases to the vertex, then increases. When a < 0, the vertex is the maximum point. This matters for optimization: maximizing revenue, minimizing cost, or finding the peak height of a projectile all reduce to finding the vertex of a quadratic.

**Large coefficients can cause floating-point precision issues.** For equations like 0.001x² + 1000x − 0.001 = 0, the discriminant (1,000,000.000004) is very close to b², and subtracting nearly equal numbers can lose significant digits. This calculator uses double-precision arithmetic (about 15 decimal digits), which is sufficient for most practical problems.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- **Assumes real-number coefficients.** The calculator accepts any real values for a, b, and c but does not handle complex-valued coefficients.
- **When a = 0, the equation is linear, not quadratic.** The calculator handles this gracefully by solving bx + c = 0, but the vertex and axis of symmetry are undefined for linear equations.
- **Floating-point precision.** Results are computed in double-precision (IEEE 754) and rounded to 6-10 significant digits. Extremely large or extremely small coefficients may produce rounding artifacts.
- **Complex roots are displayed in a + bi format.** The calculator does not decompose complex roots into polar form (modulus and argument).
- **Does not factor automatically.** While the calculator finds the roots, it does not display the factored form (a)(x − x₁)(x − x₂) unless the roots are clean integers or simple fractions.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What does the discriminant tell you about a quadratic equation?

The discriminant (Δ = b² − 4ac) reveals the number and type of solutions without solving the equation. When Δ > 0, the equation has two distinct real roots and the parabola crosses the x-axis twice. When Δ = 0, there is exactly one repeated root — the parabola touches the x-axis at a single point (its vertex). When Δ < 0, both roots are complex conjugates (a ± bi form) and the parabola does not intersect the x-axis at all. The discriminant also appears under the square root in the quadratic formula, which is why a negative value leads to imaginary numbers.

### Can a quadratic equation have complex roots?

Yes, a quadratic equation has complex roots whenever the discriminant b² − 4ac is negative. For example, x² + 1 = 0 has discriminant 0 − 4 = −4, yielding roots x = ±i. Complex roots always come in conjugate pairs: if a + bi is a root, then a − bi is also a root. Every quadratic equation with real coefficients has exactly two roots (counting multiplicity) in the complex number system — this is guaranteed by the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra.

### What is the vertex of a parabola and why does it matter?

The vertex is the highest or lowest point on the parabola y = ax² + bx + c, located at x = −b/(2a) with y-value c − b²/(4a). When a > 0 the vertex is the minimum (parabola opens upward); when a < 0 it is the maximum (parabola opens downward). The vertex matters for optimization problems: the maximum height of a projectile, the price that maximizes revenue, or the dimensions that minimize material cost are all found by computing the vertex of the relevant quadratic function.
