## Your Result Explained

A circle is defined by a single measurement — its radius — and every other property follows directly from that one number. This calculator works in reverse too: if you know the area of a circular garden plot, the circumference of a wheel, or the diameter of a pipe, it derives the radius first and then computes all remaining properties.

**From Radius or Diameter:** The most straightforward mode. Enter the radius (distance from center to edge) or diameter (distance across the full circle), and the calculator returns area and circumference. This is what you need when sizing circular objects — tabletops, ponds, pizza dimensions, or pipe cross-sections.

**From Area:** Enter a known area (such as the square footage of a circular room) and get back the radius, diameter, and circumference. This is essential when working backward from material quantities — if you need 200 square feet of turf for a circular lawn, the radius is 7.98 feet and the circumference (edging needed) is 50.13 feet.

**From Circumference:** Enter a circumference (such as the distance around a tree trunk or a wheel's rolling distance per revolution) and derive the radius, diameter, and area. A bicycle wheel with a 2,136 mm circumference has a diameter of 679.6 mm (roughly 26.75 inches).

## The Formula

All circle calculations derive from two fundamental relationships discovered by Archimedes in the 3rd century BC.

**Area of a circle:**

$$A = \pi r^2$$

**Circumference of a circle:**

$$C = 2\pi r$$

**Diameter:**

$$d = 2r$$

Where:

- **r** = radius (distance from center to any point on the circle)
- **d** = diameter (distance across the circle through the center)
- **C** = circumference (distance around the circle)
- **A** = area (surface enclosed by the circle)
- **pi** = the mathematical constant pi, approximately 3.14159265359

**Inverse formulas** (solving for radius from other properties):

$$r = \frac{d}{2} \qquad r = \frac{C}{2\pi} \qquad r = \sqrt{\frac{A}{\pi}}$$

**Source:** Archimedes, *Measurement of a Circle* (circa 250 BC). The relationship between a circle's circumference and diameter defines pi. Euclid's *Elements*, Book XII, Proposition 2 proves that circles' areas are proportional to the squares of their diameters.

## Worked Example

### Finding All Properties from a Known Radius of 7

You have a circular garden bed with a radius of 7 feet and need to calculate the area for mulch coverage and the circumference for edging material.

**Step-by-step:**

1. Radius: **r = 7 ft**
2. Diameter: d = 2 x 7 = **14 ft**
3. Circumference: C = 2 x pi x 7 = **43.9823 ft**
4. Area: A = pi x 7^2 = pi x 49 = **153.9380 sq ft**

**Practical interpretation:** You need approximately 154 square feet of mulch (at 1 inch depth, that is about 1.3 cubic yards) and approximately 44 feet of edging material. For purchasing, round the edging up to 45 feet to account for waste.

### Finding the Radius from a Known Area — Circular Patio

You are buying pavers for a circular patio advertised as 314 square feet. You need the radius to mark the center point and the circumference to order curved edging.

**Step-by-step:**

1. Known area: **A = 314 sq ft**
2. Solve for radius: r = √(A / π) = √(314 / 3.14159) = √(99.949) = **9.9975 ft** (≈ 10 ft)
3. Diameter: d = 2 × 9.9975 = **19.9949 ft** (≈ 20 ft)
4. Circumference: C = 2 × π × 9.9975 = **62.8240 ft**

**Practical interpretation:** The patio has a radius of almost exactly 10 feet and a circumference of about 63 feet. Order 65 feet of curved edging to allow for overlap and waste. To mark the circle on the ground, drive a stake at the center, attach a 10-foot string, and trace the arc — this ancient method is still the most reliable way to lay out a circular shape on a job site.

### Finding Circumference from Diameter — Bicycle Wheel Distance

A road bike wheel has a diameter of 700 mm (standard 700c size). How far does the bike travel in one full wheel revolution?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Known diameter: **d = 700 mm**
2. Radius: r = 700 / 2 = **350 mm**
3. Circumference: C = π × 700 = **2,199.11 mm** (≈ 2.199 m)
4. Area (cross-section): A = π × 350² = **384,845.10 sq mm**

**Practical interpretation:** Each wheel revolution covers approximately 2.2 meters. At a cycling cadence of 90 RPM with a gear ratio that turns the wheel once per pedal stroke, the bike covers 2.2 × 90 = 198 meters per minute, or about 11.9 km/h (7.4 mph). Bike computers use this exact circumference calculation to convert wheel rotation counts into distance traveled.

## Real-World Uses

**Landscaping and irrigation.** Sprinkler heads spray in circular patterns. A sprinkler with a 15-foot radius covers π × 15² = 706.9 square feet. Knowing this tells you how many sprinkler heads you need to cover a rectangular lawn — a 50 × 30-foot yard (1,500 sq ft) needs at least 3 heads with partial overlap, not the 2 that a naive division would suggest.

**Pizza value comparison.** A 16-inch pizza has an area of π × 8² = 201.1 square inches, while a 12-inch pizza has π × 6² = 113.1 square inches. The 16-inch pizza is 78% larger than the 12-inch, not 33% larger as the diameter ratio implies. If the 16-inch costs \$18 and the 12-inch costs \$12, the larger pizza is \$0.090/sq in versus \$0.106/sq in — 18% cheaper per bite.

**Pipe flow and plumbing.** Water flow capacity through a pipe depends on its cross-sectional area. A 1-inch diameter pipe has an area of π × 0.5² = 0.785 sq in. A 2-inch pipe has π × 1² = 3.14 sq in — four times the area, not double. Doubling the pipe diameter quadruples the flow capacity, which is why plumbers upsize pipes rather than adding parallel runs.

**Wheel and gear sizing.** In mechanical engineering, the circumference of a gear determines its pitch — the distance between teeth. A gear with a 4-inch diameter and 20 teeth has a circular pitch of (π × 4) / 20 = 0.628 inches per tooth. Meshing gears must have identical circular pitch to engage properly.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Confusing radius and diameter.** The most common circle calculation error is entering the diameter where the radius belongs (or vice versa). A circle with radius 10 has area 314.16, but a circle with diameter 10 (radius 5) has area 78.54 — exactly one-quarter as much. Always verify which measurement you have before entering it. If you measured "across the circle," that is the diameter — divide by 2 to get the radius.

**Forgetting to square the units for area.** A circle with radius 5 feet has a circumference of 31.42 feet but an area of 78.54 *square* feet. When ordering materials priced per square foot (tile, turf, mulch), use the area number. When ordering materials priced per linear foot (edging, fencing, trim), use the circumference.

**Using π ≈ 3 instead of 3.14159.** Rounding pi to 3 introduces a 4.5% error. For a circle with radius 20 feet, using π = 3 gives area = 1,200 sq ft instead of the correct 1,256.64 sq ft — a 56.64 sq ft error. For back-of-napkin estimates, π ≈ 3.14 is accurate to within 0.05%.

**Not accounting for the area-to-radius scaling.** Area scales with the *square* of the radius. Doubling the radius quadruples the area. A garden bed with radius 10 feet (314 sq ft) upgraded to radius 15 feet does not increase by 50% — it increases to 707 sq ft, a 125% jump. This nonlinear scaling consistently surprises homeowners estimating material quantities.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- Assumes a **perfect geometric circle**. Real-world circles (ponds, patios, rooms) are approximate. For irregular curves, measure the average radius at multiple points and use the mean value.
- The calculator uses **JavaScript's Math.PI** constant (3.141592653589793), which is accurate to 15 decimal places — far beyond what any physical measurement requires.
- Results are **unit-agnostic**: enter inches, get square inches for area; enter meters, get square meters. The calculator does not convert between unit systems. Use our [Square Meter Calculator](/calculators/conversion/square-meter-calculator) for unit conversion.
- Does not compute **arc length, sector area, or segment area** for partial circles. These require an additional angle measurement.
- **Three-dimensional calculations** (sphere volume, cylinder volume) are not included. A circle's area is two-dimensional only.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the area of a circle with radius 10?

The area of a circle with radius 10 is π × 10² = 314.159 square units. If the radius is in feet, the area is 314.159 square feet — roughly the size of a large living room or a single-car garage. To convert to square meters, multiply by 0.0929: 314.159 × 0.0929 = 29.19 square meters.

### How do I find the radius if I only know the circumference?

Divide the circumference by 2π. If a tree trunk measures 62.83 inches around, the radius is 62.83 ÷ (2 × 3.14159) = 10.0 inches, and the diameter is 20.0 inches. This is how foresters estimate tree diameter — they wrap a tape measure around the trunk (circumference) and divide by pi, using a specialized "diameter tape" that reads the conversion directly.

### Why is pi used in circle formulas?

Pi (π ≈ 3.14159) is the ratio of any circle's circumference to its diameter — a constant that holds for every circle regardless of size. Archimedes proved around 250 BC that this ratio is the same for all circles and approximated it between 3 10/71 and 3 1/7 (3.1408 to 3.1429). The area formula A = πr² follows from dividing a circle into infinitely thin triangular wedges whose combined base equals the circumference (2πr) and whose height equals the radius (r), giving area = ½ × 2πr × r = πr².

### How much bigger is a 14-inch pizza than a 10-inch pizza?

A 14-inch pizza (radius 7) has area π × 7² = 153.94 sq in. A 10-inch pizza (radius 5) has area π × 5² = 78.54 sq in. The 14-inch pizza is 96% larger — nearly double the food, despite being only 40% wider in diameter. This is because area scales with the square of the diameter. If the 14-inch pizza costs 50% more than the 10-inch, it is significantly better value per square inch.
