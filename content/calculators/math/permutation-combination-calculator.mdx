## Understanding Permutations vs. Combinations

The distinction between permutations and combinations hinges on a single question: does order matter?

**Permutations** count the number of ways to arrange items in a specific order. If you are awarding gold, silver, and bronze medals to 3 of 10 athletes, the order of selection matters — giving gold to Alice and silver to Bob is a different outcome than gold to Bob and silver to Alice. The number of arrangements is P(10,3) = 10 × 9 × 8 = 720.

**Combinations** count the number of ways to select items without regard to order. If you are choosing 3 players from 10 to form a team, it does not matter who is picked first. The number of selections is C(10,3) = 120 — exactly 720 / 3! = 720 / 6 = 120, because each group of 3 can be rearranged in 3! = 6 ways.

**Repetition** adds another dimension. Without repetition, each item can appear at most once in the selection. With repetition, items can be reused — like choosing 3 scoops of ice cream from 5 flavors where you can repeat a flavor. With repetition, permutations become n^r (5^3 = 125 arrangements) and combinations use the "stars and bars" formula C(n+r-1, r).

Real-world applications span nearly every field. Cryptographers use permutations to calculate password complexity — an 8-character password from 62 alphanumeric characters has 62^8 = 218 trillion permutations. Statisticians use combinations to determine sample sizes. Lottery odds are pure combinations: picking 6 numbers from 49 yields C(49,6) = 13,983,816 possible tickets.

## The Formula

### Permutation (without repetition)

$$P(n,r) = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!}$$

The number of ordered arrangements of r items chosen from n distinct items.

### Combination (without repetition)

$$C(n,r) = \binom{n}{r} = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}$$

The number of unordered selections of r items from n distinct items. Also written as "n choose r."

### Permutation (with repetition)

$$P_{\text{rep}}(n,r) = n^r$$

Each of the r positions can be filled by any of the n items, independently.

### Combination (with repetition)

$$C_{\text{rep}}(n,r) = \binom{n+r-1}{r} = \frac{(n+r-1)!}{r!(n-1)!}$$

Also called "stars and bars" — the number of ways to choose r items from n types when repetition is allowed and order does not matter.

Where:

- **n** = total number of distinct items in the pool
- **r** = number of items being chosen or arranged
- **n!** = n factorial = n × (n−1) × (n−2) × … × 1, with 0! = 1 by convention
- **P(n,r)** = number of permutations (ordered arrangements)
- **C(n,r)** = number of combinations (unordered selections)

The relationship between the two: P(n,r) = C(n,r) × r! — permutations are always r-factorial times larger because each combination can be internally rearranged in r! ways.

**Source:** Kenneth H. Rosen, *Discrete Mathematics and Its Applications*, 8th edition, McGraw-Hill, Chapters 6.1–6.3. Combinatorial formulas trace to Blaise Pascal's *Traité du triangle arithmétique* (1654).

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Arranging Books on a Shelf (Permutation)

How many ways can you arrange 3 books on a shelf chosen from a collection of 10?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Order matters — placing *Book A* first and *Book B* second is a different arrangement than *Book B* first and *Book A* second.
2. n = **10** (total books), r = **3** (shelf positions)
3. Apply the permutation formula: P(10,3) = 10! / (10−3)! = 10! / 7!
4. Cancel common factors: 10 × 9 × 8 = **720**

**Practical interpretation:** There are 720 distinct ways to fill 3 shelf positions from 10 books. If instead you only cared about which 3 books to take on a trip (order irrelevant), you would use combinations: C(10,3) = 720 / 6 = 120.

### Example 2: Lottery Odds (Combination)

A lottery requires you to pick 6 numbers from 1 to 49. How many possible tickets exist?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Order does not matter — picking 3, 17, 22, 31, 40, 45 is the same ticket regardless of the order you choose the numbers.
2. n = **49** (available numbers), r = **6** (numbers per ticket)
3. Apply the combination formula: C(49,6) = 49! / (6! × 43!)
4. Compute numerator: 49 × 48 × 47 × 46 × 45 × 44 = 10,068,347,520
5. Compute denominator: 6! = 720
6. Result: 10,068,347,520 / 720 = **13,983,816**

**Practical interpretation:** Your odds of matching all 6 numbers are 1 in 13,983,816 — roughly 1 in 14 million. This is why jackpots accumulate over many drawings before a winner emerges.

### Example 3: Ice Cream Scoops (Combination with Repetition)

You can choose 3 scoops from 5 flavors, and you are allowed to repeat flavors. How many different selections are possible?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Order does not matter and repetition is allowed.
2. n = **5** (flavors), r = **3** (scoops)
3. Apply the stars-and-bars formula: C(5+3−1, 3) = C(7,3)
4. Compute: C(7,3) = 7! / (3! × 4!) = 210 / 6 = **35**

**Practical interpretation:** There are 35 different scoop selections, including options like "3 vanilla" or "2 chocolate, 1 strawberry."

## Key Factors That Affect Your Results

**Order sensitivity is the defining distinction.** If switching the positions of any two selected items creates a new outcome, use permutations. If not, use combinations. A common test: would swapping the first and second selection give a different result? If yes, permutation. If no, combination.

**Repetition dramatically increases counts.** With repetition, permutations grow as n^r — exponentially — rather than as a falling factorial. For a 4-digit PIN from 10 digits: without repetition, P(10,4) = 5,040; with repetition, 10^4 = 10,000. The repetition option nearly doubles the possibilities in this case.

**Factorial growth limits input range.** Factorials grow astronomically: 20! = 2.43 × 10^18, and 170! ≈ 7.26 × 10^306. This calculator caps n at 170 because 171! exceeds JavaScript's maximum representable number (approximately 1.8 × 10^308). For problems requiring larger values, use logarithmic methods or arbitrary-precision libraries.

**The P-to-C ratio equals r-factorial.** For any given n and r, permutations outnumber combinations by exactly r! — because each unordered selection of r items can be internally rearranged in r! distinct ways. This means P(n,r) = C(n,r) × r!, a useful identity for checking your work.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- **Distinct items assumed.** The standard formulas assume all n items are distinguishable. If items are identical (e.g., 3 red balls and 2 blue balls), multiset or multinomial formulas are needed instead.
- **No conditional constraints.** The calculator does not handle restricted selections such as "choose 3 from 10, but items A and B cannot both be selected." For constrained counting, subtract the forbidden configurations manually.
- **n capped at 170.** JavaScript's 64-bit floating-point numbers overflow above 170! Values beyond this range require arbitrary-precision arithmetic not supported in this calculator.
- **Integer precision limits.** For very large results (above 2^53 ≈ 9 × 10^15), JavaScript loses integer precision. Results remain accurate for the vast majority of practical problems, but combinatorial values exceeding 9 quadrillion may show rounding artifacts.
- **With-repetition combinations assume indistinguishable copies.** Choosing 3 scoops of vanilla is treated as one outcome, not three — consistent with the stars-and-bars model.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the difference between a permutation and a combination?

A permutation counts ordered arrangements where the sequence of selection matters, while a combination counts unordered selections where only the group membership matters. Choosing a president, vice president, and secretary from 10 candidates is a permutation problem (P(10,3) = 720) because the roles are distinct. Choosing 3 committee members from 10 is a combination problem (C(10,3) = 120) because all members have equal standing. The two are related by P(n,r) = C(n,r) × r!.

### When do I use permutations vs. combinations?

Use permutations when the arrangement or position of each selected item creates a distinct outcome — rankings, seating orders, PIN codes, license plates, race finishes, and award assignments. Use combinations when you only care about which items are selected, not the order — lottery tickets, committee formation, card hands, sample groups, and menu selections. If swapping any two items in your selection produces a different result, use permutations; if the swap changes nothing, use combinations.

### How many lottery combinations are possible?

The number depends on the specific lottery format. For a "pick 6 from 49" lottery, C(49,6) = 13,983,816 possible tickets. For Powerball (5 from 69 plus 1 from 26), the total is C(69,5) × 26 = 292,201,338 possible combinations. For Mega Millions (5 from 70 plus 1 from 25), the total is C(70,5) × 25 = 302,575,350. These numbers explain why jackpot odds are so small — you have a roughly 1 in 292 million chance of winning Powerball with a single ticket.

## How This Is Calculated

This calculator computes permutations and combinations using iterative multiplication rather than full factorial computation to maximize numerical range. For permutations without repetition, it multiplies the falling sequence n × (n−1) × … × (n−r+1) directly. For combinations, it uses the multiplicative formula C(n,r) = ∏(n−i)/(i+1) for i = 0 to min(r, n−r)−1, which avoids intermediate overflow. With-repetition permutations use simple exponentiation (n^r), and with-repetition combinations apply the same combination algorithm to C(n+r−1, r). All inputs are floored to integers before calculation. The maximum supported input is n = 170, beyond which factorial values exceed JavaScript's floating-point range.
