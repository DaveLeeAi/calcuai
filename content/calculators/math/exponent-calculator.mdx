## Your Result Explained

Exponentiation is one of the fundamental arithmetic operations — it extends multiplication the same way multiplication extends addition. While 3 × 4 means "add 3 four times," 3^4 means "multiply 3 by itself four times" (3 × 3 × 3 × 3 = 81). Roots are the inverse operation: the cube root of 81 asks "what number, multiplied by itself 3 times, gives 81?"

**Power mode** computes base^exponent for any real-valued inputs. This includes positive integer exponents (2^10 = 1,024), zero exponents (any nonzero number to the 0th power equals 1), negative exponents (2^(-3) = 1/8 = 0.125), and fractional exponents (8^(1/3) = 2, the cube root of 8). The calculator shows which exponent rules were applied at each step.

**Root mode** computes the nth root of a value, equivalent to raising the value to the power of 1/n. The square root (n = 2) is the most common, but cube roots (n = 3), fourth roots, and higher are used throughout science, engineering, and finance. Even roots of negative numbers produce non-real (complex) results and are flagged accordingly.

## The Formula

### Power (Exponentiation)

$$b^n = \underbrace{b \times b \times \cdots \times b}_{n \text{ times}}$$

Where:
- **b** = base (any real number being raised to a power)
- **n** = exponent (positive, negative, zero, or fractional; the degree of the power)

**Key exponent rules:**

$$b^0 = 1 \quad \text{(for any } b \text{, including } 0^0 = 1 \text{ by convention)}$$

$$b^{-n} = \frac{1}{b^n}$$

$$b^{m/n} = \sqrt[n]{b^m} = \left(\sqrt[n]{b}\right)^m$$

### Root (Radical)

$$\sqrt[n]{a} = a^{1/n}$$

Where:
- **n** = root index (degree): 2 for square root, 3 for cube root, any positive integer for nth root
- **a** = radicand: the value under the radical sign (must be ≥ 0 for even roots in the reals)

- Square root: $\sqrt{a} = a^{1/2}$
- Cube root: $\sqrt[3]{a} = a^{1/3}$
- nth root: $\sqrt[n]{a} = a^{1/n}$

For negative values: odd roots are real ($\sqrt[3]{-8} = -2$), but even roots are not real ($\sqrt{-4}$ is undefined in the reals).

**Source:** Standard laws of exponents as formalized in pre-calculus and calculus textbooks, including Stewart's *Calculus: Early Transcendentals* and Blitzer's *Precalculus* (Pearson).

## Worked Examples

### Example: Compound Growth — Powers in Finance

**Scenario:** An investment of \$1,000 grows at 7% annually for 10 years. The future value formula uses exponentiation: FV = PV × (1 + r)^n.

**Calculation:**

1. Base: 1 + 0.07 = **1.07**
2. Exponent: **10** (years)
3. Compute: 1.07^10 = **1.967151**
4. Future value: $1,000 × 1.967151 = **$1,967.15**

**Interpretation:** At 7% annual return, \$1,000 nearly doubles in 10 years. The exponent (10) drives the compounding effect — each year's growth builds on the previous year's total, not just the original \$1,000. This is why compound growth is described as "exponential." Doubling the time period to 20 years does not double the result — 1.07^20 = 3.8697, nearly quadrupling the original investment. Use our [Percentage Calculator](/calculators/math/percentage-calculator) to convert between growth rates and dollar amounts.

### Example 2: Finding a Compound Annual Growth Rate — Root Mode

**Scenario:** A \$5,000 investment grew to \$8,954 over 6 years. What was the annual growth rate (CAGR)?

**Calculation:**

1. The CAGR formula uses the nth root: CAGR = ⁶√(end ÷ start) − 1
2. Growth ratio: 8,954 ÷ 5,000 = **1.7908**
3. Root index: **6** (number of years)
4. Enter in Root mode: value = 1.7908, root index = 6
5. Result: 1.7908^(1/6) = **1.1016**
6. CAGR: 1.1016 − 1 = **10.16% per year**

**Interpretation:** The portfolio compounded at 10.16% annually. The 6th root reverses 6 years of compounding to isolate the equivalent per-year rate. As a check: 1.1016^6 ≈ 1.7908 ✓. This inverse relationship — root undoes power, power undoes root — appears throughout finance (CAGR), physics (finding wave amplitudes), and geometry (finding side lengths from areas or volumes).

## Assumptions & Limitations

- Uses JavaScript's `Math.pow(b, n)` for all calculations, which follows IEEE 754 double-precision floating-point arithmetic. Results are accurate to approximately 15 significant digits for typical inputs.
- **0^0 returns 1** by convention. This is the standard definition used in combinatorics, power series, and most programming environments. Some branches of mathematics consider 0^0 indeterminate; consult your course materials for context.
- **Even roots of negative numbers** are not real numbers. Inputs such as √(−4) return "Not a real number." Complex (imaginary) results are not displayed.
- **Overflow**: exponents that produce values beyond ~1.8 × 10^308 (e.g., 2^1024) return `Infinity` due to floating-point limits. For arbitrary-precision results, use a computer algebra system.
- **Root index** must be a positive integer ≥ 1 in Root mode. Entering 0 is undefined. Fractional root indices (e.g., the 1.5th root) can be computed in Power mode using a fractional exponent.

## How This Is Calculated
