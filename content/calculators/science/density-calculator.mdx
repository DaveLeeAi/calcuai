## Your Result Explained

Density measures how tightly matter is packed into a given space. This calculator uses the fundamental relationship between density, mass, and volume to solve for whichever variable is unknown. Give it mass and volume, and it returns density. Give it density and volume, and it returns mass. Give it density and mass, and it returns volume.

The practical applications are broad. A jeweler can verify whether a gold bar is genuine by checking if its density matches 19,320 kg/m cubed. An engineer can calculate the mass of a steel beam from its dimensions and the known density of steel (7,850 kg/m cubed). A student can determine whether an object will float in water — anything with a density below 1,000 kg/m cubed floats, and anything above sinks.

## The Formula

### Density from Mass and Volume

$$\rho = \frac{m}{V}$$

Where:

- **rho (p)** = density, in kilograms per cubic meter (kg/m cubed)
- **m** = mass of the object, in kilograms (kg)
- **V** = volume of the object, in cubic meters (m cubed)

### Mass from Density and Volume

$$m = \rho \times V$$

### Volume from Density and Mass

$$V = \frac{m}{\rho}$$

These three forms are algebraic rearrangements of the same equation. Density is an intensive property — it does not depend on the amount of material. A 1 kg block of aluminum and a 100 kg block of aluminum both have the same density of 2,700 kg/m cubed. This makes density ideal for material identification: measure the mass and volume of an unknown sample, calculate its density, and compare against reference tables.

### Common Unit Conversions

$$1 \text{ g/cm}^3 = 1{,}000 \text{ kg/m}^3$$

$$1 \text{ lb/ft}^3 = 16.0185 \text{ kg/m}^3$$

$$1 \text{ kg/L} = 1{,}000 \text{ kg/m}^3$$

**Source:** The concept of density as mass per unit volume traces to Archimedes' work *On Floating Bodies* (c. 250 BC), where he used density relationships to determine the purity of a gold crown. Modern density standards are maintained by NIST, with water at 3.98 degrees C and 101,325 Pa defined as 999.972 kg/m cubed (NIST Standard Reference Data).

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Will It Float?

You have a block of unknown material with a mass of 0.5 kg and a volume of 0.0006 m cubed (600 cm cubed). Will it float in water?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Known values: m = **0.5 kg**, V = **0.0006 m cubed**
2. Calculate density: rho = m / V = 0.5 / 0.0006 = **833.33 kg/m cubed**
3. Compare to water: 833.33 < 1,000 kg/m cubed

**Result:** The object has a density of **833.33 kg/m cubed**, which is less than water's 1,000 kg/m cubed, so it **will float**.

**Practical interpretation:** The object's density is close to that of several common materials: HDPE plastic (941-965), oak wood (600-900), and olive oil (911). The fraction submerged equals the object's density divided by water's density: 833.33 / 1,000 = 0.833, meaning 83.3% of the object sits below the waterline — similar to how icebergs float with roughly 90% submerged (ice density = 917 kg/m cubed).

### Example 2: Identifying an Unknown Metal

A machinist has a solid metal sample with a mass of 2.7 kg and a volume of 0.001 m cubed (1,000 cm cubed). What metal is it?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Known values: m = **2.7 kg**, V = **0.001 m cubed**
2. Calculate density: rho = m / V = 2.7 / 0.001 = **2,700 kg/m cubed**
3. Compare to reference densities:
   - Aluminum: 2,700 kg/m cubed
   - Titanium: 4,507 kg/m cubed
   - Steel: 7,850 kg/m cubed
   - Copper: 8,960 kg/m cubed

**Result:** The density of **2,700 kg/m cubed** matches aluminum exactly.

**Practical interpretation:** Density-based identification is a nondestructive testing method used in scrap metal sorting, quality control, and forensic analysis. However, density alone cannot distinguish all materials — magnesium alloys (1,800) and beryllium (1,850) are close enough to require additional testing (hardness, conductivity, or spectrometry) for confirmation.

## Key Factors That Affect Your Results

**Temperature changes density.** Most materials expand when heated, reducing their density. Water at 4 degrees C has a density of 999.972 kg/m cubed but drops to 958.4 kg/m cubed at 100 degrees C — a 4.2% decrease. This is why hot water rises above cold water (convection) and why precise density measurements require temperature control.

**Pressure affects gas density dramatically.** Gases are compressible, so their density varies linearly with pressure. Air at sea level (101,325 Pa) has a density of 1.225 kg/m cubed, but at the summit of Mount Everest (33,700 Pa) it drops to approximately 0.426 kg/m cubed. Liquids and solids are nearly incompressible, so pressure effects are negligible under normal conditions.

**Purity and composition matter.** The density of an alloy differs from the density of its constituent pure metals. 18-karat gold (75% gold, 25% other metals) has a density of approximately 15,600 kg/m cubed versus 19,320 for pure 24-karat gold. Counterfeit gold bars sometimes use tungsten cores (19,250 kg/m cubed) because its density is very close to gold's.

**Porosity reduces effective density.** A solid block of granite has a density of about 2,750 kg/m cubed, but a porous pumice stone (also volcanic rock) has an effective density of only 250-900 kg/m cubed because of trapped air pockets. When measuring density, distinguish between true density (solid material only) and bulk density (material plus voids).

## Assumptions & Limitations

- **Homogeneous material assumed.** The formula assumes uniform density throughout the object. For composite objects (hollow spheres, layered materials), calculate the total mass and total volume separately.
- **Standard conditions.** Results are most accurate at room temperature (20-25 degrees C) and standard atmospheric pressure (101,325 Pa). Extreme temperatures or pressures change density, especially for gases.
- **Positive values only.** All inputs must be positive non-zero values. Negative mass or volume is physically meaningless.
- **SI units required.** Enter values in kg, m cubed, and kg/m cubed. Convert from g/cm cubed (multiply by 1,000) or lb/ft cubed (multiply by 16.0185) before entering.
- **Does not account for buoyancy in air.** Precise mass measurements require buoyancy corrections for the displaced air around the object — significant only for low-density or large objects in metrology labs.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Why does ice float on water?

Ice floats because it is less dense than liquid water — 917 kg/m cubed versus 999.97 kg/m cubed at 0 degrees C. Water is one of the few substances that expands upon freezing. The hydrogen bonds in ice form a crystalline lattice that spaces molecules further apart than in liquid water, reducing the density by about 8.3%. This anomalous expansion is critical for aquatic life: ice forms on the surface of lakes rather than sinking, insulating the liquid water below and preventing lakes from freezing solid.

### How is density measured in practice?

The most common laboratory method is the displacement technique: weigh the object on a balance (mass), then submerge it in a graduated cylinder of water and measure the volume of water displaced (volume). For liquids, a hydrometer floats at a depth proportional to the liquid's density, providing a direct reading. For gases, density is calculated from the ideal gas law using measured temperature, pressure, and molar mass. Digital density meters (oscillating U-tube method) measure liquid density to a precision of 0.0001 g/cm cubed.

### What is specific gravity and how does it relate to density?

Specific gravity (SG) is the ratio of a substance's density to the density of a reference substance — usually water at 4 degrees C (999.972 kg/m cubed) for solids and liquids, or dry air at standard conditions (1.225 kg/m cubed) for gases. Because it is a ratio, specific gravity is dimensionless (no units). An SG of 2.7 means the substance is 2.7 times denser than water. Numerically, specific gravity in g/cm cubed equals density in g/cm cubed (since water's reference density is 1.000 g/cm cubed), which is why the two terms are often used interchangeably — though they are technically different quantities.
