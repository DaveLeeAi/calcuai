## Your Result Explained

Newton's law of universal gravitation states that every mass in the universe attracts every other mass with a force proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between their centers. The gravitational constant G equals 6.67430 times 10 to the negative 11 N times m squared per kg squared, one of the most precisely measured fundamental constants in physics (CODATA 2018 recommended value with relative uncertainty of 2.2 times 10 to the negative 5).

The result represents the mutual gravitational attraction between two objects. The force is always attractive — gravity never repels. A 70 kg person on Earth's surface experiences about 687 N of gravitational force, which we commonly call weight. The same person standing on Mars (mass 6.417 times 10 to the 23 kg, radius 3,389,500 m) would experience only 259 N — roughly 37.7% of their Earth weight. On Jupiter (mass 1.898 times 10 to the 27 kg, radius 69,911,000 m), the same person experiences 1,740 N — about 2.53 times Earth gravity.

The inverse-square law means gravity weakens rapidly with distance. Doubling the separation between two objects reduces the gravitational force to one-quarter. At the International Space Station's orbital altitude of 408 km above Earth's surface, gravitational acceleration drops to about 8.67 m/s squared — only 11.5% less than at the surface. Astronauts appear weightless not because gravity vanishes but because they are in free fall. For related calculations, the [Force Calculator](/calculators/science/force-calculator) solves Newton's second law F = ma, and the [Acceleration Calculator](/calculators/science/acceleration-calculator) computes acceleration from force and mass.

## The Formula

### Gravitational Force from Two Masses and Distance

$$F = G \times \frac{m_1 \times m_2}{r^2}$$

Where:

- **F** = gravitational force, in newtons (N)
- **G** = gravitational constant = 6.67430 times 10 to the negative 11 N times m squared per kg squared
- **m1** = first mass, in kilograms (kg)
- **m2** = second mass, in kilograms (kg)
- **r** = center-to-center distance, in meters (m)

### Mass from Force, Other Mass, and Distance

$$m_1 = \frac{F \times r^2}{G \times m_2}$$

### Distance from Force and Both Masses

$$r = \sqrt{\frac{G \times m_1 \times m_2}{F}}$$

The gravitational force is always attractive and acts along the line connecting the two mass centers. The force on mass 1 due to mass 2 is equal in magnitude and opposite in direction to the force on mass 2 due to mass 1 (Newton's third law). The gravitational constant G was first measured experimentally by Henry Cavendish in 1798 using a torsion balance. His result of 6.74 times 10 to the negative 11 was remarkably close to the modern CODATA 2018 value.

### Gravitational Acceleration at a Surface

$$g = \frac{G \times M}{R^2}$$

Where **M** is the body's mass and **R** is its radius. For Earth: g = 6.67430 times 10 to the negative 11 times 5.972 times 10 to the 24 divided by (6.371 times 10 to the 6) squared = 9.82 m/s squared (the standard value 9.80665 m/s squared accounts for centrifugal effects and is defined at 45 degrees latitude at sea level).

**Source:** Isaac Newton, *Philosophiae Naturalis Principia Mathematica* (1687), Book III: The System of the World. Gravitational constant value from CODATA 2018 Recommended Values, National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST).

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Weight of a Person on Earth

What gravitational force does Earth exert on a 70 kg person standing at sea level?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Known values: m1 = **5.972 times 10 to the 24 kg** (Earth), m2 = **70 kg** (person), r = **6,371,000 m** (Earth's mean radius)
2. Apply the formula: F = G times m1 times m2 / r squared = 6.67430 times 10 to the negative 11 times 5.972 times 10 to the 24 times 70 / (6,371,000) squared
3. Numerator: 6.67430 times 10 to the negative 11 times 5.972 times 10 to the 24 times 70 = **2.789 times 10 to the 16**
4. Denominator: (6.371 times 10 to the 6) squared = **4.059 times 10 to the 13**
5. F = 2.789 times 10 to the 16 / 4.059 times 10 to the 13 = **687.07 N**
6. Convert: 687.07 times 0.224809 = **154.5 lbf**

**Result:** The gravitational force is **687.07 N** (154.5 lbf, 0.687 kN).

**Practical interpretation:** This value is the person's weight at sea level. It differs slightly from the commonly cited value of 686.47 N (from W = mg = 70 times 9.80665) because standard gravity g = 9.80665 m/s squared is a defined conventional value, while this calculation uses the actual gravitational formula. The difference (about 0.09%) comes from centrifugal effects of Earth's rotation and the oblate shape of the Earth, which the simple formula does not model.

### Example 2: Earth-Moon Gravitational Force

What is the gravitational force between the Earth and the Moon?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Known values: m1 = **5.972 times 10 to the 24 kg** (Earth), m2 = **7.342 times 10 to the 22 kg** (Moon), r = **3.844 times 10 to the 8 m** (mean orbital distance)
2. Apply the formula: F = 6.67430 times 10 to the negative 11 times 5.972 times 10 to the 24 times 7.342 times 10 to the 22 / (3.844 times 10 to the 8) squared
3. Numerator: 6.67430 times 10 to the negative 11 times 4.384 times 10 to the 47 = **2.925 times 10 to the 37**
4. Denominator: (3.844 times 10 to the 8) squared = **1.478 times 10 to the 17**
5. F = 2.925 times 10 to the 37 / 1.478 times 10 to the 17 = **1.978 times 10 to the 20 N**

**Result:** The Earth-Moon gravitational force is approximately **1.98 times 10 to the 20 N** (19.8 exanewtons).

**Practical interpretation:** This enormous force keeps the Moon in orbit around Earth. Despite being about 384,400 km away, the Moon's large mass (7.342 times 10 to the 22 kg) produces a gravitational pull strong enough to raise ocean tides on Earth. The Sun's gravitational pull on Earth is about 178 times stronger (3.54 times 10 to the 22 N), which keeps Earth in its annual orbit. ## Key Factors That Affect Your Results

**Distance has the strongest effect due to the inverse-square law.** Gravitational force decreases with the square of the distance. Moving from Earth's surface (6,371 km from center) to geostationary orbit (42,164 km from center) reduces gravitational acceleration from 9.82 m/s squared to just 0.224 m/s squared — a factor of 43.8 reduction. This is why satellites in high orbits need very little fuel to maintain position.

**Mass scales linearly with force.** Doubling either mass exactly doubles the gravitational force. Jupiter, with a mass 317.8 times Earth's, exerts a gravitational pull that dominates the outer solar system. Its gravity deflects asteroids and comets, acting as a partial shield for the inner planets. A 70 kg person on Jupiter's cloud tops would experience 1,740 N of gravitational force compared to 687 N on Earth.

**The gravitational constant G is universal but tiny.** At 6.67430 times 10 to the negative 11, G ensures that gravitational force between everyday objects is negligibly small. Two 100 kg people standing 1 meter apart attract each other with only 6.67 times 10 to the negative 7 N — about the weight of a grain of sand. Only astronomical masses produce noticeable gravitational effects. The [Energy Calculator](/calculators/science/energy-calculator) can compute gravitational potential energy, which depends on the same G constant.

**Oblateness and density variations affect real-world gravity.** Earth is not a perfect sphere — it bulges at the equator (equatorial radius 6,378 km vs. polar radius 6,357 km). This means surface gravity varies from 9.780 m/s squared at the equator to 9.832 m/s squared at the poles. For precision work in geodesy, satellite navigation, and ballistics, local gravity measurements or models like WGS84 replace the simple point-mass approximation.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- **Point-mass approximation.** The formula assumes both objects are point masses or perfect spheres with uniform density. For irregularly shaped bodies (asteroids, comets), the actual gravitational field can deviate significantly from this formula.
- **Newtonian gravity only.** For extremely strong gravitational fields (near black holes, neutron stars) or very precise measurements (GPS satellite clocks), general relativity corrections are required. The Newtonian formula is accurate to better than one part in a million for all solar system applications.
- **No relativistic effects.** At speeds approaching the speed of light, gravitational effects couple with spacetime curvature in ways not captured by this formula. For orbital calculations near the Sun's surface or in binary pulsar systems, post-Newtonian corrections are essential.
- **Two-body problem only.** This calculator computes the force between exactly two masses. In multi-body systems (three or more objects), the total force on each object is the vector sum of pairwise gravitational forces, which generally has no closed-form solution.
- **Static calculation.** The formula gives the instantaneous force at a specific distance. As orbiting bodies move, the distance changes continuously, and the force changes accordingly.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the gravitational constant G?

The gravitational constant G equals 6.67430 times 10 to the negative 11 newton meters squared per kilogram squared. It is a fundamental physical constant that determines the strength of gravitational attraction between masses. First measured by Henry Cavendish in 1798 using a torsion balance experiment, G remains one of the least precisely known fundamental constants due to the extreme weakness of gravity compared to other fundamental forces. The CODATA 2018 recommended value has a relative standard uncertainty of 2.2 times 10 to the negative 5, compared to the fine-structure constant which is known to about 1.5 times 10 to the negative 10.

### Why do astronauts float in the International Space Station?

Astronauts float not because gravity is absent but because they are in continuous free fall around Earth. At the ISS altitude of 408 km, gravitational acceleration is still 8.67 m/s squared — about 88.5% of the surface value of 9.81 m/s squared. The station and everyone inside it fall toward Earth at exactly the same rate, creating the sensation of weightlessness. This condition is properly called microgravity, not zero gravity, because small tidal effects and atmospheric drag create residual accelerations of roughly 10 to the negative 6 g.

### How does gravitational force compare to electrostatic force?

Gravitational force is extraordinarily weak compared to the electrostatic force described by Coulomb's law. The ratio of electrostatic to gravitational force between a proton and an electron is approximately 2.27 times 10 to the 39 — the electrostatic force wins by 39 orders of magnitude. Gravity dominates at astronomical scales only because large bodies are electrically neutral (positive and negative charges cancel), while mass always adds.
