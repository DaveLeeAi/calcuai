## Your Result Explained

Frequency measures how many complete cycles of a repeating event occur per second. A frequency of 440 Hz means the string (or air column, or electromagnetic field) completes 440 full oscillation cycles every second. The period is simply the reciprocal: each cycle takes 1/440 = 0.0022727 seconds (2.2727 milliseconds) to complete.

The calculator operates in two modes. The period mode (f = 1/T) is the direct relationship — frequency and period are mathematical inverses of each other. The wave mode (f = v/λ) connects frequency to the physical properties of waves: a wave traveling faster or having a shorter wavelength has a higher frequency. Sound waves in air at 20°C travel at 343 m/s, so a 440 Hz tone has a wavelength of 343/440 = 0.780 meters (78.0 cm). Electromagnetic waves in vacuum travel at 299,792,458 m/s, so a 100 MHz FM radio signal has a wavelength of 2.998 meters.

Unit conversions provide context across domains. ## The Formula

### Frequency from Period

$$f = \frac{1}{T}$$

Where:

- **f** = frequency, in hertz (Hz)
- **T** = period (time for one complete cycle), in seconds (s)

The relationship is perfectly reciprocal: doubling the frequency halves the period, and vice versa. A pendulum with a 2-second period swings at 0.5 Hz; a hummingbird's wings beating 80 times per second have a period of 12.5 milliseconds.

### Period from Frequency

$$T = \frac{1}{f}$$

### Frequency from Wave Speed and Wavelength

$$f = \frac{v}{\lambda}$$

Where:

- **f** = frequency, in hertz (Hz)
- **v** = wave speed (phase velocity), in meters per second (m/s)
- **λ** (lambda) = wavelength, in meters (m)

This equation connects the three fundamental properties of any wave. For a fixed wave speed (like sound in air at a given temperature), higher frequency means shorter wavelength. A 20 Hz bass note in air has a wavelength of 343/20 = 17.15 meters; a 20,000 Hz treble tone has a wavelength of 343/20,000 = 0.01715 meters (1.715 cm).

### Unit Conversions

$$1 \text{ Hz} = 0.001 \text{ kHz} = 10^{-6} \text{ MHz} = 10^{-9} \text{ GHz}$$

$$1 \text{ Hz} = 60 \text{ RPM (revolutions per minute)}$$

One hertz equals one cycle per second. The unit is named after Heinrich Hertz, who first conclusively proved the existence of electromagnetic waves in 1887.

**Source:** Heinrich Hertz demonstrated electromagnetic wave generation and detection experimentally in 1887, confirming James Clerk Maxwell's 1865 theoretical predictions. The hertz was adopted as the SI unit of frequency by the 13th General Conference on Weights and Measures (CGPM, 1967), replacing the earlier designation "cycles per second" (cps).

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Musical Note A4 from Period

A tuning fork vibrates with a measured period of 2.2727 milliseconds. What frequency does it produce, and what is the corresponding wavelength in air at 20°C?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Convert period to seconds: T = 2.2727 ms = **0.0022727 s**
2. Apply f = 1/T: f = 1 / 0.0022727 = **440.0 Hz**
3. Identify the note: 440 Hz is the concert pitch **A4**, the international tuning standard
4. Calculate wavelength in air: λ = v/f = 343 / 440.0 = **0.7795 m** (77.95 cm)
5. Convert to kHz: 440.0 / 1,000 = **0.440 kHz**
6. Convert to RPM: 440.0 × 60 = **26,400 RPM**

**Result:** The tuning fork produces a frequency of **440.0 Hz** (0.440 kHz) with a wavelength of **77.95 cm** in air.

**Practical interpretation:** The 440 Hz standard (also called "A440" or "Stuttgart pitch") was formally adopted by the International Organization for Standardization as ISO 16 in 1975, though orchestras had converged on this frequency by the 1950s. Historically, concert pitch has varied widely — Baroque ensembles often tuned to A = 415 Hz (a semitone lower), and some modern orchestras tune slightly sharp at A = 442–443 Hz for a brighter sound. Each semitone represents a frequency ratio of 2^(1/12) ≈ 1.05946, so A#4 is 440 × 1.05946 = 466.16 Hz, and G#4 is 440 / 1.05946 = 415.30 Hz.

### Example 2: FM Radio Signal Wavelength and Frequency

An FM radio station broadcasts at a wavelength of 3.125 meters. Given that radio waves travel at the speed of light (299,792,458 m/s in vacuum), what is the broadcast frequency?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Known values: v = **299,792,458 m/s**, λ = **3.125 m**
2. Apply f = v/λ: f = 299,792,458 / 3.125 = **95,933,587 Hz**
3. Convert to MHz: 95,933,587 / 1,000,000 = **95.93 MHz**
4. Calculate the period: T = 1 / 95,933,587 = **1.0424 × 10⁻⁸ s** (10.424 nanoseconds)
5. Verify: f × λ = 95,933,587 × 3.125 = 299,792,459 ≈ 299,792,458 m/s (rounding confirms)

**Result:** The station broadcasts at **95.93 MHz** with a period of **10.42 nanoseconds** per cycle.

**Practical interpretation:** This frequency falls within the FM radio band (87.5–108.0 MHz). In the United States, FM channels are spaced 200 kHz apart, so this station would be tuned as "95.9 FM." The 3.125-meter wavelength explains why FM antennas are typically 75–80 cm long — a quarter-wavelength antenna (λ/4 = 0.781 m) provides the most efficient reception. AM radio, by contrast, uses frequencies around 540–1,600 kHz (wavelengths of 188–556 meters), which is why AM signals diffract around buildings and hills more effectively but carry less audio bandwidth.

## Key Factors That Affect Your Results

**Wave speed depends on the medium, not the frequency.** Sound at 440 Hz and sound at 4,400 Hz both travel at 343 m/s in 20°C air — only their wavelengths differ (0.780 m vs. 0.078 m). When a sound wave passes from air into water (where v ≈ 1,480 m/s), its frequency stays the same but its wavelength increases by a factor of 1,480/343 = 4.31. This principle holds for all waves: frequency is set by the source, while wavelength adjusts to match the medium's wave speed.

**The electromagnetic spectrum spans over 20 orders of magnitude in frequency.** Radio waves range from 3 kHz to 300 GHz. Infrared spans 300 GHz to 430 THz. Visible light occupies a narrow band from 430 THz (red, 700 nm) to 770 THz (violet, 390 nm). Ultraviolet, X-rays, and gamma rays extend beyond 770 THz. All electromagnetic waves travel at 299,792,458 m/s in vacuum, so frequency and wavelength are inversely proportional across the entire spectrum. The [Wave Speed Calculator](/calculators/science/wave-speed-calculator) handles these conversions directly.

**Resonance occurs when a driving frequency matches a system's natural frequency.** Every physical object has natural frequencies determined by its mass, stiffness, and geometry. A wine glass shatters when exposed to a sound wave matching its resonant frequency (typically 400–700 Hz for a crystal glass) because the oscillation amplitude builds with each cycle until the glass exceeds its elastic limit. Musical instruments exploit resonance constructively — a guitar body amplifies string vibrations at its resonant frequencies, shaping the instrument's characteristic tone.

**The Doppler effect shifts the observed frequency when source and observer are in relative motion.** An ambulance siren emitting at 700 Hz sounds higher-pitched (approximately 740 Hz) when approaching at 20 m/s and lower-pitched (approximately 664 Hz) when receding at the same speed. The formula is f_observed = f_source × (v_sound ± v_observer) / (v_sound ∓ v_source). This effect applies to all waves, including light — astronomers use redshift (lower observed frequency) to measure the recession velocity of distant galaxies.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- **Ideal wave behavior assumed.** The formula f = v/λ applies to non-dispersive waves where velocity is independent of frequency. In dispersive media (glass, seawater), different frequencies travel at different speeds, and the formula applies only to individual frequency components.
- **Phase velocity only.** The wave speed v in f = v/λ is the phase velocity. For wave packets, the group velocity (which carries energy) may differ from the phase velocity.
- **Vacuum speed for electromagnetic waves.** Radio, light, and other EM waves travel at exactly 299,792,458 m/s in vacuum. In glass (n ≈ 1.5), the speed drops to about 199,861,639 m/s, changing the wavelength but not the frequency.
- **Temperature affects sound speed.** The default 343 m/s applies to dry air at 20°C. At 0°C, sound speed drops to 331 m/s; at 35°C, it rises to 351 m/s. Humidity has a smaller effect (about +1 m/s per 10% humidity increase).
- **Positive values only.** Frequency, period, wave speed, and wavelength must all be positive.
- **Single-frequency (monochromatic) waves.** Real signals are typically composed of many frequencies. This calculator handles one frequency at a time.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the difference between frequency and wavelength?

Frequency is the number of wave cycles passing a fixed point per second, measured in hertz (Hz); wavelength is the spatial distance between two consecutive identical points on a wave (such as crest to crest), measured in meters. They are inversely proportional for a given wave speed: λ = v/f. A 1,000 Hz sound wave in air (343 m/s) has a wavelength of 0.343 m (34.3 cm), while a 1,000 Hz electromagnetic wave in vacuum (299,792,458 m/s) has a wavelength of 299,792 m (299.8 km). The same frequency produces vastly different wavelengths depending on the wave's speed in its medium.

### What frequencies can humans hear?

Healthy young adults can hear frequencies from approximately 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz (20 kHz), spanning about 10 octaves. The lower limit of 20 Hz corresponds to a deep rumble felt as much as heard — pipe organ pedal notes and large subwoofers operate near this threshold. The upper limit of 20 kHz decreases with age: most adults over 40 cannot hear above 14,000–16,000 Hz, and by age 60, the upper limit typically falls to 8,000–12,000 Hz (a condition called presbycusis). Human hearing is most sensitive between 2,000 and 5,000 Hz, the frequency range of speech consonants and baby cries — an evolutionary advantage for communication and infant survival.

### What is the electromagnetic spectrum?

The electromagnetic spectrum is the complete range of electromagnetic radiation frequencies, from extremely low frequency (ELF) radio waves below 3 Hz to gamma rays exceeding 30 exahertz (3 × 10¹⁹ Hz). All electromagnetic waves travel at 299,792,458 m/s in vacuum and differ only in frequency and wavelength. The spectrum is conventionally divided into bands: radio (below 300 GHz, λ > 1 mm), infrared (300 GHz–430 THz, λ = 1 mm to 700 nm), visible light (430–770 THz, λ = 700–390 nm), ultraviolet (770 THz–30 PHz, λ = 390–10 nm), X-rays (30 PHz–30 EHz, λ = 10–0.01 nm), and gamma rays (above 30 EHz, λ < 0.01 nm). Only the visible band — spanning barely one octave of frequency — is detectable by the human eye.
