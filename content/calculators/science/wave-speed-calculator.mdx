## Your Result Explained

The wave equation v = fλ connects three quantities that fully describe a traveling wave: its speed through a medium, how many oscillations it completes per second (frequency), and the physical distance between consecutive wave crests (wavelength). This calculator rearranges that single equation to solve for whichever variable you leave blank.

Wave speed depends on the medium, not on the wave itself. Sound in air at 20°C always travels at approximately 343 m/s regardless of pitch. A low bass note at 80 Hz has a wavelength of 4.29 meters, while a high violin harmonic at 4,000 Hz has a wavelength of just 0.086 meters — but both travel at the same 343 m/s through room-temperature air. Electromagnetic waves in a vacuum always travel at 299,792,458 m/s (the speed of light), whether they are radio waves, visible light, or gamma rays.

The period (T = 1/f) is also computed, giving you the time for one complete oscillation. For related physics calculations, the [Velocity Calculator](/calculators/science/velocity-calculator) handles distance-speed-time problems, and the [Energy Calculator](/calculators/science/energy-calculator) covers photon energy from frequency via E = hf.

## The Formula

### Wave Speed from Frequency and Wavelength

$$v = f \times \lambda$$

Where:

- **v** = wave speed, in meters per second (m/s)
- **f** = frequency, in hertz (Hz, cycles per second)
- **λ** (lambda) = wavelength, in meters (m)

### Frequency from Wave Speed and Wavelength

$$f = \frac{v}{\lambda}$$

### Wavelength from Wave Speed and Frequency

$$\lambda = \frac{v}{f}$$

### Period from Frequency

$$T = \frac{1}{f}$$

Where **T** is the period in seconds — the time for one complete wave cycle.

These three rearrangements of v = fλ describe the same physical relationship. Doubling the frequency while holding wave speed constant halves the wavelength. This inverse relationship between frequency and wavelength is universal: it applies to sound waves, electromagnetic radiation, water waves, seismic waves, and waves on a vibrating string.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Speed of Sound from a Tuning Fork

A tuning fork vibrates at 440 Hz (the A4 concert pitch). The measured wavelength of the sound wave is 0.78 meters. What is the wave speed?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Known values: f = **440 Hz**, λ = **0.78 m**
2. Apply the formula: v = f × λ = 440 × 0.78 = **343.2 m/s**
3. Convert to km/h: 343.2 × 3.6 = **1,235.5 km/h**
4. Calculate the period: T = 1/f = 1/440 = **0.002273 s** (2.273 milliseconds)

**Result:** The wave speed is **343.2 m/s** (1,235.5 km/h), confirming the well-known speed of sound in air at approximately 20°C.

**Practical interpretation:** This result matches the standard speed of sound because the tuning fork produces sound waves traveling through room-temperature air. If the same 440 Hz tone were produced underwater, the wavelength would stretch to approximately 3.36 meters (1,480/440) because sound travels 4.3 times faster in water than in air.

### Example 2: Wavelength of a Wi-Fi Signal

A standard Wi-Fi router operates at 2.4 GHz. Electromagnetic waves travel at the speed of light in air (approximately 299,792,458 m/s). What is the wavelength of the Wi-Fi signal?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Known values: v = **299,792,458 m/s**, f = 2.4 GHz = **2,400,000,000 Hz**
2. Apply the formula: λ = v / f = 299,792,458 / 2,400,000,000 = **0.12491 m**
3. Convert to centimeters: 0.12491 × 100 = **12.49 cm**
4. Calculate the period: T = 1 / 2,400,000,000 = **4.167 × 10⁻¹⁰ s** (0.417 nanoseconds)

**Result:** The Wi-Fi signal has a wavelength of **12.49 cm** (about 4.9 inches).

**Practical interpretation:** This wavelength explains why Wi-Fi signals are partially blocked by walls, doors, and human bodies — objects roughly the same size as the wavelength interact strongly with the wave through diffraction and absorption. The newer 5 GHz Wi-Fi band has a wavelength of only 6 cm, which is why it offers faster speeds but shorter range and poorer wall penetration compared to 2.4 GHz.

## Key Factors That Affect Your Results

**The medium determines wave speed, not the wave.** Sound waves always travel at the same speed through a given medium at a given temperature, regardless of frequency or amplitude. In 20°C air, a 50 Hz bass rumble and a 15,000 Hz high-pitched whine both travel at 343 m/s. This is why a symphony orchestra sounds coherent from across a concert hall — all frequencies arrive simultaneously. Electromagnetic waves in vacuum always travel at exactly 299,792,458 m/s regardless of frequency, from radio waves to gamma rays.

**Temperature significantly affects sound speed.** The speed of sound in air increases by approximately 0.6 m/s for each 1°C rise in temperature. At 0°C, sound travels at 331 m/s; at 35°C, it reaches 351 m/s. This 6% variation matters in acoustic engineering, outdoor concert design, and precision sonar measurements. The formula for sound speed in air is approximately v = 331.3 + 0.606T, where T is temperature in Celsius.

**Electromagnetic waves slow down in materials.** Light travels at 299,792,458 m/s in vacuum but slows to about 225,000,000 m/s in water (refractive index n = 1.33) and roughly 200,000,000 m/s in glass (n ≈ 1.5). The refractive index n equals c/v, where c is the vacuum speed of light and v is the speed in the material. This speed reduction causes refraction — the bending of light at material boundaries that makes lenses and prisms work.

**Dispersion causes different frequencies to travel at different speeds.** In most real media, wave speed varies slightly with frequency — a phenomenon called dispersion. This is why white light splits into a rainbow through a prism: shorter wavelengths (blue/violet) slow down more than longer wavelengths (red). In non-dispersive media (sound in air over the audible range, light in vacuum), the wave speed is truly constant across all frequencies.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- **Non-dispersive medium assumed.** The calculator uses a single wave speed for all frequencies. In dispersive media (glass for light, deep water for ocean waves), the speed varies with frequency.
- **Plane wave approximation.** Results assume an idealized plane wave propagating in one direction. Near a sound source or through apertures, diffraction effects cause the wave pattern to diverge.
- **Constant medium properties.** Temperature, pressure, and density are assumed constant. In practice, sound speed varies with altitude, humidity, and air pressure gradients.
- **Phase velocity only.** This calculator computes phase velocity (the speed of individual wave crests). Group velocity — the speed at which a wave packet's envelope travels — can differ in dispersive media.
- **Positive values only.** All inputs must be positive. Frequency and wavelength cannot be zero or negative.
- **Classical physics only.** At quantum scales, wave-particle duality means photons and matter waves follow quantum mechanical rules beyond the scope of v = fλ.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How fast does sound travel?

The speed of sound in dry air at 20°C is 343 m/s (1,235 km/h, 767 mph, Mach 1). This speed increases with temperature at roughly 0.6 m/s per degree Celsius. Sound travels much faster in denser media: approximately 1,480 m/s in fresh water at 25°C, 1,531 m/s in seawater, and 5,960 m/s in steel. The speed of sound also varies with humidity — moist air is slightly less dense than dry air at the same temperature, so sound travels about 0.1–0.6% faster in humid conditions.

### What is the speed of light?

The speed of light in a vacuum is exactly 299,792,458 meters per second (approximately 3 × 10⁸ m/s, or 670,616,629 mph). This value is a defined constant in the International System of Units — the meter is actually defined in terms of the speed of light. All electromagnetic waves (radio, microwave, infrared, visible light, ultraviolet, X-rays, gamma rays) travel at this speed in vacuum. In transparent materials, light slows down by a factor equal to the material's refractive index: v = c/n.

### Why do waves travel at different speeds in different materials?

Wave speed depends on the mechanical or electromagnetic properties of the medium. For sound (mechanical waves), speed is determined by the medium's elasticity and density: v = √(B/ρ), where B is the bulk modulus (stiffness) and ρ is density. Steel is far stiffer than air relative to its density, so sound travels 17 times faster in steel (5,960 m/s) than in air (343 m/s). For electromagnetic waves, speed in a material depends on its electric permittivity and magnetic permeability, which together determine the refractive index. Denser optical materials slow light more because their atomic structure interacts more strongly with oscillating electric fields.
