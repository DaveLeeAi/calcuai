## Your Result Explained

Power measures how quickly energy is transferred or work is performed. Two engines can each do 10,000 joules of work, but the one that does it in 5 seconds (2,000 W) is twice as powerful as the one that takes 10 seconds (1,000 W). The distinction matters everywhere: a 100-watt light bulb converts 100 joules of electrical energy into light and heat every second, and a 150-horsepower car engine produces 111,855 watts at peak output.

The calculator supports two modes because power appears in different forms depending on the problem. P = W/t is used when you know the total work done and the time interval — for example, calculating how much power an elevator motor needs to lift a 1,000 kg car 3 meters in 12 seconds (W = mgh = 29,430 J, P = 2,452.5 W). P = Fv is used when you know the sustained force and the velocity — for example, calculating the power output of a cyclist pedaling against 40 N of air resistance at 12 m/s (P = 480 W).

Unit conversions place your result in practical context. For mechanical engineering and automotive applications, see the [Work Calculator](/calculators/science/work-calculator) for the energy input and the [Force Calculator](/calculators/science/force-calculator) for net force analysis. ## The Formula

### Power from Work and Time

$$P = \frac{W}{t}$$

Where:

- **P** = power, in watts (W)
- **W** = work done or energy transferred, in joules (J)
- **t** = time elapsed, in seconds (s)

One watt equals one joule per second (1 W = 1 J/s). A 60-watt light bulb consumes 60 joules of electrical energy every second.

### Power from Force and Velocity

$$P = F \cdot v$$

Where:

- **P** = power, in watts (W)
- **F** = constant force applied in the direction of motion, in newtons (N)
- **v** = velocity of the object, in meters per second (m/s)

This form is derived from P = W/t by substituting W = Fd and noting that v = d/t, giving P = Fd/t = F(d/t) = Fv. It applies when force and velocity are constant and aligned.

### Horsepower Definition

$$1 \text{ hp (mechanical)} = 550 \text{ ft·lbf/s} = 745.6999 \text{ W}$$

James Watt defined one horsepower as the rate at which a draft horse could perform sustained work: lifting 550 pounds one foot per second, or equivalently, 33,000 foot-pounds per minute.

### Unit Conversions

$$1 \text{ W} = 0.001 \text{ kW} = 0.001341 \text{ hp} = 3.41214 \text{ BTU/h} = 0.737562 \text{ ft·lbf/s}$$

**Source:** James Watt introduced the horsepower unit in 1782 to compare steam engine output to horse labor. The watt was adopted as the SI unit of power by the 11th General Conference on Weights and Measures (CGPM, 1960), defined as 1 W = 1 J/s = 1 kg·m²/s³.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Weightlifter Deadlift Power Output

A powerlifter deadlifts a 200 kg barbell from the floor to lockout height (0.70 m) in 2.0 seconds. What is the average mechanical power output?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Calculate the work done against gravity: W = mgh = 200 × 9.80665 × 0.70 = **1,372.93 J**
2. Known time: t = **2.0 s**
3. Apply P = W/t: P = 1,372.93 / 2.0 = **686.47 W**
4. Convert to horsepower: 686.47 / 745.70 = **0.921 hp**
5. Convert to kilowatts: 686.47 / 1,000 = **0.686 kW**
6. Convert to BTU/h: 686.47 × 3.41214 = **2,342.0 BTU/h**

**Result:** The lifter generates **686.47 watts** (0.921 hp) of average mechanical power during the lift.

**Practical interpretation:** Elite powerlifters pulling 400+ kg can exceed 3,000 watts during the initial acceleration phase of a deadlift — brief peak power outputs rivaling a stair-climbing motor. However, this is instantaneous peak power; the average over the full lift is lower because the barbell decelerates near lockout. A recreational athlete might generate 200–400 W during a similar movement. For comparison, sustained cycling power for a professional tour rider is about 350–400 W over a 40 km time trial.

### Example 2: Car Engine Power at Highway Speed

A car maintains a constant highway speed of 30 m/s (108 km/h, 67.1 mph) against total resistive forces (aerodynamic drag + rolling resistance) of 600 newtons. What power must the engine deliver to the wheels?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Known values: F = **600 N**, v = **30 m/s**
2. Apply P = Fv: P = 600 × 30 = **18,000 W**
3. Convert to kilowatts: 18,000 / 1,000 = **18.0 kW**
4. Convert to horsepower: 18,000 / 745.70 = **24.14 hp**
5. Convert to BTU/h: 18,000 × 3.41214 = **61,418.5 BTU/h**

**Result:** The engine must deliver **18,000 watts** (18.0 kW, 24.14 hp) to the wheels to maintain 108 km/h.

**Practical interpretation:** This 24 hp at the wheels is far less than the car's rated peak power (perhaps 150–200 hp) because peak power is only needed during hard acceleration. At steady highway speed, the engine operates at a fraction of its capacity. The actual engine output is higher than 18 kW because the drivetrain (transmission, differential, axles) loses roughly 15–20% to friction, so the engine produces approximately 21–22 kW internally. Aerodynamic drag scales with the cube of velocity (since drag force scales with v² and power = Fv), so increasing speed from 108 km/h to 144 km/h nearly triples the required power to roughly 50 kW.

## Key Factors That Affect Your Results

**Instantaneous power and average power can differ dramatically.** A sprinter exploding from the blocks generates 2,000–3,000 W of peak instantaneous power, but their average over a 10-second 100-meter dash is roughly 700–900 W. P = W/t always gives average power over the time interval. For instantaneous power, use P = Fv at a specific moment. In engine dynamometer testing, peak power (e.g., 150 hp at 6,000 RPM) is always instantaneous — the engine never sustains that output indefinitely.

**Efficiency losses mean input power always exceeds useful output power.** An incandescent bulb rated at 60 W converts only about 5% (3 W) to visible light; the rest becomes heat. A gasoline engine converts roughly 25–30% of fuel energy to mechanical work at the crankshaft. An electric motor achieves 85–95% efficiency. When calculating power requirements for a system, always divide the required output power by the efficiency to find the input: P_input = P_output / η.

**Power and energy are not the same quantity.** Power is the rate of energy transfer (joules per second); energy is the total transfer (joules). A 1,000 W microwave running for 60 seconds uses 60,000 J (60 kJ) of energy. A 100 W light bulb running for 10 hours uses 3,600,000 J (1 kWh). The power rating tells you how fast energy is consumed; the operating time determines the total energy. Use the [Energy Calculator](/calculators/science/energy-calculator) to convert between energy units.

**Human power output varies widely with activity and duration.** A sedentary adult produces about 75–100 W at rest (basal metabolic rate). Brisk walking requires 200–300 W. A trained cyclist sustains 250–400 W for an hour. An Olympic rower peaks at 2,000+ W during a race start. The critical distinction is between metabolic power (total energy burned) and mechanical power (useful work output) — human muscles are only 20–25% efficient, so a cyclist producing 300 W of mechanical power is burning roughly 1,200–1,500 W of metabolic energy.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- **Constant force and velocity assumed (Fv mode).** The P = Fv formula gives instantaneous power only if force and velocity are constant and aligned. For varying force or speed, instantaneous power changes continuously.
- **Average power only (W/t mode).** P = W/t returns the average power over the time interval. It does not reveal peak or minimum power during that interval.
- **No efficiency modeling.** The calculator computes ideal mechanical power. Real systems have losses from friction, air resistance, heat transfer, and electrical resistance that are not included.
- **Linear motion only.** Rotational power (P = τω, where τ is torque and ω is angular velocity) requires separate treatment. Engine dyno ratings use rotational power.
- **Force aligned with velocity (Fv mode).** If force and velocity are not parallel, use P = Fv cos(θ). The calculator assumes θ = 0° between force and velocity.
- **Positive values only.** Negative power (energy being absorbed, as in regenerative braking) is not modeled in this interface.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is one horsepower exactly?

One mechanical horsepower equals exactly 550 foot-pounds per second, or 745.6999 watts. James Watt defined this unit in 1782 by estimating that a strong draft horse could sustain 33,000 foot-pounds of work per minute — equivalent to lifting 550 pounds one foot every second, or lifting 33,000 pounds one foot every minute. The metric horsepower (PS, used in continental Europe) is slightly different: 1 PS = 75 kgf·m/s = 735.499 W. Modern engineering increasingly uses kilowatts instead, but horsepower remains standard in automotive and industrial contexts in the United States and United Kingdom.

### How are watts and horsepower related?

One mechanical horsepower equals 745.70 watts, so to convert watts to horsepower, divide by 745.70; to convert horsepower to watts, multiply by 745.70. A 200-horsepower car engine produces 149,140 watts (149.1 kW) at peak output. A 1,500-watt space heater generates 2.01 hp of thermal power. The relationship is purely a unit conversion — both measure the same physical quantity (rate of energy transfer). In the automotive industry, European manufacturers typically list power in kW while American manufacturers use hp, but the ratings are interchangeable: a "100 kW" engine is a "134.1 hp" engine.

### How much power does the average human produce?

A healthy adult at rest produces approximately 80–100 watts of metabolic power, which is almost entirely converted to body heat. During moderate exercise like brisk walking, total metabolic power rises to 300–500 W, of which 60–125 W is useful mechanical power (the rest is heat). A trained cyclist can sustain 250–400 W of mechanical output for one hour — professional Tour de France riders average 350–400 W during mountain stages lasting 4–5 hours. Peak instantaneous power during explosive movements reaches much higher: an Olympic sprinter generates 2,000–2,500 W during the first two seconds of a 100-meter dash, and a weightlifter can exceed 3,000 W during the pull phase of a clean and jerk.
