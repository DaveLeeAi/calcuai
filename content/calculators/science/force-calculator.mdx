## Your Result Explained

Newton's second law of motion states that force equals mass times acceleration. It is the single most applied equation in classical mechanics, connecting the three quantities that govern every push, pull, collision, and gravitational interaction you encounter. This calculator rearranges that one equation to solve for whichever variable you leave blank: enter mass and acceleration to get force, enter force and acceleration to get mass, or enter force and mass to get acceleration.

The result represents the net force — the total unbalanced force acting on the object. A 1,200 kg car accelerating at 3 m/s squared requires a net force of 3,600 N, but the engine must produce more than 3,600 N because friction, air resistance, and road grade all oppose the motion. Net force is what remains after all opposing forces cancel out. For gravitational weight calculations, the acceleration is Earth's standard gravity: 9.80665 m/s squared.

Unit conversions are provided automatically: newtons (N), kilonewtons (kN), pounds-force (lbf), and dynes (dyn). For related physics calculations, the [Acceleration Calculator](/calculators/science/acceleration-calculator) solves for velocity change over time, and the [Energy Calculator](/calculators/science/energy-calculator) computes kinetic energy from mass and velocity.

## The Formula

### Force from Mass and Acceleration

$$F = m \times a$$

Where:

- **F** = force, in newtons (N)
- **m** = mass, in kilograms (kg)
- **a** = acceleration, in meters per second squared (m/s²)

### Mass from Force and Acceleration

$$m = \frac{F}{a}$$

### Acceleration from Force and Mass

$$a = \frac{F}{m}$$

These three forms are algebraic rearrangements of Newton's second law. The relationship is linear and proportional: doubling the mass while holding acceleration constant doubles the force; doubling the acceleration while holding mass constant also doubles the force. One newton is defined as the force required to accelerate a one-kilogram mass at one meter per second squared.

### Weight as a Special Case

$$W = m \times g$$

Where **g** = 9.80665 m/s² (standard acceleration due to gravity). Weight is simply the gravitational force on an object. A 70 kg person on Earth weighs 70 times 9.80665 = 686.47 N. On the Moon, where g = 1.625 m/s², that same person weighs only 113.75 N — about one-sixth of their Earth weight.

### Unit Conversions

$$1 \text{ N} = 0.224809 \text{ lbf} = 100{,}000 \text{ dyn} = 0.001 \text{ kN}$$

To convert newtons to pounds-force, multiply by 0.224809. To convert to dynes (CGS system), multiply by 100,000. These factors derive from the definitions of the respective unit systems.

**Source:** Isaac Newton, *Philosophiae Naturalis Principia Mathematica* (1687), Axioms or Laws of Motion, Law II. Modern SI unit definitions follow the International Bureau of Weights and Measures (BIPM), *The International System of Units (SI)*, 9th edition (2019).

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Weight of a Person on Earth

What is the gravitational force (weight) acting on a 70 kg person standing at sea level?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Known values: m = **70 kg**, a = g = **9.80665 m/s²**
2. Apply the formula: F = m times a = 70 times 9.80665 = **686.4655 N**
3. Convert to pounds-force: 686.4655 times 0.224809 = **154.32 lbf**
4. Convert to kilonewtons: 686.4655 / 1,000 = **0.6865 kN**

**Result:** The person experiences a gravitational force of **686.47 N** (154.32 lbf).

**Practical interpretation:** This is what a bathroom scale reads when it displays 70 kg or 154 lb. The scale does not measure mass directly — it measures the normal force your body exerts on it, which equals your gravitational weight when you stand still on flat ground. If you stood on Jupiter, where g = 24.79 m/s squared, the same 70 kg body would weigh 1,735.3 N (390.1 lbf) — nearly 2.5 times your Earth weight.

### Example 2: Force to Accelerate a Car

A 1,500 kg car accelerates from rest to 27.78 m/s (100 km/h) in 8 seconds on a flat road. What net force is required?

**Step-by-step:**

1. First find the acceleration: a = change in velocity / time = (27.78 - 0) / 8 = **3.4725 m/s²**
2. Known values: m = **1,500 kg**, a = **3.4725 m/s²**
3. Apply the formula: F = m times a = 1,500 times 3.4725 = **5,208.75 N**
4. Convert to pounds-force: 5,208.75 times 0.224809 = **1,170.9 lbf**
5. Convert to kilonewtons: 5,208.75 / 1,000 = **5.209 kN**

**Result:** The net force required is **5,208.75 N** (5.21 kN, 1,170.9 lbf).

**Practical interpretation:** This is the net force — the force remaining after subtracting friction, air resistance, and rolling resistance from the engine's total output. The engine must produce significantly more than 5,209 N at the wheels. At highway speeds, aerodynamic drag alone can account for 400-600 N for a typical sedan, and rolling resistance adds another 150-250 N. ## Key Factors That Affect Your Results

**Friction converts applied force into heat.** When you push a box across a floor, not all of your applied force goes toward acceleration. Static friction must be overcome before the object moves at all, and kinetic friction opposes motion continuously. The coefficient of kinetic friction for rubber on dry concrete is about 0.6-0.8, meaning friction force equals 60-80% of the normal force. On ice, it drops to 0.01-0.05 — which is why cars slide so easily on icy roads.

**Gravity varies by location.** Standard gravity is 9.80665 m/s squared, but actual gravitational acceleration ranges from 9.764 m/s squared at the equator (at sea level) to 9.832 m/s squared at the poles, and decreases with altitude. At the top of Mount Everest (8,849 m), g drops to about 9.777 m/s squared. For most calculations the standard value is sufficient, but precision engineering and geophysics require local measurements.

**Net force vs. applied force.** This calculator computes the relationship between net force, mass, and acceleration. In real-world scenarios, multiple forces act simultaneously: gravity pulls down, the ground pushes up (normal force), friction opposes motion, air resistance increases with speed, and applied forces drive the object. The net force is the vector sum of all these — and only the net force determines acceleration. A free-body diagram is essential for complex problems.

**Newton's third law creates reaction forces.** Every force has an equal and opposite reaction. When a rocket expels exhaust gas downward at high speed, the gas pushes back on the rocket with equal force — this is thrust. When you push against a wall with 50 N, the wall pushes back on you with 50 N. The [Momentum Calculator](/calculators/science/momentum-calculator) is useful for analyzing these action-reaction pairs in collisions and propulsion.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- **Single net force assumed.** The calculator treats force as a single scalar value. For problems involving multiple forces at different angles, you must compute the vector sum (net force) before entering it here.
- **Classical (Newtonian) mechanics only.** At speeds approaching the speed of light, relativistic mechanics applies and F = ma becomes F = dp/dt (force equals the rate of change of relativistic momentum). For everyday speeds up to millions of km/h, the classical formula is accurate.
- **Point-mass approximation.** The calculator assumes the object behaves as a point mass — all mass concentrated at one point. For extended bodies where torque and rotation matter, you need rotational dynamics (torque = moment of inertia times angular acceleration).
- **Constant acceleration assumed.** The formula F = ma gives the instantaneous relationship. If acceleration changes over time (as with a spring or turbulent drag), the force also changes, and you need calculus-based methods.
- **Positive values only.** All inputs must be positive. Negative force (indicating direction) is not modeled in this scalar calculator.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is a newton?

A newton is the SI unit of force, defined as the force required to accelerate a one-kilogram mass at one meter per second squared. In everyday terms, one newton is roughly the weight of a small apple (about 102 grams). The unit is named after Sir Isaac Newton, who formulated the laws of motion in 1687. Common reference points: a human bite force is about 70 N, the weight of a 75 kg person is about 736 N, and a Boeing 747 engine produces roughly 250,000 N (250 kN) of thrust at takeoff.

### What is the difference between mass and weight?

Mass is the amount of matter in an object, measured in kilograms, and it does not change with location. Weight is the gravitational force acting on that mass, measured in newtons, and it depends on the local gravitational acceleration. A 70 kg person has a mass of 70 kg everywhere in the universe, but their weight is 686 N on Earth, 113.75 N on the Moon, and 0 N in deep space far from any gravitational body. Bathroom scales labeled in "kg" are technically measuring weight (force) and converting it to mass using Earth's gravity.

### How much force does gravity exert on a 1 kg object?

Gravity exerts 9.80665 newtons of force on a one-kilogram object at Earth's surface at standard conditions. This value — standard gravity, denoted g — was adopted by the 3rd General Conference on Weights and Measures (CGPM) in 1901 as the conventional standard. It approximates the actual gravitational acceleration at roughly 45 degrees latitude at sea level. In pounds-force, this is about 2.205 lbf, which is why 1 kg is often said to "weigh" 2.2 pounds. Use the [Pressure Calculator](/calculators/science/pressure-calculator) if you need to convert this weight force into pressure over an area.

## How This Is Calculated

This calculator applies Newton's second law (F = ma) to solve for any one of three variables — force, mass, or acceleration — given the other two. When force is unknown, it multiplies mass by acceleration. When mass is unknown, it divides force by acceleration. When acceleration is unknown, it divides force by mass.
