## Worked Example

### Converting 45° to All Angle Units

An engineer needs to express a 45-degree angle in radians for a trigonometric calculation and in gradians for a surveying instrument calibrated in the metric angular system.

**Step 1 — Convert to degrees (base unit):**

45 degrees x 1 = **45 degrees** (already in base)

**Step 2 — Convert to each unit:**

- Radians: 45 / (180/π) = 45 x π/180 = **0.785398 radians** (π/4)
- Gradians: 45 / 0.9 = **50 gradians**
- Turns: 45 / 360 = **0.125 turns** (1/8 of a circle)
- Arcminutes: 45 / (1/60) = 45 x 60 = **2,700 arcminutes**
- Arcseconds: 45 / (1/3600) = 45 x 3,600 = **162,000 arcseconds**

The engineer uses 0.785398 radians for the sine/cosine calculation and confirms that 50 gradians is the correct setting for the surveying instrument (where a right angle = 100 gradians).

## Key Factors in Angle Conversion

**Radians are the standard in mathematics and physics.** Most programming languages, scientific calculators, and physics formulas expect angles in radians. The trigonometric functions sin(), cos(), and tan() in JavaScript, Python, C, and nearly every other language use radians — not degrees. Entering degrees into these functions without converting first is one of the most common calculation errors in programming.

**Gradians are used in surveying and some European countries.** The gradian (also called gon or grade) divides a right angle into exactly 100 units, making it convenient for percentage-based slope calculations. A full circle is 400 gradians. French and some European topographic maps use gradians, while most other countries use degrees.

**Arcminutes and arcseconds measure very small angles.** One arcminute (1/60 of a degree) is the resolution limit of the human eye. One arcsecond (1/3600 of a degree) is roughly the apparent width of a dime viewed from 4 kilometers away. These units are essential in astronomy (star positions), geodesy (GPS coordinates), and precision optics.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the difference between degrees and radians?

Degrees divide a full circle into 360 equal parts (an ancient Babylonian convention), while radians measure angles by the ratio of arc length to radius. A full circle is 2π radians (approximately 6.28318). Radians are dimensionless and mathematically natural — the derivative of sin(x) is cos(x) only when x is in radians, which is why radians are the standard unit in calculus and physics.

### Why are there 360 degrees in a circle?

The 360-degree circle originates from ancient Babylonian astronomy (circa 2000 BCE), where a base-60 number system was used. 360 is highly divisible (by 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 8, 9, 10, 12, 15, 18, 20, 24, 30, 36, 40, 45, 60, 72, 90, 120, 180), making it practical for dividing circles into equal parts without fractions. The Babylonians also observed that a year had approximately 360 days.

### When should I use gradians instead of degrees?

Use gradians for surveying, land measurement, and slope calculations where percentage-based thinking is convenient. A 1% grade (slope) corresponds to approximately 1 gradian. European surveying instruments and topographic maps commonly use gradians. For most other applications — navigation, construction, trigonometry, physics — degrees or radians are more standard and widely understood.
