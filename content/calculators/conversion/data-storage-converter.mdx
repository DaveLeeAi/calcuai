## How This Conversion Works

This converter translates data storage sizes between two parallel unit systems: **SI decimal** (KB, MB, GB, TB, PB) based on powers of 1,000, and **IEC binary** (KiB, MiB, GiB, TiB) based on powers of 1,024. It also converts to and from raw bytes and bits. The distinction matters because storage manufacturers use SI units while operating systems historically display binary sizes, creating apparent "missing space."

Understanding the difference helps accurately estimate backup needs and cloud storage costs. For physical measurements, see our [Length Converter](/calculators/conversion/length-converter) or [Weight Converter](/calculators/conversion/weight-converter).

## The Formula

Data storage uses two distinct conversion systems:

**SI Decimal Prefixes** (powers of 1,000):

$$
1 \text{ KB} = 10^3 \text{ B} = 1{,}000 \text{ B}
$$

$$
1 \text{ MB} = 10^6 \text{ B} = 1{,}000{,}000 \text{ B}
$$

$$
1 \text{ GB} = 10^9 \text{ B} = 1{,}000{,}000{,}000 \text{ B}
$$

$$
1 \text{ TB} = 10^{12} \text{ B} = 1{,}000{,}000{,}000{,}000 \text{ B}
$$

**IEC Binary Prefixes** (powers of 1,024):

$$
1 \text{ KiB} = 2^{10} \text{ B} = 1{,}024 \text{ B}
$$

$$
1 \text{ MiB} = 2^{20} \text{ B} = 1{,}048{,}576 \text{ B}
$$

$$
1 \text{ GiB} = 2^{30} \text{ B} = 1{,}073{,}741{,}824 \text{ B}
$$

$$
1 \text{ TiB} = 2^{40} \text{ B} = 1{,}099{,}511{,}627{,}776 \text{ B}
$$

To convert between any two units, the calculator first converts to bytes (the base unit), then divides by the target unit's byte factor:

$$
\text{Result} = \text{Value} \times \frac{\text{fromUnit (bytes)}}{\text{toUnit (bytes)}}
$$

**Bits:** 1 byte = 8 bits. Network speeds are typically measured in bits per second (Mbps), while storage is measured in bytes (MB).

*Source: IEC 80000-13:2008, "Quantities and units -- Part 13: Information science and technology." SI prefixes defined by the Bureau International des Poids et Mesures (BIPM).*

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Hard Drive Capacity -- 500 GB to GiB (Why Your Drive Looks Smaller)

You buy a "500 GB" external hard drive, but your computer shows only 465 GB available. Why?

**The manufacturer uses SI (decimal):**
- 500 GB = 500 x 1,000,000,000 = 500,000,000,000 bytes

**Your OS displays in GiB (binary):**
- 500,000,000,000 / 1,073,741,824 = **465.66 GiB**

*Interpretation:* The drive genuinely contains 500,000,000,000 bytes as advertised. The manufacturer uses SI gigabytes (10^9) while the operating system reports in gibibytes (2^30). The "missing" 34.34 GiB is the 7.37% difference between SI and binary prefixes. This gap grows with drive size: a 2 TB drive shows as approximately 1.82 TiB.

### Example 2: Cloud Backup -- 1.5 TB of Photos to GiB

Your photo library is 1.5 TB. A cloud storage plan offers 1,500 GiB. Will your photos fit?

**Convert 1.5 TB to bytes:**
- 1.5 x 1,000,000,000,000 = 1,500,000,000,000 bytes

**Convert to GiB:**
- 1,500,000,000,000 / 1,073,741,824 = **1,396.98 GiB**

*Interpretation:* Your 1.5 TB photo library equals approximately 1,397 GiB, fitting within the 1,500 GiB plan with about 103 GiB to spare. If the provider means 1,500 GB (SI) instead, the available space is exactly 1.5 TB -- identical to your library size with no headroom. Always verify whether a provider advertises in GB or GiB.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- SI prefixes (KB, MB, GB, TB, PB) follow the IEC/SI standard where each step is 1,000x (not 1,024x)
- Binary prefixes (KiB, MiB, GiB, TiB) follow IEC 80000-13 where each step is 1,024x
- 1 byte = 8 bits in all cases (the historical 6-bit and 7-bit byte definitions are not used)
- Does not include network speed units (Mbps, Gbps) -- those measure bits per second, not storage capacity
- Petabyte and Tebibyte are the largest units included; exabyte and exbibyte are omitted as they exceed typical consumer usage

## How This Is Calculated

This converter handles both binary (IEC) and decimal (SI) storage units. Binary units use powers of 1,024 (KiB, MiB, GiB, TiB), while decimal units use powers of 1,000 (KB, MB, GB, TB). All conversions route through bytes as the base unit. The distinction between binary and decimal prefixes follows the IEC 80000-13 standard adopted in 1998.
