## Worked Example

### Converting a Monthly Electricity Bill to BTU

A homeowner uses 900 kWh of electricity in a month and wants to compare this with their natural gas usage, which is measured in BTU (or therms, where 1 therm = 100,000 BTU).

**Step 1 — Convert to base (joules):**

900 kWh x 3,600,000 = **3,240,000,000 J** (3.24 GJ)

**Step 2 — Convert to BTU:**

3,240,000,000 / 1,055.06 = **3,070,927 BTU** (approximately 30.7 therms)

**Step 3 — Convert to kilocalories (for comparison):**

3,240,000,000 / 4,184 = **774,378.6 kcal**

The 900 kWh of electricity equals about 3.07 million BTU or 30.7 therms. If natural gas costs $1.20 per therm, the equivalent gas energy would cost $36.84 — far less than the typical $120+ electric bill, illustrating why gas heating is often cheaper per unit of energy delivered.

## Key Factors in Energy Conversion

**kWh and BTU dominate practical energy discussions.** In the US, electricity is billed in kilowatt-hours (the energy consumed by a 1,000-watt appliance running for one hour). Heating and cooling capacity uses BTU (British Thermal Units). The key conversion — 1 kWh = 3,412.14 BTU — bridges electrical and thermal energy for HVAC sizing, energy audits, and utility cost comparisons.

**Calories measure food energy; joules measure everything else.** The "calorie" on nutrition labels is actually a kilocalorie (kcal). One food Calorie (capital C) = 4,184 joules. When comparing dietary energy to physical work, this conversion matters: burning 100 kcal (a banana) releases 418,400 joules — enough to power a 100-watt light bulb for about 70 minutes.

**Electronvolts operate at atomic scale.** One electronvolt (1.602 x 10^-19 J) is the energy gained by a single electron crossing a 1-volt potential difference. Particle physicists use MeV and GeV rather than joules because atomic-scale energies in joules produce unwieldy numbers. The proton rest mass energy, for instance, is 938.272 MeV — far more practical than writing 1.503 x 10^-10 J.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the difference between a calorie and a kilocalorie?

A calorie (cal) is the energy needed to raise 1 gram of water by 1 degree Celsius — equal to 4.184 joules. A kilocalorie (kcal) equals 1,000 calories or 4,184 joules. Nutrition labels use "Calorie" (capital C) to mean kilocalorie, so a 200-Calorie snack actually contains 200,000 small calories or 836,800 joules. This naming convention causes frequent confusion, but the scientific and food-label meanings are well established.

### How many BTU are in a kilowatt-hour?

One kilowatt-hour equals 3,412.14 BTU. This conversion is critical for comparing electricity costs with natural gas costs. A 100,000-BTU furnace running for one hour consumes the equivalent of 29.31 kWh of energy. Since electricity typically costs $0.12-0.16 per kWh and natural gas costs roughly $1.00-1.50 per therm (100,000 BTU), gas heating usually costs less per unit of energy delivered.

### Why do scientists use joules instead of calories?

The joule is the official SI unit of energy, adopted internationally to provide a single consistent standard across all branches of science and engineering. Calories are still common in nutrition and chemistry, but the joule unifies mechanical energy (work = force x distance), electrical energy (watt-seconds), and thermal energy under one measurement system. Using joules eliminates conversion errors when working across disciplines.

### What is an electronvolt used for?

An electronvolt (eV) measures energy at the atomic and subatomic scale. It equals the kinetic energy gained by one electron accelerating through a 1-volt electric potential — approximately 1.602 x 10^-19 joules. Physicists use eV, keV (thousands), MeV (millions), and GeV (billions) to describe particle energies, photon energies, and nuclear binding energies because expressing these quantities in joules would require cumbersome powers of ten.
