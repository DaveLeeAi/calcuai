## How This Conversion Works

This converter handles all four major temperature scales and displays results across every scale simultaneously. Temperature conversion is unique among unit converters because the scales have different zero points -- you cannot simply multiply by a conversion factor. Celsius and Kelvin share the same degree size but differ by an offset of 273.15. Fahrenheit and Rankine share their degree size but use different zero points.

The converter is useful for cooking (oven temperatures between American and European recipes), science (Kelvin for thermodynamic calculations), weather (Fahrenheit to Celsius for international forecasts), and engineering (Rankine for certain thermodynamic equations). For converting other physical quantities, see our [Length Converter](/calculators/conversion/length-converter) or [Weight Converter](/calculators/conversion/weight-converter).

## The Formula

Temperature conversions use **direct formulas** with offsets, not simple ratio-based multiplication. Each pair of scales has its own conversion equation:

**Fahrenheit to Celsius:**

$$
°C = (°F - 32) \times \frac{5}{9}
$$

**Celsius to Fahrenheit:**

$$
°F = °C \times \frac{9}{5} + 32
$$

**Celsius to Kelvin:**

$$
K = °C + 273.15
$$

**Fahrenheit to Kelvin:**

$$
K = (°F - 32) \times \frac{5}{9} + 273.15
$$

**Fahrenheit to Rankine:**

$$
°R = °F + 459.67
$$

**Kelvin to Rankine:**

$$
°R = K \times \frac{9}{5}
$$

**Variable definitions:**
- **°F** = temperature in degrees Fahrenheit
- **°C** = temperature in degrees Celsius
- **K** = temperature in Kelvin (no degree symbol -- Kelvin is an absolute unit)
- **°R** = temperature in degrees Rankine

*Source: Celsius scale (Anders Celsius, 1742), Fahrenheit scale (Daniel Gabriel Fahrenheit, 1724), Kelvin scale (William Thomson, Lord Kelvin, 1848). Modern definitions per BIPM -- International System of Units (SI), 9th edition (2019).*

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Body Temperature -- 98.6°F to Celsius

Normal human body temperature is commonly cited as 98.6°F. What is that in Celsius?

**Step 1: Subtract 32**
- 98.6 - 32 = 66.6

**Step 2: Multiply by 5/9**
- 66.6 x 5/9 = **37.0°C**

*Interpretation:* Normal body temperature is 37.0°C. Modern medical research (Protsiv et al., 2020, eLife) suggests the current average is actually closer to 97.9°F (36.6°C), as the original 98.6°F figure dates to Carl Wunderlich's 1868 study.

### Example 2: Baking -- 350°F Oven to Celsius

An American recipe calls for a 350°F oven. European ovens display Celsius. What setting should you use?

**Step 1: Subtract 32**
- 350 - 32 = 318

**Step 2: Multiply by 5/9**
- 318 x 5/9 = **176.67°C**

*Interpretation:* 350°F converts to approximately 177°C. Most European oven dials are marked in increments of 10°C, so you would select 180°C -- the standard "moderate oven" setting used across European baking. The 3°C difference is negligible for all but the most sensitive pastries.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- Kelvin values cannot be negative (absolute zero = 0 K = -273.15°C); the calculator accepts negative Celsius and Fahrenheit inputs
- Rankine values cannot be negative (absolute zero = 0°R = -459.67°F)
- All formulas use the exact offset values defined by the SI (273.15 for Celsius-to-Kelvin, 459.67 for Fahrenheit-to-Rankine)
- Does not include the Reaumur, Delisle, Newton, or Romer historical temperature scales

{/* Section 16: How This Is Calculated */}
## How This Is Calculated

This converter applies the standard temperature conversion formulas: °F = °C × 9/5 + 32, °C = (°F − 32) × 5/9, and K = °C + 273.15. Unlike most unit conversions, temperature conversions require both multiplication and addition (not just a single conversion factor) because the scales have different zero points. Results are displayed to 2 decimal places.
