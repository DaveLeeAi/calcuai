## The Formula

$$\text{Converted Value} = \text{Value} \times \frac{\text{From Factor}}{\text{To Factor}}$$

Where each unit's factor converts to pascals (Pa):

- **Pascal (Pa)** = 1
- **Kilopascal (kPa)** = 1,000
- **Bar** = 100,000
- **Atmosphere (atm)** = 101,325 (exact, by definition)
- **PSI (lb/in²)** = 6,894.757
- **mmHg (Torr)** = 133.3224
- **inHg** = 3,386.389

**Key relationship:** 1 atm = 101,325 Pa = 101.325 kPa = 1.01325 bar = 14.696 psi = 760 mmHg = 29.921 inHg.

**Source:** NIST Special Publication 330, The International System of Units (SI), 2019 Edition.

## Worked Example

### Converting 35 PSI (Tire Pressure) to All Units

A driver checks their tire pressure and reads 35 psi on the gauge. Their vehicle manual specifies pressure in both psi and bar, and they want to confirm the reading matches the recommended 2.4 bar.

**Step 1 — Convert to base (pascals):**

35 psi x 6,894.757 = **241,316.5 Pa**

**Step 2 — Convert to each unit:**

- Kilopascal: 241,316.5 / 1,000 = **241.317 kPa**
- Bar: 241,316.5 / 100,000 = **2.413 bar**
- Atmosphere: 241,316.5 / 101,325 = **2.382 atm**
- mmHg: 241,316.5 / 133.3224 = **1,810.029 mmHg**
- inHg: 241,316.5 / 3,386.389 = **71.262 inHg**

The tire at 35 psi = 2.413 bar, which is slightly above the recommended 2.4 bar — confirming the tires are properly inflated. The 0.013 bar difference (about 0.2 psi) is within normal measurement tolerance.

## Key Factors in Pressure Conversion

**PSI and bar are the most common practical units.** In the United States, tire pressure, water pressure, and compressed air are measured in PSI (pounds per square inch). Most of Europe, Asia, and industrial contexts use bar or kilopascal. Knowing the key conversion — 1 bar ≈ 14.504 psi — covers most everyday needs.

**mmHg is standard in medicine; inHg in weather.** Blood pressure readings (e.g., 120/80 mmHg) use millimeters of mercury, based on the original mercury sphygmomanometer. Weather barometric pressure in the US uses inches of mercury (inHg), with standard sea-level pressure at 29.921 inHg. Aviation uses hectopascals (1 hPa = 100 Pa = 1 millibar).

**Gauge pressure vs. absolute pressure matters.** A tire gauge reading of 35 psi is gauge pressure — pressure above atmospheric. Absolute pressure adds atmospheric pressure: 35 + 14.696 = 49.696 psia. This converter works with numerical values only. Ensure you know whether your input is gauge or absolute before converting.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is standard atmospheric pressure?

Standard atmospheric pressure is exactly 101,325 pascals (1 atm), defined by the International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry (IUPAC). This equals 14.696 psi, 1.01325 bar, 760 mmHg, or 29.921 inHg. It approximates the average air pressure at sea level and serves as the reference for barometric readings, altitude calculations, and gas law computations.

### What is the difference between bar and PSI?

Bar is a metric pressure unit (1 bar = 100,000 Pa) used internationally in industry, automotive (tire pressure in Europe), and meteorology. PSI (pounds per square inch) is an imperial unit (1 psi = 6,894.757 Pa) used primarily in the US and UK for tire pressure, water pressure, and compressed gas. 1 bar = 14.504 psi; 1 psi = 0.06895 bar.

### Why is blood pressure measured in mmHg?

Blood pressure is measured in millimeters of mercury (mmHg) because the original sphygmomanometer, invented by Scipione Riva-Rocci in 1896, used a column of mercury to measure pressure. A reading of 120/80 mmHg means systolic pressure raises a mercury column 120 mm and diastolic raises it 80 mm. Despite digital gauges replacing mercury columns, the mmHg unit persists as the medical standard worldwide.

### How do I convert tire pressure between PSI and bar?

Multiply psi by 0.06895 to get bar, or multiply bar by 14.504 to get psi. Common tire pressures: 30 psi = 2.07 bar, 32 psi = 2.21 bar, 35 psi = 2.41 bar, 40 psi = 2.76 bar, 44 psi = 3.03 bar. Most passenger vehicles require 30–35 psi (2.07–2.41 bar); check your door sticker or owner's manual for the exact specification.
