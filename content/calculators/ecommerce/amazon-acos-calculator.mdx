Calculate your Amazon Advertising Cost of Sales (ACoS), Total ACoS (TACoS), ROAS, and break-even ACoS from campaign spend and sales data. The critical insight most sellers miss: **break-even ACoS equals your gross margin percentage**. A seller with 35% gross margin has a 35% break-even ACoS — every dollar of ACoS above 35% loses money, every dollar below is profitable. A campaign spending \$500 to generate \$2,000 in ad-attributed sales has an ACoS of 25%. If gross margin is 35%, that campaign is generating \$700 in gross profit from \$500 in spend — a positive return. The ACoS number means nothing without knowing your gross margin.

## What Your Results Mean

**ACoS (Advertising Cost of Sales)** is the ratio of ad spend to ad-attributed revenue, expressed as a percentage. It is the primary efficiency metric for Amazon Sponsored Products campaigns. Lower ACoS means each dollar of ad spend drives more revenue — but the correct target ACoS depends entirely on your margin, not a universal benchmark.

**Break-Even ACoS** is the ACoS at which your ad campaign generates exactly zero additional profit. It equals your gross margin percentage. At break-even ACoS, ad-driven sales cover their own cost but contribute nothing extra to net profit. All ACoS targets should be set below break-even — typically at break-even minus your target net profit percentage.

**TACoS (Total Advertising Cost of Sales)** measures ad spend as a percentage of your total store revenue (not just ad-attributed sales). TACoS captures the halo effect — organic sales that resulted from ads boosting keyword rank. A TACoS below 10% is generally healthy. Rising TACoS while ACoS holds steady signals that organic sales are declining relative to ad-driven sales, a dependency risk.

**ROAS (Return on Ad Spend)** is the inverse of ACoS: revenue generated per dollar of ad spend. A 4× ROAS means \$4 in revenue per \$1 spent. The break-even ROAS equals 100 / your gross margin percentage. At 35% gross margin, break-even ROAS = 100/35 = 2.86×. Any ROAS above 2.86× is profitable; below is a loss.

## How to Use This Calculator

1. **Enter Ad Spend** — your total Sponsored Products spend for the period (day/week/month). Pull from Amazon Ads → Campaign Manager → the "Spend" column.
2. **Enter Ad-Attributed Sales** — the sales revenue attributed to your ads in the same period. In Campaign Manager, this is the "Sales" column. Amazon attributes sales within a 14-day click window by default.
3. **Enter Total Store Sales** — your total Amazon store revenue for the same period (ad + organic). Pull from Seller Central → Business Reports → Sales Summary. This is used to calculate TACoS.
4. **Enter Gross Margin %** — your product gross margin after COGS, Amazon fees, and fulfillment, but before advertising. This is the critical input that determines your break-even ACoS. If you are unsure, use our [Amazon FBA Profit Calculator](/ecommerce/amazon-fba-profit-calculator) to derive your pre-ad gross margin.

## The Formula

<div className="formula-section">

**ACoS:**

$$
\text{ACoS\%} = \frac{\text{Ad Spend}}{\text{Ad-Attributed Sales}} \times 100
$$

**TACoS:**

$$
\text{TACoS\%} = \frac{\text{Ad Spend}}{\text{Total Store Sales}} \times 100
$$

**ROAS:**

$$
\text{ROAS} = \frac{\text{Ad-Attributed Sales}}{\text{Ad Spend}}
$$

**Break-Even ACoS:**

$$
\text{Break-Even ACoS\%} = \text{Gross Margin\%}
$$

**Target (Profitable) ACoS:**

$$
\text{Target ACoS\%} = \text{Gross Margin\%} - \text{Target Net Profit\%}
$$

Where:
- **Gross Margin%** = (Selling Price − COGS − Amazon Fees − FBA Fees) / Selling Price × 100
- **Break-Even ACoS** is derived from the identity: profit = 0 when Ad Spend = Sales × Gross Margin, therefore ACoS% = Gross Margin%
- **Target Net Profit%** is your desired profit margin after advertising (typically 10–20%)

*Source: Amazon Advertising — Campaign Performance Metrics Guide (2025). Canopy Management — Amazon ACoS Benchmarks by Category (2025).*

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Profitable Campaign with Organic Halo Effect

An Amazon seller in the Home & Kitchen category reviews a month of Sponsored Products data:

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Monthly Ad Spend | \$1,200 |
| Ad-Attributed Sales | \$5,800 |
| Total Store Sales | \$11,200 |
| Gross Margin (pre-ad) | 40% |

**Calculated:**
- **ACoS:** \$1,200 / \$5,800 × 100 = **20.7%**
- **TACoS:** \$1,200 / \$11,200 × 100 = **10.7%**
- **ROAS:** \$5,800 / \$1,200 = **4.83×**
- **Break-Even ACoS:** 40% (equals gross margin)
- **Ad Profit:** \$5,800 × 40% − \$1,200 = \$2,320 − \$1,200 = **\$1,120 generated by ads**

*Interpretation:* At 20.7% ACoS against a 40% break-even, this campaign has significant headroom. Each \$100 of ad spend is generating \$40 in gross profit while only costing \$20.7 in ads — a 93% return on ad spend at the margin level. TACoS at 10.7% is at the upper boundary of healthy. The seller should investigate whether organic sales are declining (rising TACoS risk) or holding steady.

### Example 2: Campaign at Break-Even — Time to Optimize

A sports equipment seller runs a review of a stalled campaign:

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Monthly Ad Spend | \$3,400 |
| Ad-Attributed Sales | \$9,714 |
| Total Store Sales | \$14,000 |
| Gross Margin (pre-ad) | 35% |

**Calculated:**
- **ACoS:** \$3,400 / \$9,714 × 100 = **35.0%** — exactly at break-even
- **TACoS:** \$3,400 / \$14,000 × 100 = **24.3%** — dangerously high
- **Ad Profit:** \$9,714 × 35% − \$3,400 = \$3,400 − \$3,400 = **\$0**

*Interpretation:* This campaign is at exact break-even — ads are generating zero additional profit while consuming \$3,400/month. TACoS at 24.3% means nearly one-quarter of all revenue is being absorbed by ads. The seller needs to either: (1) cut spend on non-converting keywords to bring ACoS to 20–25%, or (2) improve gross margin (lower COGS, raise price) to push break-even ACoS higher and create room for the current spend level.

{/* Section 10: Key Factors That Affect Your Results */}
## Key Factors That Affect Your Results

1. **The Break-Even ACoS = Gross Margin Identity** — This is the most important conceptual model in Amazon advertising. A seller with 40% gross margin can profitably run campaigns at up to 40% ACoS. A seller with 20% gross margin breaks even at 20% ACoS. The same 30% ACoS is profitable for the first seller and a money-loser for the second. Never evaluate ACoS without knowing your gross margin.

2. **ACoS vs. TACoS: What Each Measures** — ACoS measures campaign efficiency in isolation. TACoS measures advertising dependency across your entire business. A healthy ratio between them indicates strong organic traffic. When ACoS is 25% and TACoS is 8%, roughly two-thirds of your sales are organic. When ACoS is 25% and TACoS is 22%, almost everything is ad-driven — a fragile position where pausing ads would collapse revenue.

3. **Amazon's 14-Day Attribution Window** — Amazon's default Sponsored Products attribution assigns sales to an ad if the customer clicked the ad and purchased within 14 days. This inflates ACoS favorably (some of those purchases would have happened organically) and makes ACoS comparisons between Amazon and other platforms misleading — most platforms use 7-day or 1-day click attribution. Understand what your ACoS number actually includes before comparing it to external benchmarks.

4. **Category Benchmarks Are Ranges, Not Targets** — Canopy Management's 2025 benchmark data shows ACoS averages ranging from 18% (supplements, well-established brands) to 45% (highly competitive electronics). These are averages, not targets. Your break-even ACoS is your target ceiling — the benchmark is useful only for competitive context, not as a replacement for your own margin math.

5. **New Product Launch ACoS is Not Business ACoS** — During a product launch, deliberately running above break-even ACoS to accumulate reviews and ranking velocity is a common and legitimate strategy. The question is whether the customer acquisition cost during launch converts to long-term organic rank that reduces future ad dependency. Track TACoS over 6–12 months from launch to evaluate whether launch-phase ad investment is paying off in reduced advertising dependency.

{/* Section 13: Assumptions & Limitations */}
## Assumptions & Limitations

- ACoS and TACoS are calculated on the period of data entered — use consistent time periods (same 30 days) for both ad and total sales to avoid distortion
- Gross margin input should reflect post-fee margin (after referral + FBA fees) but pre-advertising — do not include ad spend in the gross margin input
- Does not differentiate between Sponsored Products, Sponsored Brands, and Sponsored Display — combine all or enter campaign by campaign
- Amazon's attribution window (default 14 days) is embedded in the ad-attributed sales figure you pull from Campaign Manager; this calculator does not adjust for attribution window differences
- TACoS calculation requires total store sales which includes all channels if you use Amazon's unified reporting — use Amazon-only sales for accurate TACoS

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is a good ACoS on Amazon?

A good ACoS is any ACoS below your break-even ACoS, which equals your gross margin percentage. A seller with 35% gross margin should target ACoS below 35% — ideally 20–25% to leave 10–15% for net profit after advertising. There is no universal "good ACoS" number because it depends entirely on your margin structure. The benchmarks (15–30% is often cited) are only useful if your margin happens to match those assumptions.

### What is the difference between ACoS and TACoS?

ACoS (Advertising Cost of Sales) measures ad spend relative to ad-attributed revenue only. TACoS (Total ACoS) measures ad spend relative to total store revenue including organic. ACoS tells you campaign efficiency. TACoS tells you advertising dependency. A product with 20% ACoS and 5% TACoS is mostly organic with efficient ads. The same 20% ACoS with 18% TACoS means almost all sales are driven by ads — the product has no organic traction.

### How do I calculate break-even ACoS?

Break-even ACoS equals your gross margin percentage. If your product sells for \$30, has \$10 in COGS, and pays \$6 in Amazon fees (referral + FBA), your gross profit is \$14 and gross margin is 46.7%. Your break-even ACoS is 46.7% — any campaign below that is profitable, any campaign above loses money. To set a target ACoS, subtract your desired net profit margin: at 46.7% break-even with a 15% net profit target, your target ACoS is 31.7%.

### Should I pause campaigns with high ACoS?

Not automatically. First, check if the high-ACoS campaign is also generating organic rank improvements (measured by organic sales growth and keyword position). A launch campaign at 50% ACoS that moves a keyword from page 3 to page 1 may be worth running short-term if organic sales at page 1 will pay back the launch cost over time. For mature products with established rank, high-ACoS campaigns with no rank improvement benefit should be optimized (negative keywords, bid reductions) before pausing entirely.
