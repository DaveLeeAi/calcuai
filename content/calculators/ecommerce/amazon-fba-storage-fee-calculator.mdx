## What Your Results Mean

**Monthly Storage Fee** is what Amazon charges this month based on your size type and the time of year.

**Storage Fee per Unit** helps you factor storage cost into your per-unit profitability — add this to your FBA profit calculation to get true economics.

**Aged Inventory Surcharge** is the additional fee Amazon charges for inventory stored 271+ days. At 365+ days, it jumps to $6.90/cubic foot — often more expensive than the product itself.

**Estimated Annual Storage Cost** projects what you'll pay over a full year accounting for the Q4 rate spike.

## How to Use This Calculator

1. Calculate your product's **Cubic Feet per Unit**: Length (in) × Width (in) × Height (in) ÷ 1,728
2. Enter **Units in FBA Inventory** — your current stock quantity
3. Select **Product Size Type** — standard or oversize
4. Select **Storage Period** — regular (Jan–Sep) or peak Q4 (Oct–Dec)
5. Enter **Days in Storage** — triggers aged inventory surcharge display at 271+ days
6. Enter **Unit Cost** to see capital tied up in storage

## Worked Example 1 — Standard Item, Regular Season

A seller has **800 units** of a small kitchen tool stored from March through July. Each unit is 0.3 cubic feet.

| Calculation | Value |
|-------------|-------|
| Total Cubic Feet | 0.3 × 800 = 240 cuft |
| Monthly Rate (regular) | $0.78/cuft |
| **Monthly Storage Fee** | 240 × $0.78 = **$187.20** |
| **Storage Fee per Unit** | $187.20 / 800 = **$0.234** |

Over 5 months (March–July): **$936 in storage fees** — roughly $1.17/unit added to COGS.

## Worked Example 2 — Q4 Peak Season Penalty

The same seller leaves **400 units** unsold through October, November, and December.

| Calculation | Value |
|-------------|-------|
| Total Cubic Feet | 0.3 × 400 = 120 cuft |
| Monthly Rate (Q4 peak) | $2.40/cuft |
| **Monthly Storage Fee** | 120 × $2.40 = **$288.00** |
| **3-Month Q4 Cost** | $288 × 3 = **$864** |

That $864 for 400 units over Q4 adds **$2.16/unit** to storage costs. For a product with a $5 net profit margin, this erases 43% of the margin in Q4 alone.

## Key Factors That Affect Storage Costs

**Q4 planning is the most important storage decision.** Sending too much inventory in September means paying $2.40/cuft for units that don't sell until January. Sending too little means stockouts during the highest-volume selling period. Most experienced FBA sellers use 60–90 days of projected Q4 sales as their target inbound quantity, sent in August–September (before peak rates start October 1).

**Cubic feet, not weight, drives the fee.** Amazon charges storage by volume (cubic feet), not by weight. A light but bulky product like a foam pillow costs more to store per unit than a small but heavy product like a dumbbell. Always calculate cubic feet before forecasting storage costs.

**Aged inventory surcharges can exceed the product's value.** At $6.90/cubic foot for 365+ day inventory, a 1-cubic-foot product accumulates $6.90/month in surcharges on top of regular storage. For products costing $8–$12 to source, this makes aged inventory financially catastrophic. Set up inventory age alerts in Seller Central and plan liquidation before hitting the 271-day threshold.

**The Restock Limit changes how much you can send.** Amazon caps how much inventory each seller can send to FBA based on Inventory Performance Index (IPI) score. A low IPI score from too many slow-moving units reduces your restock limit, which then limits your ability to stock winners. Keeping aged inventory low is both a cost and a capacity issue.

**FBA Removal and Disposal.** If you need to clear aged inventory, Amazon charges $0.97–$13.05 per unit for removal (returned to you) or $0.97–$13.05 for disposal. Factor this into your liquidation math — sometimes selling on eBay or running a large promotion is cheaper than paying removal fees.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- Rates are Amazon's 2026 FBA storage fee schedule, effective January 2026.
- Annual cost estimate uses 9 months at regular rate + 3 months at Q4 peak rate. Your actual mix depends on when you send inventory.
- Aged inventory surcharges are assessed monthly on units stored 271+ days based on the inventory age snapshot taken on the 15th of each month.
- This calculator does not include the FBA Inbound Placement Fee or returns processing fees.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How do I calculate the cubic feet of my product?**
Measure your product's packaged dimensions in inches (the box Amazon would store, not the product itself). Multiply Length × Width × Height, then divide by 1,728. Example: a 12 × 9 × 4 inch package = 432 cubic inches ÷ 1,728 = 0.25 cubic feet. Amazon uses the greater of actual dimensions or dimensional weight when measuring.

**When does the aged inventory surcharge kick in?**
Amazon assesses aged inventory surcharges on the 15th of each month for units that have been in FBA for more than 271 days. The surcharge is $0.50/cubic foot for units aged 271–365 days and $6.90/cubic foot for units over 365 days. These are on top of regular monthly storage fees.

**How do I reduce FBA storage fees?**
The most effective strategies are: (1) Use Amazon's Manage Excess Inventory dashboard to identify slow-moving units early and run promotions before hitting the 271-day threshold; (2) Set reorder quantities based on 45–60 days of sales rather than 90–120 days; (3) For seasonal products, time inbound shipments to arrive 2–4 weeks before peak season rather than months ahead; (4) Consider using Amazon's third-party logistics (AWD) for bulk storage at lower rates.

**Is oversize inventory cheaper to store?**
Oversize inventory has a lower storage rate per cubic foot ($0.56 vs. $0.78 regular, $1.40 vs. $2.40 peak) — but oversize products are typically much larger, so the total fee is usually higher. A small standard item at 0.1 cuft costs $0.08/month in regular storage. An oversize item at 3 cuft costs $1.68/month — 21× more, despite the lower rate.
