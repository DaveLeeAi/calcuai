## What Your Results Mean

**Net system cost** is your out-of-pocket cost after the federal tax credit and any state incentives — this is your actual break-even target. **Year 1 savings** is your electricity bill reduction based on your current rate and production estimate. **Payback period** is how many years until cumulative savings equal your net cost. **Lifetime savings** accounts for annual electricity rate increases, giving a more realistic picture than a static calculation.

The year-by-year table shows exactly when you cross into profit and how your returns accelerate as electricity rates rise.

## How to Use This Calculator

1. Enter your system quote (before incentives). Average US residential system: $18,000–$30,000.
2. Get your annual kWh production estimate from your solar installer's proposal or NREL's PVWatts tool.
3. Check your utility bill for your current rate per kWh.
4. Use 3% as the default annual rate increase (US historical average since 2000).
5. Add any state or local rebates (many states offer $1,000–$5,000 additional incentives).

## The Formula

<div className="formula-section">

**Formula:**
$$
\text{Net Cost} = \text{System Cost} \times (1 - 0.30) - \text{State Incentives}
$$
$$
\text{Simple Payback} = \frac{\text{Net Cost}}{\text{Year 1 Savings}}
$$
$$
\text{Lifetime Savings} = \sum_{y=1}^{N} \text{Annual kWh} \times \text{Rate} \times (1 + r)^{y-1}
$$

Where:
- **0.30** = federal ITC rate (30% through 2032)
- **r** = annual electricity rate increase (default 3%)
- **N** = system lifetime in years

*Source: U.S. Department of Energy — Solar Investment Tax Credit Guide (2025); NREL — PVWatts Methodology (2025)*

</div>

## Worked Example

**California homeowner:** $28,000 system, 11,000 kWh/year production, $0.26/kWh rate, 3% escalation, $1,000 state rebate.

- Federal tax credit: $28,000 × 30% = **$8,400**
- Net cost: $28,000 − $8,400 − $1,000 = **$18,600**
- Year 1 savings: 11,000 × $0.26 = **$2,860**
- Simple payback: $18,600 ÷ $2,860 = **6.5 years**
- 25-year savings (with 3% escalation): **$109,000+**

High electricity rates in California make solar exceptionally attractive — payback periods of 5–7 years are common.

## Key Factors That Affect Payback

| Factor | Impact on Payback |
|---|---|
| Higher electricity rate | Shorter payback |
| More sun hours (Southwest vs. Northeast) | Shorter payback |
| Larger system (better $/watt) | Slightly shorter |
| Net metering policy | Shorter (credit for excess) |
| Panel degradation (0.5%/yr) | Slightly longer |
| State income taxes (credit may be taxable) | Varies |

## FAQ

<div className="faq-section">

**Q: How long does it actually take to pay off solar panels?**
A: The average US solar payback period is 8–12 years, though it ranges from 5–7 years in high-rate states like California, Massachusetts, and Connecticut to 14–18 years in low-rate states like Louisiana and Washington. The 30% federal tax credit, local incentives, and your electricity rate are the three biggest variables. A system that lasts 25–30 years (as modern panels do) still generates 12–22 years of free electricity after payback.

**Q: Does the 30% federal solar tax credit expire?**
A: The 30% Residential Clean Energy Credit (ITC) is locked in through 2032 under the Inflation Reduction Act. It drops to 26% in 2033, then 22% in 2034, then expires for residential installations in 2035 unless Congress acts. To claim it, you must owe at least as much in federal income tax as the credit amount — it's a non-refundable credit. Unused credit can be carried forward to the next tax year.

**Q: What factors most affect solar payback period?**
A: In order of impact: (1) Your electricity rate — high-rate states give much faster payback; (2) Net metering policy — full retail credit for exported power versus partial credit makes a major difference; (3) System cost per watt — national average is $3.00–$4.00/watt installed, with variation based on roof type, system size, and installer competition; (4) Sun hours — Arizona gets 5.5–6 peak sun hours daily vs. 3.5–4 for the Northeast; (5) Panel degradation — modern panels degrade about 0.5%/year, meaning a 25-year-old system still produces ~88% of original output.

</div>
