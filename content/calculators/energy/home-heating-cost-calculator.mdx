## What Your Results Mean

**Annual heating cost** is the estimated total fuel cost for your heating season based on your climate's heating degree days, home size, insulation, and fuel type. **Monthly breakdown** distributes that annual cost across 12 months weighted by typical degree-day patterns — heaviest in January–February, near zero June–August in most climates. **Annual fuel consumption** tells you how many therms, gallons, or kWh to budget for.

## How to Use This Calculator

1. Enter your home's conditioned square footage (the area you actually heat).
2. Select your insulation level honestly — most pre-1980 homes are "poor" to "average."
3. Find your city's heating degree days on NOAA's Climate Data Portal or use the reference below.
4. Select your fuel type and enter your actual rate (check your utility bill).
5. Enter your system's efficiency — the yellow EnergyGuide label shows this for newer equipment.

## The Formula

<div className="formula-section">

**Formula:**
$$
\text{Annual BTU} = \text{HDD} \times 24 \times \frac{\text{Area (sq ft)} \times U_{\text{avg}}}{\text{Insulation factor}}
$$
$$
\text{Annual Cost} = \frac{\text{Annual BTU}}{\text{Fuel BTU content}} \times \frac{\text{Fuel Rate}}{\text{Efficiency}}
$$

Where:
- **HDD** = Heating Degree Days (base 65°F)
- **U-factor** = average heat loss rate (BTU/hr·°F·sq ft), varies by insulation level
- **Fuel BTU content:** natural gas = 100,000 BTU/therm; heating oil = 138,500 BTU/gal; propane = 91,500 BTU/gal; electricity = 3,412 BTU/kWh

*Source: U.S. EIA — Residential Energy Consumption Survey (2025); U.S. DOE Building Technologies Office — Heating Degree Days (2025)*

</div>

## Worked Example

**Chicago suburb (6,500 HDD), 2,000 sq ft, average insulation, natural gas at $1.50/therm, 80% AFUE:**

- Annual BTU needed: 6,500 × 24 × (2,000 × 0.031) ≈ **96,720,000 BTU**
- Therms needed: 96,720,000 ÷ 100,000 = **967 therms**
- Effective therms (accounting for 80% efficiency): 967 ÷ 0.80 = **1,209 therms**
- Annual cost: 1,209 × $1.50 = **$1,814/year** ($151/month average)

## Worked Example 2

**Denver, CO (5,500 HDD), 1,600 sq ft home, good insulation, propane at $2.80/gallon, 85% AFUE:**

- Annual BTU needed: 5,500 × 24 × (1,600 × 0.024) ≈ **50,688,000 BTU**
- Gallons of propane needed: 50,688,000 ÷ 91,500 = **554 gallons**
- Effective gallons (accounting for 85% efficiency): 554 ÷ 0.85 = **652 gallons**
- Annual cost: 652 × $2.80 = **$1,826/year** ($152/month average)

Propane in Denver costs roughly $600 more per year than natural gas would for the same heating load ($1,826 vs. ~$1,230 for gas at $1.50/therm). However, many rural Colorado homes lack natural gas service, making propane the primary option. Locking in a propane contract in summer (June–August) when demand is low typically saves $0.30–$0.50/gallon — which on 652 gallons translates to $196–$326 in annual savings. Installing a 500-gallon tank (instead of renting) can save another $100–$200/year in rental fees.

## FAQ

<div className="faq-section">

**Q: What are heating degree days and how do I find mine?**
A: Heating degree days (HDD) measure how cold your climate is for heating purposes. Each degree that a day's average temperature falls below 65°F counts as one HDD — so a day averaging 45°F adds 20 HDD. A full year's HDDs measure your total heating load: Miami accumulates ~200 HDD/year, while Minneapolis accumulates ~8,000. Find your city's actual historical HDD data at the NOAA Climate Data Online portal (climate.weather.gov) or your state's energy office website.

**Q: How much does heating add to my monthly utility bill?**
A: Heating adds $60–$300/month to utility bills in cold climates during winter, depending on fuel type and home size. Natural gas heating for a 2,000 sq ft home in a cold climate (Chicago, Minneapolis) typically costs $150–$300/month in winter and near zero in summer. Propane and heating oil can cost $200–$500/month in deep winter. Electric resistance heating is typically $200–$500+/month in cold climates — the most expensive option. Budgeting 10–15% of your annual heating cost per peak winter month is a useful rule of thumb.

**Q: What are the best ways to reduce heating costs?**
A: The highest-ROI improvements in order: (1) Air sealing ($100–$500, reduces heating 10–20%) — caulk windows, weatherstrip doors, seal attic penetrations; (2) Attic insulation ($1,000–$3,000, saves $200–$600/year) — most heat escapes through the ceiling; (3) Programmable thermostat ($25–$250) — drop 7–10°F for 8 hours while sleeping or away to save 10% annually; (4) Furnace tune-up ($80–$150/year) — a dirty filter alone can cut efficiency 10–15%; (5) Window upgrades ($400–$800/window) — longest payback but meaningful in very cold climates.

**Q: How does home size affect heating costs — does doubling the square footage double the cost?**
A: Roughly, yes — heating cost scales nearly linearly with conditioned square footage assuming the same insulation level and ceiling height. A 3,000 sq ft home costs approximately 50% more to heat than a 2,000 sq ft home in the same climate. However, two-story homes are cheaper to heat per square foot than single-story ranch homes because they have less roof area (the biggest heat loss surface) relative to floor area. A 2,000 sq ft two-story home typically costs 10–15% less to heat than a 2,000 sq ft single-story home with the same insulation because the second floor acts as a buffer against roof heat loss.

**Q: Is it cheaper to heat my whole house or use space heaters in the rooms I use?**
A: Space heaters are cheaper only if you are heating 1–2 rooms while keeping the rest of the house at 55–60°F. A 1,500 W electric space heater costs $0.24/hour at $0.16/kWh, or $5.76/day running 24 hours. Heating a full house with space heaters is almost always more expensive than a central furnace or heat pump because electric resistance heating (100% efficient) cannot compete with a gas furnace (80–96% efficient at converting cheaper fuel) or a heat pump (200–400% effective efficiency via the COP). The break-even point is typically when you are heating less than 30% of your home's total area. Beyond that, central heating wins on cost per BTU delivered.

## Insulation and Weatherization: The Biggest Lever You Control

Fuel type determines your cost per BTU, but insulation determines how many BTUs you need. A poorly insulated home can require 2–3 times more heating energy than a well-insulated one of the same size in the same climate.

The DOE estimates that 90% of US homes are under-insulated. The most impactful upgrade is almost always attic insulation, because hot air rises and escapes primarily through the ceiling. Bringing a 1,500 sq ft attic from R-19 (common in homes built before 1990) to R-49 (current code for cold climates) costs $1,500–$2,500 for blown-in cellulose and typically reduces annual heating costs by 15–25%. In a 6,500 HDD climate with gas heat at $1.50/therm, that translates to $270–$450/year in savings — a payback of 4–6 years.

Wall insulation is the second priority but harder to retrofit. Dense-pack cellulose injected through small holes costs $3,000–$6,000 for a whole house and reduces heat loss through walls by 30–50%. Basement rim joists — the exposed area where the foundation meets the framing — are often completely uninsulated and easy to fix with rigid foam board for under $200 in materials.

Window replacement is the most expensive per unit of energy saved. Upgrading single-pane windows to double-pane low-E costs $400–$800 per window and saves $25–$50/year per window. The payback period is 10–20 years, making it the last priority unless your windows are physically failing. A cheaper alternative: interior window film kits ($5–$10/window) reduce heat loss through single-pane glass by 30–40% and pay for themselves in a single heating season.

## Sources & Methodology

The heating load model uses the degree-day method (base 65°F), which is the standard approach used by the U.S. Department of Energy and ASHRAE for residential heating estimates. U-factor values by insulation level are derived from DOE Building Technologies Office envelope performance data. Fuel energy content values (BTU per unit) and national average fuel prices are from the U.S. Energy Information Administration's Residential Energy Consumption Survey and Short-Term Energy Outlook (2025). Monthly distribution of heating degree days uses 30-year NOAA climate normals.
