## What Your Results Mean

**Monthly bill** is the total you owe your utility, combining your energy charge (kWh × rate) with any fixed monthly service fees your utility tacks on regardless of usage. **Annual cost** extrapolates your monthly bill across 12 months — useful for budgeting, but keep in mind that seasonal swings can push summer bills 40–80% above winter bills in warm climates and vice versa in cold ones. **Cost per day** breaks down your consumption into a daily expense, making it easier to spot waste: if your daily cost jumps from $4.50 to $7.00, something changed in your home.

The comparison panel shows how your bill stacks up against the national average. If you are paying more than $148.78/month at average consumption, your rate is above the US median — and switching plans, negotiating with your utility, or adding solar may produce meaningful savings.

## How to Use This Calculator

1. Find your monthly kWh usage on your electric bill — it is listed as "kWh used" or "total consumption." If you only have an annual figure, divide by 12.
2. Enter your electricity rate per kWh. Look for "energy charge" or "supply rate" on your bill. If you have tiered pricing, use your blended average: total energy charge divided by total kWh.
3. Enter your utility's fixed monthly charge (sometimes called "service charge," "customer charge," or "delivery charge"). This ranges from $5 to $25 for most residential accounts.
4. Review the results. Compare your estimated bill to the national and state averages to gauge whether your consumption or rate is above normal.

## The Formula

<div className="formula-section">

**Formula:**
$$
\text{Monthly Bill} = (\text{kWh} \times \text{Rate per kWh}) + \text{Fixed Charge}
$$
$$
\text{Annual Cost} = \text{Monthly Bill} \times 12
$$
$$
\text{Daily Cost} = \frac{\text{Monthly Bill}}{\text{Days in Month}}
$$

Where:
- **kWh** = kilowatt-hours consumed during the billing period (from your meter)
- **Rate** = your utility's energy charge per kWh ($/kWh)
- **Fixed Charge** = the flat monthly service fee your utility charges regardless of usage ($5–$25 typical)

*Source: U.S. Energy Information Administration — Electric Power Monthly, Table 5.6.a (March 2026); Choose Energy — Average Electricity Rates by State (March 2026)*

## Worked Example 1

**Texas homeowner with above-average summer usage:**

- Monthly usage: 1,100 kWh (Texas average is 1,132 kWh — the highest in the US due to AC load)
- Rate: $0.1587/kWh (Texas residential average, EIA 2025)
- Fixed charge: $12.00/month

Calculation:
- Energy charge: 1,100 × $0.1587 = **$174.57**
- Fixed charge: **$12.00**
- Monthly bill: $174.57 + $12.00 = **$186.57**
- Annual cost: $186.57 × 12 = **$2,238.84**
- Daily cost: $186.57 ÷ 30 = **$6.22/day**

At $186.57/month, this Texas homeowner pays about 25% more than the national average — but that is driven by usage, not rate. Texas electricity rates are actually 8% below the national average. The culprit is cooling: air conditioning accounts for 50–70% of summer electricity consumption in Texas, pushing July and August bills to $250–$350 while winter months may drop to $80–$100.

## Worked Example 2

**California homeowner on tiered rates with moderate usage:**

- Monthly usage: 700 kWh (below California average of 532 kWh due to a medium-sized home with central AC)
- Blended rate: $0.3471/kWh (California residential average, EIA 2025)
- Fixed charge: $15.00/month

Calculation:
- Energy charge: 700 × $0.3471 = **$242.97**
- Fixed charge: **$15.00**
- Monthly bill: $242.97 + $15.00 = **$257.97**
- Annual cost: $257.97 × 12 = **$3,095.64**
- Daily cost: $257.97 ÷ 30 = **$8.60/day**

Despite using 37% fewer kWh than the national average, this California homeowner pays 73% more than the national average bill. California's rate — more than double the national average — is driven by wildfire mitigation costs, grid upgrades, and aggressive renewable mandates. This is the state where solar panels produce the fastest payback: at $0.35/kWh, a 6 kW system generating 9,000 kWh/year saves $3,150 annually.

## Why Electricity Rates Vary by State

Electricity rates are not random. Five structural factors explain why North Dakota pays 11.02¢/kWh while Hawaii pays 41.62¢/kWh — a 278% difference for the same commodity.

**Generation fuel mix.** States that rely heavily on low-cost fuels have lower rates. West Virginia (93% coal and gas) and Idaho (60% hydropower) have historically low generation costs. Hawaii imports petroleum for most of its generation at global oil prices, inflating costs far above the mainland.

**Transmission and distribution infrastructure.** Rural states with long distances between power plants and customers face higher per-mile transmission costs. Conversely, states with dense population centers amortize grid costs across more ratepayers. California's wildfire liability has added $5–$8 billion in grid hardening costs, passed directly to ratepayers as surcharges.

**Regulatory structure.** Deregulated states like Texas and Pennsylvania allow consumers to choose competitive retail electricity providers, which can drive rates lower through competition — but also exposes consumers to price volatility, as Texas saw during the February 2021 winter storm when some variable-rate plans spiked to $9/kWh. Regulated states like Florida and Georgia have a single utility with fixed rates set by the Public Utilities Commission.

**Renewable mandates.** States with aggressive renewable portfolio standards (RPS) add the cost of subsidies, grid integration, and backup capacity to consumer rates. California's 60% RPS target and New York's 70% target by 2030 both add 1–3¢/kWh to retail prices. However, falling solar and wind costs mean this premium is shrinking annually.

**Weather and peak demand.** States with extreme summer heat (Texas, Arizona, Florida) build extra generation capacity to handle July–August peak demand — capacity that sits idle 9 months a year. That idle capacity is paid for through base rates year-round.

## How to Read Your Electricity Bill

Your electric bill contains several distinct charges that together make up your total. Understanding each one is essential to finding savings.

**Energy charge** is the cost of the electricity itself — your kWh consumed multiplied by the per-kWh rate. This is the number you enter into this calculator. In deregulated states, this may appear as the "supply charge" from your chosen provider.

**Delivery charge** covers transmission lines, substations, and local distribution wires. It is often a fixed fee ($8–$20/month) plus a small per-kWh component ($0.02–$0.06/kWh). You cannot avoid this charge regardless of your electricity supplier.

**Demand charge** (primarily commercial accounts, but some residential TOU plans include it) is based on your peak 15-minute draw during the billing period. If you ran your AC, dryer, oven, and EV charger simultaneously and peaked at 12 kW, you might face a $5–$15/kW demand charge on that 12 kW peak — even if you only hit it once all month.

**Regulatory surcharges** include renewable energy fund contributions, energy efficiency program costs, nuclear decommissioning fees, and wildfire mitigation surcharges. These vary by state and utility but typically add $3–$15/month.

**Taxes and fees** include state and local sales tax (if applicable), franchise fees, and miscellaneous regulatory assessments. These typically add 5–12% to the pre-tax bill.

## How to Lower Your Electric Bill

Reducing your electric bill comes down to three levers: use less electricity, shift usage to cheaper hours, or pay a lower rate. The strategies below are ordered by typical ROI, highest first.

**Switch to time-of-use (TOU) rates if offered.** Many utilities offer optional TOU plans where off-peak electricity (typically 9 PM to 7 AM) costs $0.08–$0.14/kWh versus $0.25–$0.40/kWh during peak hours. If you can shift your EV charging, dishwasher, laundry, and water heater to overnight, you can cut the energy cost of those appliances by 40–60%. A household running 400 kWh/month of shiftable load saves $50–$100/month by moving those kWh from $0.30 to $0.10.

**Replace your top three energy hogs.** Central AC (1,500–5,000 W), electric water heaters (4,500 W), and old refrigerators (400–600 W, 24/7) dominate most electric bills. Upgrading to an ENERGY STAR air conditioner with a higher SEER2 rating (16+) reduces cooling energy by 20–40%. A heat pump water heater uses 65% less electricity than a standard electric tank. A new ENERGY STAR refrigerator uses 350 kWh/year versus 600+ kWh/year for pre-2001 models.

**Seal your building envelope.** Air leaks around windows, doors, outlets, and attic penetrations force your HVAC to work harder. The DOE estimates that air sealing and insulation improvements reduce heating and cooling costs by 15–30%. A $200–$500 investment in caulk, weatherstripping, and attic sealing pays for itself in 3–8 months in most climates.

**Install solar panels.** At 17.24¢/kWh, a 6 kW system producing 9,000 kWh/year offsets $1,552 in annual electricity costs. After the 30% federal tax credit, a $20,000 system nets $14,000 — paying itself back in 9 years and generating free electricity for 16+ additional years. In high-rate states, payback drops to 5–7 years.

**Shop competitive providers in deregulated states.** In Texas, Pennsylvania, Ohio, and 12 other deregulated states, you can choose your electricity supplier. Switching from the default utility rate to a competitive fixed-rate plan can save 10–25% with no service changes. Comparison sites like EnergySage, Power to Choose (Texas), and PA Power Switch aggregate current offers.

## FAQ

<div className="faq-section">

**Q: What is the average electric bill in the US?**
A: The average US residential electric bill is approximately $148.78 per month, based on average consumption of 863 kWh at 17.24¢/kWh as of 2025–2026 EIA data. However, actual bills range from under $100/month in low-rate states like North Dakota and Washington to over $250/month in high-rate states like California, Massachusetts, and Hawaii. Your bill depends on two factors you can partially control — usage (kWh) and rate (¢/kWh) — plus fixed fees you cannot avoid.

**Q: How many kWh does the average home use per month?**
A: The US average is 863 kWh per month, but this varies significantly by state and climate. Louisiana averages 1,149 kWh/month (heavy AC use), Texas averages 1,132 kWh/month, while Hawaii averages only 505 kWh/month (mild climate, smaller homes). The biggest factor is climate: homes in hot, humid states use 30–50% more electricity than the national average due to air conditioning, while homes in moderate climates like California and the Pacific Northwest use 20–30% less. Home size, number of occupants, and appliance efficiency also matter.

**Q: Why did my electric bill go up even though I did not change anything?**
A: Three common causes: (1) Rate increase — utilities raise rates 3–6% annually, and a 5% rate hike on a $150 bill adds $7.50/month silently; (2) Seasonal demand — AC in summer and electric heating in winter can double your kWh usage without any change in habits; (3) Tiered pricing — many utilities charge a higher rate per kWh once you exceed a baseline (e.g., $0.15/kWh for the first 500 kWh, $0.25/kWh above that), so small usage increases can trigger disproportionate bill increases. Check both your kWh and your rate line items to identify which changed.

**Q: How do I find my electricity rate per kWh?**
A: Look on your electric bill for "energy charge," "supply charge," or "rate per kWh." It is typically listed in cents per kWh (e.g., 15.87¢/kWh or $0.1587/kWh). If your bill shows tiered rates — for example, 12¢/kWh for the first 500 kWh and 20¢/kWh above that — calculate your blended rate by dividing your total energy charge by total kWh. For time-of-use plans, use a weighted average based on your peak vs. off-peak consumption split. Your utility's website also lists current rate schedules.

**Q: Is it cheaper to use electricity at night?**
A: Only if you are on a time-of-use (TOU) rate plan. Standard flat-rate plans charge the same price per kWh regardless of when you use it. TOU plans offer off-peak rates (typically 9 PM to 7 AM) that are 40–65% cheaper than peak rates — for example, $0.10/kWh off-peak vs. $0.35/kWh peak. If your utility offers a TOU option, shifting heavy loads like EV charging, laundry, dishwasher, and pool pumps to nighttime can save $30–$80/month depending on your shiftable load. Contact your utility to ask about available rate plans — most do not enroll you automatically.

## Seasonal Usage Patterns and Peak Demand

Electricity consumption is not uniform across the year. The national average of 863 kWh/month masks dramatic seasonal swings that can double or triple your bill depending on climate and heating/cooling fuel type.

In the South and Southwest, summer is the expensive season. A Texas household that uses 800 kWh/month in March may consume 1,400–1,800 kWh/month in July and August when central AC runs 12–16 hours daily. At $0.16/kWh, that is a swing from $128/month to $224–$288/month — purely from cooling load. Arizona, Florida, Louisiana, and Mississippi show similar patterns.

In the Northeast and Midwest, homes with electric heat see winter spikes instead. A Massachusetts home on a heat pump might use 600 kWh/month in June but 1,200+ kWh/month in January when the heat pump works overtime against 20°F outdoor temperatures. At $0.31/kWh, that January bill exceeds $370.

Understanding your seasonal pattern is critical for budgeting. Most utilities offer budget billing — a fixed monthly payment based on your trailing 12-month average — which smooths out the spikes. The tradeoff is that you lose the price signal that motivates conservation during peak months. A better approach for most households: estimate your high month separately (check your July or January bill), set aside the difference from your average month in a dedicated savings bucket, and use that to cover the peak without surprises.
