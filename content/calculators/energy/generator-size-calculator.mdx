## What Your Results Mean

**Running watts** is what your appliances consume continuously. **Starting watts** accounts for the surge that electric motors (AC compressors, pumps, furnace blowers) draw for 1–3 seconds on startup — typically 2–3× their running draw. The **recommended generator size** adds a 25% safety margin on top of your peak starting load to prevent the generator from straining or shutting down.

The appliance breakdown table shows exactly where your load is coming from so you can prioritize which circuits to power during an outage.

## How to Use This Calculator

1. Enter the running wattage for each appliance you want to power during an outage — leave at zero for appliances you'll skip.
2. Keep "Include Motor Starting Surge" enabled unless you have soft-start equipment.
3. Find appliance wattages on the label, in the manual, or use the reference table below.
4. The recommended generator size includes a 25% safety buffer — size up, never down.

## The Formula

<div className="formula-section">

**Formula:**
$$
\text{Starting Watts} = \text{Running Watts}_{\text{largest motor}} \times 3 + \text{All other running watts}
$$
$$
\text{Recommended kW} = \frac{\text{Starting Watts} \times 1.25}{1000}
$$

Where:
- **3×** = motor starting surge multiplier (conservative estimate for AC compressors and pumps)
- **1.25** = 25% headroom factor for safe continuous operation

*Source: Generac — Generator Sizing Guide (2025); U.S. Department of Energy — Standby Generator Selection Guidelines (2025)*

</div>

## Worked Example

**Scenario:** Power outage during a heat wave. You want to run:
- Central AC: 1,500 W running / 4,500 W starting
- Refrigerator: 150 W running / 450 W starting
- Furnace blower: 500 W running / 1,500 W starting
- Lights: 300 W (LED, no surge)
- TV + computer: 300 W (no surge)

Total running watts: 2,750 W
Starting watts (AC surge dominates): 4,500 + 1,300 remaining = **5,800 W**
Recommended generator: 5,800 × 1.25 ÷ 1,000 = **7.25 kW → buy a 8–10 kW unit**

## Worked Example 2

**Winter power outage — essentials only (minimal load):**
- Gas furnace blower: 500 W running / 1,500 W starting
- Refrigerator: 150 W running / 450 W starting
- LED lights (8 bulbs): 80 W (no surge)
- Phone chargers (3): 15 W (no surge)
- Modem + router: 25 W (no surge)

Total running watts: 500 + 150 + 80 + 15 + 25 = **770 W**
Starting watts (furnace blower surge dominates): 1,500 + 270 remaining = **1,770 W**
Recommended generator: 1,770 × 1.25 ÷ 1,000 = **2.21 kW → a 3,500 W portable generator handles this easily**

A 3,500 W portable generator costs $400–$800 and burns roughly 0.5 gallons of gasoline per hour at 50% load. With a 4-gallon tank, you get about 8 hours of runtime per fill. For a 48-hour winter outage, budget 12 gallons of gasoline ($36–$48). This minimal-load approach keeps your family warm, food safe, and connected at a fraction of the cost of a whole-house standby unit.

## FAQ

<div className="faq-section">

**Q: What is the difference between running watts and starting watts?**
A: Running watts (also called rated watts) is the continuous power an appliance draws during normal operation. Starting watts (also called surge watts) is the brief spike of 2–3 times more power that electric motors need for 1–3 seconds when they first turn on. Air conditioners, refrigerators, well pumps, and sump pumps all have high starting surges. If your generator can't supply the starting watts, it will either trip its breaker or be damaged. Always size for starting watts, not running watts.

**Q: What size generator do I need for a whole house?**
A: A whole-house standby generator typically requires 20–22 kW for a 2,000–2,500 sq ft home with central AC, electric appliances, and a well pump. If you're willing to shed loads (run only essential circuits), a 10–12 kW portable generator can cover refrigerator, furnace, well pump, lights, and phone charging. The exact size depends on your specific appliance mix — use this calculator with your actual load list for a personalized recommendation.

**Q: Portable generator vs. standby generator — which should I buy?**
A: Portable generators (2–12 kW, $400–$4,000) run on gasoline or propane, require manual setup and refueling, and are suitable for occasional outages or job sites. Standby generators (10–22+ kW, $5,000–$15,000 installed) connect permanently to your natural gas line, start automatically within seconds of an outage, and can run indefinitely. If your area experiences outages more than once a year or you have medical equipment, a standby unit pays for itself in convenience and safety.

**Q: Can I run my central air conditioner on a portable generator?**
A: A typical 2.5-ton central AC unit requires 1,500 W running and 4,500 W starting — so you need a minimum 5,000 W generator just for the AC, but realistically 7,500–10,000 W to run AC plus other essentials like your refrigerator and lights. A 5-ton unit (common in homes over 2,500 sq ft) requires a 12,000+ W generator to handle its 10,500 W starting surge. If your generator is borderline, install a hard-start kit ($80–$150) on your AC compressor to reduce starting surge by 50–65%, or use a soft-start device. Never attempt to run central AC on a generator rated below its starting wattage — you will trip the breaker or damage the compressor.

**Q: How much fuel does a generator use per hour?**
A: Fuel consumption depends on load and generator size. At 50% load (the most efficient operating point), typical consumption rates are: a 3,500 W portable uses 0.4–0.6 gallons/hour of gasoline; a 7,500 W portable uses 0.7–1.0 gallons/hour; a 12,000 W portable uses 1.0–1.5 gallons/hour; and a 22 kW standby uses 2.0–3.5 gallons/hour of propane or 150–250 cubic feet/hour of natural gas. Running at full load increases consumption by 30–50%. For a 48-hour outage on a 7,500 W generator at 50% load, budget 35–48 gallons of gasoline ($105–$192 at $3.00–$4.00/gallon). Store fuel safely in approved containers in a ventilated area away from the generator.

## Generator Safety and Installation Tips

Generators cause an average of 85 carbon monoxide deaths per year in the US, according to the Consumer Product Safety Commission. Following these rules is not optional — they are life-safety requirements.

**Never run a portable generator indoors, in a garage, or in any enclosed space.** Place it outside at least 20 feet from any window, door, or vent, with the exhaust pointing away from the house. Carbon monoxide is odorless and can reach lethal concentrations indoors within minutes. Buy a generator with a built-in CO sensor and auto-shutoff — all major manufacturers (Generac, Honda, Champion) now offer this feature, and some states require it by law.

**Use a transfer switch.** Connecting a generator directly to your home's electrical panel without a transfer switch is illegal in all 50 states and creates back-feed — sending electricity out through your utility meter and into power lines, which can electrocute utility workers restoring power. A manual transfer switch costs $200–$500 installed and lets you safely power specific circuits. An automatic transfer switch ($500–$1,500 installed) is required for standby generators and switches power sources automatically.

**Load management extends runtime and protects equipment.** Stagger motor startups — start the refrigerator first, wait 10 seconds, then start the furnace blower, wait, then add lights. This prevents simultaneous starting surges from exceeding your generator's peak capacity. Run the generator at 50–75% of rated load for optimal fuel efficiency and engine longevity. Running at full load continuously shortens the engine life and increases fuel consumption by 30–50%.

## Sources & Methodology

Starting surge multipliers are based on Generac's published sizing methodology and the U.S. Department of Energy's consumer guidance on standby generators. The 25% headroom factor is the industry standard recommended by generator manufacturers including Generac, Kohler, and Briggs & Stratton to ensure safe continuous operation below 80% of rated capacity. Motor starting surge estimates use a 3× multiplier for single-phase induction motors, which is the conservative case for residential AC compressors and pumps.
