## What Your Results Mean

**Daily kWh** is the raw energy consumed each day — useful for comparing against your meter readings. **Monthly cost** is what shows up on your bill. **Annual cost** reveals which appliances are silently expensive over time: a 75-watt ceiling fan left on 24/7 costs $105/year, while a gaming PC running 6 hours daily can cost $180/year or more.

The summary panel shows all three cost periods side-by-side so you can quickly decide whether an appliance upgrade would pay off.

## How to Use This Calculator

1. Find the wattage of your appliance — it's on the label, in the manual, or use the typical values below.
2. Enter how many hours per day you actually use it (be honest — average over a typical week).
3. Enter your electricity rate from your utility bill (look for "rate per kWh" or "energy charge").
4. Adjust days per month if you only use the appliance seasonally.

## The Formula

<div className="formula-section">

**Formula:**
$$
\text{Daily kWh} = \frac{\text{Watts} \times \text{Hours/Day}}{1000}
$$
$$
\text{Monthly Cost} = \text{Daily kWh} \times \text{Days/Month} \times \text{Rate}
$$

Where:
- **Watts** = appliance power draw (from label or spec sheet)
- **Hours/Day** = average daily runtime
- **Rate** = your utility's cost per kilowatt-hour ($/kWh)

*Source: U.S. Energy Information Administration — Residential Electricity Use (2025); ENERGY STAR — Appliance Energy Use Guidelines (2025)*

## Worked Example

**Electric clothes dryer:** 5,000 watts, 1 hour per day, $0.16/kWh, 30 days/month.

- Daily kWh: 5,000 × 1 ÷ 1,000 = **5.0 kWh**
- Monthly kWh: 5.0 × 30 = **150 kWh**
- Monthly cost: 150 × $0.16 = **$24.00**
- Annual cost: $24.00 × 12 = **$288.00**

That single appliance adds $288/year to your bill. Switching to a heat-pump dryer (typically 1,500 watts) would drop that to $86/year — saving $202/year.

## Worked Example 2

**Window AC unit running through summer:** 1,200 watts, 8 hours/day, $0.17/kWh, 90 days (June–August).

- Daily kWh: 1,200 × 8 ÷ 1,000 = **9.6 kWh**
- Daily cost: 9.6 × $0.17 = **$1.63/day**
- Monthly kWh (30 days): 9.6 × 30 = **288 kWh**
- Monthly cost: 288 × $0.17 = **$48.96/month**
- Full summer cost (90 days): 9.6 × 90 × $0.17 = **$146.88**

That single window unit adds nearly $150 to your summer electric bill. If you run two units (bedroom and living room), you are looking at $294 for the season. Upgrading to an ENERGY STAR-certified window unit rated at 800 watts would cut the summer cost to $97.92 — a savings of $49 per unit per summer.

## How to Lower Your Electricity Costs

The average US household spends $1,500/year on electricity, but targeted changes can cut that by 20–40% without sacrificing comfort. Start with the biggest energy hogs: heating and cooling account for 47% of the typical home's electricity use, according to the EIA.

**Swap incandescent and CFL bulbs for LEDs.** A single 60-watt incandescent replaced by a 9-watt LED saves $7.50/year at $0.16/kWh running 5 hours daily. Across 30 bulbs, that is $225/year.

**Use a smart power strip for electronics.** Phantom loads — devices drawing power while "off" — cost the average home $100–$200/year. TVs, game consoles, computer monitors, and cable boxes are the worst offenders, drawing 5–30 watts each in standby. A $25 smart strip that cuts power to idle devices pays for itself in under 3 months.

**Shift heavy usage to off-peak hours.** If your utility offers time-of-use (TOU) rates, running your dishwasher, dryer, and EV charger after 9 PM can cut those appliances' costs by 30–50%. Peak rates commonly run $0.25–$0.40/kWh while off-peak drops to $0.08–$0.14/kWh.

**Upgrade your worst appliance first.** An old refrigerator manufactured before 2001 uses 600–800 kWh/year. A new ENERGY STAR model uses 350–450 kWh/year, saving $40–$60/year at $0.16/kWh — and often qualifies for a $50–$100 utility rebate.

## FAQ

<div className="faq-section">

**Q: How do I find the wattage of an appliance?**
A: Check the label on the back or bottom of the appliance — it lists watts (W) or amps (A). If you see amps, multiply by your home voltage (120V in the US) to get watts. For example, 12.5 amps × 120V = 1,500 watts. You can also look up the model number online or use a Kill-A-Watt meter for the most accurate real-world measurement.

**Q: What is the average US electricity rate per kWh?**
A: The US residential average is about $0.16/kWh as of 2025, according to the U.S. Energy Information Administration. However, rates vary dramatically by state: Louisiana averages $0.10/kWh, Washington state $0.11/kWh, California $0.26/kWh, and Hawaii $0.33/kWh. Check your electric bill for your exact rate — it's listed as the "energy charge" or "rate per kWh."

**Q: Which household appliances are the most expensive to run?**
A: The biggest electricity consumers are typically central air conditioning (3,000–5,000 W), electric water heaters (4,000 W), electric dryers (5,000 W), and electric furnaces (10,000–15,000 W). Among always-on devices, older refrigerators (400–600 W) and pool pumps (1,500 W running 8+ hours daily) add up fast. Switching to ENERGY STAR-certified models typically cuts consumption by 20–50%.

**Q: How do I read the kWh charge on my electricity bill?**
A: Your electricity bill lists usage in kilowatt-hours (kWh) and a rate per kWh under "energy charge" or "supply charge." Multiply those two numbers to get your energy cost before taxes and fees. Many utilities also add a fixed monthly service charge ($5–$15) that applies regardless of usage. If your bill shows tiered rates — for example, $0.12/kWh for the first 500 kWh and $0.18/kWh above that — enter a blended average rate into the calculator by dividing your total energy charge by total kWh consumed. For time-of-use plans, use the weighted average across peak and off-peak periods.

**Q: Does turning off appliances actually save money, or does the power surge on startup cancel it out?**
A: Turning off appliances saves money — the startup surge myth is exactly that, a myth. Motor-driven devices like air conditioners and refrigerators do draw 2–3× their running wattage for 1–3 seconds at startup, but that brief spike uses trivially more energy than a few extra seconds of running. For example, a refrigerator compressor surging to 450 W for 3 seconds uses 0.000375 kWh ($0.00006 at $0.16/kWh). Leaving that same refrigerator running an extra 3 minutes to "avoid" the surge wastes 0.0075 kWh — 20 times more energy. The only exception is fluorescent lighting, where very frequent on-off cycling shortens bulb life; LEDs have no such issue.

## Seasonal Usage Patterns and Peak Demand

Electricity costs are not uniform across the year. The average US household uses 886 kWh/month, but that figure hides wide seasonal swings. In summer, air conditioning pushes consumption to 1,100–1,400 kWh/month in hot states like Texas, Arizona, and Florida. In winter, electric heating in states without natural gas can drive usage above 1,500 kWh/month.

Understanding your seasonal pattern matters because many utilities charge higher rates during peak demand months (June–September in most of the US). Some utilities apply demand charges or tiered rates that increase the per-kWh price once you exceed a baseline. For example, Southern California Edison charges $0.27/kWh for the first 350 kWh and $0.39/kWh above that during summer — meaning your marginal cost per kWh is 44% higher once you cross the baseline.

To estimate seasonal costs accurately, run this calculator twice: once with your summer usage (check your July or August bill) and once with your winter usage (check January). The difference reveals exactly how much seasonal loads like AC or space heaters add to your annual total. A household using 600 kWh/month in spring but 1,200 kWh/month in summer is spending an extra $96/month at $0.16/kWh purely on cooling.
