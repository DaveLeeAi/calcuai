## What Your Results Mean

**Annual gas heating cost** is the effective cost after accounting for furnace efficiency — an 80% AFUE furnace wastes 20% of every therm up the flue. **Annual electric heating cost** adjusts for heat pump efficiency: an HSPF 9 unit produces 2.64 units of heat for every unit of electricity consumed, making it far cheaper than resistance heat. The **10-year savings** shows the compounding financial impact of choosing the right fuel type.

## How to Use This Calculator

1. Find your annual therms from your gas bill (look for "total therms" or "usage"). Typical home: 500–900 therms/year.
2. Enter your gas rate per therm — found on your gas bill or your utility's website.
3. Enter your furnace's AFUE rating (from the yellow EnergyGuide label). Older furnaces: 55–70%. Modern standard: 80%. High-efficiency: 90–98%.
4. For the electric side: if you have a heat pump, enter its HSPF rating (usually 8–13 for modern units). If you have baseboard/electric resistance heat, enter 0.
5. Enter your electricity rate per kWh from your electric bill.

## The Formula

<div className="formula-section">

**Formula:**
$$
\text{Gas Annual Cost} = \frac{\text{Therms} \times \text{Rate}_{\text{gas}}}{\text{AFUE} / 100}
$$
$$
\text{Heat Pump Annual Cost} = \frac{\text{BTU needed}}{\text{HSPF} \times 3{,}412} \times \text{Rate}_{\text{electric}}
$$

Where:
- **AFUE** = Annual Fuel Utilization Efficiency (e.g., 80 = 80% efficient)
- **HSPF** = Heating Seasonal Performance Factor
- **3,412** = BTUs per kWh (conversion constant)
- **1 therm** = 100,000 BTU = 29.3 kWh of energy

*Source: U.S. Energy Information Administration — Natural Gas and Electricity Prices (2025); ENERGY STAR — Heating System Efficiency Ratings (2025)*

</div>

## Worked Example

**Midwest home, 700 therms/year heating load:**

**Gas furnace (80% AFUE) at $1.50/therm:**
- Effective cost: 700 × $1.50 ÷ 0.80 = **$1,313/year**

**Heat pump (HSPF 9) at $0.16/kWh:**
- Energy needed: 700 therms × 29.3 kWh/therm = 20,510 kWh equivalent
- Actual kWh used: 20,510 ÷ (9 ÷ 3.412) = 7,775 kWh
- Annual cost: 7,775 × $0.16 = **$1,244/year**

In this scenario, gas is still slightly cheaper. But at $0.20/kWh electric rates (or with an HSPF 13 heat pump), electric wins by a wide margin.

## FAQ

<div className="faq-section">

**Q: Is gas or electric heating cheaper right now?**
A: It depends heavily on your local utility rates. In most of the US, natural gas heating costs $600–$1,500/year while electric resistance heating costs $1,200–$2,500/year for the same home — making gas cheaper. However, heat pumps change the equation: at HSPF 9–13 efficiency, electric heat pumps often cost **less** than gas in moderate climates, especially in high-gas-rate areas (New England) or low-electricity-rate areas (Pacific Northwest). Use this calculator with your actual rates to find out which is cheaper for your home.

**Q: What does AFUE mean for a gas furnace?**
A: AFUE (Annual Fuel Utilization Efficiency) is the percentage of fuel that becomes useful heat. An 80% AFUE furnace converts 80 cents of every dollar of gas into heat — and loses 20 cents up the flue. Federal law mandates a minimum of 80% AFUE for new furnaces in most of the US. High-efficiency condensing furnaces reach 95–98% AFUE, recovering heat from exhaust gases. Upgrading from a 60% AFUE furnace to a 96% AFUE unit cuts gas consumption by 37.5%.

**Q: Should I switch from gas to a heat pump?**
A: A heat pump makes financial sense when: (1) your electricity rate is below $0.18/kWh, (2) your climate doesn't regularly fall below 15°F (or you choose a cold-climate heat pump rated to −13°F), and (3) you're replacing an aging furnace anyway. The additional upfront cost of a heat pump ($2,000–$5,000 more than a gas furnace) is offset by the Inflation Reduction Act's $2,000 heat pump tax credit through 2032 and lower operating costs. In warm climates, the decision is almost always yes — heat pumps handle both heating and cooling, replacing two systems with one.

## Sources & Methodology

Gas cost calculations account for furnace efficiency losses using the AFUE rating. Heat pump calculations use the HSPF (Heating Seasonal Performance Factor) divided by 3.412 BTU/Wh to get the coefficient of performance. National average natural gas prices ($1.50/therm) and electricity rates ($0.16/kWh) are from the U.S. Energy Information Administration's Monthly Energy Review (2025). The 1 therm = 100,000 BTU = 29.3 kWh conversion is the standard thermodynamic equivalence used by the EIA.
