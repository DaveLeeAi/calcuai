## What Your Results Mean

**Gallons needed** is the amount of propane required to bring your tank from its current level to 80% capacity (the standard fill limit). Propane tanks are never filled to 100% — the 80% rule allows space for the liquid propane to expand safely with temperature changes. **Total cost** is the gallons needed multiplied by your local price per gallon. **Cost per fill** shows what a complete refill from empty would cost at your current price, which is useful for budgeting seasonal refills.

## How to Use This Calculator

1. Select your tank size — common residential sizes are 120-gallon, 250-gallon, 500-gallon, and 1,000-gallon. For portable tanks, select "20-lb BBQ tank" (4.7 gallons usable).
2. Enter your current tank level as a percentage. Most tanks have a gauge on top — read it before ordering to avoid overpaying for a partial fill.
3. Enter your local propane price per gallon. Check with your supplier or the EIA weekly propane price report for regional averages.
4. The calculator shows gallons needed, total cost, and cost per full fill.

## The Formula

<div className="formula-section">

**Formula:**
$$
\text{Usable Capacity} = \text{Tank Size (gallons)} \times 0.80
$$
$$
\text{Gallons Needed} = \text{Usable Capacity} \times (1 - \text{Current Level \%})
$$
$$
\text{Total Cost} = \text{Gallons Needed} \times \text{Price per Gallon}
$$

Where:
- **0.80** = industry-standard maximum fill level (80% rule) per NFPA 58, allowing expansion space for liquid propane
- **Current Level %** = your tank gauge reading as a decimal (e.g., 20% = 0.20)
- **Price per Gallon** = your local propane price (national average: $2.80/gallon, EIA 2025)

*Source: U.S. Energy Information Administration — Weekly Heating Oil and Propane Prices (2025); National Fire Protection Association — NFPA 58: Liquefied Petroleum Gas Code (2024)*

## Worked Example 1

**500-gallon residential tank at 20%, $2.80/gallon:**

- Usable capacity: 500 x 0.80 = **400 gallons**
- Current propane: 400 x 0.20 = **80 gallons remaining**
- Gallons needed: 400 - 80 = **320 gallons**
- Total cost: 320 x $2.80 = **$896.00**

Most propane suppliers recommend ordering when your tank reaches 20-25% to avoid running out during cold snaps. A 500-gallon tank providing home heating in a cold climate (2,000+ heating degree days) may use 80-120 gallons per month in winter, so a tank at 20% has roughly 2-3 weeks of supply remaining.

## Worked Example 2

**20-lb BBQ tank, empty, $4.50/gallon at retail exchange:**

- Usable capacity: 20 lbs / 4.24 lbs per gallon = **4.7 gallons**
- Current propane: 0 gallons (empty)
- Gallons needed: **4.7 gallons**
- Total cost: 4.7 x $4.50 = **$21.15**

Retail tank exchanges (at gas stations and hardware stores) typically charge $4.00-$5.50 per gallon equivalent, and many exchange tanks are only filled to 15 lbs (3.5 gallons) rather than the full 20 lbs. Refilling at a propane dealer is usually cheaper — $2.50-$3.50/gallon for the full 4.7 gallons — but requires bringing your tank to the fill station.

## FAQ

<div className="faq-section">

**Q: Why is my propane tank only filled to 80%?**
A: The 80% fill rule is a safety requirement under NFPA 58 (Liquefied Petroleum Gas Code). Liquid propane expands approximately 1.5% for every 10 degrees F increase in temperature. A tank filled to 100% on a cool morning could exceed safe pressure levels by afternoon if temperatures rise significantly. The 20% ullage (empty space) provides room for this thermal expansion without triggering the pressure relief valve. This means a "full" 500-gallon tank holds 400 usable gallons, a 250-gallon tank holds 200 gallons, and so on.

**Q: When is the cheapest time to buy propane?**
A: Propane prices are typically lowest from late spring through early fall (May-September) when heating demand drops. The EIA reports seasonal price spreads of $0.20-$0.50/gallon between summer lows and winter peaks, depending on region. Many propane suppliers offer "summer fill" or "pre-buy" programs that lock in lower per-gallon rates for the upcoming heating season. Ordering a full fill in August or September, rather than waiting until your tank runs low in January, can save $60-$160 on a 500-gallon tank refill.

**Q: How many gallons of propane does a typical home use per year?**
A: Annual propane consumption varies widely by climate and usage. A home using propane only for heating in a cold climate (northern U.S.) may use 800-1,200 gallons per year. A home using propane for heating, hot water, cooking, and clothes drying typically uses 1,000-1,500 gallons. In moderate climates or homes using propane only for water heating and cooking, annual consumption may be 200-400 gallons. The EIA reports average household propane consumption of approximately 750 gallons/year nationally, but this average blends very different usage patterns across climate zones.

## Sources & Methodology

Propane price data is from the U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA) Weekly Heating Oil and Propane Prices report (2025). The national average residential propane price of $2.80/gallon reflects the EIA's most recent heating season data — prices vary significantly by region, with the Midwest typically $0.20-$0.40/gallon lower and the Northeast $0.20-$0.40 higher than the national average. The 80% fill rule and tank specifications reference NFPA 58: Liquefied Petroleum Gas Code (2024 edition). BBQ tank capacity is based on the standard DOT 20-lb (nominal) cylinder holding 4.7 gallons (20 lbs / 4.24 lbs per gallon at 60 degrees F). Seasonal pricing patterns reflect 5-year EIA historical data (2020-2025). All costs are estimates; actual prices depend on your supplier, delivery minimums, contract terms, and market conditions at time of purchase.
