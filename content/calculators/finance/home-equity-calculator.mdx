## What Home Equity Is and What Your Results Mean

Home equity is the difference between your home's current market value and the total amount you owe on it — including your primary mortgage, any second mortgage or HELOC balance, and other liens such as tax liens or mechanic's liens. If your home is worth \$400,000 and you owe \$250,000, you have \$150,000 in equity. That equity represents real wealth: it is the portion of your home that you actually own, free and clear.

**Equity percentage** tells you what fraction of your home you own outright. At 37.5% equity (\$150,000 of a \$400,000 home), you own just over a third. The inverse — your **loan-to-value ratio (LTV)** at 62.5% — is what lenders focus on. LTV determines your eligibility for HELOCs, cash-out refinancing, and PMI removal. Most lenders require an LTV at or below 80% to approve a HELOC and below 80% to remove PMI on a conventional mortgage.

**Borrowable equity** is not the same as total equity. Lenders will not let you borrow 100% of your equity because they need a cushion against price declines. At an 80% LTV cap, borrowable equity = (home value × 0.80) − total debt. On a \$400,000 home with \$250,000 in debt, that is \$320,000 − \$250,000 = **\$70,000** you can realistically access through a HELOC or home equity loan. Some lenders extend to 90% LTV for borrowers with strong credit, increasing the accessible amount to \$110,000. For a detailed HELOC payment analysis, use our [HELOC Calculator](/finance/heloc-calculator).

**Appreciation and return on investment** show the wealth-building power of homeownership leverage. If you bought for \$300,000 with a \$60,000 down payment and the home is now worth \$400,000, your \$100,000 appreciation represents a 33.3% gain on the home's price — but a **150% return on your \$60,000 down payment**. This leverage effect is why real estate remains a primary wealth-building vehicle for American households.

## The Formula

**Home equity:**

$$\text{Equity} = \text{Home Value} - \text{Mortgage Balance} - \text{Second Mortgage} - \text{Other Liens}$$

**Equity percentage:**

$$\text{Equity \%} = \frac{\text{Equity}}{\text{Home Value}} \times 100$$

**Loan-to-value ratio:**

$$\text{LTV} = \frac{\text{Total Debt}}{\text{Home Value}} \times 100$$

**Borrowable equity at a given LTV limit:**

$$\text{Borrowable Equity} = \max\left(0,\; \text{Home Value} \times \text{LTV Limit} - \text{Total Debt}\right)$$

**Appreciation:**

$$\text{Appreciation} = \text{Home Value} - \text{Original Purchase Price}$$

**Return on investment:**

$$\text{ROI} = \frac{\text{Equity} - \text{Down Payment}}{\text{Down Payment}} \times 100$$

Where:
- **Home Value** = current estimated market value
- **Total Debt** = mortgage balance + second mortgage + other liens
- **LTV Limit** = 0.80 (standard) or 0.90 (extended)
- **Down Payment** = original down payment at purchase

**Source:** Federal Housing Finance Agency (FHFA) equity measurement standards; Freddie Mac Seller/Servicer Guide (combined LTV requirements); Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) home equity resources.

## Worked Example

### \$400,000 Home with \$250,000 Mortgage

**Inputs:**
- Home Value: \$400,000
- Mortgage Balance: \$250,000
- Second Mortgage: \$0
- Other Liens: \$0
- Original Purchase Price: \$300,000
- Down Payment: \$60,000

**Step-by-step:**

1. Total debt: \$250,000 + \$0 + \$0 = **\$250,000**
2. Equity: \$400,000 − \$250,000 = **\$150,000**
3. Equity percentage: \$150,000 / \$400,000 × 100 = **37.5%**
4. LTV: \$250,000 / \$400,000 × 100 = **62.5%**
5. Borrowable equity at 80%: (\$400,000 × 0.80) − \$250,000 = \$320,000 − \$250,000 = **\$70,000**
6. Borrowable equity at 90%: (\$400,000 × 0.90) − \$250,000 = \$360,000 − \$250,000 = **\$110,000**
7. Appreciation: \$400,000 − \$300,000 = **\$100,000** (33.3%)
8. Principal paid: (\$300,000 − \$60,000) − \$250,000 = \$240,000 − \$250,000 → **\$0** (mortgage may have included closing costs or be slightly negative due to rounding)
9. Total invested: \$60,000 + max(0, principal paid) = **\$60,000**
10. Return on down payment: (\$150,000 − \$60,000) / \$60,000 × 100 = **150%**

**Interpretation:** You have strong equity (37.5%) and a comfortable LTV (62.5%), putting you well within the range for HELOC approval, cash-out refinance, or PMI removal. The \$70,000 in borrowable equity at 80% LTV could fund a major renovation. Your 150% ROI on your initial \$60,000 investment demonstrates the power of leverage in real estate — your home appreciated \$100,000, but your actual cash outlay was only \$60,000.

## 5 Strategies to Build Home Equity Faster

**1. Make extra principal payments.** Even \$100/month extra on a \$250,000 mortgage at 6.5% saves \$47,000 in interest and pays off the loan 5 years early. Specify "apply to principal" when making extra payments.

**2. Refinance to a shorter term.** Switching from a 30-year to a 15-year mortgage increases your monthly payment but dramatically accelerates equity building. On \$250,000 at 6%, a 15-year mortgage builds \$8,400 more equity in the first year than a 30-year.

**3. Invest in value-adding improvements.** Kitchen and bathroom renovations recoup 60%–80% of their cost at resale (NAHB 2024 Remodeling Impact Report). A \$30,000 kitchen remodel that adds \$20,000 in home value increases your equity by \$20,000 while costing you only \$30,000 out of pocket.

**4. Avoid additional liens.** Every dollar of HELOC draw or second mortgage reduces your net equity. Borrow against equity only for investments that increase the home's value or generate returns exceeding your borrowing cost.

**5. Wait for natural appreciation.** The national average home appreciation rate is 3%–5% per year (FHFA House Price Index). On a \$400,000 home, that is \$12,000–\$20,000 in annual equity growth without any action on your part. In high-growth markets, appreciation can exceed 8%–10% annually.

## Equity vs. Borrowable Equity

Your total equity and your borrowable equity are not the same number, and confusing them is a common mistake.

**Total equity** is the full difference between your home value and total debt. With a \$400,000 home and \$250,000 owed, you have \$150,000 in total equity. This is your net worth in the property.

**Borrowable equity** is the portion lenders will actually let you access. Lenders cap borrowing at a combined loan-to-value (CLTV) ratio — typically 80%, sometimes 90%. The formula is: Home Value × LTV Limit − Total Existing Debt. With the same \$400,000 home and \$250,000 debt, your borrowable equity at 80% is only \$70,000 — less than half your total equity.

The gap exists because lenders need a buffer against home price declines. If your home value drops 15% (from \$400,000 to \$340,000), a lender who let you borrow up to 80% LTV is still protected — their total loan exposure is \$320,000 against a \$340,000 asset. Without this cushion, even modest price declines could leave borrowers underwater and lenders holding losses.

| LTV Requirement | Use Case | Typical Lenders |
|:---:|:---|:---|
| 80% CLTV | HELOC approval, PMI removal | Most conventional lenders |
| 80% LTV | Cash-out refinance | Fannie Mae / Freddie Mac |
| 85% CLTV | HELOC with good credit (720+) | Credit unions, online lenders |
| 90% CLTV | HELOC with excellent credit (760+) | Select credit unions |
| 78% LTV | Automatic PMI cancellation | All conventional lenders (by law) |

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much equity do I have in my home?

Your home equity equals your home's current market value minus everything you owe on it — mortgage balance, second mortgage, HELOC balance, and any liens. A home worth \$400,000 with a \$250,000 mortgage has \$150,000 in equity (37.5%). To get an accurate value, use a recent appraisal, online home value tool (Zillow, Redfin), or your county tax assessor's estimate. Lenders will require a formal appraisal for any equity-based loan.

### How much equity can I borrow against?

You can borrow against the portion of your equity that keeps your combined loan-to-value ratio below the lender's threshold — typically 80%. On a \$400,000 home with \$250,000 owed, the maximum borrowable equity at 80% CLTV is \$70,000 (calculated as \$400,000 × 0.80 − \$250,000). Some lenders allow up to 85%–90% CLTV for borrowers with credit scores above 720–760, which would increase your borrowable equity to \$90,000–\$110,000.

### How do I check my home's current value?

Start with free online estimates: Zillow Zestimate, Redfin Estimate, or Realtor.com's home value tool. These use comparable sales data and are typically accurate within 5%–10%. For a more precise figure, request a comparative market analysis (CMA) from a local real estate agent (usually free). For lending purposes, a formal appraisal (\$300–\$500) is required and provides the definitive value.

### What is a good LTV ratio?

An LTV below 80% is considered healthy — it means you have at least 20% equity, qualifies you for the best loan terms, and eliminates private mortgage insurance (PMI). An LTV between 80%–90% is acceptable but may carry PMI costs and slightly higher interest rates. Above 90% LTV limits your refinancing and borrowing options. Below 50% LTV gives you maximum flexibility and the lowest available rates on HELOCs and home equity loans.

### When can I remove PMI?

Under the Homeowners Protection Act, your lender must automatically cancel PMI when your LTV reaches 78% based on the original home value. You can request removal at 80% LTV. If your home has appreciated, you may reach 80% LTV sooner than the original amortization schedule predicts — but you will typically need a new appraisal to prove the higher value. FHA loans have different rules: FHA MIP (mortgage insurance premium) cannot be removed on loans with less than 10% down; you must refinance to a conventional loan.

### How does home equity affect my net worth?

Home equity is typically the largest single component of household net worth for American homeowners. According to the Federal Reserve's Survey of Consumer Finances, the median homeowner has approximately \$200,000 in home equity, representing over 60% of their total net worth. Changes in home value directly impact your net worth — a 10% decline on a \$400,000 home reduces your equity by \$40,000, while a 10% increase adds \$40,000.

### Can I have negative equity?

Yes. Negative equity (being "underwater") occurs when your total debt exceeds your home's market value. This can happen when home prices decline, you bought with a small down payment, or you took on additional liens. If you owe \$250,000 on a home now worth \$200,000, you have −\$50,000 in equity. Negative equity prevents refinancing, selling without bringing cash to closing, and accessing home equity products.

### Is home equity the same as home value?

No. Home value is what your property is worth on the market. Home equity is home value minus what you owe. A home worth \$500,000 with a \$300,000 mortgage has \$500,000 in value but only \$200,000 in equity. You build equity in two ways: paying down your mortgage principal and home price appreciation.
