## What a HELOC Is and What Your Results Mean

A Home Equity Line of Credit (HELOC) is a revolving credit line secured by your home equity — the difference between your home's market value and your remaining mortgage balance. Unlike a home equity loan (which provides a lump sum at a fixed rate), a HELOC works like a credit card: you draw funds as needed during the draw period, pay interest only on what you borrow, and repay the balance during the repayment period.

**Your maximum credit line depends on three factors.** First, your home equity: a \$400,000 home with a \$200,000 mortgage has \$200,000 in equity. Second, the lender's loan-to-value limit: most lenders cap the combined LTV (existing mortgage + HELOC) at 80%–85% of the home value. At 80%, your max HELOC credit line is \$120,000 (because \$200,000 mortgage + \$120,000 = \$320,000 = 80% of \$400,000). Third, your creditworthiness: lenders may offer less than the maximum based on credit score, income, and debt ratios.

**The two-phase payment structure creates a "payment shock" risk.** During the draw period (typically 5–10 years), most HELOCs require interest-only payments — on a \$50,000 balance at 8.5%, that is just \$354/month. When the draw period ends, the balance must be repaid over the repayment period (10–20 years) with fully amortized payments, jumping to \$434/month or more. Borrowers who budget only for the draw period payment may face difficulty when the higher repayment begins. For a fixed-rate alternative with predictable payments, see our [Loan Calculator](/finance/loan-calculator).

## How to Use This Calculator

1. **Enter your Home Value** — the current market value of your home, not the original purchase price. Use a recent appraisal, Zillow Zestimate, or your county assessor's estimate. Lenders will order a formal appraisal during the application process.

2. **Enter your Current Mortgage Balance** — the remaining principal on your first mortgage. Find this on your most recent mortgage statement or online portal. If your home is paid off, enter \$0.

3. **Set the Credit Limit percentage** — most lenders allow 80% of equity. Some allow up to 85%–90% for borrowers with excellent credit (740+). This percentage determines your maximum credit line.

4. **Enter the Amount to Borrow** — how much you plan to draw from the HELOC. The calculator caps this at your maximum credit line. If you need more than your max, consider a cash-out refinance instead.

5. **Set the Interest Rate** — HELOC rates are typically variable, tied to the Prime Rate plus a margin. As of 2025, the average HELOC rate is approximately 8.5%. Your actual rate depends on credit score, LTV, and lender.

6. **Set Draw and Repayment Periods** — the draw period (when you can borrow) is typically 10 years, and the repayment period (when you repay the balance) is typically 20 years. Total HELOC term is usually 30 years.

7. **Toggle Interest-Only Payments** — most HELOCs default to interest-only during the draw period. Turn this off to see fully amortized payments during draw, which costs more monthly but builds equity faster.

## The Formula

**Home equity and maximum credit line:**

$$\text{Home Equity} = \text{Home Value} - \text{Mortgage Balance}$$

$$\text{Max Credit Line} = \text{Home Equity} \times \frac{\text{Credit Limit \%}}{100}$$

**Interest-only draw period payment:**

$$M_{\text{draw}} = P \times \frac{r}{12}$$

Where:
- **$P$** = amount drawn (principal balance)
- **$r$** = annual interest rate (as decimal)

**Amortized repayment period payment:**

$$M_{\text{repay}} = P \times \frac{\frac{r}{12}\left(1+\frac{r}{12}\right)^n}{\left(1+\frac{r}{12}\right)^n - 1}$$

Where:
- **$P$** = remaining balance at start of repayment (full draw amount if interest-only draw)
- **$r$** = annual interest rate (as decimal)
- **$n$** = repayment period in months

**Combined loan-to-value ratio:**

$$\text{CLTV} = \frac{\text{Mortgage Balance} + \text{HELOC Draw}}{\text{Home Value}} \times 100$$

**Source:** Federal Reserve Board — "What You Should Know About Home Equity Lines of Credit" (consumer handbook). Repayment uses the standard fixed-rate amortization formula as defined by the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau.

## Worked Example

### \$50,000 HELOC at 8.5% — Interest-Only Draw

**Inputs:**
- Home Value: \$400,000
- Mortgage Balance: \$200,000
- Credit Limit: 80% of equity
- Draw Amount: \$50,000
- Interest Rate: 8.5%
- Draw Period: 10 years
- Repayment Period: 20 years
- Interest-Only: Yes

**Step-by-step:**

1. Home equity: \$400,000 − \$200,000 = **\$200,000**
2. Max credit line: \$200,000 × 0.80 = **\$160,000**
3. Available to borrow: min(\$50,000, \$160,000) = **\$50,000**
4. Combined LTV: (\$200,000 + \$50,000) / \$400,000 = **62.5%**
5. Draw period payment (interest-only): \$50,000 × 0.085 / 12 = **\$354.17/month**
6. Draw period total interest: \$354.17 × 120 months = **\$42,500**
7. Balance entering repayment: **\$50,000** (full amount, since interest-only)
8. Repayment period payment (amortized over 20 years): **\$434/month**
9. Repayment total interest: (\$434 × 240) − \$50,000 = **\$54,160**
10. **Total interest over 30 years: \$42,500 + \$54,160 = \$96,660**
11. **Total cost: \$50,000 + \$96,660 = \$146,660**

**Interpretation:** Over the full 30-year HELOC term, you pay nearly twice the borrowed amount in interest alone. The payment jump from \$354 to \$434 at the start of repayment is manageable here (23% increase), but on a larger draw — say \$150,000 — the jump from \$1,063 to \$1,302 could strain a household budget. Consider making principal payments during the draw period to reduce the repayment shock.

## HELOC vs. Home Equity Loan

| Feature | HELOC | Home Equity Loan |
|---|---|---|
| Rate Type | Variable (tied to Prime) | Fixed |
| Disbursement | Draw as needed | Lump sum |
| Draw Period | 5–10 years | None (receive full amount) |
| Payment During Draw | Interest-only (usually) | Full P&I from day one |
| Payment Predictability | Fluctuates with rates | Fixed for life of loan |
| Best For | Ongoing expenses, renovation phases | One-time large expense |
| Typical Rate (2025) | 8.0%–9.5% | 8.5%–10.0% |
| Closing Costs | \$0–\$500 (many waive fees) | \$2,000–\$5,000 |
| Tax Deductible | Yes, if used for home improvement | Yes, if used for home improvement |

A HELOC offers flexibility and lower upfront costs but exposes you to rate risk. A home equity loan offers payment certainty but less flexibility. If you know the exact amount needed and want fixed payments, the home equity loan is typically better. If you need funds over time — such as a phased renovation — the HELOC is more efficient.

## Draw Period vs. Repayment Period

The draw period is the initial phase (typically 5–10 years) when you can borrow, repay, and re-borrow up to your credit limit. Most lenders require only interest payments during this phase, keeping monthly costs low. At 8.5% on a \$50,000 balance, the interest-only payment is \$354/month — affordable for most homeowners.

The repayment period begins when the draw period expires. You can no longer borrow, and the outstanding balance is converted to a fully amortizing loan repaid over the remaining term (typically 10–20 years). The monthly payment increases because it now includes both principal and interest. On the same \$50,000 at 8.5% over 20 years, the payment rises to \$434/month — a 23% increase.

**Strategies to manage the transition:**
- **Make principal payments during draw.** Even \$100/month extra during the 10-year draw reduces your repayment balance by \$12,000+, lowering the future payment.
- **Refinance before repayment begins.** If rates drop, refinancing into a new HELOC resets the draw period.
- **Convert to a fixed rate.** Some lenders offer a fixed-rate lock option during the draw period, converting part or all of your balance to a predictable payment.

## Key Factors That Affect Your HELOC

**Interest rate environment** — HELOC rates move with the Federal Reserve's policy rate. Every 0.25% rate increase adds approximately \$10.42/month per \$50,000 of outstanding balance. In a rising-rate environment, a \$100,000 HELOC draw could see payments increase by \$200+/month over 2–3 years.

**Home value changes** — If your home value declines, your equity shrinks and lenders may freeze or reduce your credit line. During the 2008–2010 housing downturn, millions of HELOC credit lines were frozen. Current homeowners should maintain a cushion — avoid drawing the maximum credit line.

**Combined LTV ratio** — Lenders evaluate risk using your CLTV. Below 80% CLTV gets the best rates. Between 80%–90% carries a rate premium. Above 90% is rarely approved. If your first mortgage balance is high relative to home value, HELOC options will be limited.

**Credit score** — Most HELOC lenders require a minimum 680 credit score. Scores above 740 qualify for the lowest rate margins. The difference between a 680 and 760 score can be 1.0%–1.5% in rate, or \$42–\$63/month per \$50,000 borrowed.

## When a HELOC Makes Sense

A HELOC is best suited for situations where you need flexible access to funds over time rather than a single lump sum. The most common — and financially sound — uses include:

- **Home renovations** — especially phased projects like a kitchen remodel followed by bathroom updates. Interest may be tax-deductible if the funds improve your home (per IRS Publication 936).
- **Emergency fund backup** — a HELOC provides a low-cost safety net without drawing funds until needed. You pay nothing until you borrow.
- **Debt consolidation** — replacing 20%+ credit card debt with an 8.5% HELOC saves significant interest, but only if you avoid running up new card balances.
- **Bridge financing** — covering the gap between buying a new home and selling your current one.

A HELOC is generally a poor choice for discretionary spending, vacations, or investments — you are putting your home at risk for non-essential purchases.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much can I borrow with a HELOC?

Your maximum HELOC credit line equals your home equity multiplied by the lender's LTV limit, minus your existing mortgage. With a \$400,000 home, \$200,000 mortgage, and 80% LTV limit, your max HELOC is \$120,000 — calculated as (\$400,000 × 0.80) − \$200,000 = \$120,000. Some lenders allow up to 85%–90% LTV for borrowers with credit scores above 740, which would increase the max to \$140,000–\$160,000 on the same home. Minimum HELOC amounts are typically \$10,000–\$25,000.

### What is the current average HELOC interest rate?

As of early 2025, the average HELOC rate is approximately 8.5%, though rates range from 7.0% to 11.0% depending on your credit score, LTV ratio, and lender. HELOC rates are variable, typically calculated as Prime Rate (currently 8.50%) plus or minus a margin. Borrowers with excellent credit (760+) and low LTV ratios may qualify for Prime minus 0.5%, while those with lower scores pay Prime plus 1%–2%.

### Are HELOC interest payments tax deductible?

HELOC interest is tax deductible only if the funds are used to "buy, build, or substantially improve" the home securing the loan, per the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 (IRS Publication 936). The deduction is limited to interest on up to \$750,000 of total qualified mortgage debt (\$375,000 if married filing separately). If you use HELOC funds for a kitchen renovation, the interest is deductible. If you use the funds for a vacation or to pay off credit cards, it is not. Keep records of how HELOC funds are spent.

### What happens if I can't make HELOC payments?

A HELOC is secured by your home. If you default, the lender can foreclose — though this is typically a last resort after months of missed payments and collection attempts. Before reaching that point, contact your lender to discuss hardship options: temporary payment reduction, forbearance, or loan modification. Unlike credit card debt, HELOC debt cannot be discharged easily in bankruptcy because it is secured by a physical asset.

### Can I pay off a HELOC early?

Yes, most HELOCs allow early repayment without penalty during the draw period — you can pay down the balance and re-borrow as needed. Some lenders charge an early closure fee (\$300–\$500) if you close the HELOC account entirely within the first 2–3 years. Check your loan agreement for specific terms. Paying extra toward principal during the draw period is one of the most effective ways to reduce total interest cost.

### How is a HELOC different from a cash-out refinance?

A cash-out refinance replaces your existing mortgage with a larger one, giving you the difference in cash. A HELOC is a second lien that sits behind your first mortgage. Key differences: a cash-out refi offers a fixed rate but resets your mortgage term and requires \$3,000–\$6,000 in closing costs. A HELOC has variable rates but minimal closing costs (\$0–\$500), lets you borrow only what you need, and does not disturb your existing mortgage. If you have a low-rate first mortgage (say 3%), a HELOC preserves that rate while a cash-out refi would replace it at current rates (6.5%+).

### What credit score do I need for a HELOC?

Most lenders require a minimum credit score of 680 for HELOC approval, with 700+ preferred. Scores above 740 qualify for the best rates, typically Prime Rate minus a small margin. Below 680, options are limited and rates carry a significant premium (2%–3% higher). In addition to credit score, lenders evaluate your debt-to-income ratio (typically requiring below 43%), employment history, and the property's appraised value.

### How long does it take to get a HELOC?

The HELOC application and approval process typically takes 2–6 weeks from application to closing. The timeline includes application review (1–3 days), home appraisal (1–2 weeks), underwriting (1–2 weeks), and closing (1 day). Some online lenders advertise 2-week closings. After closing, there is a 3-day rescission period (required by federal law) before you can access funds. Once the HELOC is open, drawing funds is typically instant via checks, online transfer, or a linked debit card.
