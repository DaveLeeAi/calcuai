## What Your Tax Estimate Shows

Your federal income tax is calculated using a progressive bracket system — not a flat rate. This means your first \$11,925 of taxable income is taxed at 10% regardless of how much you earn, the next \$36,550 at 12%, and so on through seven brackets up to 37%. The result: your **effective tax rate** (what you actually pay as a percentage of gross income) is always lower than your **marginal rate** (the bracket your last dollar falls into).

This calculator reveals the gap between those two rates. A single filer earning \$75,000 has a marginal rate of 22% but an effective rate of only 10.82% — meaning more than half their income is taxed at rates below 22%. Understanding this distinction is critical for evaluating whether a raise pushes you into a "higher bracket" (it does for the incremental dollars, but your overall rate rises only slightly).

The **bracket-by-bracket breakdown table** shows exactly how much of your income falls into each bracket and the tax generated at each rate. The **pie chart** visualizes the split between federal tax, after-tax income, and pre-tax deductions. Use these to model the impact of contributing more to your [401(k) or traditional IRA](/calculators/finance/retirement-calculator) — every dollar contributed pre-tax reduces your taxable income at your highest marginal rate.

## The Formula

The U.S. federal income tax uses a **progressive marginal rate system** with seven brackets. Your taxable income is not taxed at a single flat rate — instead, each portion of income within a bracket is taxed at that bracket's rate. The total tax is the sum across all brackets:

$$\text{Federal Tax} = \sum_{i=1}^{7} r_i \times \min\!\bigl(\max(\text{taxableIncome} - B_{i-1},\; 0),\; B_i - B_{i-1}\bigr)$$

Where:

- $r_i$ = the tax rate for bracket $i$ (10%, 12%, 22%, 24%, 32%, 35%, or 37%)
- $B_i$ = the upper bound of bracket $i$ (filing-status dependent)
- $B_0 = 0$ (the lower bound of the first bracket)
- $\text{taxableIncome}$ = Gross Income − Pre-Tax Deductions − Standard/Itemized Deduction

The **effective tax rate** is the actual percentage of gross income paid in tax:

$$\text{Effective Rate} = \frac{\text{Federal Tax}}{\text{Gross Income}} \times 100$$

The **marginal rate** is the tax rate applied to your last dollar of taxable income — the highest bracket your income reaches.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Single Filer — \$75,000 Gross Income, Standard Deduction

**Inputs:**
- Gross Income: \$75,000
- Filing Status: Single
- Deduction: Standard (\$15,000)
- Pre-Tax Deductions: \$0
- Tax Credits: \$0

**Step-by-step:**

1. Taxable income: \$75,000 − $15,000 = **$60,000**
2. Bracket 1 (10%): $11,925 × 0.10 = **$1,192.50**
3. Bracket 2 (12%): (\$48,475 − $11,925) = $36,550 × 0.12 = **\$4,386.00**
4. Bracket 3 (22%): (\$60,000 − $48,475) = $11,525 × 0.22 = **\$2,535.50**
5. Total federal tax: \$1,192.50 + \$4,386.00 + $2,535.50 = **$8,114.00**
6. Effective rate: \$8,114 ÷ \$75,000 = **10.82%**
7. Marginal rate: **22%**
8. After-tax income: \$75,000 − $8,114 = **$66,886**

**Interpretation:** Despite falling in the 22% bracket, this filer's actual tax burden is only 10.82% of gross income. More than three-quarters of their taxable income (\$48,475 of \$60,000) is taxed at 10% or 12%. A \$5,000 raise to \$80,000 would add only \$1,100 in tax (\$5,000 × 22%), not \$1,600 (\$5,000 × 32%) — they remain in the 22% bracket.

### Example 2: Married Filing Jointly — \$150,000 Gross Income

**Inputs:**
- Gross Income: \$150,000
- Filing Status: Married Filing Jointly
- Deduction: Standard (\$30,000)
- Pre-Tax Deductions: \$0
- Tax Credits: \$0

**Step-by-step:**

1. Taxable income: \$150,000 − $30,000 = **$120,000**
2. Bracket 1 (10%): $23,850 × 0.10 = **$2,385.00**
3. Bracket 2 (12%): (\$96,950 − $23,850) = $73,100 × 0.12 = **\$8,772.00**
4. Bracket 3 (22%): (\$120,000 − $96,950) = $23,050 × 0.22 = **\$5,071.00**
5. Total federal tax: \$2,385.00 + \$8,772.00 + $5,071.00 = **$16,228.00**
6. Effective rate: \$16,228 ÷ \$150,000 = **10.82%**
7. Marginal rate: **22%**
8. After-tax income: \$150,000 − $16,228 = **$133,772**

**Interpretation:** A married couple earning \$150,000 combined pays the same effective rate (10.82%) as a single filer earning \$75,000. This is by design — the married-filing-jointly brackets are approximately double the single brackets through the 32% tier. The couple keeps \$133,772 after federal tax. If both spouses filed separately instead, they would each pay tax on \$60,000 of taxable income (\$75,000 − \$15,000), totaling \$16,228 combined — in this case, there is no marriage penalty or bonus because both spouses earn equal amounts.

### Example 3: High Earner with 401(k) and Tax Credits — \$250,000 Single

**Inputs:**
- Gross Income: \$250,000
- Filing Status: Single
- Deduction: Standard (\$15,000)
- Pre-Tax Deductions: \$23,500 (401k max contribution)
- Tax Credits: \$3,000 (education credits)

**Step-by-step:**

1. Adjusted gross income: \$250,000 − $23,500 = **$226,500**
2. Taxable income: \$226,500 − $15,000 = **$211,500**
3. Bracket 1 (10%): $11,925 × 0.10 = **$1,192.50**
4. Bracket 2 (12%): $36,550 × 0.12 = **$4,386.00**
5. Bracket 3 (22%): (\$103,350 − $48,475) = $54,875 × 0.22 = **\$12,072.50**
6. Bracket 4 (24%): (\$197,300 − $103,350) = $93,950 × 0.24 = **\$22,548.00**
7. Bracket 5 (32%): (\$211,500 − $197,300) = $14,200 × 0.32 = **\$4,544.00**
8. Tax before credits: \$1,192.50 + \$4,386.00 + \$12,072.50 + \$22,548.00 + $4,544.00 = **$44,743.00**
9. Tax after credits: \$44,743.00 − $3,000 = **$41,743.00**
10. Effective rate: \$41,743 ÷ \$250,000 = **16.70%**
11. Marginal rate: **32%**
12. After-tax income: \$250,000 − \$41,743 − $23,500 = **$184,757**

**Interpretation:** The 401(k) contribution alone saves this filer \$7,520 in federal taxes (\$23,500 × 32% marginal rate). Without the 401(k) deduction, taxable income would be \$235,000, pushing more income into the 32% bracket and increasing the tax bill to \$49,263 before credits. Combined with \$3,000 in education credits, this filer's total tax savings from deductions and credits reach \$10,520. For high earners, maximizing pre-tax retirement contributions is the single most impactful tax reduction strategy.

## Key Factors That Affect Your Results

**Filing Status** — This is the most impactful selection in the calculator. A single filer earning \$100,000 pays \$13,027 in federal tax (effective rate 13.03%). A head-of-household filer with the same income pays \$11,680 (11.68%). Married filing jointly on \$100,000 pays only \$8,236 (8.24%). The brackets and standard deductions shift significantly between statuses, so choosing the correct one is critical for accuracy.

**Standard vs. Itemized Deductions** — For 2025, the standard deduction is \$15,000 (Single) or \$30,000 (Married Filing Jointly). Since the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act nearly doubled the standard deduction, approximately 90% of filers now take the standard deduction. Itemizing only makes sense if your combined mortgage interest, state/local taxes (SALT, capped at \$10,000), medical expenses (above 7.5% of AGI), and charitable contributions exceed the standard amount. For a single filer, this typically requires a mortgage balance above \$300,000 or significant charitable giving.

**Pre-Tax Retirement Contributions** — Contributing to a traditional 401(k) or IRA reduces your taxable income dollar-for-dollar. At a 22% marginal rate, a \$23,500 401(k) contribution saves \$5,170 in federal tax. At 32%, the same contribution saves \$7,520. This makes pre-tax contributions more valuable as your income rises. Use our [Retirement Calculator](/calculators/finance/retirement-calculator) to model the long-term growth of those contributions.

**Tax Credits vs. Tax Deductions** — Deductions reduce your taxable income (saving you money at your marginal rate), while credits reduce your tax bill dollar-for-dollar. A \$2,000 Child Tax Credit saves exactly \$2,000 in tax regardless of your bracket. A \$2,000 deduction saves \$440 at the 22% bracket or \$640 at the 32% bracket. Credits are always more valuable per dollar than deductions.

**State Taxes** — This calculator covers federal income tax only. Most states levy an additional income tax ranging from 0% (Alaska, Florida, Nevada, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Washington, Wyoming) to 13.3% (California's top rate). Your total income tax burden is federal plus state, which can push effective rates above 30% for high earners in high-tax states.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Confusing marginal and effective tax rates.** The most common tax misconception in America. If someone tells you "I'm in the 24% bracket," they are not paying 24% on all their income. A single filer in the 24% bracket (taxable income between \$103,351 and \$197,300) has an effective rate between 17.4% and 20.9%. The difference matters enormously when evaluating a raise, bonus, or side income — the incremental tax is at the marginal rate, but your overall burden is the effective rate.

**Forgetting pre-tax deductions reduce taxable income, not tax.** A \$23,500 401(k) contribution does not save you \$23,500 in taxes — it saves \$23,500 multiplied by your marginal rate. At the 22% bracket, that is \$5,170. At 12%, only \$2,820. This distinction is essential when deciding between traditional (pre-tax) and Roth (after-tax) retirement contributions.

**Defaulting to the standard deduction when itemizing saves more.** Roughly 10% of filers benefit from itemizing, but many miss it. If you own a home with a large mortgage, live in a high-tax state, or donate significantly to charity, run the numbers both ways. This calculator lets you toggle between standard and itemized to see which produces a lower tax bill.

**Not claiming credits you qualify for.** The Child Tax Credit (\$2,000 per child under 17), Earned Income Tax Credit (up to \$7,830 for families with 3+ children in 2025), Saver's Credit (up to \$1,000 for retirement contributions by lower-income filers), and education credits are frequently left on the table. Unlike deductions, credits directly reduce your tax dollar-for-dollar.

**Using the wrong filing status.** Head of Household provides a larger standard deduction (\$22,500 vs. \$15,000) and wider brackets than Single, but requires an unmarried filer who pays more than half the cost of maintaining a home for a qualifying dependent. Filing incorrectly as Single when you qualify for Head of Household costs hundreds to thousands of dollars per year.

## Three Tax Moves That Actually Change Your Outcome

Most tax "tips" save pocket change. These three decisions move thousands of dollars and compound over a career.

**1. Max out pre-tax retirement accounts before anything else.** A single filer earning \$120,000 sits in the 22% bracket with a taxable income of \$105,000. Contributing the full \$23,500 to a traditional 401(k) drops taxable income to \$81,500 — saving \$5,170 in federal tax this year. But the real payoff is compounding: that \$5,170 in annual tax savings, invested at 7% for 25 years, grows to \$340,000. If your employer offers a 401(k) match and you're not contributing enough to capture the full match, you're declining free money — the match is a guaranteed 50–100% instant return on the matched portion.

**2. Know when itemizing flips in your favor — and when it doesn't.** The \$15,000 standard deduction (Single, 2025) means itemizing only helps if your deductible expenses exceed that threshold. Here's the math: mortgage interest on a \$400,000 loan at 6.5% generates roughly \$25,500 in first-year interest. Add \$10,000 in SALT (capped) and \$2,000 in charitable giving, and you have \$37,500 in itemized deductions — \$22,500 more than the standard deduction, saving an additional \$4,950 at the 22% marginal rate. But a homeowner with a \$200,000 loan at 4% generates only \$7,800 in interest, making the standard deduction the clear winner. Run both scenarios in this calculator before deciding — the toggle between Standard and Itemized makes it instant.

**3. Time your income and deductions across tax years.** If you're on the border between the 22% and 24% bracket (\$103,350 taxable income for Single filers), shifting even \$5,000 of income into the next year — by deferring a year-end bonus, accelerating a deductible expense, or making a January estimated tax payment in December — saves \$100 on that \$5,000 alone. For self-employed filers with more control over income timing, this strategy can redirect \$500–\$2,000 per year. Bunch charitable donations into alternating years — donate \$10,000 every two years instead of \$5,000 annually — to alternate between itemizing and taking the standard deduction, capturing the higher deduction in both years.

## What This Calculator Doesn't Include — and Why It Matters

This calculator models federal income tax on ordinary earned income. That's useful, but it's not your full tax picture.

**FICA taxes (Social Security + Medicare)** add 7.65% to your effective rate on the first \$168,600 of earned income (2025), and 1.45% on all income above that. A \$75,000 earner paying 10.82% in federal income tax actually pays 18.47% when FICA is included. Self-employed workers pay both halves: 15.3% up to the Social Security wage base. Use our [Salary Calculator](/calculators/finance/salary-calculator) to see gross-to-net breakdowns.

**State income tax** ranges from 0% (Texas, Florida, and seven other states) to 13.3% (California). For a single filer earning \$100,000, the difference between living in Texas and California can exceed \$6,000 per year in state income tax alone — equivalent to earning \$106,000 in Texas. If you're comparing job offers across states, the headline salary number is misleading without tax-adjusting it.

**Capital gains and dividend income** are taxed at preferential rates: 0% if your taxable income is below \$47,025 (Single, 2025), 15% up to \$518,900, and 20% above that. This means a retiree living on \$50,000 in qualified dividends and long-term capital gains with no other income could pay close to zero federal tax after the standard deduction. This calculator treats all income as ordinary — if investment income is a significant portion of your total, your actual tax bill will be lower than shown.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- Calculates **federal income tax only** — does not include state income tax, local taxes, Social Security tax (6.2%), or Medicare tax (1.45%/2.35%).
- Uses **2025 tax brackets and standard deductions** per IRS Revenue Procedure 2024-40. Brackets are adjusted annually for inflation.
- Does not account for the **Alternative Minimum Tax (AMT)**, which affects some high-income taxpayers with large deductions.
- Does not distinguish between **ordinary income and capital gains** — long-term capital gains are taxed at preferential rates (0%, 15%, or 20%) not modeled here.
- Does not include **self-employment tax** (15.3% on net self-employment income). Self-employed individuals should add the employer-equivalent portion (7.65%) to their tax estimate.
- Tax credits are applied as **nonrefundable credits** — they reduce tax to \$0 but do not generate a refund in this calculator. Some credits (EITC, Additional Child Tax Credit) are refundable in practice.
- Does not model the **SALT deduction cap** (\$10,000) separately — if itemizing, ensure your total state and local tax deduction does not exceed this limit.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the difference between marginal and effective tax rate?

Your marginal tax rate is the rate applied to your last dollar of taxable income — the highest bracket your income reaches. Your effective tax rate is your total federal tax divided by your gross income, expressed as a percentage. For a single filer earning \$75,000 (taxable income \$60,000), the marginal rate is 22% but the effective rate is only 10.82%. The effective rate is always lower because the U.S. uses progressive brackets: your first \$11,925 is taxed at just 10%, the next \$36,550 at 12%, and only the remaining \$11,525 at 22%. A common misconception is that earning more "puts all your income in a higher bracket" — in reality, only the dollars above each threshold are taxed at the higher rate.

### What are the 2025 federal income tax brackets?

For 2025, the seven federal income tax brackets for single filers are: 10% on income up to \$11,925, 12% on \$11,926–\$48,475, 22% on \$48,476–\$103,350, 24% on \$103,351–\$197,300, 32% on \$197,301–\$250,525, 35% on \$250,526–\$626,350, and 37% on income over \$626,350. Married filing jointly brackets are roughly double these thresholds through the 32% bracket. These brackets were set by IRS Revenue Procedure 2024-40 and are adjusted annually for inflation. The standard deduction for 2025 is \$15,000 (Single), \$30,000 (Married Filing Jointly), and \$22,500 (Head of Household).

### Should I take the standard deduction or itemize?

Take the standard deduction unless your total itemized deductions exceed it. For 2025, the standard deduction is \$15,000 (Single) or \$30,000 (Married Filing Jointly). About 90% of taxpayers benefit from the standard deduction after the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act nearly doubled it. To exceed \$15,000 in itemized deductions as a single filer, you typically need a combination of mortgage interest on a loan above \$250,000, state/local taxes near the \$10,000 SALT cap, and charitable donations exceeding \$2,000–\$3,000. Use this calculator to toggle between Standard and Itemized — enter your total itemized deductions and compare the resulting federal tax under each option.

### How does a 401(k) reduce my taxes?

Traditional 401(k) contributions reduce your taxable income dollar-for-dollar, saving you money at your marginal tax rate. If you contribute the 2025 maximum of \$23,500 and your marginal rate is 22%, you save \$5,170 in federal tax that year. At a 32% marginal rate, the same contribution saves \$7,520. The contribution grows tax-deferred until withdrawal in retirement, when it is taxed as ordinary income — ideally at a lower rate if your retirement income is less than your working income. For 2025, the contribution limit is \$23,500 for those under 50 and \$31,000 for those 50 and older (with the \$7,500 catch-up provision). HSA contributions (\$4,300 individual / \$8,550 family) work similarly, with the added benefit that qualified medical withdrawals are never taxed.

### What tax bracket am I in?

Your tax bracket is determined by your filing status and taxable income (gross income minus deductions). For a single filer in 2025: taxable income up to \$11,925 puts you in the 10% bracket, \$11,926–\$48,475 in the 12% bracket, \$48,476–\$103,350 in the 22% bracket, \$103,351–\$197,300 in the 24% bracket, \$197,301–\$250,525 in the 32% bracket, \$250,526–\$626,350 in the 35% bracket, and over \$626,350 in the 37% bracket. Remember: being "in the 22% bracket" means only the income above \$48,475 is taxed at 22%. Your overall effective rate will be lower. Enter your income above to see your exact bracket, effective rate, and tax breakdown.
