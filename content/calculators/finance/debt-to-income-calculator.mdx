## What Your DTI Ratio Means

This calculator shows both your front-end ratio (housing costs only) and back-end ratio (all debts), which lenders evaluate separately. The back-end DTI — your total monthly debt divided by gross income — is the number that determines your borrowing power.

**The 43% line is the Qualified Mortgage (QM) boundary.** Under CFPB rules, most conventional mortgages require a back-end DTI at or below 43%. Above that threshold, you're limited to non-QM loans, which typically carry higher interest rates and stricter terms. FHA loans allow up to 50% DTI with compensating factors, but that's the absolute ceiling for government-backed lending. The front-end ratio (housing only) should ideally stay below 28% — this is the standard guideline from Fannie Mae and most conventional lenders.

**Your DTI determines how much house you can afford.** If your gross monthly income is $8,000 and you have $900 in existing debt payments, a 36% target means your maximum total debt payments are $2,880. Subtract the $900, and your maximum mortgage payment is $1,980 — which supports roughly a $370,000 mortgage at 7% for 30 years. For a complete affordability analysis, try our [Mortgage Calculator](/finance/mortgage-calculator).

## How to Use This Calculator

1. **Enter your Gross Monthly Income** — your total pre-tax monthly income from all sources. Include salary, self-employment income, rental income, alimony, and any other regular income. For salaried employees, divide your annual salary by 12. For hourly workers, multiply your hourly rate by average weekly hours, then by 52, then divide by 12.

2. **Enter your monthly debt payments** — include your mortgage or rent, car loans, student loans, minimum credit card payments, and any other recurring debt obligations. Do not include utilities, groceries, insurance, or other expenses that are not debt payments. Lenders only count obligations that appear on your credit report.

3. **Review your DTI rating and breakdown** — the pie chart shows where your debt is concentrated, and the summary tells you how much borrowing capacity you have left at the 36% threshold.

## The Formula

$$DTI = \frac{\text{Total Monthly Debt Payments}}{\text{Gross Monthly Income}} \times 100$$

Where:

- **Total Monthly Debt Payments** = housing + car + student loans + credit cards + other recurring debt
- **Gross Monthly Income** = pre-tax monthly income from all sources

The front-end ratio uses only housing costs:

$$\text{Front-End DTI} = \frac{\text{Monthly Housing Payment}}{\text{Gross Monthly Income}} \times 100$$

**DTI Rating Scale (per CFPB/Fannie Mae guidelines):**
- 28% or below = Excellent
- 29–36% = Good
- 37–43% = Acceptable (QM limit)
- 44–50% = High
- Above 50% = Very High

**Source:** Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) Qualified Mortgage rules (12 CFR 1026.43); Fannie Mae Selling Guide Section B3-6-02 for DTI requirements.

## Worked Example

### $6,000 Monthly Income, $2,350 in Debts

**Inputs:**
- Gross Monthly Income: $6,000
- Mortgage/Rent: $1,500
- Car Payment: $400
- Student Loans: $300
- Credit Card Payments: $150

**Step-by-step:**

1. Total monthly debt: $1,500 + $400 + $300 + $150 = **$2,350**
2. Back-end DTI: $2,350 ÷ $6,000 × 100 = **39.2%**
3. Front-end DTI: $1,500 ÷ $6,000 × 100 = **25.0%**
4. Rating: 39.2% falls in the **Acceptable** range (37–43%)
5. Remaining capacity at 36%: $6,000 × 0.36 − $2,350 = **−$190** (over the 36% target)

**Interpretation:** Your back-end DTI of 39.2% qualifies for a QM mortgage (under 43%), but exceeds the ideal 36% target by $190 per month. Paying off the $150 credit card payment would bring your DTI to 36.7%, much closer to the preferred range. Your front-end ratio of 25% is within the recommended 28% guideline.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- Uses **gross income** (pre-tax), not take-home pay. Your actual disposable income is lower after taxes, which DTI does not reflect.
- Counts only **debt payments that appear on a credit report** — utilities, insurance premiums, groceries, and subscriptions are not included in DTI.
- Does **not account for future debt** such as a new mortgage you're applying for. When applying, lenders add the proposed mortgage payment to your existing debts.
- Assumes **stable income** — variable income (freelancers, commission-based) may require 2 years of tax returns for lender verification.
- DTI thresholds vary by **loan type**: conventional (43%), FHA (50% with compensating factors), VA (41% guideline, flexible).

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is a good debt-to-income ratio?

A DTI of 36% or below is considered good by most lenders and meets conventional mortgage guidelines. Below 28% is excellent and gives you the most borrowing flexibility. The CFPB sets 43% as the maximum for Qualified Mortgages, though FHA loans may allow up to 50% with compensating factors like strong credit scores or significant cash reserves. The lower your DTI, the more favorable your loan terms — borrowers with DTI below 30% typically receive the best interest rates.

### How do I lower my debt-to-income ratio?

You can lower your DTI by reducing debt payments or increasing income. The fastest approach: pay off your smallest debt balance entirely to eliminate that monthly payment. Paying off a $150/month credit card payment on $6,000 income reduces your DTI by 2.5 percentage points. Alternatively, increasing your gross income by $500/month (through a raise, side income, or overtime) reduces a 39% DTI to 36% without changing your debt. Refinancing existing loans to longer terms lowers monthly payments but increases total interest cost.
