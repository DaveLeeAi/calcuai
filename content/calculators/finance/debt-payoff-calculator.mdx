## What Your Payment Breakdown Means

This calculator reveals two critical insights: how long until you are completely debt-free, and how much interest you will pay along the way. By comparing the avalanche and snowball methods side by side, you can see the exact dollar difference between the mathematically optimal approach and the psychologically motivating one.

The **avalanche method** targets the debt with the highest interest rate first. Every dollar of extra payment goes toward the most expensive debt, minimizing total interest. On a typical three-debt portfolio (\$8,000 credit card at 22%, \$15,000 car loan at 6.5%, \$25,000 student loan at 5%), the avalanche method with \$200 extra per month saves \$877 more in interest compared to the snowball method. That is the price of convenience.

The **snowball method** targets the lowest balance first, generating quick wins that maintain motivation. Research from the Harvard Business Review found that consumers who focus on small balances first are more likely to eliminate all their debt, despite paying more in interest. If you have struggled with debt repayment consistency, the snowball method's psychological advantage may outweigh its mathematical disadvantage.

The **payoff order table** shows which debt gets eliminated first, second, and third, along with the total interest each one costs you. Use this with the [Loan Calculator](/calculators/finance/loan-calculator) to evaluate refinancing options for your highest-rate debts.

## The Formula

Debt payoff uses iterative monthly amortization. Each month, interest accrues and payments are applied:

$$\text{Interest}_i = B_i \times \frac{r}{12}$$

$$B_{i+1} = B_i + \text{Interest}_i - \text{Payment}_i$$

Where:

- **$B_i$** = remaining balance of debt at month $i$ (dollars)
- **$r$** = annual interest rate for that debt (decimal, so 22% = 0.22)
- **$\text{Payment}_i$** = minimum payment + any extra payment applied to this debt

**Payment cascading:** When a debt is paid off, its minimum payment amount is added to the extra payment pool. This creates an accelerating effect — each paid-off debt frees up more cash for the next target, producing a "snowball" or "avalanche" of increasing payments.

**Target selection:**
- **Avalanche:** sort active debts by rate descending; apply extra to the highest-rate debt
- **Snowball:** sort active debts by balance ascending; apply extra to the lowest-balance debt

**Source:** Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) debt management guidelines. The iterative amortization approach is the same methodology used in all standard debt management plan calculations.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Three Debts — Avalanche Method with \$200 Extra

**Inputs:**
- Credit Card: \$8,000 at 22% APR, \$200 minimum
- Car Loan: \$15,000 at 6.5% APR, \$350 minimum
- Student Loan: \$25,000 at 5% APR, \$280 minimum
- Extra Monthly Payment: \$200
- Strategy: Avalanche

**Step-by-step:**

1. Total minimum payments: \$200 + \$350 + $280 = **$830/month**
2. Total monthly outlay: \$830 + $200 extra = **$1,030/month**
3. Avalanche targets the credit card first (22% — highest rate)
4. Month 1 credit card interest: $8,000 x 0.22/12 = **$146.67**
5. Credit card receives: \$200 minimum + $200 extra = **$400/month**
6. After month 1: credit card balance = \$8,000 + \$146.67 - $400 = **$7,746.67**
7. The credit card pays off around **month 24**
8. After credit card payoff: freed-up \$200 minimum + $200 extra = **$400** now targeting car loan
9. Car loan receives: \$350 minimum + $400 = **$750/month**, paying off around **month 35**
10. Student loan receives all remaining cash, paying off around **month 46**

**Results:**
- Total months to debt freedom: **46**
- Total interest paid: **\$8,176**
- Interest saved vs. minimums only: **\$6,824**

**Interpretation:** The avalanche method eliminates the 22% credit card first, preventing \$146.67/month in interest from accruing. Each successive payoff accelerates the timeline. Without extra payments, this same debt portfolio takes 78 months and costs \$15,000 in interest.

### Example 2: Same Debts — Snowball Method

**Same debts, but snowball targets the lowest balance first:**

1. Snowball targets the credit card first (\$8,000 — also the lowest balance in this case)
2. Since the credit card is both the highest rate and lowest balance, the early results are identical to avalanche
3. After credit card payoff, snowball targets the car loan (\$15,000) next
4. Paying off around **month 48** total
5. Total interest paid: **\$9,053**

**Interpretation:** In this specific portfolio, both methods target the credit card first (it has both the highest rate and lowest balance). The difference emerges in the second and third debts. The avalanche method saves \$877 more by addressing the 6.5% car loan before the 5% student loan.

## Key Factors That Affect Your Results

**Extra Payment Amount** — This is the most impactful variable. On the three-debt portfolio above, increasing the extra payment from \$100 to \$300 cuts the payoff timeline from 56 months to 39 months and saves an additional \$2,800 in interest. Even \$50/month extra saves hundreds. Use the calculator to test different amounts against your monthly budget.

**Interest Rate Spread** — The wider the gap between your highest and lowest rates, the greater the advantage of the avalanche method. A portfolio with rates of 22%, 6.5%, and 5% has a large spread, making avalanche clearly superior mathematically. If all your debts are within 2-3 percentage points of each other, the interest savings difference between methods shrinks to under \$200, and snowball becomes more attractive for its motivational benefits.

**Number of Debts** — More debts means more opportunities for payment cascading. With 5 debts, each payoff releases a minimum payment into the pool, creating increasingly powerful extra payments for the remaining debts. The cascading effect is why both methods accelerate dramatically after the first debt is eliminated.

**Minimum Payment Levels** — Higher minimums relative to the balance mean faster payoff but less room for strategic allocation. If your minimums barely cover interest (common with credit cards at minimum percentage-based payments), extra payments have maximum impact because they go entirely toward principal reduction.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- Assumes **fixed interest rates** for all debts throughout the payoff period. Variable-rate debts (credit cards, adjustable-rate loans) may change.
- Assumes **fixed minimum payments**. Some credit cards calculate minimums as a percentage of balance, which decreases over time. This calculator uses a fixed minimum, which is the more conservative approach.
- Does **not account for new charges** added to revolving credit. If you continue using credit cards, your actual payoff timeline will be longer.
- The extra payment is applied **consistently every month** from month one. Any months where you skip the extra payment will extend the timeline.
- Does **not include balance transfer options**, debt consolidation, or refinancing. Use the [Loan Calculator](/calculators/finance/loan-calculator) to evaluate consolidation alternatives.
- Assumes all extra payment goes to the **target debt** each month. Some lenders require you to specify principal-only payments.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is the avalanche or snowball method better?

The avalanche method saves more money in total interest because it targets the highest-rate debt first. On a typical three-debt portfolio with rates ranging from 5% to 22%, the avalanche method saves \$500 to \$2,000 more than snowball over the full payoff period. However, a 2016 Harvard Business Review study found that people using the snowball method were more likely to actually eliminate all their debt because small early wins maintain motivation. If you are confident in your discipline, choose avalanche. If you need momentum from quick wins, choose snowball.

### How much extra should I pay toward debt each month?

Any amount above your minimums accelerates payoff, but \$200/month is a strong starting point for most households. On \$48,000 in combined debt, \$200 extra saves approximately \$6,800 in interest and cuts 32 months from the timeline. Financial advisors typically recommend allocating at least 20% of after-tax income to debt repayment (the "20" in the 50/30/20 budgeting rule). Test different amounts in the calculator to find the balance between aggressive payoff and comfortable monthly cash flow.

### Should I pay off debt or invest?

Compare your highest debt interest rate to your expected investment return after taxes. If your debt charges 22% APR (like a credit card), paying it off delivers a guaranteed 22% return — far exceeding the stock market's historical 10% average. If your debt is at 5% or lower (like many student loans), investing may produce better long-term returns, especially in tax-advantaged accounts. The crossover point is typically around 6-7%. Pay off anything above that rate first, then redirect extra cash to investing. Use our [Investment Calculator](/calculators/finance/investment-calculator) to model the comparison.
