## What Cap Rate Tells You

The capitalization rate measures a property's return on investment based purely on its income and expenses — independent of how you finance the purchase. A 9% cap rate means the property generates \$9 in net operating income for every \$100 of property value. This makes cap rate the universal comparison metric for real estate investors: you can directly compare a \$150,000 duplex in Cleveland with a \$2 million apartment building in Austin using the same number.

**Higher cap rates** (7–12%) typically indicate higher returns but also higher risk — often in secondary markets, lower-income neighborhoods, or older properties that may require more maintenance. **Lower cap rates** (3–5%) are common in premium markets like San Francisco, New York, and coastal cities where property values are high relative to rents, but appreciation potential and tenant stability are stronger.

The calculator also shows your **expense ratio** (what percentage of income goes to expenses), **gross rent multiplier** (how many years of gross rent equal the purchase price), and **monthly NOI** (your actual monthly cash flow before debt service). For a complete picture including mortgage payments, use the [Mortgage Calculator](/finance/mortgage-calculator). To compare the overall investment return, see the [ROI Calculator](/business/roi-calculator).

## How to Use This Calculator

1. **Enter the property value** — the purchase price or current appraised market value. For an existing investment, use the most recent appraisal or comparable sales estimate.
2. **Enter gross annual rental income** — the total rent you would collect if the property were occupied 100% of the time. For a single-family rental at \$2,000/month, this is \$24,000. For a multi-unit, sum all unit rents.
3. **Set the vacancy rate** — the expected percentage of time the property sits empty between tenants. The national average for residential rentals is approximately 5–7%. Urban apartments may be 3–5%; rural or seasonal properties may be 8–15%.
4. **Enter your expenses** — break out property tax, insurance, maintenance, and management fees. If you have additional costs (HOA dues, utilities paid by owner), enter them in "Other Operating Expenses."
5. **Review your results** — the cap rate, NOI, expense ratio, and GRM appear instantly. Compare with local market averages to evaluate whether the deal makes financial sense.

## The Formula

**Net Operating Income (NOI):**

$$
\text{NOI} = \text{Gross Rental Income} \times \left(1 - \frac{\text{Vacancy Rate}}{100}\right) - \text{Total Expenses}
$$

**Capitalization Rate:**

$$
\text{Cap Rate} = \frac{\text{NOI}}{\text{Property Value}} \times 100
$$

**Expense Ratio:**

$$
\text{Expense Ratio} = \frac{\text{Total Expenses}}{\text{Effective Gross Income}} \times 100
$$

**Gross Rent Multiplier (GRM):**

$$
\text{GRM} = \frac{\text{Property Value}}{\text{Gross Annual Rental Income}}
$$

Where:
- **Gross Rental Income** = total annual rent assuming full occupancy (\$)
- **Vacancy Rate** = expected vacancy as a percentage (%)
- **Effective Gross Income** = gross rent adjusted for vacancy (\$)
- **Total Expenses** = property tax + insurance + maintenance + management + other operating costs (\$)
- **NOI** = income remaining after vacancy and all operating expenses (\$)
- **Property Value** = purchase price or current market value (\$)
- **GRM** = number of years of gross rent needed to equal the property price

*Source: National Association of Realtors — Real Estate Investment Analysis Standards. Cap rate methodology follows Appraisal Institute (The Appraisal of Real Estate, 15th ed.) income capitalization approach.*

## Worked Example

### Single-Family Rental in a Midwestern Market

An investor is evaluating a \$175,000 single-family rental in Indianapolis that rents for \$1,650/month.

**Known figures:**
- Property Value: \$175,000
- Monthly Rent: \$1,650 → Gross Annual Income: \$19,800
- Vacancy Rate: 6%
- Property Tax: \$2,100/year
- Insurance: \$1,100/year
- Maintenance: \$1,500/year (budgeting 1% of value for repairs)
- Management: \$1,980/year (10% of gross rent)

**Step 1 — Effective Gross Income:**
- \$19,800 × (1 − 0.06) = **\$18,612**

**Step 2 — Total Operating Expenses:**
- \$2,100 + \$1,100 + \$1,500 + \$1,980 = **\$6,680**

**Step 3 — Net Operating Income:**
- \$18,612 − \$6,680 = **\$11,932**

**Step 4 — Cap Rate:**
- (\$11,932 / \$175,000) × 100 = **6.82%**

**Step 5 — Supporting Metrics:**
- Expense Ratio: (\$6,680 / \$18,612) × 100 = **35.9%**
- Monthly NOI: \$11,932 / 12 = **\$994.33**
- GRM: \$175,000 / \$19,800 = **8.8**

*Interpretation:* A 6.82% cap rate is solid for a midwestern single-family rental — above the national 5–6% average. The 35.9% expense ratio is healthy (under 40% is the target for single-family). The GRM of 8.8 means you'd recoup the purchase price in gross rent within 8.8 years. Monthly NOI of \$994 is the cash flow before mortgage payments — if the investor finances with a \$140,000 mortgage at 7%, the monthly payment is roughly \$931, leaving about \$63/month in positive cash flow while building equity.

## Cap Rate vs. Cash-on-Cash Return

Cap rate and cash-on-cash return answer different questions. **Cap rate** ignores financing entirely — it measures the property's income efficiency at its market price. **Cash-on-cash return** measures your actual return on the money you invested (your down payment), factoring in mortgage payments. A property with a 7% cap rate might produce a 12% cash-on-cash return with favorable leverage (low interest rate, high LTV) or only 4% if financing is expensive. Use cap rate to compare properties; use cash-on-cash to evaluate your personal return after financing.

## Key Factors That Affect Cap Rate

1. **Location** — Cap rates vary dramatically by market. Primary coastal markets (NYC, SF, LA, Boston) typically run 3–5% because high property values compress rates even when rents are strong. Secondary and tertiary markets (Indianapolis, Memphis, Cleveland) commonly show 7–10% because property prices are lower relative to rents.

2. **Property Condition** — Newer or recently renovated properties command lower cap rates because buyers expect fewer surprise expenses. A 2020-built apartment complex in a growth market might trade at a 4.5% cap rate, while a 1970s building in the same neighborhood trades at 7% — the premium reflects deferred maintenance risk.

3. **Vacancy Assumptions** — Every 1% increase in vacancy directly reduces NOI and compresses cap rate. A property modeled at 5% vacancy versus 10% vacancy can swing cap rate by 0.5–1.0 percentage points. Always use local market vacancy data from the Census Bureau or CoStar rather than national averages.

4. **Expense Management** — The expense ratio directly determines how much gross income reaches the NOI line. Self-managing a rental saves 8–12% in management fees, but your time has a cost. An expense ratio above 50% warrants scrutiny — something in the expense structure may be unsustainable.

{/* Section 13: Common Mistakes */}
## Common Mistakes

- **Ignoring vacancy** — Using 0% vacancy inflates NOI and makes any deal look better than reality. Even the best properties experience turnover; always model at least 5%.
- **Forgetting capital costs** — Cap rate uses operating expenses, not capital expenses (roof replacement, HVAC, etc.). Budget 1–2% of property value annually for a CapEx reserve even if it doesn't appear in the cap rate formula.
- **Comparing across property types** — A 7% cap rate on an urban apartment complex is not the same risk profile as 7% on a rural single-family rental. Compare cap rates within the same property type and market.
- **Using cap rate with financing included** — Cap rate is an unlevered metric. Never subtract mortgage payments from NOI before calculating cap rate. That's what cash-on-cash return is for.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is a cap rate in real estate?

The capitalization rate (cap rate) is the ratio of a property's net operating income to its market value, expressed as a percentage. It measures the expected annual return on a property as if it were purchased entirely with cash — no financing. A 7% cap rate means the property generates \$7 in annual NOI for every \$100 of property value. It is the most widely used metric for comparing real estate investment opportunities across markets and property types.

### How do you calculate cap rate?

Divide the property's annual net operating income (NOI) by its current market value, then multiply by 100. NOI equals gross rental income minus vacancy losses and all operating expenses (property tax, insurance, maintenance, management). For example: a property generating \$18,000 NOI on a \$200,000 value has a cap rate of 9.0%.

### What is a good cap rate for rental property?

For residential rental property, cap rates between 5% and 8% are generally considered solid. Below 5% is typical in premium urban markets where investors accept lower yields in exchange for property appreciation. Above 8% often signals higher risk — either the market is weaker, the property needs work, or vacancy is elevated. The "right" cap rate depends on your risk tolerance and investment goals.

### What does NOI include and exclude?

Net operating income includes all rental income (adjusted for vacancy) minus operating expenses: property taxes, insurance, maintenance, repairs, property management fees, HOA dues, and utilities paid by the owner. NOI excludes mortgage payments, capital costs (roof replacement, major renovations), depreciation, and income taxes. It represents the property's earning power independent of how it's financed or taxed.

### Does cap rate include mortgage payments?

No. Cap rate is an unlevered metric — it measures the property's return as if purchased entirely with cash. Mortgage payments, loan interest, and debt service are deliberately excluded so investors can compare properties regardless of their individual financing arrangements. To measure your return after financing, use cash-on-cash return instead.

### What is the gross rent multiplier?

The gross rent multiplier (GRM) divides the property price by its gross annual rental income. A \$200,000 property generating \$24,000 in annual rent has a GRM of 8.3 — meaning it takes 8.3 years of gross rent to equal the purchase price. Lower GRM suggests a better deal. GRM is a quick screening tool, but less precise than cap rate because it ignores vacancy and expenses entirely.

### How does vacancy rate affect cap rate?

Vacancy directly reduces effective gross income, which reduces NOI, which lowers cap rate. On a \$200,000 property with \$24,000 gross rent and \$4,800 in expenses: at 0% vacancy the cap rate is 9.6%, at 5% vacancy it drops to 9.0%, and at 10% vacancy it falls to 8.4%. A 5% vacancy assumption is standard for well-located residential rentals; use 8–15% for properties in weaker markets or with frequent tenant turnover.

### Should I buy a property based on cap rate alone?

No. Cap rate is one of several metrics you need. It doesn't account for financing costs, appreciation potential, tax benefits (depreciation), capital cost needs, or neighborhood trajectory. A high cap rate with declining rents and deferred maintenance is not a good investment. Use cap rate for initial screening and comparison, then layer in cash-on-cash return, debt service coverage ratio, and local market analysis before making a decision.
