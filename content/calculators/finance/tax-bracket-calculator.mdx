## What Your Tax Estimate Shows

This calculator visualizes exactly how the U.S. progressive tax system applies to your income. The most common misconception about tax brackets is that moving into a higher bracket means all of your income is taxed at the higher rate. That is false. Only the income within each bracket range is taxed at that bracket's rate — the rest continues to be taxed at lower rates.

The **bracket-by-bracket breakdown table** shows exactly how much of your income falls into each tax bracket and the tax generated by each slice. At \$85,000 taxable income (single), the first \$11,925 is taxed at 10% (\$1,192.50), the next \$36,550 at 12% (\$4,386), and the remaining \$36,525 at 22% (\$8,035.50). Your total tax is \$13,614 — not \$18,700 as it would be if the entire \$85,000 were taxed at 22%.

The **effective vs. marginal rate distinction** is the key insight. Your marginal rate (22%) is the rate on your next dollar of income. Your effective rate (16.0%) is your actual total tax burden as a percentage of income. The gap between these rates is always significant — even at \$500,000 income (37% marginal bracket), the effective rate is only about 31%. Understanding this difference prevents poor financial decisions like turning down a raise because you think it will push all your income into a higher bracket. ## The Formula

The U.S. federal income tax uses a progressive marginal rate system. Total tax is the sum of tax from each bracket:

$$\text{Total Tax} = \sum_{i=1}^{7} r_i \times \min\left(\text{Taxable Income} - B_i,\; W_i\right)^{+}$$

Where:

- **$r_i$** = tax rate for bracket $i$ (10%, 12%, 22%, 24%, 32%, 35%, 37%)
- **$B_i$** = lower bound of bracket $i$ (dollars)
- **$W_i$** = width of bracket $i$ — the difference between upper and lower bounds (dollars)
- The $^{+}$ notation means the value is floored at zero (no negative tax)

The effective tax rate is:

$$\text{Effective Rate} = \frac{\text{Total Tax}}{\text{Taxable Income}} \times 100\%$$

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Single Filer — \$85,000 Taxable Income

**Inputs:**
- Taxable Income: \$85,000
- Filing Status: Single

**Step-by-step:**

1. **10% bracket** (\$0 – \$11,925): $11,925 x 0.10 = **$1,192.50**
2. **12% bracket** (\$11,925 – \$48,475): (\$48,475 - $11,925) = $36,550 x 0.12 = **\$4,386.00**
3. **22% bracket** (\$48,475 – \$85,000): (\$85,000 - $48,475) = $36,525 x 0.22 = **\$8,035.50**
4. **Total tax:** \$1,192.50 + \$4,386.00 + $8,035.50 = **$13,614.00**
5. **Marginal bracket:** 22% (last bracket with taxable income)
6. **Effective rate:** \$13,614 / \$85,000 = **16.0%**
7. **After-tax income:** \$85,000 - $13,614 = **$71,386**

**Interpretation:** Despite being in the 22% bracket, you pay an effective rate of only 16.0%. If you receive a \$5,000 raise (pushing taxable income to \$90,000), the additional tax is $5,000 x 0.22 = $1,100 — you keep \$3,900 of the raise. You never "lose money" by earning more in a progressive system.

### Example 2: Married Filing Jointly — \$175,000 Taxable Income

**Inputs:**
- Taxable Income: \$175,000
- Filing Status: Married Filing Jointly

**Step-by-step:**

1. **10% bracket** (\$0 – \$23,850): $23,850 x 0.10 = **$2,385.00**
2. **12% bracket** (\$23,850 – \$96,950): $73,100 x 0.12 = **$8,772.00**
3. **22% bracket** (\$96,950 – \$175,000): $78,050 x 0.22 = **$17,171.00**
4. **Total tax:** \$2,385 + \$8,772 + $17,171 = **$28,328.00**
5. **Marginal bracket:** 22%
6. **Effective rate:** \$28,328 / \$175,000 = **16.2%**

**Interpretation:** A married couple filing jointly at \$175,000 taxable income is in the same 22% marginal bracket as a single filer at \$85,000, but the wider bracket ranges mean they pay a lower effective rate on their first \$96,950 of income. The "marriage bonus" for this couple — compared to filing as two singles at \$87,500 each — saves approximately \$1,100 in federal tax.

## Key Factors That Affect Your Results

**Filing Status** — The bracket ranges for married filing jointly are nearly double the single ranges up through the 32% bracket. This creates a "marriage bonus" for couples where one spouse earns significantly more than the other. However, two high earners filing jointly may face a "marriage penalty" in the top brackets because the jointly filed ranges are not exactly double. Head of household status provides wider brackets than single, benefiting single parents.

**Taxable Income vs. Gross Income** — This calculator uses taxable income (after deductions), not gross income. The standard deduction for 2025 is \$15,000 (single) or \$30,000 (married jointly). A single filer earning \$100,000 gross has a taxable income of \$85,000 after the standard deduction. Pre-tax contributions to a 401(k) also reduce taxable income — a \$23,500 contribution at \$100,000 gross reduces taxable income to \$61,500, dropping from the 22% to the 12% bracket.

**State Income Taxes** — This calculator shows federal brackets only. Forty-three states impose their own income tax, with rates ranging from a flat 2.5% (Arizona) to a top marginal rate of 13.3% (California). Combined federal and state tax can push the effective rate 5-13 percentage points higher. Nine states (Florida, Texas, Nevada, Wyoming, Washington, Alaska, Tennessee, New Hampshire, South Dakota) have no state income tax.

**Bracket Inflation Adjustments** — The IRS adjusts bracket thresholds annually for inflation. The 2025 brackets are approximately 5.4% wider than 2024 brackets, meaning you can earn more before entering the next bracket. This prevents "bracket creep" — where wage increases that merely match inflation push taxpayers into higher brackets.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the difference between marginal and effective tax rate?

Your marginal tax rate is the rate applied to your last dollar of income — it determines the bracket you are "in." Your effective tax rate is the actual percentage of total income paid in tax, calculated by dividing total tax by total taxable income. For a single filer with \$85,000 in taxable income, the marginal rate is 22% but the effective rate is only 16.0%. The effective rate is always lower than the marginal rate in a progressive system because lower portions of income are taxed at lower rates. When evaluating a raise, side income, or deduction, the marginal rate tells you the tax impact per dollar; the effective rate tells you your overall burden.

### Will earning more money put me in a higher tax bracket?

Earning more money may put additional income into a higher bracket, but it will never reduce your take-home pay. In the U.S. progressive system, only the income within each bracket range is taxed at that rate. If you are single at \$85,000 taxable income (22% bracket) and receive a \$20,000 raise, only the portion above \$85,000 is taxed at the higher rate — the first \$85,000 is taxed exactly the same as before. The additional \$18,350 from \$85,000 to \$103,350 is taxed at 22%, and only the remaining \$1,650 above \$103,350 is taxed at 24%. You keep approximately \$14,800 of the \$20,000 raise after federal tax.

### How can I lower my tax bracket?

The most effective way to lower your marginal tax bracket is to reduce taxable income through pre-tax deductions. Contributing to a traditional 401(k) (up to \$23,500 in 2025, plus \$7,500 catch-up if over 50) directly reduces taxable income. A single filer at \$105,000 gross contributing \$23,500 to a 401(k) drops taxable income from \$90,000 to \$66,500, potentially moving from the 22% to the 12% bracket on a portion of income. HSA contributions (\$4,300 individual / \$8,550 family in 2025) are another pre-tax option. Itemizing deductions for mortgage interest, state taxes (capped at \$10,000), and charitable giving also reduces taxable income if itemized deductions exceed the standard deduction.

## Methodology & Sources

The 2025 federal income tax brackets in this calculator are sourced directly from **IRS Revenue Procedure 2024-40**, published November 2024, which provides the inflation-adjusted income thresholds for tax year 2025. The progressive marginal rate structure has been in effect since the Revenue Act of 1913, with rates and brackets adjusted by Congress periodically — most recently by the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 (TCJA), which set the current seven-bracket structure (10%, 12%, 22%, 24%, 32%, 35%, 37%) through 2025. Standard deduction amounts, 401(k) contribution limits, and HSA limits reference the **IRS Notice 2024-80** and related announcements.
