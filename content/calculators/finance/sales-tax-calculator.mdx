## What Your Tax Estimate Shows

This calculator handles two scenarios. In **Add Tax** mode, enter a pre-tax price and your local sales tax rate to see the tax amount and final total. In **Extract Tax** mode, enter a tax-inclusive total to reverse-calculate the original price and the embedded tax — useful for reconciling receipts, expense reports, or splitting costs.

The results show the tax amount, the pre-tax (effective) price, and the total. This is especially valuable when comparing prices across states with different tax rates, budgeting for large purchases, or verifying that a retailer charged the correct rate.

For federal and state income tax calculations, see the [Income Tax Calculator](/calculators/finance/income-tax-calculator). To determine the tip amount on a restaurant bill, try the [Tip Calculator](/calculators/finance/tip-calculator).

## How to Use This Calculator

1. **Choose your mode** — "Add Tax to Price" if you know the pre-tax price, or "Extract Tax from Total" if you have a receipt total and want to know how much was tax.
2. **Enter the price or total** — the pre-tax price (add mode) or the final receipt amount (extract mode).
3. **Enter the sales tax rate** — your combined state + local rate. Find yours at your state's department of revenue website. The default of 8.25% reflects the Texas combined rate.
4. **Read the results** — the calculator shows the tax amount, pre-tax price, and total price instantly.

## The Formula

<div className="formula-section">

**Add Tax (pre-tax price known):**

$$
\text{Tax} = \text{Price} \times \frac{\text{Rate}}{100}
$$

$$
\text{Total} = \text{Price} + \text{Tax}
$$

**Extract Tax (total known):**

$$
\text{Pre-Tax Price} = \frac{\text{Total}}{1 + \frac{\text{Rate}}{100}}
$$

$$
\text{Tax} = \text{Total} - \text{Pre-Tax Price}
$$

Where:
- **Price** = the purchase price before sales tax
- **Rate** = the combined sales tax rate as a percentage (e.g., 8.25)
- **Total** = the final amount including tax
- **Tax** = the sales tax amount

*Source: State revenue departments. Sales tax is a standard ad valorem percentage applied at the point of sale, codified in each state's tax code (e.g., Texas Tax Code §151.051).*

</div>

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Adding Sales Tax to a Laptop Purchase

You are buying a \$1,299 laptop in California, where the combined state and local sales tax rate is 8.75%.

**Step 1:** Calculate the tax
- Tax = $1,299 × (8.75 / 100) = $1,299 × 0.0875 = **\$113.66**

**Step 2:** Calculate the total
- Total = \$1,299 + $113.66 = **$1,412.66**

*Interpretation:* The sales tax on this laptop is \$113.66, bringing the total cost to \$1,412.66. Buying the same laptop in Oregon (0% sales tax) would save the full \$113.66 — a meaningful difference on high-ticket electronics.

### Example 2: Extracting Tax from a Receipt Total

Your restaurant receipt shows a total of \$86.40 (food only, before tip). The local sales tax rate is 7.5%. How much was tax?

**Step 1:** Calculate the pre-tax price
- Pre-Tax Price = $86.40 / (1 + 0.075) = $86.40 / 1.075 = **\$80.37**

**Step 2:** Calculate the embedded tax
- Tax = \$86.40 − $80.37 = **$6.03**

*Interpretation:* Of the \$86.40 total, \$6.03 was sales tax and \$80.37 was the food subtotal. This is useful for expense reports that require separating tax from the base amount.

## Key Factors That Affect Your Results

1. **State Base Rate** — State-level sales tax ranges from 0% (Delaware, Montana, New Hampshire, Oregon) to 7.25% (California). This is the floor; local taxes add to it.
2. **Local Add-On Rates** — Counties, cities, and special districts can add their own tax. In some areas of Louisiana, the combined rate reaches 12.95%. Always use the combined rate for your specific location.
3. **Tax-Exempt Items** — Most states exempt groceries, prescription drugs, and clothing (in some states) from sales tax. This calculator assumes the item is fully taxable.
4. **Use Tax** — If you buy online from a seller that does not collect tax, you technically owe "use tax" at the same rate. Most states now require online sellers to collect sales tax under the 2018 *South Dakota v. Wayfair* Supreme Court decision.
5. **Rounding Rules** — States use different rounding methods (bracket system vs. standard rounding). Results may differ by \$0.01 from your actual receipt due to rounding conventions.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- Assumes the entire purchase amount is subject to sales tax at a single rate
- Does not account for tax-exempt items (groceries, medications, clothing in some states)
- Does not handle tiered tax rates (e.g., different rates for different item categories)
- Rounding may differ from your state's official bracket schedule by up to \$0.01
- Does not include excise taxes, luxury taxes, or special assessments
- Rates change; verify your current combined rate at your state's department of revenue

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do I find my local sales tax rate?

Your combined sales tax rate includes state, county, city, and special district taxes. The easiest way to find it is to search "[your city] sales tax rate" or visit your state's department of revenue website. Most state revenue departments offer a rate lookup tool by address or ZIP code. You can also check a recent receipt from a local store — the tax rate is typically printed on it.

### Which states have no sales tax?

Five states have no statewide sales tax: Delaware, Montana, New Hampshire, Oregon, and Alaska. However, Alaska allows local municipalities to impose their own sales tax, so some Alaska cities and boroughs charge up to 7.5%. The other four states have no state or local sales tax at all.

### How do I reverse-calculate the pre-tax price from a total?

Divide the total by (1 + tax rate as a decimal). For example, if the total is \$107.50 and the tax rate is 7.5%, the pre-tax price is $107.50 / 1.075 = $100.00. The tax portion is \$107.50 - $100.00 = $7.50. A common mistake is multiplying the total by the tax rate — that gives the wrong answer because the tax rate applies to the pre-tax price, not the total.
