## What Your Results Mean

Your results show the exact sales tax amount, the pre-tax price, and the total after-tax cost. If your combined rate exceeds 9%, you are in one of the ten highest-taxed jurisdictions in the country. If the rate is below 5%, you are among the lowest.

Compare your effective tax burden to your income using the [Income Tax Calculator](/finance/income-tax-calculator), or see how sales tax affects your take-home pay with the [Salary Calculator](/finance/salary-calculator). For large purchases, even a 1% rate difference changes the math: on a \$25,000 car, 1% is \$250.

## How Sales Tax Is Calculated

<div className="formula-section">

Sales tax in the United States is an ad valorem tax — a percentage of the purchase price collected at the point of sale. Unlike value-added tax (VAT) systems used in most other countries, U.S. sales tax is applied only at the final retail stage, not at every step of the supply chain.

This calculator supports three modes:

**Mode 1 — Add Tax (Net → Gross):**

$$
\text{Tax} = \text{Net Price} \times \frac{\text{Rate}}{100}
$$

$$
\text{Gross Price} = \text{Net Price} + \text{Tax}
$$

**Mode 2 — Extract Tax (Gross → Net):**

$$
\text{Net Price} = \frac{\text{Gross Price}}{1 + \frac{\text{Rate}}{100}}
$$

$$
\text{Tax} = \text{Gross Price} - \text{Net Price}
$$

**Mode 3 — Find Rate:**

$$
\text{Tax Rate} = \frac{\text{Gross Price} - \text{Net Price}}{\text{Net Price}} \times 100
$$

Where:
- **Net Price** = the purchase price before sales tax is applied
- **Gross Price** = the final amount including sales tax
- **Rate** = the combined state and local sales tax rate as a percentage (e.g., 8.99 for California)
- **Tax** = the dollar amount of sales tax

*Source: State revenue departments. Sales tax is a standard ad valorem percentage computation codified in each state's tax code (e.g., Texas Tax Code §151.051). Rate data from the Tax Foundation, "State and Local Sales Tax Rates, 2026."*

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: \$89.99 Purchase in Tennessee at 9.61%

You are buying a pair of running shoes priced at \$89.99 in Nashville, Tennessee, where the combined state and local sales tax rate is 9.61%.

**Step 1:** Calculate the tax amount
- Tax = \$89.99 × (9.61 / 100) = \$89.99 × 0.0961 = **\$8.65**

**Step 2:** Calculate the total
- Total = \$89.99 + \$8.65 = **\$98.64**

*Interpretation:* You will pay \$8.65 in sales tax on this purchase, bringing the register total to \$98.64. Tennessee has the second-highest combined rate in the nation (9.61%), so the same shoes in Oregon (0%) would cost \$89.99 flat — saving you \$8.65.

### Example 2: Reverse-Calculate from \$107.99 Receipt in California at 8.99%

Your electronics receipt shows a total of \$107.99 including tax. California's combined state and local rate is 8.99%. What was the pre-tax price?

**Step 1:** Calculate the pre-tax price
- Net Price = \$107.99 / (1 + 0.0899) = \$107.99 / 1.0899 = **\$99.08**

**Step 2:** Calculate the embedded tax
- Tax = \$107.99 − \$99.08 = **\$8.91**

*Interpretation:* Of the \$107.99 total, \$8.91 was sales tax and \$99.08 was the item price. This reverse calculation is essential for expense reports, business deductions, and reconciling receipts where the pre-tax amount is not printed separately.

### Example 3: Finding the Tax Rate — Item Priced \$49.99, Paid \$53.50

You know you paid \$53.50 for an item originally priced at \$49.99. What was the effective tax rate?

**Step 1:** Calculate the rate
- Tax Rate = ((\$53.50 − \$49.99) / \$49.99) × 100 = (\$3.51 / \$49.99) × 100 = **7.02%**

*Interpretation:* The effective combined sales tax rate was 7.02%. Cross-reference this with your state's posted rate — if the posted rate is 7.00% and you were charged 7.02%, the \$0.01 difference is due to rounding. If the gap is larger, the retailer may have applied an incorrect rate.

## What Changed in 2026

Ten states saw sales tax changes taking effect on or after January 1, 2026. These changes affect grocery taxation, local rates, and exemption structures.

### Grocery Tax Eliminations and Reductions

**Arkansas** eliminated its state grocery tax entirely. The previous rate of 0.125% on groceries was the last remnant of a phased reduction that began in 2023. Local taxes on groceries may still apply in some jurisdictions.

**Illinois** eliminated the 1% state sales tax on groceries effective January 1, 2026. This follows the temporary suspension that was in place during 2022–2023. Groceries in Illinois are now fully exempt at the state level, though local grocery taxes may apply in certain municipalities.

**Mississippi** reduced its grocery tax rate from 7% to 5% as part of a gradual phase-down that will continue to 2.5% by 2036. This is one of the most significant ongoing grocery tax reforms in the country.

**Alabama** reduced its state grocery tax rate from 3% to 2%, continuing its multi-year phase-down. Alabama remains one of only a handful of states that still taxes groceries above 1% at the state level.

### Other Changes

**Kansas** completed the full phase-out of its state grocery tax, which began stepping down in 2024. Groceries are now fully exempt at the state level.

**Ohio** repealed several sales tax exemptions, effectively broadening the tax base. Previously exempt categories now collect standard sales tax.

**Georgia** saw several counties increase their local option sales tax rates to provide property tax relief, increasing the average local rate statewide.

**Florida**, **Missouri**, and **Washington** had minor local rate adjustments across various jurisdictions, though state-level rates remained unchanged.

### Pending Changes to Watch

**New Mexico** has a revenue trigger mechanism that could lower the state's gross receipts tax rate if revenue targets are met. No change has taken effect yet.

**South Dakota** continues a temporary additional state sales tax, subject to legislative review.

**Tennessee** legislators have discussed eliminating the state grocery tax (currently 4% on food), though no legislation has passed as of March 2026.

## Grocery Tax by State

Grocery taxation is one of the most impactful differences between states for family budgets. A family spending \$800 per month on groceries in a state with a 7% grocery tax pays \$672 per year in grocery tax alone.

**States that fully tax groceries at the standard rate:** Hawaii, Idaho, Kansas (local only), Mississippi (reduced to 5%), New Mexico, Oklahoma, South Dakota. These seven states stand out because most states exempt groceries entirely.

**States with reduced grocery tax rates:** Alabama (2%), Louisiana (varies by parish), Missouri (1.225%), Tennessee (4%), Utah (3%), Virginia (1%). These states tax groceries, but at a lower rate than their standard sales tax.

**States that fully exempt groceries:** The majority of states — including California, Texas, New York, Florida, Illinois, Pennsylvania, and Ohio — do not charge state sales tax on groceries. Local taxes on groceries may still apply in some jurisdictions.

For the 13 states that still tax groceries in some form, this is a significant household expense. A family of four can save \$400–\$800 per year by understanding which items qualify for exemptions and which do not. Most states that tax groceries exempt SNAP-eligible items but may still tax prepared foods, supplements, and carbonated beverages.

## Clothing Sales Tax by State

Most states charge full sales tax on clothing, but several notable exceptions exist:

**States that fully exempt clothing from sales tax:** Pennsylvania, New Jersey, Minnesota, and Montana (no sales tax at all).

**Threshold exemptions:** New York exempts clothing and footwear items under \$110 per item. Massachusetts exempts clothing under \$175 per item. Rhode Island exempts clothing under \$250 per item. Vermont exempts clothing under \$110 per item. Connecticut applies a luxury tax rate of 7.75% on clothing over \$1,000 but exempts clothing below that threshold at the standard 6.35% rate.

**All other states** charge their full combined sales tax rate on clothing purchases with no exemption or threshold.

If you are making a large clothing purchase — winter coats, business wardrobes, school uniforms — buying in New Jersey or Pennsylvania can save meaningful money. A \$500 wardrobe purchase saves \$40–\$50 in tax compared to a state with an 8–10% combined rate.

## Sales Tax vs. Value-Added Tax (VAT)

The United States is one of the few developed economies that uses a retail sales tax rather than a value-added tax. The key differences:

| Feature | U.S. Sales Tax | VAT (Most Countries) |
|---|---|---|
| **Collection point** | Final retail sale only | Every stage of production |
| **Who remits** | Retailer | Each business in the chain |
| **Consumer visibility** | Added at checkout | Included in sticker price |
| **Rates** | 0–10.11% (varies by state) | 5–27% (varies by country) |
| **Coverage** | Goods (and some services) | Goods and most services |

In practice, the biggest consumer-facing difference is visibility: U.S. prices displayed on shelves do not include tax, while VAT-inclusive prices in Europe, the UK, and most other countries show what you actually pay. This is why American tourists abroad often feel prices are "already included" — because they are.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### 1. Assuming the sticker price includes tax
Unlike most countries, U.S. retail prices do not include sales tax. Always add your local rate to the displayed price. A \$999 TV in Texas (8.20%) costs \$1,080.92 at the register.

### 2. Forgetting local taxes can add 1–5% on top of the state rate
California's state rate is 7.25%, but local district taxes push the combined rate to 8.99% on average — and over 10% in some cities. Always use the combined rate for your specific location, not just the state rate.

### 3. Not checking exemptions for groceries, clothing, and medicine
Prescription drugs are exempt in every state. Groceries are exempt in most states but taxed in seven. Clothing is exempt in a handful of states with specific thresholds. Applying the standard rate to an exempt item overstates your tax.

### 4. Using last year's rates after a rate change
Ten states changed their sales tax rates or exemptions in 2026. If your accounting software or spreadsheet uses 2025 rates, you are calculating incorrect totals. Update annually.

### 5. Confusing sales tax with use tax
If you buy online from a seller that does not collect tax — increasingly rare after the Wayfair decision — you owe use tax at the same rate. Most states now require marketplace facilitators (Amazon, eBay, Walmart) to collect sales tax, but direct purchases from small out-of-state sellers may still slip through.

## Pro Tips & Advanced Usage

**Compare scenarios across states:** If you are buying a vehicle or high-ticket item and live near a state border, calculate the tax in both states. Some states charge sales tax on the full vehicle price; others allow trade-in deductions. **Tax-free weekends and holidays:** Many states offer annual sales tax holidays — typically in August for back-to-school shopping. Items like clothing, school supplies, and computers under a certain threshold are exempt during these periods. Check your state's department of revenue for 2026 dates.

**Marketplace facilitator laws:** Since 2018, most online purchases include sales tax automatically. However, the rate applied should match your *delivery address*, not the seller's location. If you see an incorrect rate on an online order, contact the seller.

## Limitations & Assumptions

- Rates shown are combined state + average local. Your actual local rate varies by city, county, and special tax district — it may be higher or lower than the average shown here.
- This calculator does not apply product-specific exemptions (groceries, clothing, medicine, manufacturing equipment). The full combined rate is applied to the entered amount.
- Rates are effective as of January 1, 2026. Mid-year rate changes are not reflected until the next data update.
- Rounding may differ by \$0.01 from your actual receipt due to state-specific bracket rounding rules.
- Does not include excise taxes, luxury taxes, hotel/occupancy taxes, or special assessments.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Which state has the highest sales tax in 2026?

Louisiana has the highest combined state and local sales tax rate at 10.11% in 2026, consisting of a 4.45% state rate and a 5.66% average local rate. Tennessee ranks second at 9.61%, followed by Arkansas at 9.49%. Louisiana's high combined rate is driven primarily by its local tax components — individual parishes can levy substantial additional taxes that push total rates well above 10% in some areas.

### Which states have no sales tax?

Five states have no statewide sales tax: Alaska, Delaware, Montana, New Hampshire, and Oregon. Of these, Alaska is a special case — while it has no state sales tax, it allows local municipalities to impose their own tax, averaging 1.82% statewide. Some Alaska boroughs charge as high as 7.5%. The remaining four states (Delaware, Montana, New Hampshire, Oregon) have no state or local general sales tax at all.

### Is food taxed in my state?

It varies widely. Most states exempt groceries from sales tax entirely, including California, Texas, New York, Florida, and Illinois (as of 2026). Thirteen states still tax groceries in some form: seven at the full rate (Hawaii, Idaho, Mississippi, New Mexico, Oklahoma, South Dakota, and Kansas at the local level only), and six at reduced rates (Alabama at 2%, Tennessee at 4%, Utah at 3%, Virginia at 1%, Missouri at 1.225%, and Louisiana which varies by parish). See the full grocery tax breakdown in the table above.

### What sales tax rates changed in 2026?

Four states changed grocery taxation: Arkansas and Illinois eliminated their state grocery tax, Mississippi reduced its grocery rate from 7% to 5%, and Alabama cut its grocery rate from 3% to 2%. Kansas completed its grocery tax phase-out. Ohio repealed several exemptions, broadening its tax base. Georgia, Florida, Missouri, and Washington had local rate adjustments. These are the most significant changes since the Wayfair-driven collection expansion of 2018–2020.

### How do I calculate tax backwards from a total?

Divide the gross price by (1 + rate as a decimal). For example, if you paid \$107.99 total in California (8.99% combined rate), the pre-tax price is \$107.99 ÷ 1.0899 = \$99.08, and the tax was \$8.91. A common mistake is multiplying the total by the tax rate — that gives the wrong answer because the tax rate applies to the pre-tax price, not the total. Use the "Extract Tax from Total" mode in the calculator above for instant results.
