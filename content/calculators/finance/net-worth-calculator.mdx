## What Your Projection Shows

Your net worth is the single most important number in personal finance — it represents the gap between everything you own and everything you owe. Unlike income, which measures cash flow, net worth measures accumulated wealth. A household earning \$150,000 per year with \$200,000 in debt and \$50,000 in assets has a negative net worth of -\$150,000, while a household earning \$60,000 with a paid-off home and retirement savings may have a net worth exceeding \$400,000.

The **asset breakdown** pie chart reveals how your wealth is distributed. Most Americans hold the majority of their net worth in their primary residence — the 2022 Federal Reserve Survey of Consumer Finances found that the median homeowner holds 62% of their total assets in home equity. Over-concentration in any single asset category represents risk; financial advisors typically recommend that no more than 30-40% of net worth be tied to any single asset class.

The **debt-to-asset ratio** is a key measure of financial health. A ratio below 50% is generally considered healthy — you own more than you owe. A ratio above 80% signals high leverage and financial vulnerability. Track this number quarterly alongside your total net worth. Use the [Retirement Calculator](/calculators/finance/retirement-calculator) to project how your net worth will grow over time as you save and invest.

## How to Use This Calculator

1. **Enter your liquid assets** — start with cash and savings accounts (checking, savings, CDs, money market). Check your bank statements for current balances. Then add non-retirement investments — brokerage accounts, stocks, bonds, mutual funds, and ETFs that are not inside retirement accounts.

2. **Enter retirement accounts** — include the current value of all retirement accounts: 401(k), 403(b), traditional IRA, Roth IRA, and any pension value. Log into your retirement account provider to get the latest balance. Use the vested balance if you have employer matching that has not fully vested.

3. **Enter property values** — for your home, use a recent appraisal or Zillow/Redfin estimate (keeping in mind these are approximations). Add vehicles at their current trade-in value (not original purchase price) from Kelley Blue Book or Edmunds. Include other real estate at estimated market value.

4. **Enter all liabilities** — list your mortgage balance (not the original loan amount — check your latest statement), student loans, auto loans, credit card balances, and any other debts. Use your current outstanding balances, not your original borrowed amounts.

## The Formula

Net worth is calculated using the fundamental accounting equation:

$$\text{Net Worth} = \text{Total Assets} - \text{Total Liabilities}$$

Where:

- **Total Assets** = Cash & Savings + Investments + Retirement Accounts + Home Value + Other Real Estate + Vehicle Value + Other Assets
- **Total Liabilities** = Mortgage Balance + Student Loans + Auto Loans + Credit Card Debt + Other Debts

The debt-to-asset ratio provides additional context:

$$\text{Debt-to-Asset Ratio} = \frac{\text{Total Liabilities}}{\text{Total Assets}} \times 100\%$$

A ratio below 50% indicates that you own at least twice what you owe. A ratio above 100% means your liabilities exceed your assets (negative net worth).

**Source:** This methodology follows the balance sheet approach used by the **Federal Reserve's Survey of Consumer Finances (SCF)**, which has measured American household wealth triennially since 1989.

**Step-by-step:**

1. Total Assets: \$20,000 + \$35,000 + \$120,000 + \$380,000 + $22,000 = **$577,000**
2. Total Liabilities: \$280,000 + \$15,000 + \$12,000 + $3,000 = **$310,000**
3. Net Worth: \$577,000 - $310,000 = **$267,000**
4. Debt-to-Asset Ratio: (\$310,000 / \$577,000) x 100 = **53.7%**

**Interpretation:** A net worth of \$267,000 at age 40 is above the U.S. median for the 35-44 age group (\$135,600 median per the 2022 SCF). The debt-to-asset ratio of 53.7% is slightly above the 50% healthy threshold, primarily driven by the mortgage. Home equity accounts for (\$380,000 - $280,000) = $100,000, or 37% of net worth. As the mortgage is paid down, both the net worth and debt-to-asset ratio will improve significantly.

## Key Factors That Affect Your Results

**Home Equity** — For most Americans, the home is the largest single asset. A \$400,000 home with a \$280,000 mortgage contributes \$120,000 to net worth. Home values fluctuate — during the 2008 financial crisis, median home prices dropped 27%, pushing millions of homeowners into negative equity. Use a conservative estimate based on recent comparable sales, not peak market pricing.

**Retirement Account Growth** — Retirement accounts benefit from compound growth and tax advantages. A 30-year-old with \$50,000 in a 401(k) at 7% average returns will have approximately \$380,000 by age 50 without additional contributions. However, retirement accounts are illiquid — you cannot access them without penalty before age 59.5 in most cases. They should be counted in net worth but not considered available cash.

**Consumer Debt** — Credit card debt at 20%+ APR is the fastest destroyer of net worth. Every \$1,000 in credit card debt costs \$200+ per year in interest alone. Paying off \$5,000 in credit card debt immediately improves your net worth by \$5,000 and eliminates \$1,000+ in annual interest that would otherwise erode future savings.

**Vehicle Depreciation** — New vehicles lose approximately 20% of their value in the first year and 60% over five years. A \$35,000 car with a \$30,000 loan is underwater from day one. Always use current trade-in value, not purchase price, when calculating vehicle asset value.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- Home value is an **estimate**. Actual market value is only confirmed when you sell. Use a recent appraisal or reputable online estimate.
- Retirement account values are **pre-tax** for traditional accounts. The actual after-tax value of a traditional 401(k) or IRA is approximately 70-80% of the stated balance (depending on your tax bracket at withdrawal). Roth accounts are post-tax.
- Does **not include future Social Security benefits**, pensions, or other expected income streams. These have value but are not typically included in net worth calculations.
- Vehicle and personal property values are **approximations**. Kelley Blue Book or NADA guides provide reasonable estimates for vehicles.
- This is a **point-in-time snapshot**. Net worth changes daily as asset values fluctuate and debt is paid down. Track quarterly for meaningful trend analysis.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is a good net worth for my age?

The median net worth by age group according to the 2022 Federal Reserve Survey of Consumer Finances is: under 35: \$39,000; 35-44: \$135,600; 45-54: \$247,200; 55-64: \$364,500; 65-74: \$409,900; 75+: \$335,600. These are medians, meaning half of households in each group have more and half have less. A common benchmark is that your net worth should be approximately your annual salary times your age divided by 10 (for example, a 40-year-old earning \$80,000 should target \$320,000). However, this formula is a rough guideline — your situation depends on cost of living, debt history, and financial goals.

### Should I include my home in net worth?

Yes, your home should be included in your net worth calculation, but with context. The Federal Reserve includes primary residences in their Survey of Consumer Finances. However, since you need a place to live, home equity is not available to spend or invest (unless you sell or borrow against it). Some financial planners calculate "liquid net worth" by excluding the primary residence and its mortgage. Both metrics are useful — total net worth shows your complete financial picture, while liquid net worth shows what is actually accessible.

### How often should I calculate net worth?

Calculate your net worth quarterly for the most useful trend analysis. Monthly is too frequent — short-term market fluctuations create noise. Annually is too infrequent to catch problems early. Set a quarterly calendar reminder to update all balances. Track it in a spreadsheet or use this calculator. The direction of the trend matters more than the absolute number — a net worth increasing by \$10,000-\$20,000 per year indicates healthy financial progress for most middle-income households.
