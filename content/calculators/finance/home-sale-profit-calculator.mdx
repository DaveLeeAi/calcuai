## What Your Home Sale Profit Estimate Shows

**Net proceeds** is the single most important number when selling a home — it is the cash you actually walk away with after every cost is subtracted from the sale price. Many sellers focus on gross profit (sale price minus purchase price), but gross profit ignores the substantial transaction costs that reduce your real return.

**Agent commission** is typically the largest single cost of selling. At 5% on a \$400,000 sale, the commission is \$20,000. Since the 2024 NAR settlement, commission structures have shifted — sellers are no longer required to offer buyer agent compensation through the MLS, which means the total rate is increasingly negotiable. Some sellers pay as little as 3%–4% total, while full-service arrangements still run 5%–6%.

**Closing costs** for sellers typically range from 1% to 3% of the sale price and include title insurance, transfer taxes, escrow fees, recording fees, and prorated property taxes. These costs vary significantly by state — transfer taxes alone range from zero (in states like Texas and Wyoming) to over 2% (in New York City and Washington, D.C.).

**Capital gains tax** applies when your sale price exceeds your cost basis (purchase price plus qualifying improvements). The IRS Section 121 exclusion lets you exclude \$250,000 (single) or \$500,000 (married filing jointly) of gain on a primary residence you have owned and lived in for at least 2 of the past 5 years. Only the gain above the exclusion is taxed — at 15% for most taxpayers (long-term rate) or at ordinary income rates if you owned the home for less than 1 year.

**Improvements** added to the home increase your cost basis and reduce your taxable capital gain. A \$15,000 kitchen renovation reduces the gain by \$15,000. Qualifying improvements must add value, prolong the home's life, or adapt it to new uses — routine maintenance and repairs do not count. Keep receipts for all capital improvements.

## The Formula

**Net proceeds:**

$$\text{Net Proceeds} = \text{Sale Price} - \text{Commission} - \text{Closing Costs} - \text{Mortgage Balance} - \text{Capital Gains Tax}$$

**Agent commission:**

$$\text{Commission} = \text{Sale Price} \times \frac{\text{Commission Rate}}{100}$$

**Closing costs:**

$$\text{Closing Costs} = \text{Sale Price} \times \frac{\text{Closing Cost Rate}}{100}$$

**Capital gain:**

$$\text{Capital Gain} = \text{Sale Price} - \text{Purchase Price} - \text{Improvements}$$

**Taxable gain (with Section 121 exclusion):**

$$\text{Taxable Gain} = \max\left(0,\; \text{Capital Gain} - \text{Exclusion}\right)$$

Where:
- **Exclusion** = \$250,000 (single) or \$500,000 (married) if owned and lived in 2+ of the last 5 years
- **Long-term tax rate** = 15% (most common bracket, for assets held 1+ years)
- **Short-term tax rate** = 22% (estimated, for assets held less than 1 year)

**Source:** IRS Publication 523 — Selling Your Home (Section 121 exclusion rules); National Association of Realtors (NAR) 2024 commission data; Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) closing cost guidelines.

### Selling a \$400,000 Home (Single Filer, 7 Years Owned)

**Inputs:**
- Sale Price: \$400,000
- Purchase Price: \$300,000
- Mortgage Balance: \$220,000
- Agent Commission Rate: 5%

- Improvements: \$15,000
- Years Owned: 7
- Filing Status: Single

**Step-by-step:**

1. Agent commission: \$400,000 × 5% = **\$20,000**
2. Closing costs: \$400,000 × 2% = **\$8,000**
3. Cost basis: \$300,000 + \$15,000 = **\$315,000**
4. Capital gain: \$400,000 − \$315,000 = **\$85,000**
5. Section 121 exclusion (single, 7 years): **\$250,000**
6. Taxable gain: max(0, \$85,000 − \$250,000) = **\$0**
7. Estimated tax: **\$0**
8. Net proceeds: \$400,000 − \$20,000 − \$8,000 − \$220,000 − \$0 = **\$152,000**

**Interpretation:** You walk away with \$152,000 in cash. Despite a \$100,000 gross profit, transaction costs consume \$28,000. The Section 121 exclusion eliminates all capital gains tax because your \$85,000 gain is well below the \$250,000 single-filer threshold. If you had not made the \$15,000 in improvements, your capital gain would have been \$100,000 — still fully excluded. ## Key Factors That Affect Your Home Sale Profit

**Agent commission negotiation.** The standard 5%–6% commission is not a legal requirement — it is a market convention that is actively changing. Since the 2024 NAR settlement, buyers increasingly pay their own agent directly. Sellers who negotiate a 4% total commission on a \$400,000 sale save \$4,000 compared to 5%. Flat-fee and discount brokerages offer listing services for \$3,000–\$5,000 regardless of sale price.

**Closing cost variation by state.** Transfer taxes are the single largest variable in seller closing costs. States like Texas, Wyoming, and Indiana have zero transfer taxes. New York City charges 1%–2.625% depending on property value. Factor in your specific state and county rates for an accurate estimate.

**Section 121 exclusion eligibility.** To qualify for the \$250K/\$500K capital gains exclusion, you must have owned AND used the home as your primary residence for at least 2 of the past 5 years. Investment properties, vacation homes, and homes owned less than 2 years do not qualify. Partial exclusions may apply if you moved due to a job change, health condition, or unforeseen circumstances.

**Improvements vs. repairs.** Only capital improvements — additions, remodels, new systems — increase your cost basis and reduce your taxable gain. Painting, patching drywall, and fixing a leaky faucet are repairs that do not affect your cost basis. The IRS distinction matters: keep detailed records and receipts for all improvement work.

Your actual tax rate depends on your total taxable income, filing status, and applicable state taxes. Closing costs are estimated as a percentage of the sale price; actual costs vary by location, title company, and negotiation. The Section 121 exclusion follows current IRS rules as of 2025.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much money do I keep when I sell my house?

Your take-home amount is the sale price minus agent commissions (typically 5%–6%), closing costs (1%–3%), your remaining mortgage payoff, and any applicable capital gains tax. On a \$400,000 sale with a \$220,000 mortgage and 5% commission, expect approximately \$144,000–\$160,000 in net proceeds depending on your specific closing costs and tax situation. Use this calculator with your actual numbers for a precise estimate.

### How can I reduce agent commission fees?

Negotiate the rate directly — commissions are never fixed by law. Consider flat-fee listing services (\$3,000–\$5,000), discount brokerages (1%–2% listing side), or selling without a buyer's agent offer (post-NAR settlement, buyers can negotiate their own agent fees). On a \$400,000 home, reducing total commission from 6% to 4% saves \$8,000. Compare at least three agents before signing a listing agreement. For a detailed commission breakdown, use our [Real Estate Commission Calculator](/finance/real-estate-commission-calculator).

### Do I have to pay capital gains tax when I sell my home?

Most primary residence sellers pay zero capital gains tax thanks to the IRS Section 121 exclusion, which lets you exclude up to \$250,000 of gain (single) or \$500,000 (married filing jointly) if you owned and lived in the home for at least 2 of the past 5 years. You only owe tax on gain exceeding the exclusion. Investment properties and homes owned less than 2 years do not qualify for this exclusion and are taxed at 15%–20% (long-term) or ordinary income rates (short-term).

### What happens if I sell at a loss?

If you sell your primary residence for less than you paid, you do not owe any capital gains tax. However, you cannot deduct the loss on your personal tax return — losses on personal residences are not tax-deductible. You will still owe agent commissions and closing costs, which come out of your sale proceeds. If your sale proceeds do not cover your mortgage balance, you may need to bring cash to closing or negotiate a short sale with your lender.

### What closing costs does the seller pay?

Sellers typically pay 1%–3% of the sale price in closing costs, including title insurance (owner's policy), transfer taxes (varies by state), escrow and settlement fees, prorated property taxes, any outstanding HOA dues, and recording fees. The largest variable is the transfer tax — which ranges from \$0 in some states to over 2% in high-tax jurisdictions. Your title company or closing attorney provides a detailed settlement statement (HUD-1 or Closing Disclosure) before closing.
