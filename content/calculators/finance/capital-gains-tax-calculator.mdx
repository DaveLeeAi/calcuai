## What Your Capital Gains Tax Estimate Shows

Your result reveals the federal tax you owe on investment profits and, critically, the difference between short-term and long-term treatment. **Long-term capital gains** (assets held more than one year) receive preferential rates: 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on your total taxable income. **Short-term gains** (assets held one year or less) receive no such benefit — they are stacked on top of your ordinary income and taxed at your marginal income tax rate, which can reach 37%.

The **effective tax rate** shows the actual percentage of your gain consumed by taxes. This is often lower than the bracket rate because gains can span multiple brackets. A single filer with \$40,000 in ordinary income and a \$20,000 long-term gain pays 0% on the first \$8,350 of gain (which fills the 0% bracket up to \$48,350) and 15% on the remaining \$11,650 — yielding an effective rate of approximately 8.7%, not the full 15%.

The **net proceeds** figure is what you actually keep after tax. This is the number that matters when comparing the true return on your investment. If you bought stock for \$50,000 and sold for \$80,000, your nominal return is 60%. But after \$4,500 in long-term capital gains tax, your real return is \$25,500 on a \$50,000 investment — 51% after tax. Use the [investment calculator](/finance/investment-calculator) to model after-tax growth projections.

## How to Use This Calculator

1. **Enter your purchase price (cost basis)** — this is what you originally paid for the investment, including commissions and fees. For stocks, use the total amount paid including brokerage fees. For real estate, your cost basis includes the purchase price plus capital improvements (renovations, additions) minus depreciation claimed. The default is \$50,000.

2. **Enter your sale price** — the actual or expected sale price. For stocks, use the net proceeds after brokerage fees. For real estate, use the contract sale price before closing costs (you may subtract selling costs from the gain separately). If the sale price is less than the purchase price, you have a capital loss and owe no capital gains tax.

3. **Select your holding period** — choose Short-Term (1 year or less) or Long-Term (more than 1 year). The distinction is based on the exact dates of purchase and sale. An asset bought on January 15, 2024 becomes long-term if sold on or after January 16, 2025. This single factor can change your tax rate from 22% to 15% — a difference of thousands of dollars on large gains.

4. **Enter your annual taxable income** — your other income excluding the gain being calculated. This determines which capital gains bracket your gain falls into. Include wages, salary, business income, and other investment income. Do not include the capital gain itself — the calculator adds it.

5. **Select your filing status** — Single, Married Filing Jointly, or Head of Household. MFJ filers have significantly wider 0% and 15% brackets. A married couple pays 0% on long-term gains up to \$96,700 in total taxable income, versus \$48,350 for a single filer.

## The Formula

**Capital gain:**

$$\text{Capital Gain} = \text{Sale Price} - \text{Purchase Price}$$

**Short-term gains** are taxed as ordinary income. The incremental tax is:

$$\text{Tax on Gain} = T(\text{Income} + \text{Gain}) - T(\text{Income})$$

Where $T(x)$ is the progressive federal income tax function applied to taxable income $x$ (after subtracting the standard deduction).

**Long-term gains** use preferential brackets. The gain "stacks on top" of ordinary taxable income:

$$\text{LTCG Tax} = L(\text{Ordinary Taxable} + \text{Gain}) - L(\text{Ordinary Taxable})$$

Where $L(x)$ applies the 2025 long-term capital gains brackets:

| Filing Status | 0% Rate | 15% Rate | 20% Rate |
|---------------|---------|----------|----------|
| Single | Up to \$48,350 | \$48,351 – \$533,400 | Over \$533,400 |
| Married Filing Jointly | Up to \$96,700 | \$96,701 – \$600,050 | Over \$600,050 |
| Head of Household | Up to \$64,750 | \$64,751 – \$566,700 | Over \$566,700 |

**Effective rate and net proceeds:**

$$\text{Effective Rate} = \frac{\text{Tax Owed}}{\text{Capital Gain}} \times 100$$
$$\text{Net Proceeds} = \text{Sale Price} - \text{Tax Owed}$$

**Source:** IRS Topic No. 409 — Capital Gains and Losses. 2025 bracket thresholds per IRS Revenue Procedure 2024-40.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Long-Term Gain — \$30K Profit, Single Filer, \$80K Income

**Inputs:**
- Purchase Price: \$50,000
- Sale Price: \$80,000
- Holding Period: Long-Term (> 1 year)
- Annual Income: \$80,000
- Filing Status: Single

**Step-by-step:**

1. Capital gain: \$80,000 − \$50,000 = **\$30,000**
2. Taxable ordinary income: \$80,000 − \$15,000 (std. deduction) = **\$65,000**
3. Gain stacks from \$65,000 to \$95,000 in LTCG brackets
4. LTCG single 0% bracket ends at \$48,350 — ordinary income already past this
5. Entire \$30,000 gain falls in the 15% bracket
6. Tax: \$30,000 × 15% = **\$4,500**
7. Effective rate: \$4,500 ÷ \$30,000 = **15.0%**
8. Net proceeds: \$80,000 − \$4,500 = **\$75,500**

**Interpretation:** The entire gain is taxed at 15% because this filer's ordinary income already exceeds the 0% LTCG threshold (\$48,350). Had their income been \$30,000 (taxable \$15,000), the first \$33,350 of gains would have been tax-free at 0%, dramatically reducing the bill.

### Example 2: Short-Term vs. Long-Term Comparison — Same \$30K Gain

**Short-term (held ≤ 1 year):**

1. Taxable without gain: \$80,000 − \$15,000 = \$65,000
2. Taxable with gain: \$110,000 − \$15,000 = \$95,000
3. Tax on \$95,000: \$15,814 (10%: \$1,192.50 + 12%: \$4,386 + 22%: \$10,235.50)
4. Tax on \$65,000: \$9,214 (10%: \$1,192.50 + 12%: \$4,386 + 22%: \$3,635.50)
5. Tax on gain: \$15,814 − \$9,214 = **\$6,600**
6. Effective rate: **22.0%**

**Long-term (held > 1 year):** Tax = **\$4,500**, effective rate = **15.0%**

**Difference:** Holding the investment for one additional day (crossing the 1-year threshold) saves **\$2,100** in federal tax — a 31.8% reduction. This is why tax-loss harvesting strategies and holding-period awareness are critical for investment tax planning.

### Example 3: Low-Income Filer — 0% Long-Term Rate

**Inputs:**
- Purchase Price: \$10,000
- Sale Price: \$20,000
- Holding Period: Long-Term
- Annual Income: \$30,000
- Filing Status: Single

**Calculation:**

1. Capital gain: **\$10,000**
2. Taxable ordinary income: \$30,000 − \$15,000 = **\$15,000**
3. Gain stacks from \$15,000 to \$25,000 — entirely within the 0% bracket (ends at \$48,350)
4. Tax: **\$0**
5. Net proceeds: **\$20,000** (full sale price retained)

**Interpretation:** Single filers with total taxable income (including gains) below \$48,350 pay zero federal tax on long-term capital gains. This makes selling appreciated investments during low-income years — such as during a career transition, sabbatical, or early retirement — a powerful tax planning strategy.

## Key Factors That Affect Your Capital Gains Tax

**Holding period** is the single most impactful factor. The difference between short-term (ordinary rates up to 37%) and long-term (0%/15%/20%) treatment can cut your tax bill by 30–50% on the same gain. Assets held for 366 days qualify for long-term treatment; 365 days or fewer are short-term.

**Total taxable income** determines which long-term bracket applies. Low-income earners can realize gains at 0%. The 2025 thresholds for the 0% bracket are \$48,350 (Single), \$96,700 (MFJ), and \$64,750 (HOH). This creates strategic opportunities: retirees living on savings, workers between jobs, or graduate students with low W-2 income can sell appreciated assets tax-free.

**Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT)** adds 3.8% on top of capital gains for high earners (AGI above \$200,000 Single or \$250,000 MFJ). This calculator does not include NIIT — high-income filers should add 3.8% to their effective rate for gains above these thresholds.

**State capital gains taxes** vary significantly. Nine states have no income tax and therefore no state capital gains tax. California taxes capital gains as ordinary income at rates up to 13.3%. This calculator estimates federal tax only — add your state rate for the full picture.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the capital gains tax rate for 2025?
Long-term capital gains (assets held over 1 year) are taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20% for 2025 depending on your total taxable income and filing status. Single filers pay 0% on taxable income up to \$48,350, 15% from \$48,351 to \$533,400, and 20% above \$533,400. Short-term gains (1 year or less) are taxed as ordinary income at your marginal rate, which ranges from 10% to 37%. Most taxpayers fall in the 15% long-term bracket.

### How do I avoid capital gains tax legally?
Several strategies reduce or eliminate capital gains tax: (1) hold assets for more than one year to qualify for preferential long-term rates, (2) sell during low-income years when you fall in the 0% bracket, (3) use tax-loss harvesting — sell losing investments to offset gains, (4) donate appreciated assets to charity (deduct full market value, no gain recognized), (5) invest through Roth IRAs where gains are never taxed, (6) use the primary residence exclusion (\$250K single / \$500K married for homeowners who lived in the property 2 of the last 5 years).

### Do I pay capital gains tax on my home?
If the home was your primary residence for at least 2 of the last 5 years, you can exclude up to \$250,000 in gains (Single) or \$500,000 (Married Filing Jointly) under IRS Section 121. Gains beyond the exclusion are taxed at long-term rates. Investment properties and second homes do not qualify for this exclusion — all gains are taxable, though 1031 exchanges can defer the tax on investment properties.

### What is the difference between short-term and long-term capital gains?
Short-term capital gains apply to assets held for one year or less and are taxed at your ordinary income tax rate (10%–37%). Long-term gains apply to assets held for more than one year and receive preferential rates (0%, 15%, or 20%). For a single filer in the 22% marginal bracket with a \$30,000 gain, the difference is \$6,600 (short-term) versus \$4,500 (long-term) — a \$2,100 savings simply by holding the asset one day longer than the one-year mark.
