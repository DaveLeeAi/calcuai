## What Your Projection Shows

This calculator answers the most fundamental investment question: "How much will my money grow to?" It projects the future value of an initial investment, periodic contributions, or both, at a given rate of return over any time horizon.

**Future value makes the cost of waiting visible.** A 25-year-old investing $10,000 at 7% has $149,745 at age 65. A 35-year-old with the same $10,000 at 7% has only $76,123 at 65 — half the money, just by starting 10 years later. The extra $73,622 isn't from any additional contribution; it's purely the compound interest earned during those 10 early years. This asymmetry is why financial advisors emphasize starting early over investing more.

**The growth acceleration is non-linear.** At 7% annually, your first $10,000 earns $700 in year 1. By year 10, the same investment earns $1,318 that year alone (because you're earning 7% on $18,815, not $10,000). By year 30, annual interest hits $7,129. This exponential curve — visible in the growth chart below — is why Einstein allegedly called compound interest the eighth wonder of the world. For the inverse question — what a future sum is worth today — see our [Present Value Calculator](/calculators/finance/present-value-calculator).

## How to Use This Calculator

1. **Enter your Initial Investment (PV)** — the lump sum you're starting with today. This could be your current savings balance, an inheritance, a bonus, or any amount available to invest right now. Enter $0 if you're starting from scratch with contributions only.

2. **Enter the Periodic Contribution (optional)** — the amount you'll add regularly. For annual compounding, enter your yearly contribution. For monthly compounding, enter your monthly contribution. The calculator assumes contributions occur at the end of each period.

3. **Set the Annual Interest Rate** — your expected annual rate of return. Historical averages: S&P 500 = ~10% nominal (7% inflation-adjusted), bonds = ~4–5%, savings accounts = ~4–5% (2025), real estate = ~8–10% including appreciation. Use a conservative estimate for planning.

4. **Set the Time Horizon** — the number of years until you need the money. Retirement accounts might be 30–40 years. College funds: 18 years from birth. Short-term goals: 1–5 years.

5. **Choose Compounding Frequency** — how often returns compound. Most investment accounts compound daily or monthly. Bank savings compound daily or monthly. For simplicity, annual compounding gives a close approximation. More frequent compounding produces slightly higher future values.

## The Formula

**Future value of a lump sum:**

$$FV = PV \times (1 + r/n)^{n \times t}$$

**Future value of an annuity (series of equal contributions):**

$$FV_{annuity} = PMT \times \frac{(1 + r/n)^{n \times t} - 1}{r/n}$$

**Total future value (when both apply):**

$$FV_{total} = FV_{lump\,sum} + FV_{annuity}$$

Where:

- **FV** = future value — the total amount at the end of the investment period (dollars)
- **PV** = present value — the initial investment today (dollars)
- **PMT** = periodic contribution per compounding period (dollars)
- **r** = annual interest/return rate (decimal, e.g., 7% = 0.07)
- **n** = compounding periods per year (1 = annual, 12 = monthly, etc.)
- **t** = time in years

The **(1 + r/n)^(nt)** term is the compound growth factor. At 7% annual for 10 years, this factor is 1.9672 — meaning each dollar nearly doubles. The annuity formula captures the separate growth of each contribution, each compounding for a different number of remaining periods.

At 0% interest rate, the formula simplifies to: $FV = PV + PMT \times n \times t$ (pure accumulation).

**Source:** CFA Institute Level I Curriculum, Quantitative Methods: Time Value of Money; SEC Investor.gov compound interest education resources.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Lump Sum — $10,000 at 7% for 10 Years (Annual Compounding)

**Inputs:**
- Initial Investment: $10,000
- Annual Rate: 7%
- Time: 10 years
- Compounding: Annual

**Step-by-step:**

1. Growth factor: (1.07)^10 = **1.9672**
2. Future value: $10,000 × 1.9672 = **$19,672**
3. Interest earned: $19,672 − $10,000 = **$9,672**
4. Growth multiplier: 1.97× your original investment

**Interpretation:** Your $10,000 nearly doubles in 10 years at 7%, earning $9,672 in compound interest. The Rule of 72 approximation (72 ÷ 7 ≈ 10.3 years to double) confirms this. In the last year alone, you earn $1,287 — 2.8× more than the $700 earned in year 1.

### Example 2: Monthly Contributions — $500/Month at 7% for 30 Years

**Inputs:**
- Initial Investment: $0
- Periodic Contribution: $500/month
- Annual Rate: 7%
- Time: 30 years
- Compounding: Monthly

**Step-by-step:**

1. Monthly rate: 7% ÷ 12 = **0.005833**
2. Total periods: 12 × 30 = **360**
3. Growth factor: (1.005833)^360 = **8.1165**
4. FV of contributions: $500 × (8.1165 − 1) / 0.005833 = **$610,994**
5. Total contributed: $500 × 360 = **$180,000**
6. Interest earned: $610,994 − $180,000 = **$430,994**

**Interpretation:** You contribute $180,000 of your own money over 30 years, but compound interest adds $430,994 — 2.4× your contributions. This is the power of consistent long-term investing. If you waited 10 years and then contributed $500/month for only 20 years, the FV would be just $263,191 — less than half, despite contributing $120,000 (only $60,000 less).

## Key Factors That Affect Your Results

**Rate of Return** — The most powerful variable. $10,000 over 30 years: at 5% → $43,219. At 7% → $76,123. At 10% → $174,494. The difference between 7% and 10% is a factor of 2.3× — a seemingly small rate gap that creates a massive outcome gap over long time horizons.

**Time** — Compound interest rewards duration disproportionately. At 7%, your money doubles every ~10.3 years (Rule of 72). In 10 years: 2×. In 20 years: 4×. In 30 years: 7.6×. In 40 years: 15×. The last decade of a 40-year investment generates more growth than the first 30 years combined.

**Contribution Consistency** — Regular contributions matter enormously because each one begins its own compounding timeline. $500/month at 7% for 30 years grows to $611K. Missing just 2 years of contributions (reducing to 28 years at $500/month) drops it to $523K — an $88K loss from $12,000 in skipped contributions, because each missed $500 also loses decades of compounding.

**Compounding Frequency** — More frequent compounding increases FV slightly. $10,000 at 7% for 30 years: annual = $76,123; monthly = $81,165; daily = $81,662. The jump from annual to monthly matters ($5K); monthly to daily is negligible ($497).

## Assumptions & Limitations

- Assumes a **constant rate of return** for the entire period. Real investment returns fluctuate annually. The S&P 500 returned +26% in 2023 but −18% in 2022. Use long-term averages for planning, not recent performance.
- Assumes **contributions occur at the end of each period** (ordinary annuity). If contributions happen at the beginning (annuity due), the FV is slightly higher — multiply by (1 + r/n).
- Does **not account for inflation**. A 7% nominal return with 3% inflation yields approximately 3.9% in real purchasing power growth. $100,000 in 30 years will buy what $41,199 buys today at 3% inflation.
- Does **not account for taxes** on investment gains. Tax-deferred accounts (401k, IRA) grow at the full rate; taxable accounts lose 15–37% of gains annually depending on tax bracket and holding period.
- Does **not model market volatility** or sequence-of-returns risk. The same average return can produce very different outcomes depending on the order of annual returns.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What rate of return should I use for a future value calculation?

Use the long-term historical average for your asset class, adjusted for your risk tolerance. The S&P 500 has returned approximately 10.0% annually from 1926–2024 (nominal) or 6.8% after inflation. A balanced 60/40 stock/bond portfolio has averaged about 8.5% nominal. For conservative planning, many financial advisors recommend using 6–7% for stock-heavy portfolios and 4–5% for bond-heavy portfolios. Avoid using recent high returns (2023–2024) as your baseline — they're above the long-term average and unlikely to persist every year.

### What is the Rule of 72?

The Rule of 72 is a quick mental math shortcut to estimate how long it takes for money to double: divide 72 by the annual return rate. At 6%, money doubles in 72 ÷ 6 = 12 years. At 8%, it doubles in 9 years. At 12%, just 6 years. The rule is accurate within 0.5 years for rates between 2% and 15%. For example, at 7%, the rule predicts doubling in 10.3 years; the exact answer is 10.24 years. It's useful for quick comparisons without a calculator.

### How does compound interest differ from simple interest?

Simple interest calculates returns only on the original principal: $10,000 at 7% earns $700/year, every year, regardless of how long it's invested ($7,000 after 10 years, $21,000 after 30 years). Compound interest calculates returns on the growing balance including prior interest: the same $10,000 at 7% earns $9,672 after 10 years and $66,123 after 30 years — more than three times the simple interest amount. The longer the time period, the wider the gap. See our [Simple Interest Calculator](/calculators/finance/simple-interest-calculator) for a direct comparison.
