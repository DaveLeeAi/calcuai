## What Real Estate Commissions Are and What Your Results Show

Real estate commission is the fee paid to the agents who help a home sale. Historically, the seller paid the full commission — typically 5%–6% of the sale price — which was then split between the listing agent and the buyer's agent. Following the 2024 NAR settlement, commission structures are evolving: sellers are no longer required to offer buyer agent compensation through the MLS, and buyers increasingly negotiate and pay their own agent fees separately.

**Total commission** is the full dollar amount deducted from the sale price. On a \$400,000 sale at 5.5%, that is \$22,000 — the single largest transaction cost for most sellers. At 6%, it jumps to \$24,000. The difference between 5% and 6% on a \$400,000 home is \$4,000, making rate negotiation one of the highest-value conversations in a real estate transaction.

**Agent split** determines how the total commission is divided between the listing (seller's) agent and the buyer's agent. A 50/50 split is traditional, but splits of 60/40 or even 70/30 in favor of the listing agent are increasingly common — especially when the listing agent handles both sides (dual agency) or when seller and buyer negotiate separate fee agreements.

**Brokerage split** is the division within each agent's share between the agent personally and their employing brokerage. New agents typically start at a 50/50 or 60/40 split with their brokerage. Experienced agents may negotiate 70/30 or 80/20 splits, and top producers at some brokerages keep 90%–100% (paying a flat desk fee instead). The brokerage split determines the agent's actual take-home pay from your transaction.

**Seller net proceeds** is your sale price minus the total commission. On a \$400,000 sale with 5.5% commission, you net \$378,000 before other closing costs, mortgage payoff, and taxes. For a complete picture of your walk-away cash, use our [Home Sale Profit Calculator](/finance/home-sale-profit-calculator).

## The Formula

**Total commission:**

$$\text{Total Commission} = \text{Sale Price} \times \frac{\text{Commission Rate}}{100}$$

**Agent splits:**

$$\text{Listing Agent Share} = \text{Total Commission} \times \frac{\text{Listing Split \%}}{100}$$

$$\text{Buyer Agent Share} = \text{Total Commission} - \text{Listing Agent Share}$$

**Agent take-home (after brokerage cut):**

$$\text{Take\text{-}Home} = \text{Agent Share} \times \frac{\text{Brokerage Split \%}}{100}$$

**Seller net proceeds:**

$$\text{Seller Net} = \text{Sale Price} - \text{Total Commission}$$

Where:
- **Commission Rate** = total percentage (typically 5%–6%)
- **Listing Split %** = listing agent's share of total commission (typically 50%)
- **Brokerage Split %** = agent's personal share of their commission (typically 60%–80%)

**Source:** National Association of Realtors (NAR) — 2024 Member Profile; Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) — Real Estate Brokers and Sales Agents compensation data.

## Worked Example

### \$400,000 Home Sale at 5.5% Commission

**Inputs:**
- Sale Price: \$400,000
- Total Commission Rate: 5.5%
- Listing Agent Split: 50%
- Agent/Brokerage Split: 70%

**Step-by-step:**

1. Total commission: \$400,000 × 5.5% = **\$22,000**
2. Listing agent share: \$22,000 × 50% = **\$11,000**
3. Buyer's agent share: \$22,000 − \$11,000 = **\$11,000**
4. Listing agent take-home: \$11,000 × 70% = **\$7,700** (brokerage keeps \$3,300)
5. Buyer's agent take-home: \$11,000 × 70% = **\$7,700** (brokerage keeps \$3,300)
6. Seller net proceeds: \$400,000 − \$22,000 = **\$378,000**

**Interpretation:** Of the \$22,000 in total commission, each agent personally earns \$7,700 after their brokerage takes a 30% cut. The two brokerages collectively retain \$6,600. The seller nets \$378,000 before other closing costs and mortgage payoff. Reducing the commission rate from 5.5% to 5% would save the seller \$2,000 (\$22,000 → \$20,000).

## Key Factors in Real Estate Commissions

**The NAR settlement changed everything.** Since 2024, listing agents are no longer required to offer buyer agent commission through the MLS. Buyers may now negotiate and pay their own agent fees. This is driving down total commission rates in many markets, with some transactions closing at 4%–5% total instead of the traditional 5%–6%.

**Commission rates are always negotiable.** No law sets a standard commission rate. The typical 5%–6% range is a market norm, not a legal requirement. Flat-fee listing services charge \$3,000–\$5,000 regardless of sale price. Discount brokerages offer listing services at 1%–2%. On a \$400,000 home, the difference between a 4% and 6% total commission is \$8,000.

**Agent experience affects the brokerage split.** New agents typically surrender 40%–50% of their commission to their brokerage. After 2–5 years, splits improve to 70/30 or 80/20. Top-producing agents at some brokerages keep 100% and pay a flat monthly desk fee instead. The brokerage split is invisible to the seller — the listing agreement specifies the total commission, not the agent's personal take.

**Dual agency reduces total commission (with caveats).** When the listing agent also represents the buyer, the total commission is sometimes reduced to 4%–5% since only one agent is involved. However, dual agency means neither party has exclusive fiduciary representation, which creates potential conflicts of interest. Dual agency is prohibited in several states.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the average real estate commission rate?

The average total real estate commission in the United States is approximately 5.3%–5.8% of the sale price, according to NAR data. However, rates have been declining since the 2024 NAR settlement and vary significantly by market, property price, and service level. Luxury homes often carry lower percentage rates (4%–5%) because the dollar amount is still substantial. Discount and flat-fee brokerages offer alternatives at 1%–3% for the listing side. Commission rates are always negotiable — there is no legally mandated standard rate.

### Who pays the real estate agent commission?

Traditionally, the seller paid the full commission, which was split between the listing agent and buyer's agent. After the 2024 NAR settlement, this structure is changing: sellers may offer buyer agent compensation, but are no longer required to do so through the MLS. Buyers may negotiate and pay their own agent directly. In practice, the commission is funded from the sale proceeds — so the cost is effectively embedded in the sale price regardless of which party technically pays.

### How much does a real estate agent actually make per sale?

On a \$400,000 home sale with 5.5% total commission, each agent's gross share is \$11,000 (at a 50/50 split). After a 70/30 brokerage split, the agent takes home \$7,700. Additional deductions — self-employment tax (15.3%), income tax, E&O insurance, marketing costs, and transaction fees — can reduce the agent's actual net income to \$4,000–\$5,500 per transaction. The typical agent closes 8–12 transactions per year.

### Can I negotiate a lower commission rate?

Yes. Commission rates are always negotiable. Strategies include: asking for a reduced rate if you expect a quick sale, offering a higher rate to the buyer's agent to incentivize showings while reducing the listing side, using a flat-fee or discount listing service, or negotiating a lower rate for the listing side only (post-NAR settlement). On a \$400,000 home, each 0.5% reduction saves \$2,000.

### What is the difference between commission split and brokerage split?

The commission split divides the total commission between the listing agent side and buyer's agent side (e.g., 50/50 or 60/40). The brokerage split then divides each agent's share between the agent personally and their employing brokerage (e.g., 70% to agent, 30% to brokerage). Both splits reduce the agent's personal income from any given transaction. Sellers negotiate the commission split; agents negotiate the brokerage split with their broker.
