## What Your APY Result Shows

This calculator converts the advertised interest rate (APR) on a savings account, CD, or investment into the effective annual yield you actually earn after compounding. The APY is always equal to or greater than the APR — the gap depends entirely on how often interest compounds.

**Why this matters:** Banks can advertise the same 5.00% rate, but one might compound daily while another compounds quarterly. The daily-compounding account earns an APY of 5.1267%, while the quarterly account earns 5.0945% — a difference of \$3.22 per year per \$10,000 deposited. Over 5 years on \$100,000, that gap widens to roughly \$175. The Truth in Savings Act (Regulation DD) requires all U.S. depository institutions to disclose APY, but not all financial products are covered by this rule — making a quick APY calculation essential for comparing rates across products.

## The Formula

The APY formula prescribed by the Truth in Savings Act:

$$APY = \left(1 + \frac{r}{n}\right)^{n} - 1$$

For continuous compounding:

$$APY = e^{r} - 1$$

Where:

- **APY** = Annual Percentage Yield (effective annual rate, as a decimal)
- **r** = nominal annual interest rate / APR (decimal, e.g., 5% = 0.05)
- **n** = number of compounding periods per year
- **e** = Euler's number (approximately 2.71828)

The effective monthly rate, useful for monthly budgeting:

$$r_{monthly} = (1 + APY)^{1/12} - 1$$

At 5% APR compounded monthly: $APY = (1 + 0.05/12)^{12} - 1 = 5.1162\%$. Compounded daily: $(1 + 0.05/365)^{365} - 1 = 5.1267\%$. The difference between daily and monthly compounding at 5% is just 0.0105 percentage points.

**Source:** FDIC; Truth in Savings Act (12 CFR Part 1030 — Regulation DD); Federal Reserve Board APY disclosure requirements.

**Inputs:** APR = 5.00%, Compounding = Monthly (12)

3. Effective monthly rate: $(1.051162)^{1/12} - 1 = 0.4166\%$
4. APY - APR difference: $5.1162\% - 5.00\% = 0.1162\%$

**Interpretation:** On \$10,000 you earn \$511.62 per year, not \$500.00 — an extra \$11.62 from compounding. The effective monthly rate of 0.4166% is what you'd use for month-by-month balance projections.

**Interpretation:** The 4.50% advertised rate effectively yields 4.6025% annually, earning \$460.25 per \$10,000 versus \$450.00 with simple interest.

## Key Factors That Affect APY

**Compounding Frequency** — More frequent compounding always produces a higher APY, but the marginal gain shrinks quickly. Going from annual to monthly compounding at 5% adds 0.1162%. Going from monthly to daily adds only 0.0105% more. Continuous compounding adds another 0.0004%. For practical purposes, daily and monthly compounding produce nearly identical results.

**Nominal Rate Level** — The APY-APR gap widens as the nominal rate increases. At 1% APR, monthly compounding adds only 0.005% to the APY. At 10% APR, it adds 0.47%. At 20% APR, the difference jumps to 1.94%. This makes APY comparison especially important for credit cards and high-yield instruments.

**Time Horizon** — APY itself doesn't change with time, but the dollar impact of a higher APY compounds over time. A 0.10% APY difference on \$50,000 is only \$50 in year one, but over 10 years with reinvestment, the cumulative gap exceeds \$550.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the difference between APR and APY?

APR (Annual Percentage Rate) is the stated interest rate without accounting for compounding. APY (Annual Percentage Yield) is the effective annual rate after compounding is applied. A 5.00% APR compounded monthly equals a 5.1162% APY. Always compare financial products using APY, not APR, because APY reflects what you actually earn (or owe) over a year.

### Why does compounding frequency matter?

Compounding frequency determines how often earned interest is added to your balance, where it begins earning interest on itself. Monthly compounding adds interest 12 times per year; daily compounding adds it 365 times. More frequent compounding means your interest starts earning its own interest sooner, producing a slightly higher effective yield. The practical difference between daily and monthly compounding is very small — typically less than 0.02% APY at rates below 10%.

### Is APY the same as compound interest?

APY is the result of compound interest expressed as a single annual percentage. It tells you the effective annual rate after all compounding for the year. Compound interest is the mechanism (interest on interest); APY is the standardized measurement of that mechanism's annual impact. A 5% APR compounded monthly produces compound interest that results in a 5.1162% APY.
