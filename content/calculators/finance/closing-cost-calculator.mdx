## What Your Closing Cost Breakdown Means

Closing costs are the transaction fees paid when ownership of a property officially transfers from seller to buyer. They are separate from the down payment and are due at the closing table — typically within 30–60 days of the accepted offer. For buyers, these costs fund everything the lender, government, and third parties require to process, insure, and record the mortgage. For sellers, the largest cost is the agent commission, followed by transfer taxes and title fees.

**Buyer closing costs fall into five categories.** Lender fees include the loan origination fee (typically 0.5%–1% of the loan amount), appraisal fee (\$300–\$500), and credit report fee (\$30–\$50). Title and legal fees cover title insurance (0.5% of home price), title search (\$150–\$300), and attorney fees (\$500–\$1,000). Government fees include recording fees (\$50–\$250) and transfer taxes (varies by state). Prepaid items — often the largest surprise — include 15 days of prepaid mortgage interest, 12 months of homeowner's insurance, and 2–6 months of property tax escrow. Finally, mortgage insurance fees apply based on loan type: conventional loans with less than 20% down require PMI, FHA loans charge 1.75% upfront MIP plus annual premiums, and VA loans charge a one-time funding fee of 2.3%.

**Seller closing costs are simpler but larger in dollar terms.** The 5%–6% agent commission on a \$400,000 home is \$20,000–\$24,000 alone. Transfer taxes, seller-paid title insurance, attorney fees, and pro-rated property taxes add another 1%–2%. Sellers should expect total costs of 6%–8% of the sale price. The "net proceeds" figure in your results shows what you actually take home after all seller costs are deducted. Use our [Mortgage Calculator](/finance/mortgage-calculator) to estimate the buyer's monthly payment after these closing costs are finalized.

## How Closing Costs Are Calculated

Closing costs are the sum of fixed fees and percentage-based charges applied to either the home price or the loan amount:

$$\text{Total Closing Costs} = \sum(\text{Lender Fees}) + \sum(\text{Title Fees}) + \sum(\text{Gov't Fees}) + \sum(\text{Prepaids}) + \sum(\text{Insurance Fees})$$

Key percentage-based components:

- **Loan Origination Fee** = Loan Amount × 1%
- **Title Insurance** = Home Price × 0.5%
- **Homeowner's Insurance (prepaid)** = Home Price × 0.35%
- **Property Tax Escrow (3 months)** = (Home Price × 1.2% ÷ 12) × 3
- **Prepaid Interest (15 days)** = Loan Amount × 7% ÷ 365 × 15
- **PMI (if < 20% down)** = Loan Amount × 0.5% (first year)
- **FHA Upfront MIP** = Loan Amount × 1.75%
- **VA Funding Fee** = Loan Amount × 2.3%

For sellers:
- **Agent Commission** = Home Price × 5%
- **Transfer Tax** = Home Price × 0.2%

$$\text{Total Cash Needed} = \text{Down Payment} + \text{Total Closing Costs}$$

**Source:** Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) — "What are closing costs?" and Loan Estimate form requirements under TILA-RESPA Integrated Disclosure (TRID) rule.

## Worked Example

### Buyer — \$300,000 Home, 20% Down, Conventional Loan

**Inputs:**
- Home Price: \$300,000
- Down Payment: 20% (\$60,000)
- Loan Type: Conventional
- New Construction: No

**Step-by-step:**

1. Loan amount: \$300,000 − \$60,000 = **\$240,000**
2. Origination fee (1%): \$240,000 × 0.01 = **\$2,400**
3. Appraisal fee: **\$400**
4. Credit report: **\$50**
5. Title insurance (0.5%): \$300,000 × 0.005 = **\$1,500**
6. Title search: **\$200**
7. Attorney fee: **\$750**
8. Recording fee: **\$125**
9. Home inspection: **\$350**
10. Flood certification: **\$25**
11. Prepaid interest (15 days at 7%): \$240,000 × 0.07 ÷ 365 × 15 = **\$690**
12. Homeowner's insurance: \$300,000 × 0.0035 = **\$1,050**
13. Property tax escrow (3 months): (\$300,000 × 0.012 ÷ 12) × 3 = **\$900**
14. PMI: \$0 (20% down)
15. **Total closing costs: ~\$8,440**
16. **Total cash needed: \$60,000 + \$8,440 = \$68,440**

**Interpretation:** At 2.8% of the purchase price, these closing costs are on the lower end of the typical 2%–5% range because the 20% down payment eliminates PMI and the conventional loan has no government fees. A buyer putting only 10% down on the same home would add \$1,200 in first-year PMI, pushing total closing costs above \$9,600.

## Ways to Reduce Closing Costs

**Negotiate with the seller.** In buyer-friendly markets, sellers may agree to pay 2%–3% of closing costs as a concession. On a \$300,000 home, that saves \$6,000–\$9,000 at closing. FHA allows up to 6% seller concessions; VA allows up to 4%.

**Shop lender fees.** Origination fees range from 0.5% to 1.5% depending on the lender. On a \$240,000 loan, the difference between 0.5% and 1.5% is \$2,400. Get Loan Estimates from at least three lenders and compare Section A (origination charges) line by line.

**Ask about lender credits.** Some lenders offer credits that reduce closing costs in exchange for a slightly higher interest rate. A 0.25% rate increase might save \$3,000 at closing — worthwhile if you plan to refinance or sell within 5 years.

**Close at the end of the month.** Prepaid interest is calculated from your closing date to the end of that month. Closing on the 28th instead of the 15th saves 13 days of prepaid interest — roughly \$600 on a \$240,000 loan at 7%.

**Skip optional services.** Home warranties (\$300–\$600) and owner's title insurance are optional in most states. Evaluate whether each optional service is worth the cost for your situation.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much are closing costs on a \$300,000 home?

Buyer closing costs on a \$300,000 home typically range from \$7,500 to \$15,000 (2.5%–5% of the purchase price), depending on loan type, down payment size, and state. With 20% down and a conventional loan, expect approximately \$8,000–\$9,000. With less than 20% down, PMI adds \$600–\$1,500. FHA loans add \$4,200 in upfront MIP on a \$289,500 loan (3.5% down). Seller closing costs on the same home run \$18,000–\$24,000, primarily driven by the 5%–6% agent commission.

### Who pays closing costs — the buyer or the seller?

Both buyer and seller pay closing costs, but for different items. Buyers pay lender fees, appraisal, inspection, prepaid items (interest, insurance, escrow), and title insurance on the loan. Sellers pay the real estate agent commission (typically 5%–6% of the sale price), transfer taxes, and their share of title fees. In negotiation, sellers may agree to cover a portion of buyer closing costs — up to 3% on conventional loans, 6% on FHA, and 4% on VA.

### Can closing costs be rolled into the loan?

On FHA loans, the 1.75% upfront mortgage insurance premium is routinely financed into the loan balance. On VA loans, the funding fee can also be rolled in. Other closing costs generally cannot be added to a conventional mortgage, though some lenders offer "no-closing-cost" options where you accept a higher interest rate in exchange for a lender credit that covers fees. This increases your monthly payment for the life of the loan.

### Are closing costs tax deductible?

Some closing costs are tax deductible in the year you purchase. Mortgage points (origination fees paid to reduce your rate) are deductible if you itemize. Property taxes paid at closing (the escrow amount) are deductible up to the \$10,000 SALT cap. Prepaid mortgage interest is deductible. However, title insurance, appraisal fees, recording fees, and home inspection costs are not deductible for a home. Consult a tax professional for your specific situation.

### What is the difference between closing costs and prepaids?

Closing costs are one-time transaction fees: origination, appraisal, title, recording, and attorney fees. Prepaids are advance payments for recurring expenses: homeowner's insurance (typically 12 months prepaid), property tax escrow (2–6 months), and prepaid mortgage interest (from closing date to month-end). Both are due at closing, but prepaids fund your escrow account — money you would have paid anyway over the coming months.

### How can I estimate closing costs before getting a Loan Estimate?

A quick estimate uses 2%–5% of the home price for buyer costs and 6%–8% for seller costs. For a more accurate figure, use 3% of the home price as a starting point for conventional buyers with 20% down, then add 0.5% if your down payment is below 20% (PMI), 1.75% of the loan amount for FHA, or 2.3% of the loan amount for VA. This calculator provides a detailed breakdown that matches the Loan Estimate format lenders provide within 3 business days of application.

### Do closing costs vary by state?

Yes, closing costs vary significantly by state due to differences in transfer taxes, title insurance regulation, attorney requirements, and recording fees. States like New York, Connecticut, and New Jersey have the highest closing costs due to state transfer taxes and mandatory attorney involvement. States like Missouri, Indiana, and Iowa tend to have the lowest. According to CoreLogic ClosingCorp (2024), the national average for buyer closing costs including taxes is approximately \$6,900 on a median-priced home.

### When are closing costs due?

Closing costs are paid at the settlement table on your closing date, typically 30–60 days after the accepted offer. You will receive a Closing Disclosure at least 3 business days before closing with the final dollar amounts. Payment is usually by cashier's check or wire transfer. Your lender may allow some fees to be paid earlier (such as the appraisal at time of order) or rolled into the loan for certain loan types.
