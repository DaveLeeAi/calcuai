## What Your Payment Breakdown Means

This home affordability calculator determines the maximum home price you can finance based on the 28/36 rule — the standard debt-to-income framework that Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac, and most conventional lenders use to qualify borrowers. It answers the question every prospective buyer asks: "How much house can I actually afford without overextending my finances?"

**The 28/36 rule sets two ceilings on your housing costs.** The front-end ratio caps total housing expenses (principal, interest, taxes, insurance, and PMI) at 28% of your gross monthly income. The back-end ratio caps all debt payments — housing plus car loans, student loans, credit card minimums, and other obligations — at 36% of gross monthly income. Your maximum affordable payment is the lower of these two limits. For a \$100,000 income, the front-end cap is \$2,333/month on housing; the back-end cap is \$3,000/month on all debts combined. If you already carry \$800/month in non-housing debts, the back-end rule limits your housing to \$2,200/month — below the front-end cap.

**Down payment size determines more than just loan amount.** Putting down less than 20% triggers private mortgage insurance (PMI), typically 0.5% of the loan amount annually, which reduces your borrowing power by consuming part of your monthly housing budget. A \$60,000 down payment on a \$370,000 home is 16.2% — just below the PMI threshold — adding roughly \$129/month to your housing cost. Increasing the down payment to \$74,000 (20%) eliminates PMI entirely and shifts that \$129 toward principal and interest, increasing your maximum home price. To estimate your monthly mortgage payment for a specific home price, see our [Mortgage Calculator](/calculators/finance/mortgage-calculator).

## How to Use This Calculator

1. **Enter your Annual Household Income** — your total gross (pre-tax) income from all sources. Include both partners' income for joint applications. Lenders use gross income, not take-home pay, so enter the number from your W-2 or tax return, not your direct deposit amount.

2. **Enter your Monthly Debt Payments** — the total of all minimum monthly payments for non-housing debts: car loans, student loans, credit card minimums, personal loans, and any other recurring obligations. Do not include utilities, groceries, or subscriptions — only debts reported to credit bureaus.

3. **Set your Down Payment** — the cash you'll bring to closing. A larger down payment increases your purchasing power and eliminates PMI once you cross the 20% threshold. The median down payment for first-time buyers is 8%, and for repeat buyers it is 19%, according to the National Association of Realtors 2024 Profile of Home Buyers and Sellers.

4. **Enter the Mortgage Interest Rate** — the current rate for your target loan term. Check Freddie Mac's Primary Mortgage Market Survey for this week's average. As of March 2025, 30-year fixed rates average approximately 6.5%.

5. **Choose the Loan Term** — 30-year terms maximize affordability (lower monthly payments), while 15-year terms reduce total interest but require higher monthly payments, lowering your maximum home price.

6. **Adjust Property Tax Rate and Insurance** — property tax rates vary widely by location, from 0.28% in Hawaii to 2.49% in New Jersey (Tax Foundation, 2024). The default 1.2% reflects the national average. Annual homeowner's insurance defaults to \$1,500, close to the national average.

7. **Toggle PMI** — if your down payment is less than 20% of the calculated home price, PMI is added at 0.5% of the loan amount annually. Turn this off if you have a VA loan or plan to use lender-paid PMI.

## The Formula

The home affordability calculation applies the 28/36 DTI rule, then reverses the mortgage payment formula to find the maximum loan amount:

**Step 1 — Maximum monthly housing payment:**

$$M_{\text{max}} = \min\left(\frac{I}{12} \times 0.28,\quad \frac{I}{12} \times 0.36 - D\right)$$

Where:
- **$I$** = annual gross income (dollars)
- **$D$** = monthly non-housing debt payments (dollars)
- **0.28** = front-end DTI ratio limit
- **0.36** = back-end DTI ratio limit

**Step 2 — Maximum loan amount (reverse mortgage formula):**

$$P_{\text{max}} = (M_{\text{max}} - T - Ins - PMI) \times \frac{(1+r)^n - 1}{r \times (1+r)^n}$$

Where:
- **$P_{\text{max}}$** = maximum loan amount (dollars)
- **$T$** = monthly property tax (estimated from home price and tax rate)
- **$Ins$** = monthly homeowner's insurance (dollars)
- **$PMI$** = monthly private mortgage insurance, if applicable (dollars)
- **$r$** = monthly interest rate (annual rate / 12)
- **$n$** = total monthly payments (term in years x 12)

**Step 3 — Maximum home price:**

$$\text{Max Home Price} = P_{\text{max}} + \text{Down Payment}$$

Because property tax and PMI depend on the home price (which depends on the loan amount), the calculation uses iterative convergence — recalculating taxes and PMI based on the estimated home price until the values stabilize.

**Source:** Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB); Fannie Mae Selling Guide (B3-6-02: Debt-to-Income Ratios); Freddie Mac Seller/Servicer Guide (Section 5401.1).

For a deeper explanation of how the fixed-rate mortgage payment formula works, see our [methodology page on mortgage amortization](/methodology/mortgage-amortization).

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Dual-Income Household — \$100,000 Income, \$500/Month Debts

**Inputs:**
- Annual Income: \$100,000
- Monthly Debts: \$500
- Down Payment: \$60,000
- Interest Rate: 6.5%
- Loan Term: 30 years
- Property Tax Rate: 1.2%
- Annual Insurance: \$1,500
- PMI: Included

**Step-by-step:**

1. Gross monthly income: $100,000 / 12 = **$8,333**
2. Front-end limit (28%): $8,333 x 0.28 = **$2,333**
3. Back-end limit (36%): \$8,333 x 0.36 - $500 = **$2,500**
4. Max housing payment: min(\$2,333, $2,500) = **$2,333** (front-end binds)
5. Subtract insurance: \$2,333 - \$125 ($1,500/12) = **$2,208**
6. After iterative calculation with property taxes (~\$370/mo) and PMI (~\$129/mo): Available for P&I ≈ **\$1,709**
7. Max loan: reverse mortgage formula at 6.5%/12 for 360 months ≈ **\$270,400**
8. Max home price: \$270,400 + $60,000 = **~$330,400**

**Interpretation:** With a 6.5% rate and \$500 in existing debts, this household can afford approximately \$330,000–\$370,000 depending on exact tax and PMI calculations. The front-end ratio (28%) is the binding constraint here, not the back-end (36%), because monthly debts are relatively low.

### Example 2: First-Time Buyer — \$70,000 Income, No Debts, Minimal Down Payment

**Inputs:**
- Annual Income: \$70,000
- Monthly Debts: \$0
- Down Payment: \$15,000
- Interest Rate: 6.5%
- Loan Term: 30 years
- Property Tax Rate: 1.2%
- Annual Insurance: \$1,500
- PMI: Included

**Step-by-step:**

1. Gross monthly income: $70,000 / 12 = **$5,833**
2. Front-end limit: $5,833 x 0.28 = **$1,633**
3. Back-end limit: \$5,833 x 0.36 - $0 = **$2,100**
4. Max housing payment: **\$1,633** (front-end binds)
5. With zero debts, the front-end ratio is the constraint. After taxes, insurance, and PMI: Available for P&I ≈ **\$1,150**
6. Max loan ≈ **\$182,000**
7. Max home price: \$182,000 + $15,000 = **~$197,000**

**Interpretation:** A \$70,000 income with minimal down payment limits the buyer to roughly \$197,000 — well below the national median home price of \$412,000 (NAR, Q4 2024). This illustrates why down payment assistance programs and dual-income households are critical for first-time buyers in many markets. Increasing the down payment to \$40,000 would raise affordability to approximately \$225,000 and potentially eliminate PMI.

## Key Factors That Affect Your Results

**Interest Rate** — Every 0.5% increase in mortgage rates reduces your purchasing power by approximately 5–6%. At 6.0%, a \$100,000 income supports roughly \$390,000 in home price; at 7.0%, that drops to approximately \$340,000 — a \$50,000 reduction from a single percentage point. Rate shopping among 3–5 lenders can save 0.25–0.50%, directly translating into thousands more in affordable home price.

**Existing Debt Load** — Monthly debts directly reduce your back-end DTI capacity. Every \$100/month in existing debts reduces your maximum home price by roughly \$15,000–\$18,000. Paying off a \$350/month car loan before house hunting can increase your budget by \$50,000 or more. This is often the highest-leverage financial move a buyer can make.

**Down Payment Size** — Beyond reducing the loan amount, crossing the 20% down payment threshold eliminates PMI, freeing \$80–\$200/month for principal and interest. On a \$300,000 home, 20% down (\$60,000) versus 10% down (\$30,000) not only reduces the loan by \$30,000 but also eliminates approximately \$100/month in PMI — effectively increasing purchasing power by \$15,000 beyond the direct down payment difference.

**Property Tax Rate** — Local property tax rates vary from under 0.3% (Hawaii) to over 2.4% (New Jersey). On a \$400,000 home, the difference between a 0.5% and 2.0% tax rate is \$500/month — an enormous impact on affordability that many buyers overlook when comparing homes across state or county lines.

**Loan Term** — A 15-year mortgage reduces total interest by 50–60% but increases monthly payments by approximately 40%, dramatically lowering your maximum affordable home price. A buyer who can afford a \$350,000 home on a 30-year term may only qualify for \$250,000 on a 15-year term.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- Uses the **standard 28/36 DTI rule** as the qualifying framework. Some loan programs allow higher ratios: FHA loans permit up to 31/43, and VA loans have no front-end limit with a 41% back-end guideline. Manual underwriting exceptions may allow even higher ratios with compensating factors.
- Assumes a **fixed-rate mortgage**. Adjustable-rate mortgages (ARMs) may qualify you for a higher initial amount due to lower starting rates, but future rate adjustments could increase your payment.
- PMI is estimated at **0.5% of loan amount annually**. Actual PMI rates range from 0.2% to 2.0% depending on credit score, loan-to-value ratio, and insurer. Borrowers with credit scores above 760 pay significantly less.
- Does **not include HOA fees**, which can range from \$100 to \$1,000+/month in condos and planned communities. HOA fees reduce your available budget for P&I.
- Does **not account for closing costs**, which typically add 2–5% of the home price. A \$350,000 home may require \$7,000–\$17,500 in additional cash at closing beyond the down payment.
- Property tax and insurance are estimated, not quoted. Actual amounts vary by location and coverage level.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much house can I afford on a \$100,000 salary?

On a \$100,000 annual salary with no existing debts, a 20% down payment, and a 6.5% mortgage rate, you can afford approximately \$420,000 in home price under the 28/36 rule. With \$500/month in existing debts, that drops to roughly \$370,000. With \$1,000/month in debts, it falls to approximately \$310,000. The exact number depends heavily on your local property tax rate, insurance costs, and how much you've saved for a down payment. As a quick rule of thumb, most buyers can afford a home priced at 3–4.5 times their annual gross income, depending on debts and rates.

### What is the 28/36 rule for mortgages?

The 28/36 rule is a lending guideline that sets two limits on how much of your income can go toward debt. The front-end ratio (28%) means your total monthly housing costs — mortgage payment, property taxes, insurance, and PMI — should not exceed 28% of your gross monthly income. The back-end ratio (36%) means all monthly debt payments combined — housing plus car loans, student loans, credit cards, and other debts — should not exceed 36% of gross monthly income. For a \$100,000 income (\$8,333/month gross), the 28% cap allows \$2,333/month for housing and the 36% cap allows \$3,000/month for all debts. These thresholds are used by Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac as standard qualifying criteria for conventional loans.

### Should I put 20% down on a house?

A 20% down payment eliminates private mortgage insurance (PMI), which typically costs 0.5–1.0% of the loan amount annually — saving \$125–\$250/month on a \$300,000 loan. However, saving 20% of a \$400,000 home (\$80,000) takes the median American household approximately 7–10 years. Many financial advisors recommend buying with 10–15% down if you have stable income, adequate emergency savings, and can afford the PMI, rather than delaying homeownership for years to reach 20%. FHA loans allow as little as 3.5% down, and VA loans require zero down payment for eligible veterans. The optimal strategy depends on your local market appreciation rate, rent costs, and opportunity cost of capital.

## Methodology & Sources

This calculator uses the 28/36 debt-to-income framework as specified in the **Fannie Mae Selling Guide (Section B3-6-02)** and the **Freddie Mac Seller/Servicer Guide (Section 5401.1)**, the two Government-Sponsored Enterprises that set conventional mortgage underwriting standards. The mortgage payment formula follows the standard annuity calculation as defined in **Regulation Z (Truth in Lending Act)**. PMI cost estimates are based on average rates published by the **Urban Institute's Housing Finance Policy Center**. Property tax averages reference the **Tax Foundation's** annual state-by-state property tax analysis. Home price and down payment statistics are from the **National Association of Realtors (NAR) Profile of Home Buyers and Sellers, 2024**. Mortgage rate benchmarks reference **Freddie Mac's Primary Mortgage Market Survey (PMMS)**.
