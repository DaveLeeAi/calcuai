## What Your Self-Employment Tax Estimate Shows

Your result breaks down the two components of self-employment tax separately: **Social Security (12.4%)** and **Medicare (2.9%)**. Together, these total 15.3% of your taxable self-employment income — but not 15.3% of your gross earnings. The IRS first reduces your net earnings by 7.65% (the "92.35% adjustment"), which simulates the employer-share deduction that W-2 workers receive automatically. On $75,000 in net earnings, your taxable SE base is $69,263, not $75,000.

The **SE tax deduction** is particularly valuable. You deduct 50% of your self-employment tax as an above-the-line deduction on Form 1040. The **quarterly estimated payment** divides your total tax liability (SE tax plus income tax) by four. ## The Formula

**Step 1 — Taxable self-employment income (the 92.35% adjustment):**

$$\text{Taxable SE Income} = \text{Net SE Income} \times 0.9235$$

This adjustment accounts for the employer-equivalent portion of FICA. W-2 employees only pay 7.65% (their half); self-employed people pay both halves but get this reduction on the base.

**Step 2 — Social Security tax (12.4%, capped):**

$$\text{SS Tax} = \min(\text{Taxable SE Income},\ \$168{,}600 - \text{Other Wages}) \times 0.124$$

The $168,600 is the 2025 Social Security wage base. If W-2 wages already exceed this amount, the SE Social Security portion is $0.

**Step 3 — Medicare tax (2.9%, no cap):**

$$\text{Medicare Tax} = \text{Taxable SE Income} \times 0.029$$

**Step 4 — Additional Medicare tax (0.9% above threshold):**

$$\text{Additional Medicare} = \max(0,\ \text{Total Income} - \text{Threshold}) \times 0.009$$

| Filing Status | Threshold |
|---------------|-----------|
| Single | $200,000 |
| Married Filing Jointly | $250,000 |
| Married Filing Separately | $125,000 |
| Head of Household | $200,000 |

**Step 5 — Total SE tax and deduction:**

$$\text{Total SE Tax} = \text{SS Tax} + \text{Medicare Tax} + \text{Additional Medicare Tax}$$
$$\text{SE Tax Deduction} = \text{Total SE Tax} \times 0.50$$

**Step 6 — Quarterly estimate:**

$$\text{Quarterly Payment} = \frac{\text{Total Tax Liability}}{4}$$

**Source:** IRS Publication 334 — Tax Guide for Small Business (2025); IRS Schedule SE (Form 1040); SSA wage base announcement for 2025.

## Worked Example: $100,000 Freelance Income, Single Filer

**Inputs:**
- Net self-employment income: $100,000
- Filing status: Single
- Other W-2 income: $0
- Business expenses: $0
- Quarterly payments made: $0

**Step-by-step:**

1. **Taxable SE income:** $100,000 × 0.9235 = **$92,350**
2. **Social Security tax:** min($92,350, $168,600) × 0.124 = $92,350 × 0.124 = **$11,451.40**
3. **Medicare tax:** $92,350 × 0.029 = **$2,678.15**
4. **Additional Medicare tax:** max(0, $100,000 − $200,000) × 0.009 = **$0** (below threshold)
5. **Total SE tax:** $11,451.40 + $2,678.15 + $0 = **$14,129.55**
6. **SE tax deduction:** $14,129.55 × 0.50 = **$7,064.78**
7. **AGI:** $100,000 − $7,064.78 = **$92,935.22**
8. **Taxable income:** $92,935.22 − $15,000 (standard deduction) = **$77,935.22**
9. **Estimated income tax:** 10% on first $11,925 + 12% on next $36,550 + 22% on remaining $29,460.22 = $1,192.50 + $4,386 + $6,481.25 = **$12,059.75**
10. **Total tax liability:** $14,129.55 + $12,059.75 = **$26,189.30**
11. **Quarterly estimate:** $26,189.30 ÷ 4 = **$6,547.33**
12. **Effective SE rate:** $14,129.55 ÷ $100,000 = **14.13%**
13. **Effective total rate:** $26,189.30 ÷ $100,000 = **26.19%**

**Interpretation:** At $100,000 in freelance income, self-employment tax alone consumes $14,130 — more than many freelancers expect. The 50% SE deduction saves approximately $1,554 in income tax (at the 22% marginal rate). Quarterly estimated payments of $6,547 keep you on track to avoid the IRS underpayment penalty, which applies if you owe more than $1,000 at filing time.

## Quarterly Estimated Tax Payments

The IRS requires self-employed people to make estimated tax payments if they expect to owe $1,000 or more when filing their return. The four quarterly deadlines for the 2025 tax year are:

| Quarter | Period Covered | Due Date |
|---------|---------------|----------|
| Q1 | January 1 – March 31 | April 15, 2025 |
| Q2 | April 1 – May 31 | June 16, 2025 |
| Q3 | June 1 – August 31 | September 15, 2025 |
| Q4 | September 1 – December 31 | January 15, 2026 |

Missing a quarterly payment triggers an underpayment penalty calculated at the federal short-term rate plus 3 percentage points, compounded daily. The safe harbor rule: pay at least 100% of last year's total tax (110% if AGI exceeded $150,000) to avoid penalties entirely, even if you owe more at filing.

## Deductions That Reduce Your Self-Employment Tax Bill

Self-employed people have access to deductions that directly reduce the income subject to SE tax:

**Above-the-line deductions (reduce AGI):**
- 50% of self-employment tax (automatic — the calculator applies this)
- Self-employed health insurance premiums (100% deductible)
- SEP-IRA or Solo 401(k) contributions (up to $69,000 for 2025)
- Student loan interest (up to $2,500)

**Schedule C deductions (reduce net SE income):**
- Home office deduction ($5 per square foot, up to 300 sq ft, or actual expenses)
- Vehicle mileage ($0.70/mile for 2025) or actual auto expenses
- Business equipment and supplies
- Professional services (accounting, legal, software subscriptions)
- Business insurance premiums

Every $1,000 in Schedule C deductions reduces your SE tax by approximately $141 (at the 14.13% effective rate).

## Common Mistakes Self-Employed Taxpayers Make

**Confusing gross revenue with net earnings.** SE tax applies to your net profit (Schedule C, line 31), not your gross receipts. A freelancer earning $120,000 in gross revenue with $45,000 in legitimate expenses has $75,000 in net SE income — and $45,000 less in SE tax liability.

**Forgetting the 92.35% adjustment.** Many freelancers estimate SE tax as 15.3% of their full net income. The actual taxable base is 92.35% of net income, making the true effective rate 14.13% (15.3% × 92.35%). On $100,000, this saves $765 compared to the naive 15.3% calculation.

**Not accounting for W-2 income against the SS cap.** If you have both W-2 wages and SE income, your employer already withholds Social Security on your wages. You only owe the 12.4% SS portion on SE income up to the remaining wage base. A freelancer with $120,000 in W-2 wages and $80,000 in SE income only pays SS tax on $48,600 of SE income ($168,600 − $120,000).

**Skipping quarterly payments.** The underpayment penalty is not optional. Setting aside 25–30% of each payment received into a dedicated tax savings account is the simplest way to stay current.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the self-employment tax rate for 2025?
The self-employment tax rate is 15.3% — composed of 12.4% for Social Security and 2.9% for Medicare. However, the IRS applies a 92.35% adjustment to your net earnings before calculating the tax, making the effective rate 14.13% of your net self-employment income. High earners (above $200,000 for single filers) also owe an additional 0.9% Medicare tax on income above the threshold.

### Who has to pay self-employment tax?
Anyone with net self-employment earnings of $400 or more in a tax year must pay self-employment tax. This includes freelancers, independent contractors (1099 workers), sole proprietors, gig workers, and general partners. It does not apply to S-corp distributions, rental income (in most cases), or investment income. You report SE tax on Schedule SE attached to your Form 1040.

### Can I deduct self-employment tax?
You can deduct 50% of your self-employment tax as an above-the-line deduction on Form 1040. This reduces your adjusted gross income (AGI), which lowers your income tax — but it does not reduce the SE tax itself. On a $14,130 SE tax bill, the $7,065 deduction saves approximately $1,554 in income tax at a 22% marginal rate.

### How do I calculate quarterly estimated tax payments?
Divide your total expected tax liability (self-employment tax plus federal income tax, minus any withholding from W-2 jobs) by four. Pay each quarter by the IRS deadline using Form 1040-ES. Most freelancers who owe more than $1,000 at tax time are required to make quarterly payments to avoid underpayment penalties.

### Is self-employment tax the same as income tax?
No. Self-employment tax is the self-employed person's equivalent of FICA (Social Security + Medicare). It is separate from and in addition to federal income tax. A freelancer earning $75,000 owes approximately $10,597 in SE tax plus approximately $8,152 in federal income tax — a combined $18,749. W-2 employees pay FICA too, but their employer covers half (7.65%), so the employee only sees 7.65% on their paycheck.

### What is the Social Security wage base for 2025?
The Social Security wage base for 2025 is $168,600. You pay the 12.4% Social Security portion of SE tax only on net self-employment earnings (after the 92.35% adjustment) up to this cap. Earnings above $168,600 are exempt from Social Security tax but still subject to the 2.9% Medicare tax. If you also have W-2 wages, your employer's Social Security withholding counts toward this cap.

### What triggers the Additional Medicare Tax?
The 0.9% Additional Medicare Tax applies to combined earnings (W-2 wages plus net SE income) exceeding $200,000 for single filers, $250,000 for married filing jointly, or $125,000 for married filing separately. This is in addition to the standard 2.9% Medicare tax. Unlike regular SE tax, you cannot deduct 50% of the Additional Medicare Tax.

### How does self-employment tax differ from payroll tax for employees?
W-2 employees pay 7.65% of their wages in FICA taxes (6.2% Social Security + 1.45% Medicare), and their employer pays a matching 7.65%. Self-employed people pay both halves — the full 15.3%. To compensate, the IRS allows the 92.35% adjustment and the 50% deduction. The net effect: self-employed workers pay roughly 2% more in total FICA-equivalent taxes than W-2 employees on the same income.
