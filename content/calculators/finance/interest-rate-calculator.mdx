## What Your Payment Breakdown Means

This calculator solves the reverse problem: given a known loan amount, monthly payment, and term, it determines the annual interest rate (APR). The standard loan payment formula cannot be algebraically rearranged to isolate the rate, so this calculator uses Newton-Raphson numerical iteration to converge on the precise answer.

This is useful when you know what you are paying but do not know the exact rate — common with dealer-financed auto loans, inherited debts, or older loan agreements where the paperwork is unclear. It also helps you verify that a lender quoted rate matches the actual payment they are requiring.

To calculate the payment when you already know the rate, use the [Payment Calculator](/calculators/finance/payment-calculator). For a complete month-by-month schedule, see the [Amortization Calculator](/calculators/finance/amortization-calculator).

## How to Use This Calculator

1. **Enter the loan amount** — the original principal balance (the amount borrowed, before any payments).
2. **Enter the monthly payment** — the fixed amount you pay each month. Do not include insurance, taxes, or fees — principal and interest only.
3. **Enter the loan term in months** — the total number of payments in the loan. For example, a 5-year loan = 60 months.
4. **Read the result** — the calculator displays the annual interest rate (APR), the monthly rate, total payment over the loan life, and total interest paid.

## The Formula

<div className="formula-section">

**The standard loan payment equation:**

$$
M = P \times \frac{r(1+r)^n}{(1+r)^n - 1}
$$

This equation cannot be solved for **r** algebraically. Instead, the calculator uses **Newton-Raphson iteration**:

$$
r_{k+1} = r_k - \frac{f(r_k)}{f'(r_k)}
$$

Where:

$$
f(r) = P \times \frac{r(1+r)^n}{(1+r)^n - 1} - M
$$

The derivative $f'(r)$ is computed analytically. The algorithm iterates until convergence (change in r < 10^{-10}), typically within 5–15 iterations.

Where:
- **M** = known monthly payment
- **P** = known loan principal
- **n** = known number of monthly payments
- **r** = monthly interest rate (solved for)
- **APR** = r × 12 × 100 (annualized percentage)

*Source: Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) for the loan payment formula; Newton-Raphson method is a standard numerical analysis technique described in Numerical Recipes (Press et al., Cambridge University Press).*

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Finding the Rate on an Auto Loan

You financed a car for \$32,000 and pay \$615 per month for 60 months. What interest rate are you paying?

**Known values:**
- P = \$32,000
- M = \$615
- n = 60 months

**Total payment:** $615 × 60 = $36,900
**Total interest:** \$36,900 - $32,000 = $4,900

**Newton-Raphson iteration converges to:**
- Monthly rate r = 0.004167 (0.4167%)
- **Annual rate = 0.4167% × 12 = 5.00% APR**

**Verification:** Plugging 5.0% back into the payment formula:
- M = \$32,000 × [0.004167 × (1.004167)^60] / [(1.004167)^60 - 1]
- M = \$32,000 × [0.004167 × 1.2834] / [0.2834]
- M = $32,000 × 0.01886 = **$603.64**

The slight difference (\$615 vs. \$604) indicates the actual rate is slightly higher than 5.0% — closer to **5.40% APR**. The calculator performs this iteration automatically to find the exact rate.

### Example 2: Verifying a Personal Loan Offer

A lender offers a \$10,000 personal loan with payments of \$322/month for 36 months. They claim the rate is 10%. Is that accurate?

**Known values:**
- P = \$10,000
- M = \$322
- n = 36 months

**Total payment:** $322 × 36 = $11,592
**Total interest:** \$11,592 - $10,000 = $1,592

**Calculator result:**
- **Annual rate = 9.72% APR**

The lender's claimed rate of 10% is slightly higher than the calculated 9.72%. This small difference is common and may be due to rounding or the inclusion of a small origination fee in the APR disclosure. The payments are consistent with approximately 9.7% — the offer is reasonable.

## Key Factors That Affect Your Results

1. **Payment Amount Accuracy** — The rate calculation is highly sensitive to the payment amount. A \$5 difference in monthly payment on a \$25,000/60-month loan can shift the calculated rate by 0.3–0.5 percentage points. Use the exact payment amount from your statement.
2. **Fees Included in Payment** — If your monthly payment includes insurance, servicing fees, or other charges beyond principal and interest, the calculated rate will appear higher than the actual interest rate. Enter only the P&I portion.
3. **APR vs. Interest Rate** — The APR includes certain fees (origination, closing costs) spread over the loan term, making it slightly higher than the nominal interest rate. This calculator solves for the rate that produces the stated payment — it matches APR if fees are included in the payment.
4. **Rounding** — Lenders round monthly payments to the nearest cent, which can cause the calculated rate to differ from the contract rate by up to 0.05 percentage points.
5. **Variable Rate Loans** — This calculator assumes a fixed rate. If your loan has a variable or adjustable rate, the result represents the effective average rate over the term.

## Assumptions & Limitations

- Assumes a fixed interest rate for the entire loan term
- Payment must be principal and interest only — exclude insurance, taxes, and fees
- Assumes payments are made monthly on the same day
- Does not account for compounding methods other than monthly
- The Newton-Raphson solver converges for all reasonable loan parameters but may return 0% if the total payment equals or is less than the principal
- Very high rates (above 100% APR) may require additional iterations

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Why can't you just solve the formula for the interest rate algebraically?

The loan payment formula M = P[r(1+r)^n]/[(1+r)^n-1] contains the rate variable r both as a base and as an exponent, making it a transcendental equation with no closed-form algebraic solution. This is similar to how you cannot algebraically solve x = e^x. Numerical methods like Newton-Raphson start with an initial guess and iteratively refine it until the calculated payment matches the known payment within a tiny tolerance (10^-10 in this calculator).

### What is the difference between APR and interest rate?

The nominal interest rate is the percentage used to calculate interest charges on your balance. The APR (Annual Percentage Rate) includes the interest rate plus certain loan fees (origination fees, points, closing costs) expressed as an annualized percentage. APR is always equal to or higher than the nominal rate. For example, a loan at 6% interest with a 2% origination fee might have an APR of 6.8%. This calculator solves for the rate that reproduces your payment — if your payment includes amortized fees, the result aligns with APR.

### How accurate is the Newton-Raphson method for finding interest rates?

Newton-Raphson converges extremely quickly for loan rate calculations — typically within 5–15 iterations to reach 10-digit precision. For a \$25,000 loan with \$500/month payments over 60 months, the solver finds 7.42% APR with accuracy to 0.0001%. The method is the same one used by financial calculators (HP 12C, TI BA II Plus) and banking software. The only limitation is that it requires a reasonable starting estimate, which this calculator handles automatically.
