## What Your Payment Breakdown Means

This auto loan calculator shows the true monthly cost of financing a vehicle purchase, including the often-overlooked impact of sales tax on your financed amount. It breaks down every dollar into principal, interest, and tax so you can compare dealer offers with confidence.

**The loan term decision is the biggest lever on total cost.** Extending from 60 months to 72 months on a $30,000 loan at 6.5% APR drops your monthly payment by $67 (from $587 to $520), but adds $1,618 in total interest — $4,843 for the shorter term versus $6,461 for the longer one. Stretching to 84 months saves another $52/month but costs $8,098 in total interest, a 67% increase over the 60-month option. The sweetest spot for most buyers is 48–60 months, balancing affordable payments with reasonable total cost.

**Trade-in value delivers a double tax benefit.** In 44 of 50 U.S. states, your trade-in reduces the taxable purchase price. Trading in a $10,000 vehicle on a $35,000 purchase means you pay sales tax on $25,000, not $35,000 — saving $350–$600 in tax depending on your state rate. This calculator applies that deduction automatically. For a broader view of loan payoff strategies, see our [Debt Payoff Calculator](/calculators/finance/debt-payoff-calculator).

## How to Use This Calculator

1. **Enter the Vehicle Price** — the sticker price or negotiated purchase price of the car, truck, or SUV. This is the "out-the-door" price before tax and fees but after any dealer discount. Use Kelley Blue Book or Edmunds for fair market pricing — the average new car transaction price was $48,528 in Q4 2024 per Cox Automotive.

2. **Enter your Down Payment** — the cash you'll put toward the purchase. Financial advisors recommend 20% down on new cars and 10% on used cars to avoid being "upside down" (owing more than the car is worth). On a $35,000 car, that's $7,000 down.

3. **Enter Trade-In Value** — the amount the dealer offers for your current vehicle. Get quotes from CarMax, Carvana, and the dealer to negotiate the best number. The trade-in reduces both your loan amount and your sales tax in most states.

4. **Set the Interest Rate (APR)** — your annual percentage rate from the lender. Average rates in 2025: new cars 6.5–7.5%, used cars 9.5–11.5%, excellent credit (750+) 4.5–5.5%. Get pre-approved at your bank or credit union before visiting the dealer — their rate is often 1–2% lower than dealer financing.

5. **Choose your Loan Term** — 24 to 84 months. Shorter terms mean higher monthly payments but dramatically less total interest. The 60-month term is the most popular choice in the U.S.

6. **Enter your Sales Tax Rate** — your combined state and local sales tax rate. Ranges from 0% (Montana, Oregon, New Hampshire, Delaware, Alaska) to over 10% in parts of Tennessee, Louisiana, and Arkansas.

## The Formula

Auto loan payments use the standard fixed-rate amortization formula:

$$M = P \times \frac{r(1+r)^n}{(1+r)^n - 1}$$

Where:

- **M** = monthly payment (dollars)
- **P** = financed amount = Vehicle Price − Trade-In − Down Payment + Sales Tax (dollars)
- **r** = monthly interest rate = annual APR ÷ 12 (decimal)
- **n** = loan term in months

Sales tax is calculated on the net price after trade-in (in most states):

$$\text{Sales Tax} = (\text{Vehicle Price} - \text{Trade-In Value}) \times \text{Tax Rate}$$

At 0% APR (promotional financing), the formula simplifies to: $M = P / n$.

**Source:** Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) auto lending disclosure guidelines; Federal Reserve G.19 Consumer Credit statistical release for average auto loan rates.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: New Car — $35,000, $5K Down, No Trade-In, 6.5% for 60 Months

**Inputs:**
- Vehicle Price: $35,000
- Down Payment: $5,000
- Trade-In: $0
- APR: 6.5%
- Term: 60 months
- Sales Tax: 6%

**Step-by-step:**

1. Taxable amount: $35,000 − $0 = **$35,000**
2. Sales tax: $35,000 × 0.06 = **$2,100**
3. Financed amount: $35,000 − $0 − $5,000 + $2,100 = **$32,100**
4. Monthly rate: 6.5% / 12 = **0.005417**
5. Factor: (1.005417)^60 = **1.3829**
6. Monthly payment: $32,100 × (0.005417 × 1.3829) / (1.3829 − 1) = **$627.53**
7. Total payments: $627.53 × 60 = **$37,652**
8. Total interest: $37,652 − $32,100 = **$5,552**

**Interpretation:** You pay $5,552 in interest over 5 years, and the $2,100 sales tax is folded into your loan. Your true total cost is $42,652 ($5,000 down + $37,652 in payments). The first month, $174 of your $628 payment goes to interest; by month 60, only $3 does.

### Example 2: Used Car with Trade-In — $22,000, $8K Trade-In, 9% for 48 Months

**Inputs:**
- Vehicle Price: $22,000
- Trade-In: $8,000
- Down Payment: $2,000
- APR: 9%
- Term: 48 months
- Sales Tax: 7%

**Step-by-step:**

1. Taxable amount: $22,000 − $8,000 = **$14,000**
2. Sales tax: $14,000 × 0.07 = **$980**
3. Financed amount: $22,000 − $8,000 − $2,000 + $980 = **$12,980**
4. Monthly rate: 9% / 12 = **0.0075**
5. Monthly payment: **$323.01**
6. Total interest: $323.01 × 48 − $12,980 = **$2,525**

**Interpretation:** The $8,000 trade-in saved $560 in sales tax (versus no trade-in). Total interest of $2,525 on a 48-month term is manageable. Extending to 60 months would drop the payment to $269/month but add $1,180 in interest.

## Key Factors That Affect Your Results

**Credit Score Determines Your Rate** — The single biggest factor in your monthly payment isn't the car price — it's the interest rate, which is driven by your credit score. A buyer with a 750+ score might qualify for 4.5% on a new car; a 620-score buyer faces 12%+ or higher. On a $30,000 loan for 60 months, the difference between 4.5% and 12% is $117/month ($560 vs $677) and $7,000 in total interest ($3,598 vs $10,620).

**Loan Term Length** — Longer terms reduce monthly payments but increase total cost dramatically. The 72- and 84-month terms that have become popular create "negative equity" risk — owing more than the car is worth — because vehicles depreciate faster than these slow payment schedules reduce the balance.

**Down Payment** — Putting 20% down on a new car ($7,000 on $35,000) keeps you right-side-up on equity from day one. The average new car loses 20% of its value in the first year, so a smaller down payment means you'll owe more than the car is worth within months.

**Sales Tax Financing** — Rolling sales tax into the loan is common but costly. On $2,100 in tax financed at 6.5% for 60 months, you pay an additional $366 in interest just on the tax portion. If you can pay tax upfront, do it.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much car can I afford on my salary?

The 20/4/10 rule provides a reliable guideline: put at least 20% down, finance for no more than 4 years (48 months), and keep total transportation costs (payment + insurance + fuel) below 10% of gross monthly income. On a $60,000 salary ($5,000/month gross), that's $500/month for everything. With insurance at $150 and fuel at $100, you have $250/month for the car payment — which supports roughly a $12,000 financed amount at 6.5% for 48 months, or about a $15,000 car with $3,000 down.

### Is a longer auto loan term worth it?

A longer term lowers your monthly payment but almost always costs more overall. On a $30,000 loan at 6.5%: a 48-month term costs $4,114 in interest; 60 months costs $5,192; 72 months costs $6,303; and 84 months costs $7,447. The 84-month loan costs $3,333 more than the 48-month loan — that's an extra 11% of the car's price paid purely in interest. Only extend the term if the monthly savings prevents you from carrying high-interest credit card debt.

### Should I finance through the dealer or my bank?

Get pre-approved at your bank or credit union first, then let the dealer try to beat that rate. Credit unions often offer rates 1–2% lower than dealer financing. However, manufacturers sometimes offer 0% or low-rate promotional financing (usually on new cars only, for well-qualified buyers). Compare the total cost: a $2,000 manufacturer rebate with bank financing at 5% may cost less overall than 0% financing with no rebate, depending on the loan amount and term.
